## INSTRUCTOR DISAGREEMENT: Niche Breadth

### The Disagreement
- **Shan Hanif:** Niche = type of client (e.g., "e-commerce brands"), NOT hyper-specific sub-vertical. "Lead gen for dentists in Ohio" is too narrow.
- **Dee Deng:** Specialize first but check TAM. Over-specialization is dangerous. Expand services over time.
- **Donvesh:** Provides a detailed niche-service matrix with very specific sub-niches (HVAC, chiropractors, dentists).

### Resolution
Two valid approaches depending on service type:
- **For performance marketing/creative agencies:** Shan and Dee's broader niche (e.g., "DTC e-commerce") is correct -- service skills transfer across sub-verticals.
- **For local lead gen/outbound agencies:** Donvesh's specific sub-niches (dentists, HVAC, remodelers) work because each local vertical has unique buying psychology.

**Always verify TAM before committing.**

## IACP Framework (Shan Hanif)

IACP = Ideal Agency Client Profile (like ICP but agency-specific).

### Definition Components
1. What industry/vertical are they in?
2. What is their approximate revenue/size?
3. What platforms are they active on?
4. What specific problems are they experiencing?
5. What is their current approach to the thing you do?
6. Why would they need YOUR specific skills?

### Validation Exercise
Find 3 exact examples of people/companies you could sign RIGHT NOW. Study them deeply: their business, their gaps, their competitors. If you cannot find 3, your IACP is not clear enough.

## Customer Persona Development (Dee Deng)

### Three Lenses
1. **Geographic:** Where do clients/businesses reside? What markets do they sell to?
2. **Demographic (business filters):** Revenue band, team size, product-market fit, unit economics, ad spend levels
3. **Psychographic (founder/stakeholder qualities):** Willingness to listen, collaborative nature, not finger-pointers

### Right Hook's Persona Results (Hard Numbers)

| Metric | Before (no persona) | After (ideal persona) |
|---|---|---|
| Cost per lead | $40 | ~$80 (doubled) |
| Lead-to-call conversion | 70% | 50% |
| Cost per discovery call | $53 | $196 (4x) |
| Call-to-close conversion | 20% | 40% |
| Cost per new client | $265 | $490 |
| Avg 3-month client value | $9,000 | $27,600 |

**[CONTRARIAN] Dee Deng:** All acquisition metrics got WORSE but business got dramatically better. Pure metric optimization without persona work is a trap.

### How to Build Personas
1. Get out of the building -- talk to prospects (Reddit, Facebook groups, Discord, conferences, direct calls)
2. Use early outreach/pitching conversations to gather intel
3. Review wins AND losses to identify success ingredients
4. Do NOT make it a theoretical academic exercise -- battle test through real interactions
5. If cash is king right now, take non-ideal clients but KNOW what ideal looks like

### Niche Testing Timeframe (Donvesh)
- Try a niche for **4 weeks**; if not working, try something else
- You are not married to a single niche
- But every time you change your niche, you are essentially starting from zero

## Audience Research Methods

### Paid Expert Consultation (Donvesh)
- Pay industry experts $200/hour for their time
- Spend $600 for 3 hours with 3 different experts
- Learn ALL the problems, obstacles, and barriers the niche faces
- Most people will not spend $600 talking to people, which is why this is a competitive advantage

### Free Research Methods (Donvesh)
- Reddit: Find niche-specific subreddits, read complaints and questions
- Quora: Search for niche pain points
- Niche blogs: Industry publications and forums

### 2nd-Degree Client Research (Donvesh)
- Talk to your client's clients/leads
- Example: selling FB ads to HVAC companies -> ask homeowners "How do you find HVAC companies?"
- Example: selling to dentists -> ask people "How do you choose a dentist?"

### Research Questions Template (Donvesh)
1. What is the typical story of someone who would buy what you are offering?
2. What problem are you trying to solve?
3. What are they struggling with?
4. What would make them buy?
5. What results do they hope to get?
6. What have they done before to solve this?
7. What would change their life forever?
8. What are their objections and concerns?

## USP Framework (Donvesh)

**[CONTRARIAN] Donvesh:** You do not FIND your USP -- you INVENT it. If you "find" a USP, someone already has it, so it is not unique.

### Rules
- USP cannot be: "I charge less" or "I get results fast"
- Must be more creative than competitors
- USP is the reason clients choose you when someone else is cheaper or better

### Future Potential
- Choose a niche with growth trajectory
- Industries like no-code, integrations, and automation are growing exponentially
- Get in early to emerging industries

## Positioning Your Agency (Dee Deng)

### The Utility Trap
If you look at yourself as a utility (just a service), you will be treated and priced like a utility. Water from the tap is free; bottled water is a multi-billion dollar industry.

### Positioning Grid (Jeffrey Liker)
Two dimensions:
1. **Tangibility:** Intangible experience <-> Tangible deliverable
2. **Customization:** Routine/productized <-> Deeply personalized

As you move from routine to personalized: complexity increases AND pricing power increases.

**Key insight:** It is not the work alone that differentiates -- it is the EXPERIENCE layered on top. Even templatized deliverables can have deeply personalized experiences (bi-weekly strategy calls, Zoom walkthroughs of reports, kickoff meetings, treating their business as your own).

### The "Why Us" Question
Every messaging point (website, social, content, discovery call) must answer: Why should I stay here? Why should I book? Why should I pick YOU?

**The goal: Be THE choice, not A choice.**

## Specialization Strategy (Dee Deng)

### Benefits of Specializing
1. Clear messaging and positioning
2. Efficient systems and SOPs for specialist streams
3. Better hiring (can interview for specific expertise)
4. Factory analogy: your factory is optimized for one type of car

### Over-Specialization Test
Check your TAM. If you are saying "DTC jewelry, but only luxury, and only high-ticket" -- is the pie big enough?

**[CONTRARIAN] Dee Deng:** Taking on out-of-niche clients is like a Chinese restaurant trying to make spaghetti for a $40K customer. You do not have the ingredients. Short-term money, long-term chaos.

### Evolution Path
Start specialized. Get really good at one thing. Then expand services over time. Right Hook evolved: Facebook ads for DTC brands -> expanded to Google, email, influencer, Pinterest, Snapchat, TikTok.
