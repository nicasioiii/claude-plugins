# Communication Frameworks: The Sandwich, Data Backup, and Beyond

## The Three-Point Sandwich: Universal Recommendation Structure

Every financial recommendation follows the same pattern. This structure ensures your advice lands, sticks, and drives action.

### The Framework

**Point 1: Lead with the Recommendation**
- State the action clearly and confidently
- Make it short (1–2 sentences)
- Be direct, not tentative

**Point 2: Back with Three Data Points**
- Provide context (what's the baseline?)
- Show the gap (how far off is current state?)
- Demonstrate the impact (what's the financial consequence?)

**Point 3: Restate the Recommendation**
- Close by restating the action
- Connect back to their goals
- Make it actionable this week, not "someday"

### Why This Structure Works

The sandwich achieves three things:
1. **Memory**: Repetition of recommendation (bookends) helps it stick
2. **Credibility**: Three data points prevent "gut feel" dismissal
3. **Ownership**: The middle section makes the case without being preachy

## Real Example: AR Optimization

### The Situation
Client runs a $4M consulting business. Average AR is 45 days; they're confused about cash flow despite being "profitable."

### The Sandwich

**Lead:**
"We should change your AR collection process. You're leaving money on the table and it's hurting your cash flow."

**Three data points:**
- **Baseline:** "Your AR days outstanding is 45 days. Best-in-class for your industry is 28."
- **Gap:** "That 17-day gap means roughly $189K is stuck in receivables that you've already earned."
- **Impact:** "Reducing DSO by 10 days frees $44K. At 30% cost of capital, that's $13K in annual interest savings, plus the cash improves your buffer for unexpected expenses."

**Restate:**
"Let's implement three changes this month: bill on day 1 of project completion (not month-end), send automated reminder at day 15, and escalate unpaid invoices at day 21. I'll send you the email templates and follow-up script by Friday."

### What Made This Work

- **Not vague:** "You have a cash flow problem" becomes "45 vs 28 days = $189K stuck"
- **Not overwhelming:** Exactly 3 data points (not 10)
- **Not theoretical:** Concrete action items (email templates, script, deadline)
- **Respects their intelligence:** Data leads; they understand the math

## Real Example: Inventory Reduction

### The Situation
E-commerce client holds 140 days of inventory. Gross margin is 42% but cash is tight. They think inventory is "necessary safety stock."

### The Sandwich

**Lead:**
"Your inventory position is too high relative to your demand pattern. Let's right-size it and free up $280K in working capital."

**Three data points:**
- **Baseline:** "You're holding 140 days of stock. Your actual replenishment cycle is 45 days. Top performers in your space hold 70–80 days."
- **Gap:** "That excess 60–70 days of stock represents $280–320K tied up."
- **Impact:** "If we reduce to 80 days—still safe against stock-outs—you free $280K. That capital can go to paid ads (higher ROI than inventory carrying costs) or reduce your credit card balance at 22% APR."

**Restate:**
"Here's the plan: This month, we identify dead stock and run a clearance promotion. Next month, we implement a reorder-point formula based on lead times. By Q2, we're at 80 days and you've freed the capital. I'll build the inventory model this week."

### What Made This Work

- **Validates their concern:** "Safe against stock-outs" addresses the real worry
- **Compares to peers:** "Top performers hold 70–80 days" is credible
- **Gives them control:** Three-month timeline, step-by-step
- **Offers a trade-off:** Capital redeployed to growth, not just cut

## Three-Point Data Backup: How to Get Your Numbers Right

The three data points must be:
1. **Specific** (not "your DSO is high" but "45 vs 28 days")
2. **Comparative** (vs industry benchmark, prior year, or logical baseline)
3. **Consequential** (tied to a financial outcome they care about)

### The Pattern

```
[Current State] vs [Benchmark] = [Gap] = $[Financial Impact]
```

### Examples of Weak vs Strong Data Backups

**Weak backup:**
"Your COGS is too high. Other companies have better COGS."

**Strong backup:**
"Your COGS is 52% of revenue. Best-in-class for your product category is 38%. That 14-point gap represents $560K annually. If we tighten procurement and consolidate vendors, we can close 8 of those 14 points—freeing $320K."

---

**Weak backup:**
"You're overstaffed relative to revenue."

**Strong backup:**
"You have 12 FTEs generating $1.8M revenue ($150K per person). Your industry standard is $200K per person. At benchmark, you'd need 9 FTEs. Three full salaries ($240K) are excess. But before cutting, let's ask: is the problem staffing or pricing? If we raise prices 8%, you hit $1.94M—above benchmark—without cutting anyone."

## Opening Loops: Create Curiosity Before Data

Sometimes, jump straight to the data point first—but lead with a question to "open the loop."

### The Pattern

**Open loop:** "I noticed something unusual in your financials."
→ [Client is now curious and listening]

**Deliver the data:** "Your returns rate is 8% but industry average is 2%. We're returning $180K in products monthly. That's $2.16M annually."

**Close loop:** "Let's diagnose where the returns are coming from—product quality, customer expectations, or shipping damage—and solve it."

---

### Real Coaching Call Example

Client (manufacturing): "I don't know why my margins are shrinking."

**CFO:** "I have a hypothesis. Can I walk you through what I see?" [Open loop]

Client: "Yes."

**CFO:** "Your revenue is up 22% year-over-year, which is great. But your COGS is up 31%—that's the margin compression. Let me show you what's driving it." [Deliver data]

[Shows 3 cost drivers: material costs up 8%, labor up 18%, manufacturing overhead not being absorbed properly]

**CFO:** "Three things are happening here. Material costs went up industry-wide—that's external. Labor costs surged because you hired 4 new people at $55K each to handle the volume. But you're not utilizing them at 80% capacity yet. Once you hit 85% utilization, overhead spreads better. And I see an opportunity: overhead allocation. Which one should we tackle first?" [Close loop + move to action]

## Visual Hierarchy: How to Format Recommendations in Writing

When presenting in email, Slack, or a document, use formatting to guide the eye.

### Strong Format

**[RECOMMENDATION] Renegotiate vendor terms on your top 3 suppliers**

Why: $1.2M in annual spend is underlevered. Standard practice is to renegotiate every 2 years.

Data points:
- Current: Net 30 terms across all vendors
- Benchmark: Net 45–60 is standard when you're the customer
- Impact: Moving to Net 45 frees $50K working capital; Net 60 frees $100K

Next steps:
1. I'll send you the leverage letter by Friday [outlining why you deserve better terms]
2. You call Vendor A, B, C and reference the letter
3. Report back with their responses next week
4. We'll negotiate together if they push back

---

### Weak Format

We should negotiate vendor terms to improve cash flow. You spend $1.2M with your vendors and they probably give you standard terms. If we can move from 30 to 60 days, we'd free more cash and improve our position overall. Let me know when you want to do this.

## Client-Friendly Report Template Structure

When writing monthly or quarterly financial reports, use this structure:

### Section 1: The Good News (First)
- Highlight 2–3 wins or positive trends
- Examples: Revenue up 12%, Customer acquisition up 8%, Gross margin improved 2 points
- Format: "What's working well"

### Section 2: Areas of Focus (Next)
- Present 2–3 concerns or areas needing attention
- Examples: Cash position down, Customer churn up, DSO increasing
- Format: "Where we need to pay attention"

### Section 3: What We're Doing About It (Action)
- For each concern, present the recommendation sandwich (1 action + 2–3 data points)
- Make it clear and actionable
- Assign ownership (you'll do X, client does Y, CFO does Z)

### Section 4: What's Next (Forward Look)
- One metric or initiative for the next reporting period
- Example: "We're going to improve AR DSO from 45 to 38 days by implementing the collection process next month"
- Make it specific and measurable

## Opening Loop Technique: When to Use It

Use opening loops when:
- You're about to deliver surprising or uncomfortable news
- You want the client to be mentally prepared
- The finding is counterintuitive

Don't use when:
- The recommendation is obviously needed ("Let's fix this overdue AP balance")
- The client is already aware of the problem
- You want to move fast in a crisis

## Real Script: Managing Bad News

**Situation:** Discovered that client's "star salesperson" has a 12% close rate vs team average of 28%. Bad for morale, needs careful framing.

**Script (opening loop + sandwich):**

"I looked at your sales pipeline data and noticed something I want to walk you through. Can I share what I see?" [Open loop]

[Client agrees]

"Your sales team is crushing it overall—close rate of 28% is excellent. But I noticed one rep is at 12%. Before jumping to conclusions, I want to understand the context because I might be missing something." [Validation]

"Tell me about [Rep's name]. What's their story? Are they new? Different territory?" [Ask for context]

[Client responds: "Actually, they're handling our hardest accounts, enterprise prospects"]

"Ah, that changes the picture. So the 12% reflects a different sales motion—longer cycles, bigger deals?" [Re-contextualize]

"Let's model this: if their average deal is 3x larger despite lower close rate, they might actually be more valuable to revenue. Can you share the average deal size for that rep vs the team?" [Move to data]

This frames the finding as data to understand together, not judgment.

## The "Redirect Loop" Technique

Sometimes a client will propose a bad idea. Use this pattern to guide without dismissing:

**Client:** "I think we should drop prices 15% to win more market share."

**You (redirect):** "I understand the thinking—volume covers margin. Before we do that, can I show you something?" [Open loop]

[Show data: lowering price 15% requires volume to increase 28% to maintain profit, given your 45% COGS]

"You'd need to acquire 28% more customers at the same cost to break even. Can your ops and marketing support that?" [Guide to reality]

[Client thinks: "Actually, no"]

"So instead of dropping price, what if we looked at: which customers can we raise price on without losing them? Or which product lines have enough margin to absorb a tactical price cut as an acquisition lever?" [Offer alternatives]

This avoids "that's a bad idea" and instead reveals the tradeoff.

## Summary: The Communication Checklist

Before presenting any recommendation:

- [ ] Do I have the recommendation stated clearly (one sentence)?
- [ ] Do I have exactly 3 data points (not 2, not 4)?
- [ ] Does each data point have: baseline, comparative benchmark, and financial impact?
- [ ] Am I restating the recommendation at the end?
- [ ] Is the action specific and assigned (who does what by when)?
- [ ] Have I validated their concern before presenting the data?
- [ ] Am I using opening loops if the news is surprising?

If you hit all of these, your recommendation will land.
