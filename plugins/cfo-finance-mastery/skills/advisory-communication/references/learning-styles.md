# Learning Styles: The What/Why/How/What-If Framework

## The Four Learning Styles

Not everyone processes financial information the same way. Identify which style your client prefers, and tailor your communication to match. Using the wrong style wastes time and causes them to tune out.

---

## Style 1: WHAT Learners (Just the Facts)

### Characteristic
- Wants definitions, structure, and clear categories
- Asks: "What exactly is a DSO?" not "Why does DSO matter?"
- Needs the terminology and the formula
- Gets frustrated with context or storytelling
- Example person: Accountant, engineer, detail-oriented

### How to Identify
- Uses precise language
- Asks for definitions
- Wants to see the formula or structure
- Takes notes on exact terms

### What NOT to do
- Don't spend time on "why" this metric matters to the business
- Don't use analogies
- Don't tell stories
- Do waste time saying "let me explain the big picture first"

### How to Explain Cash Conversion Cycle to a WHAT Learner

"Cash conversion cycle measures how many days your cash is tied up in working capital. It has three components:

- **Days Sales Outstanding (DSO):** (Accounts Receivable ÷ Revenue) × 365
  Your DSO = 45 days. Best-in-class = 28 days. Gap = 17 days.

- **Days Inventory Outstanding (DIO):** (Inventory ÷ COGS) × 365
  Your DIO = 95 days. Optimal = 60 days. Gap = 35 days.

- **Days Payable Outstanding (DPO):** (Accounts Payable ÷ COGS) × 365
  Your DPO = 30 days. Benchmark = 45 days. Gap = -15 days (you're paying too fast).

- **Cash Conversion Cycle:** DSO + DIO − DPO = 45 + 95 − 30 = 110 days

That means your cash is tied up for 110 days before you see it again. Reducing by 20 days saves $180K in working capital."

---

## Style 2: WHY Learners (Strategic Context)

### Characteristic
- Wants reasoning, big picture, and business relevance
- Asks: "Why does DSO matter to my business?"
- Wants to understand the strategy behind the metric
- Gets bored with definitions
- Example person: CEO, founder, visionary

### How to Identify
- Asks "why" frequently
- Wants to understand strategy and priorities
- Skips over details
- Thinks in business outcomes, not numbers

### What NOT to do
- Don't open with the formula
- Don't use jargon without context
- Don't present a bunch of numbers without tying to strategy
- Don't assume they understand why the metric matters

### How to Explain Cash Conversion Cycle to a WHY Learner

"Cash conversion cycle matters because it determines how much working capital your business needs. Here's why:

Your customers buy on payment terms (they don't pay immediately). Your suppliers expect payment on their terms (you can't stretch indefinitely). The gap between when you pay your suppliers and when your customers pay you is your cash need.

For every $100 in monthly revenue at your current cycle, you need roughly $37 in working capital sitting in AR and inventory.

If you could compress the cycle by 20 days, you'd free $180K. That capital goes to:
- No more credit card debt at 22% APR
- Buffer for hiring or marketing
- Less financial stress
- Better negotiating power with lenders

Strategic implication: Fast cash collection isn't just about accounting—it's about business flexibility. Companies with tight cash cycles scale faster because they don't need to borrow as much."

---

## Style 3: HOW Learners (Process & Mechanics)

### Characteristic
- Wants step-by-step procedure
- Asks: "How do we actually improve DSO?"
- Needs to understand the mechanism and process
- Gets frustrated with theory or big pictures
- Example person: Operations person, manager, implementer

### How to Identify
- Asks "how" very frequently
- Wants to know the steps and process
- Takes action quickly once they understand the mechanics
- Needs written procedures

### What NOT to do
- Don't spend time on why it matters strategically
- Don't just show the formula
- Don't present it as a concept; present it as steps
- Prepare a step-by-step process document

### How to Explain Cash Conversion Cycle to a HOW Learner

"Here's exactly how cash conversion cycle works and what we're going to change:

**Current Process (110-day cycle):**
1. You deliver product to customer (day 0)
2. You send invoice (day 1)
3. Customer receives and processes (days 3–5)
4. You wait for payment (days 6–45, average DSO)
5. Meanwhile, you hold inventory (95 days)
6. You pay suppliers (day 30)

**New Process (90-day cycle target):**
1. Deliver product (day 0)
2. Send invoice same day (day 1)
3. Automated reminder email (day 15 if unpaid)
4. Follow-up call (day 21 if unpaid)
5. Stop delivery (day 28 if unpaid)
6. Inventory reduced via bi-weekly forecast (reduce from 95 to 70 days)
7. Extend supplier payment terms (negotiate Net 45 instead of Net 30)

**Your action items:**
- Step 1 (this week): I'll send email templates for days 15, 21, and 28 follow-ups
- Step 2 (next week): You approve the template and we set up automation
- Step 3 (week 3): Sarah implements the process with the ops team
- Step 4 (week 4): We measure first results and adjust

**Expected outcome:** DSO drops from 45 to 35 in 8 weeks."

## Style 4: WHAT-IF Learners (Impact & Scenarios)

### Characteristic
- Wants to see consequences and tradeoffs
- Asks: "What if we cut DSO by 15 days? What changes?"
- Thinks in scenarios and modeling
- Needs to see impact across the business
- Example person: Analyst, financial person, data-driven

### How to Identify
- Asks "what if" constantly
- Wants to see models and scenarios
- Asks about ripple effects
- Wants numbers to test assumptions

### What NOT to do
- Don't just describe the metric
- Don't skip the modeling
- Don't ignore second-order effects
- Prepare scenario comparisons

### How to Explain Cash Conversion Cycle to a WHAT-IF Learner

"Let me show you the impact of improving cash conversion cycle using three scenarios:

**Scenario 1: Current State (110-day cycle)**
- Monthly revenue: $400K
- Working capital locked up: $147K
- Financing need (at 30% cost of capital): $44K annual interest
- Cash buffer: Low (forces reliance on credit cards, 22% APR)

**Scenario 2: Improve DSO from 45 to 35 days (cut by 10)**
- Release $27K from AR
- Cost of capital saved: $8K annually
- Cash stress: Reduced

**Scenario 3: Improve DSO (10 days) + DIO (20 days) + DPO (10 days) = 40-day improvement**
- Release $148K in working capital
- Cost of capital saved: $44K annually
- That's equivalent to a $44K price increase with 20% margin
- Cash buffer: $148K available for growth or debt paydown
- Strategic implication: You could hire 2 salespeople ($80K salaries) without borrowing

What-if we only achieved 70% of this? You'd still free $100K. What would you do with that capital?"

[This invites them to think through the second-order effects]

## How to Identify Someone's Learning Style (In Real Time)

### Technique 1: Ask a Test Question

You explain a concept. Then ask: "What questions do you have?"

**WHAT learner responds:** "Can you clarify what DSO stands for again? And give me the exact formula."

**WHY learner responds:** "Why is this the most important metric? What should it be for us specifically?"

**HOW learner responds:** "How do we actually measure this? What system tracks it? What do we do first?"

**WHAT-IF learner responds:** "If we improve this, what happens to our cash balance? How much working capital would we free?"

---

### Technique 2: Test Them Early

In your first call, present a concept three ways (lightly) and see which version they engage with:

"Let me explain AR optimization three ways and you tell me which resonates:

1. **The formula:** DSO is calculated as (AR ÷ Revenue) × 365. Your DSO is 45 days.

2. **The strategy:** Improving AR is important because it frees cash that's better deployed in marketing or reduces borrowing costs.

3. **The process:** We'll bill on day 1, send reminders at day 15, escalate at day 21. That typically reduces DSO by 10 days in 8 weeks.

4. **The scenario:** If we cut DSO from 45 to 35 days, we'd free $180K, which we could use to eliminate your credit card debt and save $40K annually in interest.

Which of these felt most useful?"

[Their answer reveals their style]

## Toning Matched to Learning Style

Beyond the content, adjust your tone and pacing:

### WHAT Learners
- Tone: Precise, neutral, clinical
- Pace: Slow down on definitions
- Examples: Use technical examples
- Visual aids: Formulas, tables, definitions

### WHY Learners
- Tone: Inspiring, strategic, big-picture
- Pace: Fast (they get bored with details)
- Examples: Use business case examples
- Visual aids: Market comparisons, strategic frameworks

### HOW Learners
- Tone: Practical, action-oriented, step-by-step
- Pace: Medium (they need clarity but not lengthy intro)
- Examples: Use operational examples ("Here's what your team will do...")
- Visual aids: Process flowcharts, checklists, timelines

### WHAT-IF Learners
- Tone: Analytical, curious, exploratory
- Pace: Go deep on scenarios
- Examples: Use modeling examples
- Visual aids: Tables, scenarios, sensitivity analysis

## Real Example: Same Concept, Four Different Presentations

### The Setup
Quarterly review. Client is concerned about cash flow. You're explaining why AR optimization should be priority #1.

### For a WHAT Learner (Accountant-type)
"Your DSO is 45 days. That's calculated as (Accounts Receivable ÷ Revenue) × 365. Best-in-class is 28 days. The 17-day gap means $189K is stuck. To improve, we measure: invoice date, first reminder sent (day 15), escalation (day 21), and payment received. Standard metrics: % invoices paid on time, % over 30 days outstanding."

[Precise. Formulaic. Definitions clear.]

### For a WHY Learner (CEO-type)
"Cash conversion speed is your #1 constraint on growth. You're profitable but cash-constrained because $189K sits in AR. This forces you to manage payroll tightly and use credit cards at 22% APR. If we free that $189K, you have flexibility to hire or invest in marketing without financing costs. Think of it as buying growth without debt."

[Strategic. Connects to big picture. Talks about constraints.]

### For a HOW Learner (Operations-type)
"Here's the process: We bill on day 1 (not month-end). On day 15, our system sends an automated reminder email. On day 21, Sarah calls and asks for payment. On day 28, we escalate and may pause deliveries. You'll track: invoices sent, reminders sent, follow-up calls made, and payment date. We measure weekly."

[Process-focused. Assigns responsibility. Clear steps.]

### For a WHAT-IF Learner (Financial analyst-type)
"Today, you have $189K locked in AR. At 30% cost of capital, that's $57K in annual financing cost. Scenario: if we cut DSO from 45 to 35 days in 8 weeks, we free $60K per month on average, totaling $240K freed. That gives us a $240K buffer to avoid the credit card. Scenario: What if we only achieve 70% improvement? We'd still free $168K."

[Modeling. Scenarios. Impact quantified.]

## Real Coaching Call: Identifying Style in Action

**Client:** "My team says we have a cash flow problem. I don't really understand the numbers. Can you explain it?"

**You:** "Sure. I'll explain it a few ways. What sounds most helpful: the definition and formula, the big-picture reason it matters, the step-by-step process to fix it, or seeing how much cash we'd free if we improve?"

**Client:** "I want to understand how we'd actually fix it. What do we do first?"

[HOW learner identified]

**You:** "Perfect. Here's what happens. Month one, we implement a new invoicing process. Month two, we track results. By month three, we expect cash to improve by $X. Let me walk you through exactly what your team will do starting week 1..."

[Adjusted to HOW style: Process-heavy, action-oriented, step-by-step]

## Multi-Style Clients: Adapt Within the Conversation

Some clients will ask WHAT, then WHY, then HOW questions in the same call. Don't assume one style—answer the question they just asked in their preferred style.

**Client:** "What's DSO?" [WHAT question]
→ Answer with the formula

**Client:** "But why does mine matter more than profitability?" [WHY question]
→ Reframe: It's not vs profitability; fast cash enables profitability

**Client:** "So how do we actually reduce it?" [HOW question]
→ Walk through the process

**Client:** "What if we cut DSO in half? Would that solve all our problems?" [WHAT-IF question]
→ Model the scenario and show limitations

By answering each type of question in kind, you're showing respect for how they think.

---

## Summary: Learning Style Matching Checklist

When presenting financial information:

- [ ] Did I identify the client's learning style (WHAT/WHY/HOW/WHAT-IF)?
- [ ] Am I using the language they prefer (formula, strategy, process, or scenario)?
- [ ] Am I avoiding the styles they don't prefer (no formula for WHY learners, no long process for WHAT-IF learners)?
- [ ] Did I test for style early in the relationship?
- [ ] Am I adapting mid-conversation when they ask questions in a different style?
- [ ] Am I providing examples in their preferred mode?

If you get the learning style right, comprehension and buy-in increase dramatically.
