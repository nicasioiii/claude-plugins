# Resistance Handling: Questions That Guide to Their Own Conclusions

## The Core Principle

When a client resists your recommendation, your instinct is to push harder—to provide more data or stronger arguments.

**This is wrong.** Pushing harder creates more resistance.

The skill is guiding them to their *own* conclusion through questions. When they arrive at your recommendation themselves, they own it completely. And they're far more likely to implement.

---

## The Question-Led Framework

Instead of:
"You're making a mistake. You need to implement this change now."

Ask:
"Help me understand. If you don't make this change, what happens?"

This is magic because:
1. It's not confrontational (no "should")
2. It invites them to think through consequences
3. They often arrive at your recommendation independently
4. They own the decision, not you

---

## Real Example: The Forecasting Resistance

**Client:** "I'm not interested in forecasting. My business moves too fast."

### Wrong Approach (Pushing)
You: "Forecasting is essential. You can't plan cash, hiring, or inventory without it."

Client: "You don't understand how fast my market moves. Forecasting won't work."

[You've dug in. Now they have to defend their position. Resistance hardens.]

### Right Approach (Guiding)
You: "Help me understand. When you make big decisions—like hiring someone or expanding the office—how do you know if you have the cash?"

Client: [Thinks] "I... don't, really. I kind of just do it and hope."

You: "Okay. So if you hired someone today, how would you know in 60 days whether it was the right move?"

Client: "I'd look at cash flow?"

You: "Right. But you'd already have hired them. Forecasting is just doing that thinking before you hire. Does that make sense?"

Client: "Yeah, actually it does."

[They've arrived at the conclusion: forecasting matters. Now they own it.]

## Common Resistance Patterns & Question-Led Responses

### Pattern 1: "I've Already Tried This and It Failed"

**Client:** "I've tried optimizing AR before. It didn't work."

#### Wrong Response
"Things are different now. Let's try again."

[No acknowledgment of why it failed]

#### Right Response
**Your question:** "Help me understand what happened. What was the approach you tried?"

[Listen. Often the old approach was flawed, not the strategy]

Client: "I had Sarah handle collections but she left after 6 months."

**Your follow-up:** "So the approach was good, but the execution was inconsistent. What was different about Sarah compared to who would own it now?"

Client: "The difference is I have Rachel now and she stays in roles."

**Your next question:** "If Rachel owned AR and we had a defined process instead of hoping it happens, could it work this time?"

Client: "Maybe. Yeah, probably."

[You've reframed the old failure as an execution issue, not a strategy issue. And they see why it could work now.]

---

### Pattern 2: "My Industry Is Different"

**Client:** "Standard metrics don't apply to my business."

#### Wrong Response
"These metrics apply to every business."

[You're dismissing their expertise. They'll dig in.]

#### Right Response
**Your question:** "I hear you. What makes your business different?"

[Genuine curiosity. Let them explain.]

Client: "We have really long sales cycles and contract value varies wildly."

**Your follow-up:** "Okay, so standard metrics might not apply directly. Let me ask: regardless of your industry, do you care about how long cash sits in AR?"

Client: "Of course."

**Your next question:** "That's what DSO measures—even with long cycles and variable contracts. We might adjust the benchmarks for your industry, but the metric itself still matters. Does that make sense?"

Client: "Yeah, I guess it does."

[You've validated their concern, then shown that the metric is still relevant. They're now open.]

---

### Pattern 3: "This Will Take Too Much Time"

**Client:** "Implementing AR optimization will require constant attention. I don't have the bandwidth."

#### Wrong Response
"It's worth the investment."

[You're minimizing their real concern about time]

#### Right Response
**Your question:** "What specifically concerns you about the time investment? Walk me through what you think it would require."

[Listen. Often they overestimate the effort.]

Client: "I'd have to monitor collections daily, send reminders, chase down invoices..."

**Your follow-up:** "What if we automated the reminders and you only jumped in on exceptions? How much time would that actually be?"

Client: "Maybe 30 minutes a week?"

**Your next question:** "And if 30 minutes a week frees $60K in working capital, what would that $60K be worth to your business?"

Client: "That's... actually a good trade."

[They've realized their time estimate was wrong and the ROI justifies the effort. They own the decision to proceed.]

---

### Pattern 4: "I Don't Have the Right Data"

**Client:** "I can't implement this without perfect data. My books are a mess."

#### Wrong Response
"Let's get perfect data first."

[Enables perfectionism. Nothing ever happens.]

#### Right Response
**Your question:** "What data do you have today? Let's start there."

[Invite them to work with reality]

Client: "Bank statements. QuickBooks. But it's incomplete and messy."

**Your follow-up:** "Can we work with 80% of the data and improve it later?"

Client: "I guess, but it won't be accurate."

**Your next question:** "What if accuracy is less important than direction? If the data shows AR is 45 days, and the real number is 43 or 47, does that change our recommendation?"

Client: "No, we'd still optimize AR."

**Your next question:** "So let's start with what you have and refine it over time. Sound good?"

Client: "Yeah, that makes sense."

[They've moved from "need perfect" to "good enough." Progress begins.]

---

### Pattern 5: "It's Not Urgent"

**Client:** "This is a good idea, but I have other priorities right now."

#### Wrong Response
"This should be your priority."

[They decide what's urgent, not you.]

#### Right Response
**Your question:** "I understand. Help me understand your priority list. What are the top 3 things you're focused on?"

[Listen and learn]

Client: "Hiring, new product launch, customer retention."

**Your follow-up:** "Got it. And how much is cash flow affecting each of those?"

Client: "Cash flow... is tight with the hiring. Why?"

**Your next question:** "What if improving AR gave you $60K more breathing room? Would that reduce the stress on hiring decisions?"

Client: [Pause] "Yeah, it would actually."

**Your next statement:** "So it's not either/or. AR optimization supports your hiring priority. It's not a separate project; it enables your main project. Want to start next month after the product launch?"

Client: "That works."

[You've reframed: not a new priority, but something that enables their existing priorities. Now it's worth doing.]

## The "What Happens If You Don't Change?" Technique

This is powerful. Instead of selling them on the benefit of change, help them see the cost of *not* changing.

**Client:** "I don't think we need to raise prices. We're doing fine."

**Your question:** "Help me understand what 'fine' means. If nothing changes in your business—revenue, costs, everything flat—where are you in 3 years?"

Client: "We'd maintain our current profit level."

**Your follow-up:** "And if inflation hits (which it does), your costs will rise 3–5% annually. What happens to profit if revenue is flat but costs go up?"

Client: [Pauses] "Profit shrinks."

**Your next question:** "Right. And then what happens when you need to hire someone? Or invest in equipment? Where does that cash come from?"

Client: "I'd have to... borrow probably."

**Your statement:** "Exactly. So by not raising prices now, you're setting yourself up for borrowing costs in 3 years. Which path would you rather take: raise prices 5% now or pay 10% interest on borrowing later?"

Client: "When you put it that way, raising prices now makes sense."

[They've realized the cost of inaction exceeds the cost of the change.]

## The "What's the Real Concern?" Technique

Sometimes resistance isn't about the recommendation itself. It's about an unstated fear.

**Client:** "I don't want to reduce inventory. We need safety stock."

### The Surface Response
"You're right, safety stock is important. But 95 days is excessive."

[You're arguing about inventory levels. The real issue is unstated.]

### The Real-Concern Response
**Your question:** "I hear the safety stock concern. Help me understand what you're worried about. What's the downside you're protecting against?"

Client: "If we run out, we lose customers. We can't recover."

**Your follow-up:** "That's a real concern. How often do we actually stock out today?"

Client: "...Maybe twice a year for certain SKUs?"

**Your next question:** "And when we stock out, what actually happens? Do we lose customers permanently or do they wait?"

Client: "Some wait. But some go to competitors."

**Your statement:** "So we're holding 30 extra days of inventory to protect against an occasional stockout. And the cost of that inventory is $120K annually. What if we invested $30K in a faster reorder system that reduces stockouts from 2 to 1 per year? You'd save $90K net and still have the safety net."

[You've addressed the real concern (losing customers) instead of just the inventory number. Now they're open to solutions.]

## Real Coaching Call: Resistance Handling in Action

**Setting:** CEO of manufacturing business. CFO recommends renegotiating vendor terms.

**CFO:** "I recommend renegotiating vendor payment terms. You're at Net 30; you should be at Net 45."

**CEO:** "I can't do that. My suppliers have been loyal to me. They've supported me through downturns. It would feel like betrayal."

[Resistance rooted in relationship/loyalty, not logic]

**Wrong approach (push harder):**
"Loyalty is good, but business is business. You should negotiate."

[CEO will defend suppliers. Conversation ends.]

**Right approach (guide):**

**CFO:** "I hear that. Those relationships matter. Help me understand something—when you ask suppliers for better terms, what do you think they say?"

**CEO:** "They'd say no? They expect Net 30."

**CFO:** "Have you ever asked?"

**CEO:** "No, never."

**CFO:** "So you're protecting them from a conversation that might never happen. What if I told you that standard business practice is to renegotiate terms every 2 years, and most suppliers expect it?"

**CEO:** "I didn't know that."

**CFO:** "Right. So asking isn't betrayal—it's normal business. Suppliers who won't negotiate with a long-time partner like you probably wouldn't survive anyway. They work with larger accounts at better terms. Here's my suggestion: Call your top 3 suppliers. Say: 'We've been partnering for [X years]. I want to discuss terms that reflect the volume and longevity of our relationship.' What's the worst they say?"

**CEO:** "...No?"

**CFO:** "Exactly. And if they say no, you're no worse off than today. But I'd bet 2 out of 3 say yes. Want to try?"

**CEO:** "Yeah, actually. That doesn't feel like betrayal when you put it that way."

[By guiding through questions, the CEO arrived at: negotiation is normal, not betrayal. They own the decision to proceed.]

## When to Stop Guiding and Just Accept Their Answer

Sometimes you ask all the right questions and they still say no. That's okay. Your job is to illuminate, not force.

**Signs to stop:**
- You've asked 3+ guiding questions and they still resist
- They say "I understand what you're saying, but I'm just not comfortable with it"
- They're getting defensive or emotional
- You're repeating the same question in different ways

At this point, accept it and move on.

**Script:**
"I respect your decision. This isn't my business, it's yours. I wanted to make sure you had the data. If you change your mind, let's revisit. For now, let's focus on what you *do* want to tackle."

[You've kept the relationship intact and showed respect for their autonomy]

## Building Conviction Without Forcing

The goal is to build *conviction*, not just agreement.

**Weak commitment:** "Okay, I'll try it."
**Strong commitment:** "Yeah, I get it. This is the right move. Let's go."

**How to build strong commitment:**
1. Ask guiding questions (they think it through)
2. Wait for *them* to voice the conclusion
3. Validate their conclusion
4. Move to action together

**Don't:**
- Tell them what to think
- Oversell the benefit
- Rush the decision
- Assume they're on board just because they said "okay"

---

## The Resistance-Handling Checklist

When you encounter resistance:

- [ ] Did I get curious instead of pushing?
- [ ] Did I ask about the original failure (if applicable)?
- [ ] Did I explore the real concern (not just the surface objection)?
- [ ] Did I use "what if" and "help me understand" language?
- [ ] Did I invite them to arrive at the conclusion themselves?
- [ ] Did I respect their autonomy if they still said no?
- [ ] Did I keep the relationship intact regardless of the decision?

If you can answer yes to most of these, you've handled resistance well. And often, they'll implement the recommendation—because they chose to, not because you forced them.

---

## Summary: The Power of Question-Led Guidance

The best CFOs don't convince. They guide.

They ask questions that reveal the truth. They let clients arrive at conclusions themselves. They build conviction, not just agreement.

When a client resists your recommendation, that's not a failure. It's an opportunity to help them think more deeply about the decision. And often, they'll thank you for it—because they came to your recommendation on their own.
