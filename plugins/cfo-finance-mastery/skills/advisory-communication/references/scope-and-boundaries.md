# Scope & Boundaries: Managing Expectations, Preventing Creep, and Protecting Your Sanity

## The Scope Creep Problem

Most CFO nightmare scenarios come from one source: unclear boundaries at the start.

You agree to "help with financials." By month three, you're:
- Running payroll
- Handling tax filing
- Managing customer disputes
- Being treated as a general business consultant
- Taking calls at midnight
- Burning out and resenting the client

The problem isn't the client. The problem is you didn't define scope upfront.

---

## What's IN Scope (CFO Work)

**Financial analysis and interpretation**
- P&L, balance sheet, cash flow deep-dives
- Identifying trends and anomalies
- Red flag detection

**Forecasting and scenario planning**
- 12-month projections
- Base/optimistic/pessimistic scenarios
- STEP and Porter's analysis

**Profit lever optimization**
- Working capital improvements (AR, AP, inventory)
- Pricing strategy
- Cost analysis and reduction opportunities
- Margin improvement

**Financing strategy**
- Loan evaluation and comparison
- MCA trap avoidance
- Capital structure decisions
- Cost of capital analysis

**KPI dashboarding and monitoring**
- Key metric definition
- Monthly/quarterly reporting
- Trend analysis

**Strategic financial planning**
- Cash runway planning
- Hiring/expansion financial viability
- Risk identification
- Strategic recommendations

**Financial communication frameworks**
- How to present findings to stakeholders
- How to influence board/partner decision-making
- Positioning recommendations

---

## What's OUT of Scope (Don't Do This)

**Bookkeeping corrections**
- Fixing ledger entries
- Reconciling accounts monthly
- Journal entry adjustments
→ **Refer to:** Bookkeeper or accounting team

**Tax preparation or planning**
- Filing returns
- Quarterly tax payments
- Tax strategy decisions
→ **Refer to:** CPA or tax advisor

**General business consulting**
- Operations decisions
- HR strategy
- Marketing strategy
- Product decisions
→ **Refer to:** Appropriate specialist (COO, HR consultant, marketing consultant)

**Operational execution**
- Implementing changes yourself
- Running day-to-day operations
- Replacing business owner decision-making
→ **Stay in:** Advisory role only

**Moving client funds or running payroll**
- Accessing business bank accounts
- Initiating payments
- Running payroll system
→ **Reason:** Liability risk, separation of duties

**Personal financial planning**
- Individual retirement strategy (outside business scope)
- Personal investment advice
- Estate planning
→ **Refer to:** Personal CFP (Certified Financial Planner)

**Accounting system implementation**
- Setting up QuickBooks
- Data migration
- Training on accounting software
→ **Refer to:** Bookkeeper or accounting software specialist

## Setting Scope Upfront: The Engagement Conversation

This happens in your first meeting or proposal. Be explicit.

### Script: Clear Scope Setting

"Here's what I do as your CFO: [list IN scope items]. Here's what I don't do: [list OUT scope items]. If questions come up in gray areas, I'll recommend a specialist.

On timing: I'm available for [monthly calls, async Slack, scheduled reviews—pick your model]. I work [hours/days], not 24/7. If there's an emergency, we can arrange it, but that's exceptional, not the norm.

On decision-making: I advise; you decide. I present recommendations with data. You determine what to implement and when. I don't run your business.

On execution: I show you what to do. My team or your team implements. I don't do the operational execution myself.

Does this model work for you? Any changes before we start?"

This is unsexy but it prevents 90% of problems.

---

## Real Example: Scope-Setting Email

```
Hi [Client],

I'm excited to work together. Before we start, I want to be clear about how we'll structure this so we're both successful.

MY ROLE — What I Do:
- Monthly financial analysis (P&L, balance sheet, cash flow)
- Quarterly 12-month forecast updates
- Identification of profit levers and recommendations
- Strategic advice on pricing, working capital, and financing
- Monthly reporting and KPI dashboard
- Communication strategy for board/investor updates

OUTSIDE MY ROLE — What I Don't Do:
- Bookkeeping or tax filing (our CPA handles that)
- General business consulting on operations/marketing/HR
- Operational execution (I advise; you implement)
- Moving your funds or managing payroll systems

TIMING & COMMUNICATION:
- Monthly calls: 1 hour, 2nd Thursday at 10am
- Async updates via Slack (I respond within 24 hours)
- Emergency access: You can call, but it's not the norm
- I work 9am–5pm EST, Monday–Friday

DECISION-MAKING:
- I present data and recommendations
- You make all final decisions
- My job is to show implications, not force outcomes

Does this align with what you need? Any adjustments before we start?
```

This takes 5 minutes to write and saves months of friction.

## Scope Creep Red Flags (Catch These Early)

### Flag 1: "Can You Just..."
**Client:** "Can you just run payroll this month? Our person is out."

**Your response:**
"I can't run payroll—that's a liability and control issue. But I can show you how to delegate it to [freelancer/service] or help you set it up for the future. Want help finding someone?"

[You're helpful without breaking scope]

---

### Flag 2: Expanding Without Asking
**Client:** "This month can you also look at inventory? And maybe help us hire?"

**Your response:**
"Inventory optimization is in my wheelhouse. Let's add that to the monthly scope and adjust timing. Hiring is operations—that's outside my role. I can advise on headcount from a cash flow perspective, but you'll lead the hiring decision. Sound good?"

[You're expanding one thing, declining another, keeping it explicit]

---

### Flag 3: After-Hours Requests Becoming Normal
**Client:** [Texts at 9pm] "Quick question about cash..."

**First time:** Okay, you're nice and responsive.

**Third time:** Establish boundary.

**Your response:**
"I saw your message. Let's put that on our Slack thread and I'll get back to you tomorrow. I keep evenings off to stay sharp. Anything urgent can wait until morning, or if it's truly emergency, call me."

[You've set the norm. No guilt required.]

---

### Flag 4: General Business Consultant Expectations
**Client:** "What do you think about our pricing strategy? Should we hire a VP? What about this partnership offer?"

**Your response:**
"Those are great strategic questions. Pricing is in my wheelhouse—I can advise on the financial implications. Hiring/org design and partnership strategy are outside my scope. I'd recommend a business advisor or COO for those. Happy to provide the financial lens for any of them, though."

[You're clarifying what's financial vs. what's operational]

---

### Flag 5: Weekly Requests for Detailed Reports
**Client:** "Can you send a detailed report every week instead of monthly?"

**Your response:**
"I could, but that level of detail weekly will be less accurate and more noise. Here's what I recommend: keep our monthly call cadence for deep analysis, and I'll send you a simple weekly KPI snapshot for cash/AR/inventory only. That gives you frequent touch-points without burying you in data. Sound good?"

[You're protecting quality and your time while staying responsive]

## Managing Difficult Clients: Setting Hard Boundaries

### Type 1: The Demander
**Behavior:** Unlimited access requests, calls/texts constantly, expects immediate responses.

**Your boundary setting:**
"I want to be responsive, and I also need to give you my best work. Here's how we'll stay connected: Slack only (no calls), 24-hour response time, scheduled monthly call. If there's an emergency, you can call. But frequent calls signal we need to restructure. Thoughts?"

[You're being clear without being rude]

**If they persist:**
"I notice we're drifting from our agreed cadence. That's not sustainable for me. Let's reset: what's driving the extra requests? Is it cash flow anxiety? Do we need to add a weekly check-in? Or do we need to find someone with more bandwidth?"

[You're identifying the root, not just pushing back]

---

### Type 2: The Non-Implementer
**Behavior:** Takes your advice but never acts on it. Shows up to meetings but doesn't execute. Blames external factors.

**Your approach:**
**First approach:**
"I notice we've recommended AR optimization three times and it hasn't been implemented. What's blocking it? Is it resource constraints? Disagreement with the strategy? Or just not a priority?"

[Get clarity]

**Second approach:**
If they won't commit: "Here's my concern: if you're not going to implement recommendations, we're wasting both our time. For this to work, you need to own execution. Can you commit to moving forward on [specific recommendation] by [date]? If not, let's pause until you're ready."

[You're drawing a line]

**Exit condition:**
If no progress after two quarters, it's time to transition out. They're not getting value, you're not getting satisfaction.

---

### Type 3: The Disagreer
**Behavior:** Dismisses your data, says "you don't understand my business," resists all recommendations.

**Your approach:**
**First conversation:**
"I sense some resistance to my recommendations. Help me understand: is it the data I'm missing? The strategy doesn't fit your business? Or something else? I want to make sure I'm actually being helpful."

[Get to the real issue]

**Second conversation (if patterns continue):**
"Here's my honest take: our styles might not be a fit. You trust your gut; I'm data-driven. That's not bad—it's a difference. We can work together, but only if you're open to exploring the data even if you don't agree. Can you do that?"

[You're giving them a choice to adapt or part ways]

**Exit condition:**
If they won't engage with data or are combative, exit. Life's too short.

## How to Exit Gracefully (When You Need To)

Sometimes a relationship isn't working. How do you end it professionally?

### The Exit Script

"I want to be direct with you. I don't think I'm the right fit for your needs right now. There are a few reasons:

[Pick 1–2 from below]
- You need someone with more [specific expertise], and I'm not that person
- Our working styles don't align well, and I want you to have someone you trust completely
- The scope keeps expanding in ways I can't sustainably manage, and I don't want to disappoint you

I respect your business and your vision. I want to set you up with someone who's a better match. Here's what I'll do: I'll document my findings and recommendations. I'll introduce you to [CFO/consultant] who I think will be a great fit. And I'll transition cleanly.

You deserve someone who's 100% aligned with you. I don't think that's me right now."

[This is kind, clear, and professional]

## The Scope-Setting Checklist (First Engagement)

Before taking on a client:

- [ ] Have I defined what's IN scope explicitly?
- [ ] Have I defined what's OUT of scope explicitly?
- [ ] Have I discussed communication cadence and boundaries?
- [ ] Have I explained decision-making (I advise, you decide)?
- [ ] Have I discussed execution roles (I advise, you/team executes)?
- [ ] Have I provided this in writing (email or proposal)?
- [ ] Have I asked if they have questions or concerns?
- [ ] Have I set expectations about emergencies (rare, not normal)?

Do this once, in writing. It prevents 90% of problems.

---

## The Ongoing Scope-Management Checklist (Monthly)

To prevent creep over time:

- [ ] Am I staying in my lane (financial advisor, not general consultant)?
- [ ] Are new requests documented and discussed before I say yes?
- [ ] Am I protecting my time (enforcing response time, hours, cadence)?
- [ ] Have I removed underperforming clients (who won't listen or implement)?
- [ ] Am I getting paid fairly for the scope I'm doing?
- [ ] If scope is expanding, is compensation increasing?

---

## Summary: Boundaries Protect Both of You

Clear scope and boundaries:
- **For the client:** Realistic expectations, clearer deliverables, better outcomes
- **For you:** Sustainable hours, energy, confidence, profitability

The best client relationships have clear boundaries. They're not restrictive—they're liberating.

You can give your best work because you know what success looks like. The client knows what to expect. Everyone wins.

Start explicit. Stay consistent. Adjust when needed. Exit when it's not working.

That's how you build a sustainable CFO practice.
