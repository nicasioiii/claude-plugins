# Status Positioning: Establishing the Trusted Advisor Frame

## The Three Possible Relationship Frames

Every CFO-client relationship operates within one of three power dynamics. Your job is to occupy the third frame—and hold it consistently.

---

## Frame 1: The Equal Frame (Peer-to-Peer)

### How It Shows Up
- Client treats you as a peer or colleague
- Language: "You make a fair point, but I'll stick with my plan"
- You feel like you're negotiating, not advising
- Both people have equal decision power

### The Problem
You have no authority to influence decisions. When the client disagrees, the conversation ends. Your insights are "suggestions," not recommendations.

### Real Scenario
You: "I recommend raising prices 8% to improve margins."
Client: "I appreciate the suggestion, but I think we should hold steady."
You: "Okay, whatever you think is best."

[You had no influence]

### Why This Happens
- You positioned yourself as "available if needed" instead of "expert"
- You used tentative language ("maybe we should consider")
- You treated your advice as equal to theirs, not as expertise

---

## Frame 2: The Inferior Frame (Subordinate/Servant)

### How It Shows Up
- Client treats you as a vendor or subordinate
- Language: "Let me know if you need anything, but it's your call"
- You feel like you're serving, not advising
- Client makes all decisions; your role is execution

### The Problem
You're subordinate. You lose all influence. Even if you see a critical problem, you can't speak up without sounding like you're overstepping.

### Real Scenario
You: "I've noticed your COGS is rising faster than revenue. We should investigate."
Client: "Thanks for the heads up. I'll think about it."
You: "Of course. Just let me know if you want me to look deeper."

[You've positioned yourself as support staff, not advisor]

### Why This Happens
- You used permission-seeking language ("Can I show you something?")
- You deferred all decisions to the client
- You treated them as the expert and yourself as the helper

---

## Frame 3: The Superior Frame (Trusted Advisor)

### How It Shows Up
- Client treats you as an expert they rely on
- Language: "That makes sense. How should we approach this?"
- You feel like you're guiding the decision, not serving or negotiating
- Client respects your judgment; you make recommendations, they decide

### The Advantage
You have influence. When you recommend something, they listen. You can speak up about problems without it feeling like overstepping. They value your perspective.

### Real Scenario
You: "Based on the data, here's what I recommend and why. Let's discuss."
Client: "I hadn't thought about it that way. What's the first step?"

[You've influenced their thinking without being pushy]

### Why This Works
- You used confident, direct language
- You positioned yourself as having expertise they don't
- You treated recommendations as data-driven guidance, not suggestions

---

## How to Establish the Superior Frame (Without Arrogance)

The key: Be confident about your expertise while respecting their judgment.

### 1. Use Direct, Confident Language

**Frame 1 language (equal):**
"Do you think we should maybe consider negotiating vendor terms?"

**Frame 2 language (inferior):**
"If you want, I could look into vendor negotiations for you."

**Frame 3 language (superior):**
"I recommend we renegotiate vendor terms. Here's why and how."

---

### 2. Show Your Expertise Subtly

**Not by name-dropping or showing off:**
"In my 20 years as a CFO..."
"Most people don't understand this, but..."

**By asking probing questions they haven't asked themselves:**
"Tell me: what would your cash position be if AR was 28 days instead of 45?"

"If your COGS is 42% and your competitor's is 35%, what does that tell you about your pricing power vs theirs?"

These questions reveal expertise (you know what matters) without being preachy.

---

### 3. Reference Specific Data Points

**Weak (no expertise signal):**
"I think your margins could be better."

**Strong (expertise signal):**
"Your margins are 44% when best-in-class for your product category is 48%. That 4-point gap is worth $160K annually. We can close half of it through vendor consolidation."

Specific data shows you've done the work. Vague comments sound like opinions.

---

### 4. Demonstrate Comparative Thinking

**Weak:**
"Your inventory seems high."

**Strong:**
"Your inventory is 95 days of stock. Your lead time is 45 days. Top performers in your space carry 65 days. You're holding 30 extra days—$120K in unnecessary capital."

Benchmarking shows you've done homework. It's not your opinion; it's data.

---

### 5. Show Deference to Their Judgment

**This is critical.** You can't be an arrogant superior. You must be a confident advisor who respects that they know their business better than you do.

**Weak (sounds arrogant):**
"You should definitely do this. Trust me."

**Strong (confident + respectful):**
"Here's my recommendation based on the data. You know your business and customers better than I do, so you might see risks I'm missing. Let's discuss."

## Real Script: Establishing Superior Frame in First Meeting

**Setting:** First CFO call. Client is skeptical about your value.

**You:** "Before we dive in, I want to be clear about how I work. My job is to show you what the numbers reveal about your business and recommend changes. You decide what to do. I'm not here to manage your business—I'm here to interpret your financials and guide strategy. Sound good?"

[Already establishing: I have expertise, you have control, we're partners]

**Client:** "Sure."

**You:** "I've reviewed your financials. I see three opportunities: cash tied up in AR, vendor terms that are below market, and margin compression from COGS inflation. I'll show you each one with data, and we'll prioritize which matters most to your business. My hunch is AR is the fastest win."

[Expertise signal: You've identified three distinct problems + one priority + data support coming]

**Client:** "How bad is the AR situation?"

**You:** "Your DSO is 45 days. Best-in-class for your industry is 28. That 17-day gap means $189K is sitting in receivables. Reducing by 10 days frees $60K. That's usually a 6–8 week process with the right collection process. Worth exploring?"

[Specific data + realistic timeline + asking permission = confident but respectful]

**Client:** "Yeah, I didn't realize it was that much."

**You:** "Most owners don't until we quantify it. Here's what I'm thinking: Let's make AR our first project. When it's dialed in, we move to vendor terms. Sound like a plan?"

[You're guiding the priority, not demanding. Client feels involved in the decision]

## The Language of Superior Frame

### Opening Statements

**Use this:**
"Based on the numbers, here's what I recommend..."

**Not this:**
"I'm thinking maybe you could consider..."

---

**Use this:**
"Here's my analysis. Where do you see it differently?"

**Not this:**
"What do you think we should do?"

---

**Use this:**
"I recommend we start here. You know if there are reasons it won't work."

**Not this:**
"Do you want me to look at this?"

---

### Responding to Pushback

**Client:** "I'm not sure about that recommendation. My competitors don't do it."

**Frame 1 response (equal):**
"That's a fair point. Maybe it's not the best idea."

[You've given up]

**Frame 2 response (inferior):**
"You know your industry better than I do, so if you don't think it's right, I won't push."

[You're deferring entirely]

**Frame 3 response (superior):**
"Interesting. Most don't, which means it's likely a competitive advantage if you do. But help me understand: what have you observed about competitors that makes you hesitant?"

[You're maintaining your position while being curious about their perspective]

---

## When to Show Vulnerability (And Maintain Frame)

Showing vulnerability doesn't mean weakening your frame. It means being honest about what you don't know.

**Weak (loses frame):**
"I'm not really sure about this. Maybe you should check with someone else."

**Strong (maintains frame):**
"That's a great question. I don't have expertise in [X]. Here's what I'd suggest: let's confirm with a tax advisor, and then I'll incorporate their view into the strategy."

[You've acknowledged a gap while staying in control of the process]

## Real Example: Handling a Client Who Disagrees

**Client:** "I tried AR optimization 3 years ago. It didn't work. My customers pay when they pay."

**Wrong approach (Frame 1 — equal):**
"Fair point. You're probably right. Maybe it won't work now either."

**Wrong approach (Frame 2 — inferior):**
"If you don't think it will work, then let's skip it. Whatever feels right to you."

**Right approach (Frame 3 — superior):**
"I hear that. AR initiatives don't work if the process is wrong or if you don't have someone owning it. What was different three years ago? Were you or someone else running the collection process? And what changed—did you hire new customers or did payment behavior shift?"

[You're not dismissing their experience. You're asking clarifying questions that—if answered honestly—will reveal why it failed (and that conditions have changed)]

**Client:** "Actually, Sarah was handling it then, and she left. And we have different customers now."

**You:** "That changes the picture. So the failure was in execution, not the strategy. And your customer base is different now. This time, I'd recommend we have someone own it explicitly—maybe Rachel—and we measure weekly instead of hoping it happens. You want to run a four-week pilot?"

[You've reframed the old failure as a learning opportunity + set up a low-risk test]

## Positioning Yourself as Trusted Advisor (Not Executive)

Critical distinction: You're an advisor, not a decision-maker. You recommend; they decide.

**Trusted advisor language:**
"Here's my recommendation. You might see reasons it won't work. What's your thinking?"

**Executive language (wrong):**
"We need to do this. Here's the plan."

---

The superior frame is about having influence, not having control. The client respects your judgment but makes the final call.

---

## The Power of Subtle Indirect Influence

Sometimes the most effective way to guide a client is to make *their* idea, not yours.

### The Technique

Instead of: "I recommend we renegotiate vendor terms."

Try: "Tell me—what would happen if we could push our biggest vendor from Net 30 to Net 45 terms?"

The client thinks about it. They realize: that would be great.

Then they say: "Yeah, we should try that."

Now it's their idea. They own it more than if you'd recommended it.

### Real Scenario

**Client:** "Our cash is tight. I'm thinking about getting a loan."

**Direct recommendation (you own it):**
"Before you borrow, let's see if we can free cash through working capital. That's cheaper than a loan."

**Indirect influence (they own it):**
"Borrowing makes sense if you've maxed out working capital. Have you? What would happen if you cut AR from 45 to 35 days? How much cash would that free?"

[Client does the math in their head]

Client: "Wait, that's $60K. Maybe we don't need a loan yet."

[Now it's their idea, and they're more committed]

## Positioning Yourself in Written Communication

The frame shows up in writing too.

### Email Recommending AR Optimization

**Frame 1 (equal):**
"I was thinking... maybe we should look at your AR? You might want to consider a collection process. Let me know what you think."

**Frame 2 (inferior):**
"I reviewed your AR. If you'd like, I can look into optimizing it. Let me know if that interests you."

**Frame 3 (superior):**
"I've identified an opportunity: your DSO is 45 days, best-in-class is 28. That's $189K unnecessarily stuck in receivables.

Here's my recommendation: implement a collection process with reminders at day 15 and escalation at day 21. Expected result: DSO drops to 35 days, freeing $60K, in 8 weeks.

Next step: I'll send you an email template you can customize and we'll discuss rollout timing.

Does this priority make sense for next quarter?"

[Confidence. Data. Clear recommendation. Next step assigned. Respects their decision.]

---

## Summary: How to Hold the Superior Frame

1. **Use direct, confident language** — "I recommend" not "maybe consider"
2. **Back every recommendation with specific data** — Benchmarks, financial impact, not just opinions
3. **Ask probing questions** — Signal expertise without being preachy
4. **Show respect for their judgment** — "You know your business better than I do"
5. **Offer next steps** — Don't leave them hanging; propose action
6. **Listen when they push back** — Ask clarifying questions; maybe they have context you're missing
7. **Maintain the frame consistently** — Don't slip into Frame 1 or 2 when they disagree

If you do these, clients will see you as a trusted expert they want to listen to—without feeling like you're imposing.
