# Balance Sheet Relationships & Working Capital Diagnosis

## The Balance Sheet Equation (The Only Rule That Never Breaks)

**Assets = Liabilities + Equity**

Every transaction impacts this equation. The balance sheet never lies—but the P&L can. A profitable P&L combined with a deteriorating balance sheet signals working capital problems consuming cash.

---

## The Three Balance Sheet Sections

### Assets: What You Own

**Current Assets (convert to cash within 12 months):**

**Cash & Equivalents:**
- Checking account, savings, money market
- What it means: Available liquidity for operations
- Red flag: Declining month-to-month with positive P&L (working capital eating cash)
- Healthy: 1-3 months of operating expenses

**Accounts Receivable:**
- What customers owe you (invoice issued, cash not yet received)
- Formula: (AR / Monthly Revenue) × 30 = Days Sales Outstanding (DSO)
- Red flag: DSO rising (Jan=30 days, Jun=45 days) = collection problem
- Healthy: DSO matching your stated terms (if Net 30, DSO ≈ 30)
- Problem: DSO 60+ = customers taking 2+ months to pay (cash trap)

**Inventory:**
- Products held for sale, raw materials, work-in-progress
- Formula: (Inventory / COGS) × 30 = Days Inventory Outstanding (DIO)
- Red flag: DIO growing while revenue flat (excess stock)
- Healthy: DIO stable or declining
- Problem: Inventory growing 20% while revenue flat = dead cash

**Other Current Assets:**
- Prepaid expenses (insurance, software licenses paid in advance)
- Deposits, refundable amounts, short-term investments

**Long-term Assets:**

**Equipment & Furniture:**
- Computers, machinery, vehicles
- Listed at cost, not current value

**Accumulated Depreciation:**
- Running total of depreciation charges (negative number)
- Example: Bought equipment for $10,000; accumulated depreciation is $2,000 now; "book value" is $8,000
- Red flag: Accumulated depreciation exceeds equipment cost (fully depreciated assets still in use)

**Intangible Assets:**
- Patents, trademarks, goodwill (usually from acquisitions)
- Less relevant for typical small business

---

### Liabilities: What You Owe

**Current Liabilities (due within 12 months):**

**Accounts Payable:**
- What you owe vendors (invoice received, cash not yet paid)
- Formula: (AP / COGS) × 30 = Days Payable Outstanding (DPO)
- Red flag: DPO declining (Jan=45 days, Jun=30 days) = you're paying faster (cash outflow)
- Healthy: DPO matching vendor terms (if Net 30, DPO ≈ 30)
- Opportunity: DPO rising = you're delaying payments (preserves cash, but risks vendor relationships)

**Accrued Expenses:**
- Costs you've incurred but haven't paid yet (payroll, taxes, professional fees)
- Example: January payroll ($20K) usually paid in early February = recorded as accrued expense Jan 31

**Credit Card Balances:**
- Short-term debt on business credit cards
- Should be paid monthly (carrying balance = high interest costs)

**Sales Tax Payable:**
- Tax collected from customers, owed to government
- Red flag: Negative balance = error (you can't owe negative tax). Usually "undeposited funds" miscategorized

**Deferred Revenue:**
- Customers who prepaid for future service/delivery (liability because you owe them)
- Example: Annual software contract paid upfront = deferred revenue
- Good sign: Deferred revenue growing (customers paying in advance)

**Short-term Debt:**
- Portion of long-term loan due within 12 months
- Credit lines
- Merchant cash advances (predatory; avoid)

**Long-term Liabilities:**

**Long-term Debt:**
- Bank loans, bonds, contracts payable beyond 12 months
- Interest-bearing or interest-free

**Other Long-term Obligations:**
- Deferred revenue (multi-year contracts)
- Lease obligations

---

### Equity: What You've Built

**Contributed Capital:**
- Cash and assets you or other owners invested (not debt)
- "Common stock" or "paid-in capital"

**Retained Earnings:**
- Accumulated profits from all prior years (minus any dividends/draws)
- Formula: Prior Year Retained Earnings + Current Year Net Income - Owner Draws = Current Year Retained Earnings
- Red flag: Retained earnings declining while P&L shows profit (owner taking more than profit)
- Red flag: Retained earnings negative (accumulated losses exceed contributions)

## The Cash Crisis Diagnosis: Profit ≠ Cash

**This is the single most important balance sheet lesson:**

A company can be profitable (positive P&L) and insolvent (negative cash flow).

### The Working Capital Triangle

Three balance sheet items control your cash flow:

**1. Accounts Receivable (AR)** — Money customers owe you (negative for cash)
- Increase in AR = cash outflow (customer owes you; you haven't received cash)
- Decrease in AR = cash inflow (collected from customers)

**2. Accounts Payable (AP)** — Money you owe vendors (positive for cash)
- Increase in AP = cash inflow (delayed payment preserves cash)
- Decrease in AP = cash outflow (paid vendors early)

**3. Inventory** — Products you own (negative for cash)
- Increase in inventory = cash outflow (purchased inventory)
- Decrease in inventory = cash inflow (sold inventory)

### The Working Capital Crisis Pattern

```
Month 1:
- P&L shows $50K profit
- But AR increased from $100K to $150K (customers owe $50K more)
- AP stayed flat (no new vendor relationships)
- Inventory flat
- Cash Impact: Profit $50K - AR increase $50K = Net cash $0 (no real cash generated)

Month 2:
- P&L shows $60K profit
- But AR increased from $150K to $220K (customers owe $70K more)
- Inventory increased from $80K to $110K (purchased more stock)
- Decreased AP from $50K to $40K (paid vendors early)
- Cash Impact: Profit $60K - AR increase $70K - Inventory increase $30K + AP decrease $10K = NEGATIVE $40K cash

Result: Company is "profitable" but cash declined $40K. Unsustainable.
```

### Diagnosing the Crisis

**Check the formula:**
```
Working Capital = Current Assets - Current Liabilities
Cash Conversion Cycle = DSO (days to collect) + DIO (days in inventory) - DPO (days to pay)
```

**If cash is declining with positive P&L, one of these is true:**

1. **AR is aging** (DSO rising)
   - Customers taking longer to pay
   - Collections process broken
   - Customer quality declining (risky accounts)

2. **Inventory is building** (DIO rising)
   - Over-forecasted demand
   - Procurement error (ordered too much)
   - Slow-moving or dead stock

3. **You're paying vendors faster** (DPO declining)
   - Vendors demanding faster payment
   - You're paying early for discounts (usually not worth it unless >30% savings)
   - Cash management error

4. **All three simultaneously** (most common in scaling companies)
   - Growth strains working capital
   - AR grows with revenue but collection lags
   - Inventory grows to support revenue forecasts
   - Payables stay same size (no improvement)

## Specific Balance Sheet Red Flags

### Cash Declining While P&L is Positive

**Diagnosis process:**
1. Check AR balance. Is it rising? If AR is up 50% and revenue is up 10%, collection problem is severe.
2. Check inventory. Is it building? If inventory is up while revenue is flat/declining, you have dead stock.
3. Check AP. Is it declining? If you're paying vendors faster while revenue is stagnant, cash is being pulled backward.
4. Check loan balance. Are you repaying debt? Debt repayment is a cash outflow not on P&L.

**Common culprits:**
- New customers with Net 60 terms (instead of your usual Net 30)
- Seasonal inventory buildup (normal if seasonal revenue follows)
- Tax payment (quarterly estimated taxes, annual tax bill)
- Loan repayment (principal payment shows on balance sheet, not P&L)
- Owner draws (equity reduction, not P&L expense)

### Accounts Receivable Aging Problem

**Formula:** DSO = (AR Balance / Monthly Revenue) × 30 Days

**Example:**
```
AR Balance: $150,000
Monthly Revenue: $100,000
DSO = ($150,000 / $100,000) × 30 = 45 days
```

**Interpretation:**
- DSO 45 days = customers take 45 days to pay
- If you offer Net 30 terms, 45-day DSO means collections lag 15 days (problem)
- If you offer Net 45 terms, 45-day DSO is healthy (matches terms)

**Red flags:**
- DSO rising month-to-month (30 → 35 → 40 → 45): Collection problem escalating
- DSO 60+: Customers taking 2+ months; major cash drain
- DSO rising while revenue flat: Collection deterioration (bad customer mix)
- Concentrated AR: Top 3 customers represent 50%+ of AR (concentration risk)

**Solutions:**
1. **AR aging report:** Bucket AR by 30/60/90 days overdue. Call all 60+ day customers.
2. **Collection policy:** Clear policy (invoice immediately, payment 30 days, auto-reminder at 25 days, call at 45 days)
3. **Customer qualification:** New customers with uncertain payment history = Net 15 or Net 20 (not Net 60)
4. **Early payment discount:** "2/10 Net 30" offers 2% discount if paid within 10 days (only worth if you need cash badly)

**Real impact example:**
```
Current DSO: 45 days (customers owe 45 days of revenue)
Target DSO: 30 days

If monthly revenue is $100K:
Current AR: $150,000 (45 days of revenue)
Target AR: $100,000 (30 days of revenue)
CASH FREED: $50,000

One month of focused AR collection = $50K cash improvement (no profit change).
```

### Inventory Management Problems

**Formula:** DIO = (Inventory Balance / COGS) × 30 Days

**Example:**
```
Inventory Balance: $200,000
COGS: $300,000/month
DIO = ($200,000 / $300,000) × 30 = 20 days
```

**Interpretation:**
- DIO 20 days = inventory sits 20 days on average before being sold
- Manufacturing or trading companies: DIO 30-60 days is normal
- E-commerce: DIO 20-30 days is healthy
- Retail: DIO 30-45 days typical

**Red flags:**
- DIO rising (20 → 25 → 30) while revenue flat: Inventory buildup
- DIO 60+ (inventory sits 2 months): Slow-moving or dead stock
- Inventory growing faster than revenue: Over-purchasing

**Solutions:**
1. **Inventory audit:** What's in the warehouse? How old is it? When was it last sold?
2. **Dead stock strategy:** Move slow items via clearance (offer structure, not discounts)
3. **Reorder points:** Only purchase based on actual demand forecasts, not optimistic projections
4. **Lead time tracking:** Account for supplier lead times in reorder calculations
5. **Seasonal adjustment:** Expect DIO to rise pre-season (Black Friday stock), fall post-season

**Real impact example:**
```
Current DIO: 40 days (inventory sitting 40 days)
Target DIO: 25 days

If COGS is $300K/month:
Current Inventory: $400,000 (40 days)
Target Inventory: $250,000 (25 days)
CASH FREED: $150,000

Reduce inventory by 15 days through better forecasting = $150K cash improvement.
```

### Accounts Payable Opportunity (& Risk)

**Formula:** DPO = (AP Balance / COGS) × 30 Days

**Example:**
```
AP Balance: $80,000
COGS: $300,000/month
DPO = ($80,000 / $300,000) × 30 = 8 days
```

**Interpretation:**
- DPO 8 days = you pay vendors within 8 days of invoice
- If vendor terms are Net 30, you're paying 22 days early (cash wasted)
- DPO 30 days = paying on terms
- DPO 45 days = delaying payment beyond terms (risky if overdone)

**Opportunity:**
- If vendors offer Net 30 and you're paying in 10 days, negotiate to pay Net 30 (frees cash)
- Each 10-day delay in payment = 10 days of revenue you keep as cash

**Risk:**
- Vendors remember late payers; they may reduce credit or demand prepayment
- Over-delaying AP damages relationships and can trigger payment holds

**When to NOT negotiate early payment discounts:**
- Discount: "2/10 Net 30" (pay in 10 days, get 2% off)
- Your cost of capital: ~30% annualized
- 2% for 20 days saved = 36% annualized (sounds good)
- BUT if you're cash-constrained, the 2% discount is worth less than keeping the cash

## Capital Allocation & Depreciation Timing

### Depreciation Recording

**Critical error:** Recording depreciation only at year-end (tax-only approach).

**Right way:** Record depreciation monthly or quarterly (matches asset consumption to periods).

**Why it matters:**
```
Equipment purchase: $120,000 (5-year life = $2,000/month depreciation)

Wrong way (annual depreciation):
Jan-Nov P&L: Equipment depreciation = $0
Dec P&L: Equipment depreciation = $24,000 (entire year)
Result: November looks more profitable than December; distorts monthly analysis

Right way (monthly depreciation):
Jan-Dec P&L: Equipment depreciation = $2,000/month
Result: Consistent monthly profitability; accurate comparison

If you acquired equipment mid-year and recorded full-year depreciation in one month,
that month looks artificially unprofitable.
```

**Fix:** Pro-rata depreciation across all months of asset life, not annual lump sum.

### Capital Allocation by Business Type

**Scaling business (growth priority):**
- Retain earnings, reinvest in growth
- Equity on balance sheet stays high
- Dividend = zero
- Example: E-commerce company doubles to $2M revenue; all profit reinvested in inventory and ads

**Mature business (cash extraction priority):**
- Extract profits as dividends/owner draws
- Equity on balance sheet declines as draws exceed reinvestment
- Dividend = 50-80% of profit
- Example: Established HVAC service; owner draws $100K/year from $150K profit

**Startup (burn phase):**
- Negative equity (accumulated losses exceed contributions)
- Funded by debt or investor capital
- No dividend
- Example: SaaS startup loses $200K/year while growing ARR

**Benchmark:** Compare capital allocation across periods. If owner draws exceeded profit for multiple years, business is being harvested (unsustainable).

## Balance Sheet Diagnostic Checklist

- [ ] **Cash and AR reconciliation:** If cash declining and P&L positive, AR or inventory is the problem
- [ ] **AR aging calculation:** Calculate DSO; confirm it matches your payment terms
- [ ] **Inventory aging:** Calculate DIO; identify slow-moving items
- [ ] **AP payment analysis:** Calculate DPO; determine if you should negotiate longer terms
- [ ] **Cash conversion cycle:** DSO + DIO - DPO should be stable or shrinking
- [ ] **Depreciation timing:** Monthly or quarterly, not annual only
- [ ] **Debt schedule:** Know maturity dates and repayment requirements
- [ ] **Equity trend:** Retained earnings growing (profit retained) or declining (draws exceeding profit)?
- [ ] **Current ratio:** Current assets / current liabilities should be >1.5
- [ ] **Three-statement reconciliation:** Net income should increase retained earnings
