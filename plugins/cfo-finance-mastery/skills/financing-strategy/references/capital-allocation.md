# Capital Allocation: Reinvestment vs. Extraction for Concentrated Founders

## The Founder's Blind Spot: Over-Concentration in Own Business

Most founders make a critical capital allocation error: They reinvest all profits back into their business because they believe it's their "safest" investment.

This is a blind spot.

**Reality:**
- Your business is not diversified; it's 100% concentrated in a single entity
- If the business fails, you lose 100% of your net worth
- A diversified portfolio (S&P 500 index, real estate, bonds) beats concentrated holdings on risk-adjusted basis
- Even a mediocre business returning 20% annually, plus a diversified portfolio returning 10%, beats 100% business concentration

**The math:**
- Scenario A (all-in): $1M business at 25% return = $250K/year but 100% correlated to business risk
- Scenario B (50/50 split): $500K business at 25% + $500K portfolio at 10% = $125K + $50K = $175K/year, BUT with 50% uncorrelated risk (portfolio doesn't fail when business struggles)
- Scenario C (full extraction, then diversify): Extract $1M, invest $500K at 10%, keep $500K liquid = $50K/year but extreme liquidity and safety

**Most founders choose A (all-in) and regret it if the business hits trouble.**

## The Capital Allocation Decision Tree

### Starting Point: Do You Have Excess Cash?

```
Do you have monthly operating cash flow > monthly operating needs?
├─ YES: You have potential excess capital
│  ├─ Will it grow the business at >30% ROI?
│  │  ├─ YES: Reinvest (payback <3 years, strategic)
│  │  └─ NO: Extract (capital is better used elsewhere)
│  └─ Is the business at growth stage or mature stage?
│     ├─ Growth: Reinvest (scale while opportunity exists)
│     └─ Mature: Extract (stable margins, slow growth, time to diversify)
└─ NO: Keep all cash in business (build 3-month operating reserve first)
```

### Reinvestment Decision: When to Put Money Back In

**Criteria for reinvestment:**

1. **ROI >30% on capital deployed**
   - New product line: Can it generate 40%+ returns?
   - Market expansion: Will it scale revenue 50%+ in 2 years?
   - Equipment: Will it reduce COGS or enable new revenue streams?

2. **Payback period <3 years**
   - You recover your capital investment within 3 years
   - By year 4+, you're generating pure upside

3. **Growth stage (not mature)**
   - Business is in scaling phase (0–$10M revenue typical)
   - Market opportunity is expanding
   - Competitive advantage is defensible
   - Execution risk is manageable

**Real example (justified reinvestment):**
- Current business: E-commerce, $1M revenue, 40% margins = $400K gross profit
- Reinvestment opportunity: Launch white-label product (lower risk than expansion)
- Expected return: 60% margins, 50% of current business volume = $500K additional revenue at 60% margins = $300K gross profit
- Capital required: $75K (for inventory, automation, marketing)
- Payback: $75K / $300K = 2.5 months (exceptional)
- Decision: **Reinvest** (fast payback, high ROI)

**Real example (unjustified reinvestment):**
- Current business: Services firm, $2M revenue, 35% margins = $700K gross profit
- Reinvestment opportunity: Hire VP of Sales and expand sales team
- Expected return: 20% revenue growth, but requires $200K in salary + $50K in training = $250K investment
- Expected gross profit improvement: $2M × 20% × 35% = $140K
- Payback: $250K / $140K = 1.8 years (acceptable, but tight)
- Growth stage? No (mature services firm, slow growth market)
- Decision: **Extract** (reinvestment is marginal, business is mature, risk-adjusted returns aren't there)

### Extraction Decision: When to Take Money Out

**Criteria for extraction:**

1. **Business is mature**
   - Revenue growth <10% annually
   - Margins are stable and predictable
   - No major strategic opportunities (>30% ROI)
   - Business model is proven

2. **Your net worth is 90%+ in business**
   - You're overly concentrated
   - Diversification would improve risk profile
   - You lack liquidity (can't sell business quickly)

3. **External investment opportunity exists**
   - 401(k) contribution room available (tax-advantaged)
   - Market returns are attractive (not in crash)
   - You have portfolio strategy (not reactive)

**Real example (justified extraction):**
- Business: Staffing firm, $5M revenue, 25% EBIT margins = $1.25M annual EBIT
- Growth: Stable, 5% annual growth (mature)
- Your net worth: $3M in business equity, $100K in personal accounts (98% concentrated)
- Annual distributions potential: Extract $500K (30% of EBIT, sustainable)
- Decision: **Extract $300K this year**
  - Contribute $23K to Solo 401(k) (owner-only, tax-deductible)
  - Invest $100K in diversified portfolio (S&P 500 index, bond fund)
  - Keep $77K as liquid emergency reserve
  - Repeat annually until business/personal net worth ratio is 70/30

## The Reinvestment Framework: Strategic Capex vs. Growth

### Strategic Capex (Equipment, Systems, Infrastructure)

**Characteristics:**
- Fixed assets (equipment, software, facilities)
- Typically 2–5 year payback
- Recurring benefit (reduces COGS or enables scale)
- Lower risk (well-understood ROI)

**Example:**
- Manufacturing equipment: $200K investment, reduces labor costs by $50K/year = 4-year payback
- But enables 30% production capacity increase
- At 40% margins, this opens $300K in new revenue potential
- **Decision: Invest** (strategic, enables growth)

### Growth Capex (Market Expansion, New Products, Acquisitions)

**Characteristics:**
- Offensive investment (grows business, not just optimizes)
- 1–3 year payback (if successful)
- Higher risk (unproven market, competition, execution)
- Requires clear strategy and milestones

**Example:**
- New geographic market: $150K to enter (marketing, hiring, inventory)
- Expected revenue: $400K in year 1, $700K in year 2
- Margins: Same as existing business (40%)
- Payback: Year 1 profit is $160K, covers investment in less than a year
- **Decision: Invest** (high ROI, fast payback, manageable risk)

### Decision: Reinvest or Not

Before deploying capital on reinvestment:

**Question 1: What's the ROI?**
- Be conservative (project 20% lower revenue, 5% margin compression)
- Calculate payback period (months or years)
- If payback >3 years or ROI <30%, don't invest

**Question 2: What happens if 20% of revenue disappears?**
- Stress test: Can the business handle downturn while servicing this investment?
- If investment is equipment with fixed payments, can you service payments in downturn?
- If not, don't invest yet

**Question 3: Do I have proven execution capability?**
- Have you successfully launched new products/markets before?
- Do you have team in place?
- Or is this your first attempt?
- Inexperienced teams should be more conservative (lower risk tolerance)

## Extraction Strategy: Building Liquidity & Diversification

### The Owner Compensation Model

Most business owners take too little salary and too much distributions (or vice versa). Here's the optimal structure:

**Owner compensation formula:**

```
W-2 Salary = Target amount to maximize 401(k) contributions

Distributions = Remaining profit after salary + retained earnings for growth
```

**Example:**
- Business profit: $200K
- Owner W-2 salary: $150K (allows $23K Solo 401(k) contribution if self-employed)
- Remaining profit: $50K
- Distributions: $50K dividend (taxed at capital gains rates, lower than ordinary income)

**Tax impact:**
- W-2 salary: $150K × 35% (fed + state + FICA) = $52.5K taxes, net $97.5K
- Distribution: $50K × 20% (capital gains + NIIT) = $10K taxes, net $40K
- **Total take-home: $137.5K on $200K profit** (vs $130K if all salary)

This structure optimizes:
- Tax efficiency (lower rates on distributions)
- Retirement savings (maximizes 401(k) room)
- Flexibility (distributions can adjust; salary is fixed)

### Portfolio Diversification Strategy

Once you're extracting from the business, diversify systematically:

**Year 1–2 (Accumulation):**
- Extract $50K–$100K annually
- Max out Solo 401(k): $23K individual, $69K self-employed (if self-employed)
- Remaining funds: Invest in broad index funds (S&P 500, Total Market)
- Target: Build $100K–$200K outside business within 2 years

**Year 3–5 (Expansion):**
- Continue extractions ($50K–$100K annually)
- 401(k) is maxed out (limit reached)
- Excess funds: Diversified portfolio (60% stocks, 30% bonds, 10% cash)
- Target: Build $300K–$500K outside business

**Year 5+ (Rebalancing):**
- Continue moderate extractions
- Rebalance portfolio annually (maintain 60/30/10 split)
- Consider real estate investment (separate from business)
- Target: Build net worth outside business to match business value (1:1 ratio)

**Concentration risk reduction target:**
- Year 1: 95% in business, 5% outside
- Year 3: 85% in business, 15% outside
- Year 5: 75% in business, 25% outside
- Year 10: 60% in business, 40% outside (ideal)

### Real Example: Jewelry Company Founder

**Starting point:**
- Business value: $3M (based on 5× EBITDA multiple)
- Owner net worth: $3M in business, $50K personal = 98% concentrated

**Year 1:**
- Extract: $150K (after reinvestment needs)
- 401(k): $69K (max)
- Brokerage investment: $81K (stocks + bonds)
- Result: $50K + $81K = $131K outside business (4% diversification)

**Year 3:**
- Cumulative extractions: $450K
- 401(k) balance: $207K (3 years of maxing)
- Brokerage balance: $243K
- Result: $450K outside business (13% diversification)

**Year 5:**
- Cumulative extractions: $750K
- 401(k) balance: $345K
- Brokerage + other: $405K
- Result: $750K outside business (20% diversification)

**Outcome after 5 years:** Owner went from 98% concentrated to 80% concentrated, built $750K in diversified assets, reduced personal financial risk by 20 percentage points.

## Special Situation: The Highly Profitable Business

### When You're Generating 40%+ EBIT Margins

**Problem:** You're generating more profit than you can reinvest productively

**Example:**
- Business: $2M revenue, 40% EBIT margin = $800K profit annually
- Reinvestment opportunities: Only $200K available (limited growth options)
- Excess profit: $600K

**You have four options:**

**Option 1: Reinvest aggressively (accept lower ROI)**
- Take lower-return opportunities (20–25% ROI instead of 40%)
- Risk: Capital gets deployed poorly, returns disappoint

**Option 2: Extract and diversify**
- Take $300K annually as distribution
- Invest in portfolio ($100K), 401(k) ($23K), emergency fund ($177K)
- Risk: Lower business control and growth

**Option 3: Debt-financed growth (leverage)**
- Borrow $500K at 8% to fund expansion
- Use debt to scale business without tying up excess capital
- Risk: Leverage increases, makes business fragile

**Option 4: Share with employees (equity, ESOP)**
- Distribute excess profit as bonus
- Offer equity options (small stakes to employees)
- Create ownership culture
- Risk: Dilution, complexity

**Recommendation:** Combination of 1 + 2
- Reinvest $300K (growth opportunities, even at 25% ROI)
- Extract $300K (diversification, owner returns)
- Use $200K to strengthen balance sheet (cash reserves)

## Liquidity Planning: Thinking Beyond the Business

### The Founder's Liquidity Dilemma

Business equity is **illiquid**. You can't sell a $5M business in a week. This creates risk:

- Medical emergency: Need $50K immediately (must sell business? Take out loan? Can't rely on business equity)
- Job loss for spouse: Need 6 months of income ($30K) while business adjusts
- Stock market crash: Personal portfolio is down 30%, you need cash for leverage (illiquidity forces you to lose)

**Solution:** Build external liquidity reserves (3–6 months operating expenses).

### Liquidity Reserve Targets

**Rule: Keep 3–6 months of personal operating expenses in liquid accounts**

**Example:**
- Monthly personal expenses: $10K (living expenses, taxes)
- 6-month reserve target: $60K
- Where to keep it: High-yield savings account (earning 4–5% APY)
- Status: Separate from business, separate from brokerage

**Why separate?**
- Brokerage accounts are invested (can lose 20% in downturn)
- Business accounts are for operations (can't touch for personal use)
- Liquid reserve is emergency buffer (available immediately, no loss of principal)

### Building Liquidity Systematically

**Year 1–2:** Build 3-month reserve
- Extract $30K annually (beyond reinvestment)
- Keep in high-yield savings
- Target: $60K (3 months at $10K/month spending)

**Year 3–5:** Build 6-month reserve
- Extract $20K+ annually (ongoing)
- Build to $120K
- Once hit, redirect excess to investment portfolio

**After 6-month reserve is built:** All extractions go to portfolio diversification

## Tax-Advantaged Capital Allocation

### Solo 401(k) (For Self-Employed/Business Owners)

**Contribution limits (2024):**
- Employee deferral: $23,000 (if under 50) or $30,500 (if 50+)
- Self-employed contribution: Up to 20% of net self-employment income
- **Maximum combined: ~$69,000** (varies by income)

**Example:**
- Net self-employment income: $200K
- Employee deferral: $23K
- Self-employed contribution: 20% × ($200K - 1/2 SE tax) = ~$18.6K
- **Total: ~$41.6K** (less than max, limited by income)

**How to optimize:**
- Try to reach $23K W-2 salary (captures full employee deferral)
- Self-employed contribution = 20% of (net income - 1/2 SE tax)
- Goal: Max out every year (tax-deductible contributions reduce taxable income)

### Backdoor Roth Conversion

If your income exceeds Roth IRA limits:

1. Contribute to traditional IRA (non-deductible, if over income limit)
2. Immediately convert to Roth IRA
3. Pay taxes on the conversion (minimal if you have no pre-tax IRA balance)
4. **Result:** $7,000 added to Roth (tax-free growth forever)

**Caution:** Pro-rata rule applies if you have existing traditional IRA balance. Consult CPA.

### Health Savings Account (HSA) Triple Tax Advantage

If you have a high-deductible health plan:

**HSA contributions:**
- Individual: $4,150 (2024)
- Family: $8,300 (2024)
- **Employer contribution:** If self-employed, contribute additional amount (S-corp optimization, complex)

**Tax advantages:**
1. **Deductible going in** (reduces taxable income)
2. **Tax-free growth** (investment earnings aren't taxed)
3. **Tax-free withdrawal** (for qualified medical expenses)

**This is the only triple-tax-advantaged account.** Max it out annually if available.

## The Founder's Exit Readiness Checklist

When should you start thinking about exits or transitions?

**Checklist for extraction/succession planning:**

- [ ] Business is 5+ years old and mature (predictable, stable)
- [ ] Revenue is $2M+ annually (valuable enough to sell, if desired)
- [ ] You have strong management team (doesn't depend solely on you)
- [ ] EBITDA margins are >20% (desirable to buyers)
- [ ] Systems are documented (not in your head)
- [ ] You're 80% concentrated in business (time to diversify)
- [ ] You have debt under control (or have plan to pay down)
- [ ] Your personal net worth outside business is <$500K (need to build)

**If you check 5+ boxes:** It's time to start extraction and diversification strategy.

## Summary: Capital Allocation Framework

**The rule:**

1. **Does the capital generate >30% ROI?** → Reinvest (payback <3 years)
2. **Is the business mature?** → Extract (diversify concentration risk)
3. **Do you have 3–6 month liquidity reserve?** → Extract and invest
4. **Are you 90%+ concentrated in business?** → Extract aggressively
5. **After reserve and 401(k) maxing?** → Diversified portfolio (60/30/10 stocks/bonds/cash)

**The outcome:**
- Growth-stage founder: Reinvest for 3–5 years, build business to $5M+
- Mature founder: Extract 30–50% of EBIT, diversify over 3–5 years
- Successful founder (10+ years): 40–60% in business, 40–60% in diversified assets

**The mindset:**
- Your business is one investment, not your only investment
- Concentration risk is real; diversification compounds over time
- A $5M business is only valuable if you're healthy and alive to enjoy it
- Extract, diversify, and build sustainable wealth outside the business

Capital allocation is the difference between building a business and building a life.
