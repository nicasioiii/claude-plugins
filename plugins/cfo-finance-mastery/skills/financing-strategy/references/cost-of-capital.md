# Cost of Capital: The 30% Hurdle Rate for Small Business

## The Foundation: Why 30%?

The cost of capital is the minimum return a business must generate to justify any financing decision. For small business, this number is approximately **30% per year**.

This is not an arbitrary figure. It reflects the blended cost across all capital sources available to a small business owner:

- **Debt capital:** 10–15% annually (bank loans, business credit cards, SBA loans)
- **Equity capital:** 30%+ annually (investor expectations, owner required returns given risk)
- **Blended cost:** Weighted average of debt and equity typically converges to ~30%

**The Decision Rule:**
Any financing decision—borrowing money, taking an investment, or allocating retained earnings—must generate returns **exceeding 30% annually** to create real economic value for the business.

## Why This Matters: Value Creation vs. Value Destruction

Many business owners borrow without understanding this framework. They see:
- "I can get a $100K loan at 12% interest"
- "This feels cheap compared to credit cards"
- "I need cash, so I should borrow"

**This is backwards.** A 12% loan is only valuable if it generates >30% returns.

### Real-World Example: The Working Capital Trap

**Scenario:** E-commerce business generating $400K/month revenue, 35% gross margin.

Owner thinks: "I'm tight on working capital. Let me get a Merchant Cash Advance."

- Capital needed: $100K (for inventory, AR float)
- MCA offered: $100K advance, repay $156K over 6 months
- Stated rate: 56%
- True APR (daily repayments): ~85%

**Analysis:**
- At 35% margins on $400K/month = $140K/month gross profit
- But working capital is tied up, slowing inventory turnover
- MCA repayment: $26K/month
- Real question: Does that $100K generate incremental revenue?

**If the $100K generates $35K in gross profit (35%):** Return is 35% over 6 months = ~70% annualized. This beats the 30% hurdle narrowly—but barely.

**If the $100K is just bridging cash flow (not generating new revenue):** Return is 0%. The 85% APR destroys $15K–$20K in value.

**The lesson:** Understand what the borrowed capital generates before borrowing.

## Time Value of Money for Small Business Owners

Small business owners must think like investors about their own capital.

### The Compounding Effect

If you have $50K in excess cash, what should you do?

**Option A:** Reinvest in inventory (generates 40% margin growth = $20K annually)
**Option B:** Extract as dividend and invest in index funds (generates 10% = $5K annually)
**Option C:** Pay down debt at 12% (generates 12% return = $6K annually)

Ranking by value creation:
1. Reinvestment (40% return)
2. Debt repayment (12% return, saves 12% interest)
3. External investment (10% return)

But only if Option A generates >30% returns. If the $50K reinvestment generates $15K profit (30% return), you're indifferent between reinvesting and extracting. If it generates <30%, extraction wins.

### The Discount Rate Framework

When evaluating financing, always ask: "What's the time value of my money?"

**Lump-Sum vs. Monthly Payments Example:**

A vendor offers two payment terms:
- **Option 1:** Pay $100K lump sum today
- **Option 2:** Pay $8,500/month for 12 months ($102K total)

**Analysis at 30% cost of capital:**
- Paying early ties up $100K cash today
- Monthly payments preserve cash flow for 12 months
- At 30% cost of capital, having $100K in hand for 12 months is worth $30K in economic benefit
- The 2% premium ($2K) on the monthly option is cheap

**Decision:** Take monthly payments.

But if your cost of capital is only 10% (you can borrow at prime + 2%), then the 2% premium is expensive, and lump-sum is better.

## Decision Economics: Evaluating Financing Options

### The Financing Evaluation Framework

Before taking on any debt, run through this checklist:

**1. What is the incremental return on this capital?**
- If you borrow $100K, how much incremental profit does it generate?
- Be conservative; use the lower of historical margin or market benchmark

**Example (accurate):**
- Current revenue: $1M, gross margin 40% = $400K available
- Borrowing $100K for inventory expansion to $1.2M
- Projected new revenue: $1.2M (20% growth)
- Incremental profit: $1.2M × 40% - $1M × 40% = $480K - $400K = $80K
- Return on $100K: 80% (beats 30% hurdle ✓)

**Example (inaccurate—common mistake):**
- "I'm getting a loan for $100K and I think it'll help"
- No clear link between capital and revenue impact
- This is a red flag; don't borrow

**2. What's the true cost of the financing?**
- Not the stated rate, but the annualized cost including all terms
- For MCAs: Calculate true APR (see MCA reference file)
- For bank loans: Include fees, prepayment penalties, collateral costs
- For credit cards: 0% promo APR for 12 months, then 18–23%

**3. Can you afford fixed payments in a 20% revenue downturn?**
- Project cash flow with debt service included
- If revenue drops 20%, can you still make monthly payments?
- If not, debt is too aggressive

**Example:**
- Current revenue: $500K/month
- Loan payment: $20K/month
- In 20% downturn: $400K/month revenue
- COGS (50%): $200K, OpEx (30%): $120K, Debt service: $20K
- Remaining: $60K (tight but viable)

If downturn would make payments impossible, restructure the loan or don't borrow.

**4. What's the payback period?**
- How many months until the capital pays for itself through profit?
- Target: Payback within 12–36 months depending on business stage
- Anything longer is speculative

**Example:**
- Loan: $100K
- Incremental monthly profit: $8K
- Payback period: 100K / 8K = 12.5 months ✓ (good)

Compare to:
- Loan: $100K
- Incremental monthly profit: $2K
- Payback period: 100K / 2K = 50 months ✗ (risky; don't borrow)

## Cost of Capital by Business Stage

Cost of capital varies by business maturity and risk:

### Startup (0–1 year revenue history)
- Cost of capital: **50%–100%+**
- Rationale: High failure risk, unproven business model
- Financing available: Founder capital, friends/family, angel investors
- Bank loans: Not available without collateral
- MCAs/predatory lending: Common trap (avoid)

### Growth Stage (1–3 years, $500K–$5M revenue)
- Cost of capital: **30%–50%**
- Rationale: Some track record, but execution risk remains
- Financing available: SBA loans (8–12%), business credit cards (0% promo + 18–23%), vendor terms
- Bank loans: Available if >1 year of profitability
- **Decision rule:** Finance growth initiatives generating >40% returns

### Mature Stage (3+ years, $5M+ revenue)
- Cost of capital: **15%–25%**
- Rationale: Proven business model, predictable cash flow
- Financing available: Traditional bank loans (6–10%), lines of credit (6–12%), equipment financing
- Market-rate lending: SBA loans + traditional bank products
- **Decision rule:** Finance initiatives generating >20% returns; optimize capital structure

### Declining/Distressed Stage
- Cost of capital: **50%+** (or unavailable)
- Rationale: High default risk
- Financing available: MCAs (high risk), hard money lenders, seizure-based
- **Strategy:** Focus on operational turnaround first; avoid financing until stable

## Common Mistakes in Cost of Capital Analysis

### Mistake #1: Confusing APR with True Cost

A business sees a 15% loan rate and thinks "That's cheap."

**The issue:** At 30% cost of capital, 15% is **cheap** (you're borrowing below your hurdle rate), but that doesn't mean you should borrow.

**The real question:** Does the capital generate >30% returns? If yes, borrow (even at 15%). If no, don't borrow (even at 5%).

### Mistake #2: Assuming Reinvestment Always Beats Extraction

Many founders reinvest all profits because they think "the business is my best investment."

**Reality check:**
- If your business is mature (10% organic growth, 25% EBIT margins), it's returning only 25%
- An S&P 500 index fund returns ~10% average, but with zero operational risk
- Extracting dividends and diversifying is often smarter

**Rule:** If business ROI < 30% and you have no proven growth opportunity >30%, extract and diversify.

### Mistake #3: Underestimating Risk in ROI Projections

Founders project 50% ROI on capital: "I know my business; it'll work."

**The CFO perspective:** Projections rarely account for:
- Market saturation or competition
- Execution delays (hiring, product development)
- Working capital strain (more inventory, more AR)
- Churn risk (customers don't stick around)

**Conservative rule:** Reduce projected ROI by 20–30% for uncertainty.

Example:
- Founder projects: $100K investment → $50K profit = 50% ROI
- CFO conservatively estimates: $100K → $35K profit = 35% ROI
- Still beats 30% hurdle ✓, but with more margin of safety

### Mistake #4: Ignoring Opportunity Cost

"I borrowed $100K at 12%. Since my WACC is 30%, I'm winning."

**Missing perspective:** What else could you do with that capital?

- Pay down higher-cost debt (20% credit card) instead?
- Build cash reserves for operational flexibility?
- Acquire a competitor or product line with better unit economics?

**Principle:** Choose the capital allocation that maximizes economic value, not just beats the hurdle rate.

## Stress-Testing Your Cost of Capital Assumption

Before finalizing a financing decision, stress-test the math:

### Base Case (Conservative)
- Revenue grows 20% from capital investment
- Incremental margin: 35%
- Return on capital: 35% ✓

### Downside Case (Cautious)
- Revenue grows 10% (slower adoption)
- Incremental margin: 30% (pricing pressure)
- Return on capital: 30% (just at hurdle)
- **Verdict:** Risky; requires near-perfect execution

### Severe Downturn (Prudence Check)
- Revenue actually declines 5% (market shift)
- Margin compresses to 20%
- Return on capital: **-5% (you lose money)**
- **Verdict:** This scenario must be survivable (you can absorb the loss without defaulting)

**Rule:** Only borrow if you can survive the severe downturn scenario.

## Integrating Cost of Capital into Financing Decisions

### Practical Application: The $250K Expansion Question

A manufacturing client asks: "Should I borrow $250K for new equipment?"

**Step 1: Quantify the return**
- Equipment generates incremental capacity for $500K/year in additional revenue
- At 45% gross margin: $225K additional gross profit
- Minus equipment depreciation (allocated cost): $225K - $50K = $175K net contribution
- ROI: $175K / $250K = 70% ✓

**Step 2: Check the true cost of financing**
- Bank quote: $250K at 9%, 5-year term = $48K/year in interest + principal
- True cost of capital: 9%
- Spread: 70% return vs 9% cost = 61% margin of safety ✓

**Step 3: Stress-test the assumptions**
- If revenue projections are off by 30%: ROI drops to 49% (still healthy)
- If margins compress by 10 points: ROI drops to 53% (still healthy)
- If equipment takes 6 months to install: Payback extends to 1.8 years (acceptable)

**Step 4: Assess downside risk**
- If market crashes and revenue drops 50%, can the company absorb $250K in stranded equipment?
- Current EBITDA: $500K (assume)
- Loss absorbed: $250K (50% of EBITDA, manageable but tight)

**Verdict:** Borrow. The 70% ROI far exceeds the 30% hurdle, financing costs are reasonable, and downside risk is manageable.

## Summary: Cost of Capital Decision Framework

1. **Know your cost of capital** (30% for most small businesses)
2. **Quantify the return** on any capital investment (ROI >30% required)
3. **Calculate the true cost** of financing (including all fees and terms)
4. **Stress-test assumptions** (base, downside, severe cases)
5. **Assess sustainability** (can you afford payments in a downturn?)
6. **Compare alternatives** (is this the best use of capital vs. other options?)
7. **Make the decision** with economic clarity, not desperation

The cost of capital framework ensures you borrow strategically, not reactively.
