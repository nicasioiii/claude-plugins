# Credit Card Strategy for Working Capital & Cash Flow Optimization

## The Credit Card as a Financing Tool

Business credit cards are among the most underutilized financial tools in small business. Used strategically, they provide:

- **Zero-cost working capital** for 6–12 months (0% promotional APR)
- **Rapid deployment** (approval in days, not weeks)
- **Flexible repayment** (unlike loans with fixed payment obligations)
- **Reward upside** (1–4% cash back on purchases)
- **No collateral** required (unsecured debt)

The key: Use business credit cards for **short-term working capital** (3–12 months), not long-term financing. Treat them as tools, not solutions.

### How to Use It for Working Capital

**Phase 1: Acquisition (Month 1)**

1. Apply for Chase Ink Unlimited
   - Requires: EIN, business info, personal credit check (expect 670+ credit score)
   - Timeline: 1–3 days approval
   - Likely approval: $25K–$50K limit (first time)

2. Get approved and receive card
   - Activate card immediately
   - Set up online account
   - Configure autopay (important for cash management)

**Phase 2: Deployment (Months 1–3)**

Use the card strategically for working capital:

**What to charge:**
- Inventory purchases (suppliers accept credit cards; may charge 2–3% processing fee)
- Software subscriptions and business services
- Vendor deposits and prepayments
- Office supplies and equipment

**Example scenario:**
- Current cash position: $20K
- Inventory need: $50K (for growth)
- Operating cash required: $15K (payroll, fixed costs)
- Total cash need: $50K + $15K = $65K

**Without credit card:** Use $20K cash + borrow $45K (costs money)

**With credit card:**
- Use $20K cash
- Charge $45K on Ink Unlimited (0% APR for 12 months)
- Repayment required: Nothing for 12 months (statement must be paid in full, but no interest)
- Result: Free $45K working capital for 12 months

**Phase 3: Repayment (Months 10–12)**

Plan repayment before the 0% promo expires:

**Option A: Pay in full before APR kicks in**
- Pay full balance in Month 11 or 12
- Cost: $0 (0% APR for full 12 months)
- Requirement: Business must have generated cash flow to repay

**Option B: Refinance to another 0% card (if approved)**
- Apply for second 0% promotional card (month 10)
- Balance transfer credit limit from Ink Unlimited to new card
- New 0% window for 12 months
- Timeline of this strategy: 24 months interest-free (if executable)

**Option C: Repay and rotate**
- Pay off Ink Unlimited before month 12
- Recharge new inventory/working capital on same card
- Many people can reset to 0% again (Chase offers periodic promotional resets)

### Credit Score Impact

**Positive impacts:**
- Building credit history (on-time payments build creditworthiness)
- Installment variety (credit mix improves scores; "revolving credit" is helpful)

**Negative impacts:**
- High utilization (high balance = lower score temporarily)
- Hard inquiry (new card application = 5–10 point dip, recovers in 6 months)

**Strategy to minimize damage:**
- If using card heavily, request credit limit increase (doesn't require new hard inquiry if Chase does it automatically)
- Keep utilization under 50% if possible (though 0% promo makes high utilization OK)
- Pay on-time every month (builds positive history)

## The Authorized User Hack: Boost Credit Score Fast

### Why It Works

Credit scores depend on several factors:
1. Payment history (35%)
2. Credit utilization (30%)
3. Length of credit history (15%)
4. Credit mix (10%)
5. New credit inquiries (10%)

**The hack:** Adding yourself as an authorized user on someone else's card can boost your credit score by 40–100 points in 30 days, because you inherit their credit history.

### How to Execute

**Step 1: Find a partner with excellent credit**
- Spouse, partner, family member, or trusted friend
- Must have: 750+ credit score, old credit card (15+ years preferred), low balance (<10% utilization)

**Step 2: Ask them to add you as authorized user**
- They call the credit card issuer (American Express, Chase, etc.)
- Request to add authorized user by name and SSN
- Issuer adds you to account within 1–2 business days

**Step 3: Monitor your credit**
- Wait 30 days for credit bureaus to update
- Check your credit report at annualcreditreport.com
- You should see score improvement

**Step 4: Use the new credit score**
- Apply for business credit cards (higher approval odds)
- Apply for business loan or SBA (improved credit helps)
- Remove yourself from account if needed (doesn't typically hurt score if account stays open and in good standing)

### Real Impact

**Example:**
- Your credit score: 620 (poor; subprime range)
- Partner's card: 790 score, 3% utilization, 20-year history
- Action: Added as authorized user
- Result: Your score improves to 680–700 in 30 days
- Impact: Suddenly qualify for business credit cards, lower loan rates

**Why lenders care:** High-utilization owner + authorized user on clean card = appears more creditworthy.

## Early Payment Discount Strategy: When It Makes Economic Sense

### The Framework: Cost of Capital Decision

Suppliers often offer early payment discounts:
- "2% discount if you pay in 10 days vs 30 days" (2/10 Net 30)
- "1% discount if you pay in 5 days vs 45 days" (1/5 Net 45)

**The question:** Should you take the discount?

**The answer:** Only if the annualized discount rate exceeds your **cost of capital (30%)**.

### Calculating Discount Value

**Formula:**

```
Discount % / (100 - Discount %) × (365 / Days Accelerated) = Annualized Rate
```

**Example 1: 2% discount for paying 20 days early (2/10 Net 30)**

```
2 / (100 - 2) × (365 / 20) =
2 / 98 × 18.25 =
0.0204 × 18.25 =
37.2% annualized rate
```

**Decision:** 37.2% > 30% hurdle rate → **Take the discount** (pay early)

**Real cost:** Pay $980 instead of $1,000 = $20 savings = 37.2% annualized ROI

**Example 2: 1% discount for paying 40 days early (1/10 Net 50)**

```
1 / (100 - 1) × (365 / 40) =
1 / 99 × 9.125 =
0.0101 × 9.125 =
9.2% annualized rate
```

**Decision:** 9.2% < 30% hurdle rate → **Don't take the discount** (pay late)

**Why:** Having that cash for 40 extra days is worth more than the 1% savings.

### Practical Application

**Vendors offering early payment discounts:**

1. **Manufacturing suppliers** (common: 2/10 Net 30)
   - Usually worth taking (37%+ annualized)
   - Preserve cash flow only if you have other 40%+ ROI uses

2. **Wholesale distributors** (common: 1–2% depending on order size)
   - Calculate specific rates (some worth it, some not)
   - Larger discounts on bigger orders often justify early payment

3. **Service providers** (rare to offer discounts, but ask)
   - "Can you offer 2% for paying within 5 days instead of 30?"
   - Many will negotiate if asked

**Strategic approach:**
- Run the calculation before every payment decision
- If rate > 30%, pay early
- If rate < 30%, pay on normal terms (use the cash for operations or debt reduction)

## Credit Card Stacking: Multiple Cards for Different Uses

### Optimal Credit Card Portfolio

Most businesses do well with 2–3 business credit cards, each optimized for a different purpose:

**Card 1: Chase Ink Unlimited (primary working capital)**
- Purpose: General business spending, working capital bridge
- Limit: $25K–$100K
- Rewards: 1.5% cash back on all (simple, no categories)
- APR: 0% for 12 months, then 18–23%
- Use: Default card for inventory, supplies, subscriptions

**Card 2: American Express Gold or Platinum (high-value spending)**
- Purpose: High-value categories (4% flights, restaurants, office supplies)
- Limit: $25K–$75K
- Rewards: 4% on bonus categories, 1% base
- APR: Variable (no 0% promo, so only for spending you pay off monthly)
- Use: Concentrate all travel, dining, and office supply spend here

**Card 3: Capital One Spark (backup/subprime option)**
- Purpose: If other cards are maxed or you need rotating credit
- Limit: $10K–$50K
- Rewards: 2% cash back on all (solid alternative to Ink)
- APR: 18–25% (higher rates; use only for 0% promo or backup)
- Use: Second card if Ink is fully utilized

### How to Manage Multiple Cards

**Organization:**
- Track all 3 cards in single spreadsheet (balances, limits, due dates)
- Set payment reminders (3 days before due date for each card)
- Monitor utilization across all cards (combined shouldn't exceed 30% if DSCR matters for loans)

**Repayment strategy:**
- If 0% promo is active (Ink): Pay minimums until month 11, then full balance
- If APR is standard (AMEX, Capital One): Pay in full monthly (no interest accrual)
- If cash is tight: Pay minimums on all cards, focus on the highest-cost card first (typically 23%+ APR cards)

## Business Credit Card Mistakes to Avoid

### Mistake 1: Blurring Personal & Business Spend

**The trap:** Using business card for personal expenses (occasional and routine)

**Why it's bad:**
- Business accounts get flagged (issuer may revoke card)
- Tax complications (commingled spending makes deductions harder)
- Accounting becomes messy (harder to reconcile)

**Rule:** Business card = business spend only. Get a personal card for personal spend.

### Mistake 2: Not Paying Off Before APR Kicks In

**The trap:** Forgetting that 0% APR expires after 12 months

**Example:**
- Month 12: Ink Unlimited balance: $50K, 0% expires
- Didn't plan repayment
- Month 13: APR of 21% activates
- Monthly interest: $50K × 21% / 12 = $875/month

**Rule:** Set reminder on calendar (Month 10) to plan repayment.

### Mistake 3: Taking Out Too Much Debt

**The trap:** Getting $100K limit and using $95K

**Why it's bad:**
- High utilization hurts credit score (important if you need SBA loan)
- Interest accumulation when promo expires
- Psychological burden of heavy debt

**Rule:** Use cards strategically, not maximally. Borrow only what you need.

### Mistake 4: Missing Minimum Payments

**The trap:** Missing even one minimum payment while in 0% promo period

**Result:**
- 0% APR is revoked immediately
- Full APR (18–23%) applies to entire balance
- Late payment fee ($35–$50)
- Credit score damage

**Rule:** Set autopay for minimum payment, even if you pay the balance manually later.

## Summary: Credit Card Strategy Framework

1. **Use 0% promotional cards for short-term working capital** (3–12 months)
2. **Chase Ink Unlimited is the gold standard** (0% for 12 months, 1.5% rewards)
3. **Consider authorized user hack for credit score boost** (fast, legal, effective)
4. **Optimize AMEX for high-spend businesses** (4% categories + Schwab conversion)
5. **Calculate early payment discounts against 30% hurdle** (only take if discount rate > 30%)
6. **Stack 2–3 cards for different purposes** (primary, rewards optimization, backup)
7. **Plan repayment BEFORE APR expires** (don't let 0% disappear)
8. **Keep personal and business spend separate** (simplifies accounting, avoids card revocation)

Business credit cards are powerful tools for working capital. Used strategically, they can replace expensive financing options and provide 6–12 months of free capital.
