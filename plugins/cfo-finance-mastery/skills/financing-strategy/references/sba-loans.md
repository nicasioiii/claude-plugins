# SBA Loans: Mechanics, Underwriting & Success Strategy

## What is an SBA Loan?

SBA (Small Business Administration) loans are government-backed debt products designed to make lending to small businesses more accessible. The SBA guarantees 75–90% of the loan amount, which dramatically reduces the lender's risk and allows them to offer:

- **Lower interest rates:** Prime + 2–3% (typically 8–12%) vs. unsecured business loans (15%+)
- **Longer terms:** 7–10 years (vs. 3–5 years for conventional bank loans)
- **Larger amounts:** $250K–$5M+ (depending on business size and use of proceeds)
- **Flexible collateral:** Primarily business assets, with personal guarantee

The SBA guarantee is the key. Without it, a bank would demand 15%+ rates and would skip many small businesses entirely.

---

## The SBA Guarantee: How It Works

**Example scenario:**

Bank makes a $300K SBA loan to a small business.

- SBA guarantees 80% of the loan = $240K guaranteed
- Bank's exposure = $60K unguaranteed
- If business defaults, SBA pays the bank $240K, and bank pursues the remaining $60K through collateral

**Result:** Banks love SBA loans because their risk is capped at 20%. This willingness to lend translates to better rates and terms for borrowers.

## The Three Repayment Sources: Underwriting Hierarchy

SBA lenders evaluate your ability to repay in this specific order:

### Primary Source: Cash Flow (Operating Income)

The lender asks: "Can the business generate enough operating profit to service this debt?"

**Key metric:** Debt-Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR)

```
DSCR = Annual Operating Income / Annual Debt Payments
```

**Target:** DSCR > 1.25 (the business generates 25% more cash flow than required debt payments)

**Example:**
- Annual operating income: $100K
- Annual debt payments (principal + interest): $75K
- DSCR: $100K / $75K = 1.33 ✓ (strong)

**Interpretation:** For every dollar of debt payment due, the business generates $1.33 in operating profit. This provides a safety margin.

**Red flags:**
- DSCR < 1.0 (operating income doesn't cover debt payments; immediate default risk)
- DSCR between 1.0–1.1 (razor-thin margin; risky)
- DSCR 1.1–1.25 (acceptable, but watch closely)

### Secondary Source: Collateral

If cash flow fails, the lender looks at collateral: business assets and personal guarantees.

**Collateral hierarchy (what lenders take first):**
1. **Equipment and inventory** (business assets directly related to operations)
2. **Accounts receivable** (customer invoices)
3. **Real estate** (if business owner owns commercial property)
4. **Personal assets** (home, other property owned personally)

**Lender perspective:** Equipment and real estate are "hard" collateral. AR is "soft" collateral (depends on customer quality). Personal assets are backstop.

### Tertiary Source: Personal Guarantee

If the business can't pay and collateral is insufficient, the lender pursues the owner personally.

**Personal guarantee means:** Your personal assets (home, car, savings) are on the line. For a $300K loan, the bank can pursue you for $300K.

**Implication:** SBA loans are not truly "business-only" debt. As a small business owner, you are personally liable.

## SBA Loan Underwriting: The Five C's of Credit

SBA lenders formally evaluate loans using the "Five C's of Credit":

### 1. Character (Trustworthiness)

**What lenders evaluate:**
- Personal credit score (target: 670+; preferably 700+)
- Payment history (late payments, charge-offs, bankruptcies)
- Business credit profile (D&B rating, vendor payment history)
- Industry experience and background
- Personal stability (residence history, employment continuity)

**How to assess yourself:**
- Pull your credit report (annualcreditreport.com)
- Check for errors or negative items
- Improve score by paying down high-utilization credit cards (aim for <30% utilization)
- Build business credit by paying vendors on time

**Deal-breaker scenarios:**
- Bankruptcy within the last 2 years
- Active tax liens or legal judgments
- Recent fraud conviction or felony
- Outstanding child support or alimony default
- Undisclosed prior business failures

**Tip:** Tell the lender bad news upfront (old bankruptcy, credit issue). They'll tell you immediately if it's disqualifying, saving you weeks of application time.

### 2. Capacity (Ability to Pay)

**What lenders evaluate:**
- Historical profitability and cash flow
- Debt-service coverage ratio (DSCR > 1.25)
- Revenue trends (growing, stable, or declining)
- Seasonal patterns and cyclicality
- Industry trends and market conditions

**How to present capacity:**
- Provide 3 years of personal and business tax returns
- Include detailed P&L for the last 12 months
- Show current balance sheet (within 90 days)
- Explain any negative periods (one-time events, seasonal dips)

**Red flags:**
- Declining revenue trend (3-year revenue down)
- Volatile earnings (boom-and-bust cycles)
- Negative operating cash flow
- DSCR < 1.25 (insufficient cash flow buffer)

**Tip:** If your DSCR is marginal (1.1–1.25), reduce the loan amount requested or extend the term to improve DSCR.

Example:
- Operating income: $80K annually
- Your DSCR on $300K loan @ 9% over 7 years: Only 1.1 (barely acceptable)
- Solution: Request $250K instead → DSCR improves to 1.35

### 3. Capital (Owner's Equity Stake)

**What lenders require:**
- Owner equity investment (typically 10–20% of loan amount)
- Demonstrates "skin in the game" and reduces lender risk
- Shows owner commitment (not just seeking free money)

**Example:**
- Loan request: $300K
- Required owner equity: 15% × $300K = $45K
- Owner must put $45K of personal capital into the business
- Loan finances remaining $255K

**Why it matters:** Lenders know that if the owner has invested personal capital, they'll work harder to make the business succeed. It also protects the lender—if the business fails, the owner loses their $45K first.

**How to show capital:**
- Bank deposit documentation (personal bank statements showing cash deposit)
- Sweat equity or prior investment (previous years' tax returns showing owner capital contributions)
- Retained earnings in the business (accumulated profits from prior years)

**Trap:** Don't borrow the owner equity requirement. This defeats the purpose. The capital must be "at risk."

### 4. Collateral (Security)

**What lenders will take as collateral:**

**Ranked by preference:**
1. **Real estate** (strongest; most recoverable)
   - Commercial property (valued at 75–85% of appraisal)
   - Owner's personal residence (valued at 70% of appraised value)
   - Farmland or land holdings

2. **Equipment** (good; business-specific)
   - Machinery, vehicles, computers
   - Valued at 40–60% of replacement cost (depreciation is significant)
   - Lender takes UCC-1 filing (priority claim)

3. **Inventory & AR** (weak; liquid but risky)
   - Inventory valued at 50% of cost (clearance issues, obsolescence)
   - AR valued at 70–80% of face value (only if customers are strong)

4. **Personal assets** (backstop; not primary)
   - Savings or investment accounts (taken dollar-for-dollar)
   - Personal vehicles (50–70% of market value)
   - Home equity (if not already pledged)

**Lender perspective:** They'll take as much collateral as you offer, but real estate is most valuable. If you have commercial real estate, offer it (first lien position). This dramatically improves approval odds.

**Example collateral package for $300K loan:**
- Commercial property worth $500K (take $400K at 80% LTV) → Covers loan
- Equipment worth $150K (take $90K at 60% recovery) → Additional security
- Personal residence (keep as backstop if possible, but may be required)

### 5. Conditions (Loan Purpose & Use)

**What lenders evaluate:**
- Stated use of proceeds (equipment, working capital, inventory, real estate)
- Reasonableness of the use (does $300K for equipment match your business type?)
- Timing (when will the capital be deployed?)
- Expected payoff (will the investment generate returns to service debt?)

**Acceptable uses:**
- Equipment and machinery purchases (with quotes/invoices)
- Inventory for proven product lines
- Real estate for business operations
- Working capital for established businesses (must DSCR be strong)
- Refinancing existing debt (consolidation, improve terms)
- Debt payoff (if refinancing to lower rate)

**Questionable uses:**
- Owner distributions (paying yourself dividends; lenders resist)
- Personal expenses disguised as business (red flag)
- Startup capital for unproven business model (high risk)
- Speculative ventures (no clear ROI path)

**Tip:** Use of proceeds must be clear and documented. Get quotes from vendors (equipment), purchase orders (inventory), or business plans (expansion).

## Documentation Checklist: What Lenders Require

Gather complete documentation upfront. Incomplete applications delay approval by weeks.

### Personal & Business Information

- [ ] Personal financial statement (PFS) — assets, liabilities, net worth (current as of today)
- [ ] Resume or business biography (1–2 pages; experience and background)
- [ ] Personal credit report authorization (lender pulls this; expect 620+ score required)
- [ ] Social Security number and address history (for background check)

### Personal Tax Returns (3 years)

- [ ] 1040 with all schedules (C, E, K-1 if applicable)
- [ ] If married, joint 1040
- [ ] Any 1099 or self-employment income
- [ ] Business loss carryforwards (Schedule C)

### Business Tax Returns (3 years)

- [ ] Corporate or partnership returns (1120-S, 1120-C, or 1065)
- [ ] If sole proprietor, Schedule C from personal 1040
- [ ] Year-to-date extension (if current year not filed yet)
- [ ] Prior year extension (if last year was extended)

### Current Financial Statements

- [ ] Profit & loss statement (last 12 months, monthly detail preferred)
- [ ] Balance sheet (current; within 90 days)
- [ ] Year-to-date balance sheet (if more recent than full-year statement)
- [ ] Bank statements (business account, last 6 months)
- [ ] Bank statements (personal account, last 6 months)

**Why 6 months?** Lenders want to see cash flow patterns, transfers to/from personal account, and debt repayment consistency.

### Explanation of Negative Items

- [ ] Letter explaining any late payments (30+ days late)
- [ ] Letter explaining any charge-offs or collections
- [ ] Letter explaining any gaps in income or employment
- [ ] Letter explaining any business losses or unprofitable years

**Tone:** Honest, no excuses. "In 2023, we experienced a supply chain disruption that reduced revenue by 15%. We've since diversified suppliers and recovered. 2024 revenue is up 25% YoY."

### Collateral Documentation

- [ ] Personal residence appraisal (or recent tax assessment)
- [ ] Commercial property appraisal (if pledging business real estate)
- [ ] Equipment list with approximate values (or recent invoice prices)
- [ ] Lease agreements (if leasing instead of owning equipment)
- [ ] UCC search results (to identify existing liens on business assets)
- [ ] Deed of trust or mortgage (if offering real estate)

**Tip:** Run a UCC search on your own business name before applying. If there are existing liens, disclose them upfront.

### Business Plan (If Startup or Significant Change)

- [ ] Executive summary (1 page; what problem are you solving?)
- [ ] Use of proceeds (detailed breakdown of where $X is going)
- [ ] Market analysis (market size, competition, your positioning)
- [ ] Financial projections (monthly P&L for 2 years, balance sheet, cash flow)
- [ ] Contingency plan (if revenue falls short, how will you service debt?)

**For established businesses:** A formal business plan isn't always required, but a clear written explanation of the loan use is essential.

### Miscellaneous

- [ ] Articles of incorporation or business license (proof of business entity)
- [ ] List of owners and guarantors (names, SSNs, ownership %)
- [ ] Resolution authorizing borrowing (if corporate, board must approve)

## The DSCR Deep Dive: Why It Matters Most

**Debt-Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR)** is the single most important metric for SBA loan approval.

### Calculating DSCR Correctly

```
DSCR = Annual Operating Income / Annual Debt Service Payments
```

**Operating income** (also called EBIT or earnings before interest and taxes):
- Start with net income (bottom line of P&L)
- Add back interest expense (you're paying this; lenders account for it)
- Add back taxes (seasonal impact)
- Subtract owner distributions (if taken, shown as draw)
- Result: cash available for debt payments

**Debt service payments** = Principal + Interest for the year

**Example:**
- Net income (from tax return): $80,000
- Interest expense (paid during year): $12,000
- Owner distribution (draw): $20,000
- **Operating income:** $80K + $12K - $20K = $72K

- Loan request: $250,000 at 9% over 7 years
- Annual debt service: ~$43,000 (principal + interest)

- **DSCR:** $72K / $43K = **1.67** ✓ (strong)

### DSCR Thresholds & Approval Likelihood

| DSCR | Lender Decision | Risk Level |
|------|-----------------|-----------|
| >1.5 | Strong approval | Low risk; fast process |
| 1.25–1.5 | Likely approval | Acceptable; standard terms |
| 1.1–1.25 | Conditional approval | Marginal; may require collateral boost or rate premium |
| 1.0–1.1 | Difficult approval | Risky; lender may decline or require larger down payment |
| <1.0 | **Denial** | **Cannot support debt** |

**Key insight:** If your DSCR is below 1.25, the lender is nervous. They'll either:
1. Require additional collateral
2. Demand a higher down payment (more owner equity)
3. Charge a higher interest rate (risk premium)
4. Decline the application

### Improving DSCR Before Applying

If your DSCR is marginal, improve it before submitting:

**Option 1: Request less money**
- Reduce loan request from $300K to $250K
- Lower annual debt service → higher DSCR

**Option 2: Extend the loan term**
- Request 10-year term instead of 7-year
- Spreads payments over longer period → lower annual service → higher DSCR

**Option 3: Reduce debt, increase equity injection**
- Request $200K loan (borrow less) + $100K owner equity (invest more)
- Lower debt service → higher DSCR

**Option 4: Improve profitability upfront**
- Cut unnecessary expenses (reduce OpEx)
- Renegotiate supplier terms (improve gross margin)
- Launch cost-cutting initiative
- Goal: Demonstrate cash flow trending upward

## Pre-Application Strategy: Preparing to Succeed

### 90 Days Before Applying

1. **Pull and review your credit report**
   - Check annualcreditreport.com (free)
   - Dispute any errors immediately
   - Pay down credit card balances to <30% utilization

2. **Organize financial statements**
   - Gather last 3 years of tax returns
   - Prepare current balance sheet (within 90 days)
   - Calculate DSCR (know your number)

3. **Identify collateral**
   - List business assets (equipment, inventory, AR)
   - Get real estate appraised if offering as collateral
   - Run UCC search to identify existing liens

4. **Clean up the balance sheet**
   - Pay off or reduce personal guarantees on existing debt (improves credit)
   - Close unnecessary business accounts (simplifies picture)
   - Reconcile retained earnings to tax filings (consistency matters)

### 30 Days Before Applying

1. **Select the right lender**
   - Contact 2–3 SBA-preferred lenders
   - Ask which loan products they recommend for your business
   - Inquire about turnaround time and documentation

2. **Prepare the loan application**
   - Complete formal SBA loan application (Form 1919)
   - Write 1-page business summary (what you do, why you need capital)
   - Draft written explanation of use of proceeds (be specific)

3. **Get quotes for equipment or inventory**
   - If buying equipment, get formal quotes from vendors
   - If buying inventory, get purchase orders from suppliers
   - Lenders want to verify the $X is reasonable for what you're buying

4. **Contact a bookkeeper/accountant**
   - Have them verify financial statements are accurate
   - Flag any unusual items on the balance sheet
   - Ensure tax returns reconcile to bank statements

### Application Submission

1. **Choose your lender**
   - Contact preferred lender; submit complete application package
   - Expect initial decision within 2–3 weeks
   - Lender requests clarifications or additional documents (plan for 1–2 weeks)

2. **Loan approval**
   - Conditional approval (contingent on appraisal, updated financials, insurance)
   - Clear conditions; provide updated documents
   - Appraisal ordered if real estate is collateral (1–3 weeks)

3. **Final approval & funding**
   - Final underwriting review (1 week)
   - Loan agreement signed
   - Funds disbursed (typically within 5–10 days of closing)

**Total timeline:** 6–12 weeks from application to funded

## Red Flags: Instant Deal-Breakers

**Tell the lender upfront if you have any of these. They'll tell you immediately whether to proceed.**

- **Bankruptcy:** Within the last 2 years → likely decline
- **Tax liens or judgments:** IRS or court liens on personal or business assets → likely decline
- **Fraud or felony conviction:** Prior fraud, embezzlement, or felony → automatic decline
- **Active default on other business debt:** Currently in default on existing loan → decline
- **Undisclosed liabilities:** Hidden debt, guarantees, or obligations → deal-breaker when discovered
- **Business in illegal operation:** Unlicensed, prohibited industry, or illegal activity → decline
- **Non-disclosure of facts:** Lying on application → fraud charge (criminal liability)

**Best practice:** Contact lender with deal-breaker upfront. You lose time, but you avoid the embarrassment of being declined mid-process.

## Real Case Study: What Approval Looks Like

**Business:** Manufacturing supply company, $1.8M annual revenue, 28% EBIT margin

**Loan request:** $400,000 at 9.5% over 7 years

**Character assessment:**
- Personal credit score: 695 (acceptable; target 670+)
- No bankruptcy, tax liens, or fraud history
- 15 years in manufacturing industry
- **Verdict:** ✓ Approval path

**Capacity assessment:**
- Historical EBITDA: $560K (last 3 years average)
- Projected operating income with capital deployment: $580K
- Annual debt service on $400K loan: ~$76K
- DSCR: $580K / $76K = **7.6** (excellent)
- **Verdict:** ✓ Approval path

**Capital assessment:**
- Owner equity injection required: 15% × $400K = $60K
- Owner committed: $60K from savings
- **Verdict:** ✓ Approval path

**Collateral assessment:**
- Manufacturing equipment (offered at 60% LTV): ~$150K
- Commercial real estate (offered at 80% LTV): ~$320K
- Total collateral available: ~$470K (covers $400K loan)
- **Verdict:** ✓ Approval path

**Conditions assessment:**
- Use of proceeds: $300K for new machinery, $100K for working capital
- Payoff: New machinery generates $150K incremental annual profit
- Contingency: If revenue drops 20%, still generates $80K/year (covers debt service)
- **Verdict:** ✓ Approval path

**Result:** Approved in 8 weeks. Loan closed, funds disbursed.

## Summary: SBA Loan Success Strategy

1. **Know your DSCR** before applying (target >1.25)
2. **Organize complete documentation** (3-year tax returns, current financials)
3. **Be honest about negatives** (tell lender upfront about red flags)
4. **Prepare collateral** (identify real estate, equipment, valuables)
5. **Write clear use of proceeds** (be specific about how capital is deployed)
6. **Work with preferred lender** (they know the process, faster approval)
7. **Expect 6–12 weeks** from application to funding
8. **Follow through** on contingencies (appraisals, updated statements, insurance)

SBA loans are the gold standard for small business financing. With proper preparation and clear documentation, approval is highly likely.
