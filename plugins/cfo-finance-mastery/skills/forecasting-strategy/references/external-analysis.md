# External Analysis Frameworks

## STEP Framework -- Detailed

### S -- Social Factors
| Factor | Impact on Forecast | Example |
|--------|-------------------|---------|
| Demographics | Market size changes | Aging population reduces demand for certain products |
| Attitudes/Preferences | Product mix shifts | Health-consciousness drives organic food demand |
| Lifestyle Changes | Channel shifts | Remote work reduces office supply demand |
| Seasonal Patterns | Revenue timing | Pool supplies peak summer, dead winter |
| Cultural Events | Demand spikes | Black Friday, back-to-school, holidays |

### T -- Technological Factors
| Factor | Impact on Forecast | Example |
|--------|-------------------|---------|
| Platform Changes | Ad cost/efficiency | Facebook algorithm update (October) affects ROAS |
| AI/Automation | Labor cost reduction | AI tools reduce bookkeeping hours |
| E-commerce Tech | Channel expansion | New marketplace integration opens revenue |
| Cybersecurity | Cost increases | Compliance requirements add expense |
| Innovation | Competitive disruption | New entrant with superior technology |

### E -- Economic Factors
| Factor | Impact on Forecast | Example |
|--------|-------------------|---------|
| Interest Rates | Debt cost, buyer affordability | Falling rates boost home purchases and renovations |
| Consumer Spending | Revenue volume | Recession reduces discretionary spending |
| Inflation | COGS and wage pressure | Input costs rise faster than pricing ability |
| Currency | Import/export margins | Strong dollar reduces export competitiveness |
| Employment | Labor availability/cost | Tight labor market drives wage increases |

### P -- Political Factors
| Factor | Impact on Forecast | Example |
|--------|-------------------|---------|
| Legislation | Cost structure changes | New minimum wage laws increase labor cost |
| Tax Policy | Net income impact | Tax rate changes, new deductions/credits |
| Regulations | Compliance cost | Industry-specific regulation (FDA, EPA) |
| Trade Policy | Supply chain cost | Tariffs on imported goods increase COGS |
| Election Cycles | Market uncertainty | Election year reduces ad effectiveness, spending |
| Competition Policy | Market structure | Antitrust action changes competitive landscape |

## Headwind/Tailwind Classification

After identifying STEP factors, classify each as:

**Tailwind (growth driver):**
- Favorable economic conditions
- Technology improvements reducing costs
- Regulatory changes opening markets
- Demographic shifts toward your product

**Headwind (barrier):**
- Rising input costs
- Platform algorithm changes reducing ad efficiency
- New regulations adding compliance cost
- Increased competition

**Quantification:** For each headwind/tailwind, estimate the percentage impact on affected line items. Example: "Facebook October algorithm update = estimated 15% increase in CPM = headwind on advertising ROI."

## Porter's Five Forces -- Applied to Forecasting

### Force 1: Competitive Rivalry
**High rivalry affects:** Revenue growth assumptions (harder to grow), pricing assumptions (downward pressure), marketing spend (must spend more to compete).

**Forecast adjustment:** Conservative revenue growth rate. Higher marketing expense as % of revenue.

### Force 2: Threat of New Entrants
**High threat affects:** Market share stability, pricing power, innovation pressure.

**Forecast adjustment:** Model market share erosion scenarios. Increase R&D or marketing defensive spending.

### Force 3: Supplier Power
**High supplier power affects:** COGS stability, gross margin predictability, inventory costs.

**Forecast adjustment:** Build in COGS increases. Use higher buffer on cost projections.

### Force 4: Buyer Power
**High buyer power affects:** Pricing flexibility, discount frequency, customer retention cost.

**Forecast adjustment:** Conservative pricing, higher customer acquisition cost, model discount scenarios.

### Force 5: Threat of Substitutes
**High substitute threat affects:** Long-term revenue sustainability, pricing ceiling.

**Forecast adjustment:** Model customer switching scenarios. Invest in differentiation.

## Applied Example: Pool Supply Company (Leikauf)

### STEP Analysis
- **Social:** Summer crazy busy, winter dead (strong seasonal pattern). Growing interest in backyard living post-COVID (tailwind).
- **Technological:** Facebook October algorithm update hurts digital ad efficiency (headwind). New e-commerce platform improves customer experience (tailwind).
- **Economic:** Interest rates flat (neutral). Home improvement spending strong (tailwind).
- **Political:** Election year instability (headwind on consumer confidence). No industry-specific regulation changes (neutral).

### Porter's Five Forces
- **Suppliers:** 96 suppliers (complex supply chain, moderate power)
- **Rivalry:** 4 major competitors (company is smallest of big 4 -- moderate rivalry)
- **Buyer power:** Customers click cheapest on Google (high buyer power, not sophisticated)
- **Substitutes:** Many alternatives exist (high threat)
- **New entrants:** Rare at scale, but local shops encroach (low-moderate threat)

### Forecast Actions Taken
- Started marketing to spring crowd earlier (counter-seasonal strategy)
- Promoted hot tubs in winter (revenue diversification)
- Built seasonal impact: +35% in months 5-7 (summer), +10% month 8, flat rest of year
- Increased marketing budget for October-December to offset algorithm headwind
- Conservative pricing growth (high buyer power limits increases)
