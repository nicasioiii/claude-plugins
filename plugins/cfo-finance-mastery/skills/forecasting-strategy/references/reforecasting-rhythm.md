# Reforecasting Rhythm & Quarterly Planning Cadence

## Why Reforecast?

Your original forecast was built on assumptions as of Day 1. As months pass:
- **Actuals come in** and reveal whether assumptions were right
- **Market conditions shift** (competitor moves, macro change, regulation)
- **Execution differs** (you discover new opportunities, hit roadblocks)

Reforecasting keeps your plan **current and actionable** instead of stale.

---

## The P1 / P2 / P3 Quarterly Cycle

Reforecasting happens on a **quarterly rhythm**, producing three forecast vintages per year:

### P1 (Plan 1) — Annual Forecast
**Published:** December for the following calendar year
**Based on:** Nov/Dec actuals, holiday pipeline, seasonal assumptions
**Horizon:** 12 months forward (Jan–Dec of next year)
**Purpose:** Sets annual budget, hiring plan, strategic priorities

**Example timeline:**
- Dec 15: Publish P1 for 2026 (Jan–Dec 2026 forecast)
- Nov actuals in, Christmas/New Year pipeline visible
- Marketing, ops, finance finalize 2026 plans

### P2 (Plan 2) — Updated Forecast
**Published:** March for the remainder of year
**Based on:** Q1 actuals, updated competitive/macro view, revised assumptions
**Horizon:** 9 months forward (Apr–Dec of same year)
**Purpose:** Adjust if Q1 surprises shift trajectory

**Example timeline:**
- March 31: Close Q1 books
- April 1–15: Analyze Q1 vs. P1 forecast
- April 20: Publish P2 for Apr–Dec 2026
- Variance analysis: Did revenue hit? Did churn? Did costs align?
- Adjust hiring, marketing spend, inventory plans based on Q1 reality

### P3 (Plan 3) — Mid-Year Reforecast
**Published:** June for remainder of year
**Based on:** H1 actuals, mid-year performance, 6-month look-ahead
**Horizon:** 6 months forward (Jul–Dec of same year)
**Purpose:** Final adjustment before second-half execution

**Example timeline:**
- June 30: Close H1 books
- July 1–15: Analyze H1 vs. P2 forecast
- July 20: Publish P3 for Jul–Dec 2026
- Longer-term strategic adjustments (full-year outlook becomes clearer)
- Set H2 priorities and resource allocation

## The Reforecasting Process (Per Quarterly Cycle)

### Step 1: Close Actuals & Analyze Variance (2–3 days)

**Pull three months of actual results:**
- P&L (revenue, COGS, OpEx by category)
- Balance sheet (cash, AR, AP, inventory)
- Cash flow (operating, investing, financing activities)
- KPIs (customer acquisition, churn, ARPU, CAC, LTV)

**Calculate variance for each major line:**

```
Line Item          P1 Forecast   Actual    Variance    % Variance   Explanation
Revenue            $270K         $280K     +$10K       +3.7%        Seasonal strength, CAC working
COGS               $94K          $98K      -$4K        -4.3%        Supplier costs up
Gross Margin       $176K         $182K     +$6K        +3.4%        Better than forecast
Labor              $90K          $92K      -$2K        -2.2%        One early hire
Rent               $12K          $12K      $0          0%           Fixed, on budget
Marketing          $25K          $30K      -$5K        -20%         More aggressive CAC push; working
Net Income         $49K          $48K      -$1K        -2%          Nearly on target

Key Observation: Revenue and profitability tracking forecast; marketing spend higher
                 (planned push working, customer acquisition strong)
```

### Step 2: Identify Forecast Misses >10% (30 min)

Investigate variance sources:

```
Variance >10%:
- Marketing: P1 assumed $25K/month, actual $30K/month (+20%)
  Root cause: New campaign launched early (Q1 vs. Q2 plan)
  Impact: Higher CAC but stronger customer growth, validates ROI
  Forecast implication: Assume higher marketing spend forward (if ROI continues)

No other major variances >10%.
```

### Step 3: Update Key Assumptions (1–2 hours)

Based on Q1 actuals, update forward assumptions:

```
Prior Assumption (P1): Customer acquisition = 25/month, churn = 3%

Q1 Actuals: Acquisition = 28/month, churn = 2.8%

New Assumption (P2):
- Acquisition = 26/month forward (28 from Q1 is strong; normalize to 26 to be conservative)
- Churn = 2.9% forward (slightly better than baseline; credit CAC quality)

Seasonal adjustments:
- Q2 normally softer than Q1; apply 0.9x multiplier to forward months
- Acquisition forecast Apr–Jun: 26 × 0.9 = 23/month
```

### Step 4: Rebuild Forward Forecast (2–3 hours)

Using updated assumptions, project remaining 9 months:

```
P2 Forecast (Apr–Dec 2026):

Revenue Projection:
Apr–Jun (Q2):    23 customers/mo acquisition, churn 2.9%, existing base grows
                 Forecast: $240K/quarter (0.9x Q1 due to seasonality)
Jul–Sep (Q3):    25 customers/mo, churn 3%, back-to-school inventory buildup
                 Forecast: $270K/quarter (1.0x average)
Oct–Dec (Q4):    28 customers/mo, churn 3.2% (holiday customer volatility), Q4 spike
                 Forecast: $310K/quarter (1.15x average due to holidays)

Total Apr–Dec:   $820K revenue (vs. P1 forecast of $810K; slightly up)

COGS projection: Maintain 35% of revenue assumption
OpEx projection: Assume labor +$1K/month (one hire already in), marketing $30K/month

Net Income forecast (Apr–Dec): $175K (9 months)
Annualized: $233K (if 12-month run rate)
```

### Step 5: Publish P2 with Variance Analysis (1–2 hours)

Document:
1. **Quarterly variance summary** (what changed from P1?)
2. **Updated full-year forecast** (project Apr–Dec and annualize)
3. **Key assumption changes** (what's different?)
4. **Monitoring/triggers** (what would cause another reforecast?)
5. **Action items** (what do we adjust based on new forecast?)

**Example P2 Summary:**

```
P2 FORECAST SUMMARY (Published April 20, 2026)

Q1 2026 Actuals vs. P1 Forecast:
- Revenue: $280K actual vs. $270K P1 (+3.7%)
- Net Income: $48K actual vs. $49K P1 (-2%)
- Key variance: Marketing spend $30K (vs. $25K budgeted); driven by strong CAC ROI

Forward Assumptions Updated:
- Customer acquisition: 26/month (vs. 25/month in P1)
- Churn: 2.9% (vs. 3.0% in P1) — slightly better retention
- Marketing: $30K/month (vs. $25K/month P1) — maintaining higher spend given ROI
- Seasonal adjustment: Q2 0.9x, Q3 1.0x, Q4 1.15x

Full-Year 2026 Forecast (Revised):
- Revenue: $1,100K (vs. $1,080K P1; +1.8%)
- Net Income: $281K (vs. $275K P1; +2.2%)
- Full-year net margin: 25.5% (consistent with P1)

Go/No-Go Decisions for H2:
- Proceed with planned hires in Q2 and Q3 (forecast supports growth)
- Increase marketing budget Q4 (seasonal opportunity)
- No contingency actions needed (on track)

Next Reforecasting: June 30 (P3 publish July 20)
```

## When to Reforecast Outside the Quarterly Cycle

Major business changes **trigger mid-cycle reforecasting**:

### Reforecasting Triggers (Outside Normal Cadence)

**Revenue Trigger:**
- Actual revenue misses forecast by >15% for 2 consecutive months
- Major customer acquisition (>10% of monthly revenue) or loss (>5%)
- Market disruption or regulatory change affecting addressable market

**Cost Trigger:**
- Supplier prices spike >5% (above inflation forecast)
- Unexpected hiring needs (turnover, growth acceleration)
- New regulatory requirement (compliance cost)

**Execution Trigger:**
- Product launch delayed >60 days (affects revenue timing)
- Merger/acquisition announced in market (competitive shift)
- Key team departure (productivity impact)

**Example:**
```
Trigger: Major customer (15% of revenue) announces they're leaving
Action: Reforecast immediately (don't wait for quarterly cycle)
New forecast: Revenue down 15%, requires cost reductions in Q2

Publication: May 5 (mid-Q2)
Revised forecast: Rest of Q2 + Q3-Q4 adjusted downward
Contingency: Cut discretionary spending, defer hiring, reassess strategy
```

## Reforecasting Responsibilities

**Finance Lead (1–2 hours/month):**
- Track actuals vs. forecast continuously
- Spot major variances (>10%) in near real-time
- Prepare variance analysis for leadership

**Operations/Sales Lead (1 hour/quarter):**
- Provide forward pipeline visibility (Q2 sales expected, customer churn outlook)
- Update seasonal assumptions if market conditions shift
- Validate operational feasibility (can we execute the forecast?)

**Finance + Leadership (2–3 hours/quarter at reforecasting meeting):**
- Review Q1/Q2 variance together
- Agree on updated assumptions
- Debate high-impact changes (budget, hiring, marketing spend)
- Publish final forecast

---

## Reforecasting Governance & Decision-Making

**Small business (< $10M revenue):**
- One reforecasting meeting per quarter (3 people, 2–3 hours)
- Finance lead + ops/sales + CEO
- Publish within 2 weeks of quarter-end

**Medium business ($10M–$100M):**
- Formal reforecasting committee
- Cross-functional: Finance, Sales, Operations, Product
- Rolling 3-quarter forecast (current + next 2 quarters)
- Publish within 3 weeks of quarter-end

**Larger business (>$100M):**
- Formal rolling forecasts (3-quarter always forward)
- Monthly forecast reviews (non-published, internal tracking)
- Formal reforecasting cycle every quarter (published to board)
- Variance analysis required for >5% misses

## What to Track Between Quarterly Reforecasts

**Monthly monitoring** (to catch mid-cycle triggers early):

```
KPI                    Target        Trigger to Reforecast
Revenue                On forecast   Miss by >10% (2 months running)
Churn                  3%            Jump to >4.5%
CAC                    $50           Spike to >$70
ARPU                   $500          Drop to <$450
Gross Margin           65%           Drop to <62%
Labor Cost %           23%           Increase to >26%
```

**Monthly reporting** (15 minutes for CFO to scan):
- Revenue vs. forecast (YTD and current month)
- Key KPIs vs. plan
- Major variances (any item >5% different from plan?)
- One-page summary of "anything to watch?"

---

## Reforecasting Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Reforecasting after every miss** → Too frequent. Wait for quarterly cycle unless major trigger.
2. **Not adjusting forward based on actuals** → Reforecasting must incorporate lesson from variances.
3. **Reforecasting only when results are bad** → Update when results are good too (upside case).
4. **Forgetting to reforecast seasonality** → If Q1 actuals show different seasonality pattern, adjust Q2–Q4 multipliers.
5. **Publishing P1 in January instead of December** → Late publishing wastes 1 month of planning; publish in prior year December.
6. **Burying assumptions in model instead of documenting** → Assumptions must be visible and justified, not hidden in spreadsheet formulas.

## Example Annual Reforecasting Calendar

```
December 2025:
- Nov 30: Close November books
- Dec 5–10: Build P1 forecast for 2026 (Jan–Dec)
- Dec 15: Publish P1 2026 forecast to leadership/board
- Dec 20: Finalize 2026 budget and hiring plan based on P1

March 2026:
- March 31: Close Q1 books
- April 5–15: Analyze Q1 vs. P1, update assumptions
- April 20: Publish P2 forecast for Apr–Dec 2026
- April 25: Adjust H2 hiring/marketing plans based on P2

June 2026:
- June 30: Close H1 books
- July 5–15: Analyze H1 vs. P2, finalize full-year outlook
- July 20: Publish P3 forecast for Jul–Dec 2026 (also implies full-year view)
- July 25: Lock H2 strategic priorities and resource allocation

September 2026:
- Mid-cycle monitoring: Any major variances or triggers?
- If issues: Supplemental reforecast in early September
- If on track: Continue to Q4 quarterly reforecast

December 2026:
- Repeat cycle: Close Nov, build P1 for 2027

This rolling cadence keeps the forecast current and actionable throughout the year.
```

## Reforecasting Done Right

When you reforecast **on rhythm and trigger**, you:
- **Stay agile:** Adjust plan quarterly, not annually
- **Reduce surprises:** Catch bad news early, fix before it cascades
- **Build credibility:** Forecast becomes trusted planning tool, not ignored guess
- **Enable decisions:** Leadership makes better calls based on current data, not stale assumptions
- **Keep team aligned:** Everyone operates from same forecast baseline

Done well, reforecasting becomes a **continuous planning discipline** that keeps your business responsive to change.
