# Scenario Planning & Multi-Case Forecasting

## Why Build Multiple Scenarios?

A single forecast is a **point estimate** that assumes everything goes according to plan. Reality rarely cooperates.

Instead, build three scenarios:
1. **Base Case (60% probability)** — Most likely outcome
2. **Optimistic Case (20% probability)** — Upside if things break your way
3. **Pessimistic Case (20% probability)** — Downside if market/execution struggles

This approach gives you:
- **Ranges, not false precision** (revenue could be $900K–$1.1M, not exactly $1M)
- **Contingency plans** (What do we do if pessimistic case hits?)
- **Stakeholder alignment** (Board/lenders see downside, not blindsided if it happens)
- **Decision tree for major bets** (Which decisions assume base case? What changes in pessimistic?)

## Building the Three Scenarios

### Base Case (Probability: 60%)

**Definition:** Historical trends continue, market conditions stable, execution stays consistent.

**Assumptions:**
- Revenue growth: Historical average ± seasonal adjustment
- Cost structure: Current margins, no major input inflation
- Hiring: Current staffing plan, no sudden departures or growth
- Market: Competitive intensity unchanged, no major disruption

**Method:** Build base case first (the most likely outcome).

**Example:**

```
Base Case: Coffee shop, $800K revenue baseline

Assumptions:
- Same 12-month seasonality pattern (Q4 spike from holidays, summer slight dip)
- 8% YoY growth (historical 3-year average, conservative)
- COGS 35% (historical average)
- Labor costs +4% (wage inflation, no net new hires)
- Rent flat ($4K/month, locked in 2-year lease)

Forecast:
Revenue            $864,000    (800K × 1.08)
COGS              ($302,400)   (35%)
Gross Profit       $561,600    (65%)
Labor             ($216,000)   (25% of revenue, up from 24% baseline)
Rent               ($48,000)   (5.5% of revenue, up from 6% baseline)
Other OpEx         ($80,000)   (9.2% of revenue, flat)
Net Operating Income $217,600 (25% margin)
```

### Optimistic Case (Probability: 20%)

**Definition:** Key upside assumptions play out; execution exceeds expectations.

**Typical upside drivers:**
- New market opportunity or customer segment tapped
- Competitor exit or stumble, taking share
- Product innovation resonates stronger than expected
- Market growth accelerates
- Operational efficiency better than expected

**Constraints:** Keep it realistic. "Optimistic" means +15–25% above base, not fantasy growth of +100%.

**Example - Coffee shop optimistic case:**

```
Optimistic Case Assumptions:
- New drive-through window opens (Q2 2026), adds $150K annual revenue
- Viral social media following (local influencer endorsement), +3% organic growth
- Wholesale coffee line to local offices, +$50K annual revenue
- Labor efficiency improves (better scheduling, cross-training), labor as 23% of revenue vs. 25%

Forecast:
Revenue            $1,064,000  (800K baseline × 1.08 + 150K new unit + 50K wholesale + 30K viral growth)
COGS              ($372,400)   (35%, similar to base; wholesale higher margin offset by drive-thru cost)
Gross Profit       $691,600    (65%)
Labor             ($245,000)   (23% of revenue, better efficiency)
Rent               ($60,000)    (6% due to new drive-thru lease)
Other OpEx         ($90,000)    (8.5% of revenue; slight increase for new marketing/systems)
Net Operating Income $296,600 (28% margin)

Upside vs. base: +$79,000 net income (36% improvement)
```

### Pessimistic Case (Probability: 20%)

**Definition:** Headwinds materialize; market weakens or execution falters.

**Typical downside drivers:**
- Major customer loss or churn acceleration
- Competitive price war or new competitor
- Recession/consumer spending decline
- Key staff departure, productivity drops
- Input cost inflation (labor, materials) higher than expected
- Regulatory change increasing costs

**Constraints:** Keep it realistic. Pessimistic usually means -10–20% below base, not collapse.

**Example - Coffee shop pessimistic case:**

```
Pessimistic Case Assumptions:
- New competitor (Starbucks-owned location) opens 2 blocks away, takes 15% market share
- Economic recession (local unemployment +2%), foot traffic down 10%
- Wage inflation higher than expected (+6% vs. +4%), labor costs up
- Rent increases (new lease renewal in Q3 2026), +$500/month
- Marketing required to compete, +$20K annual spend

Forecast:
Revenue            $680,000    (800K baseline × 0.85, accounting for 15% loss + recessionary headwinds)
COGS              ($238,000)   (35%)
Gross Profit       $442,000    (65%)
Labor             ($169,000)   (24.8% of revenue, up due to +6% wage inflation = +$12K)
Rent               ($54,000)    (7.9% of revenue, up $6K from new lease rate)
Other OpEx        ($100,000)    (14.7% of revenue, includes +$20K marketing)
Net Operating Income $119,000  (17.5% margin)

Downside vs. base: -$98,600 net income (45% decline)
```

## Probability-Weighted Forecast

Once you have three scenarios, calculate a **probability-weighted outcome** to guide planning:

```
Probability-Weighted Revenue = (Base × 60%) + (Optimistic × 20%) + (Pessimistic × 20%)

Coffee shop example:
= ($864,000 × 0.6) + ($1,064,000 × 0.2) + ($680,000 × 0.2)
= $518,400 + $212,800 + $136,000
= $867,200

This is your "expected value" forecast to use for budgeting, hiring decisions.
```

## Using Scenarios for Decision-Making

### Example 1: Marketing Budget Decision

**Question:** Should we increase marketing spend by $50K/year to drive more customer acquisition?

**Three-scenario test:**

```
Base Case (60% probability):
Current marketing: $80K/year
Revenue growth without increase: 8% = $864K

With $50K increase, expect CAC to drop (better targeting), +2% incremental growth = $893K
Net income change: -$50K (marketing) + $23K (revenue at 45% gross margin) = -$27K
Decision: NOT recommended in base case (negative ROI)

Optimistic Case (20% probability):
With $50K increase, market accelerates, compound effect, +4% incremental growth = $1,107K (vs. $1,064K)
Net income change: -$50K (marketing) + $40K (revenue) = -$10K
Still slightly negative; marketing ROI weak

Pessimistic Case (20% probability):
With $50K increase, market down anyway, increased marketing doesn't help
Marketing wasted
Decision: STRONG "no" in pessimistic case

Probability-weighted ROI: Negative across all cases
→ DECISION: Don't increase marketing spend; focus on efficiency in base case.
```

### Example 2: Hiring Decision

**Question:** Should we hire a sales manager (+$100K annual cost) to accelerate growth?

```
Base Case:
Without manager: Revenue $864K, Net Income $217.6K
With manager: Sales team 20% more productive, +$150K revenue, Net Income ~$230K
ROI: ($230K - $217.6K - $100K) = -$87.6K
Decision: Not recommended in base case

Optimistic Case:
With manager, team jells faster, +$300K incremental revenue (vs. optimistic case)
Revenue $1,364K, Net Income ~$380K
ROI: ($380K - $296.6K - $100K) = -$16.6K
Still marginally negative; but with upside potential

Pessimistic Case:
Revenue pressure anyway, new manager salary drags down profitability
Not recommended

Probability-weighted:
= (-$87.6K × 0.6) + (-$16.6K × 0.2) + (-$50K × 0.2)
= -$52.6K - $3.3K - $10K = -$66K overall

Decision: DON'T hire manager. Invest in efficiency instead.
```

## Sensitivity Analysis: What Assumptions Really Matter?

Not all assumptions are created equal. Some have huge impact on forecast; others are minor.

**Run sensitivity analysis on key drivers:**

```
Base Case Net Income: $217,600

Change one assumption at a time, hold others constant:

Assumption         Change              New Net Income    % Impact
Revenue +/- 5%    +5% → $907.2K      +$30,300          +14%
Revenue +/- 5%    -5% → $820.8K      -$30,300          -14%
COGS +/- 2%       +2% → 37% COGS     -$15,600          -7%
COGS +/- 2%       -2% → 33% COGS     +$15,600          +7%
Labor +/- 10%     +10% labor costs   -$21,600          -10%
Labor +/- 10%     -10% labor costs   +$21,600          +10%
Rent +/- $500/mo  +$500/mo rent      -$6,000           -3%
```

**Insights:**
- Revenue swings have **largest impact** (±14% on net income for ±5% revenue change)
- Labor costs have **high impact** (±10% swing on net income for ±10% labor cost)
- COGS has **moderate impact** (±7% swing)
- Rent has **low impact** (±3% swing)

**Decision implication:** Focus forecasting precision and contingency planning on **revenue and labor cost**, which drive 80% of net income variation. Monitor COGS. Don't sweat rent changes.

## Scenario Range Presentation

When presenting to stakeholders (board, lenders, partners), show all three scenarios:

**Table Format:**

```
Scenario          Revenue    Net Income   Net Margin   Key Assumption
Pessimistic       $680,000   $119,000     17.5%       Competitor disruption, -15% market share
Base Case         $864,000   $217,600     25.2%       Historical growth, stable market
Optimistic        $1,064,000 $296,600     27.9%       New unit, new revenue stream, viral growth

Probability       20%        60%          20%
Weighted Value    $867,200   $215,760     24.9%       Expected value for planning
```

**Ranges to emphasize:**
- Revenue range: $680K–$1.06M (57% spread)
- Net income range: $119K–$297K (150% spread)
- This shows **significant uncertainty** and importance of contingency planning

## Contingency Planning: "If X, Then Y"

For each scenario, document triggers and contingency actions:

```
PESSIMISTIC TRIGGER & RESPONSE

If: Revenue down 15%+ YoY for 2 consecutive months
Then: 1. Reduce labor to 20% of revenue (cut hours/staff)
      2. Pause non-essential OpEx (no new training, defer maintenance)
      3. Increase marketing to 12% of revenue (try to recover market share)
      4. Target: Maintain 15% net margin through cost cuts

If: Rent increases >$500/month
Then: 1. Renegotiate lease terms
      2. Explore relocation to lower-cost space
      3. Model breakeven on relocation costs

If: Key staff departs
Then: 1. Accelerate hiring (cost +$30K/year)
      2. Temporarily reduce hours (preserve margin)
      3. Monitor profitability closely
```

## Updating Scenarios Quarterly

As you get **actual results**, update your scenarios:

```
Q1 2026 Actuals:
Revenue: $215K (tracking base case + 3% above due to new drive-thru early ramp)
Reforecast Q2-Q4:
- Optimistic case now 30% probability (early success visible)
- Base case now 50% probability
- Pessimistic case now 20% probability (competitor hasn't moved yet)

Probability-weighted forecast:
= ($864K × 0.5) + ($1,100K × 0.3) + ($680K × 0.2)
= $432K + $330K + $136K = $898K (up from $867K prior)

Hiring decision: Maybe NOW justified given shifted probabilities (optimistic case more likely)
```

## Common Scenario Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Optimistic case too optimistic** → Should still be realistic, not fantasy. Keep within ±25% of base.

2. **Pessimistic case too pessimistic** → Should model real downside (15–20% below base), not business failure.

3. **Assumptions not documented** → "Optimistic" without stating what drives it is useless. Always document.

4. **Only using base case for decisions** → Ignores risk. Good decisions stress-test across all three.

5. **Not updating with actuals** → Scenarios become stale. Reforecast quarterly as actual results come in.

6. **Probabilities summing to >100%** → Should be 60/20/20 or similar; don't use 50/30/20 unless justified by high uncertainty.

Done well, scenario planning turns a single forecast into a **strategic decision-making tool** that keeps you prepared for multiple futures.
