# Profit Levers & Strategic Optimization

## The Three Profit Levers (Choose Your Lever)

Every business profit comes down to **three levers**:

1. **Pricing** — Charge more per unit
2. **Costs** — Reduce unit economics (COGS, labor, overhead)
3. **Productivity** — Increase volume without proportional cost increase

Which lever you pull depends on **market position and constraints**:

| Lever | Best For | Constraint | Risk |
|-------|----------|-----------|------|
| Pricing | Differentiated, strong demand, low competition | Customer willingness-to-pay cap | Churn if too aggressive |
| Costs | Undifferentiated/commodity, scale-dependent, operational inefficiency | Can't cut quality below minimum | Margin compression if competitors cut prices |
| Productivity | High fixed costs, operational leverage opportunity | Execution complexity, capital investment | Cannibalization if growth demands exceed capacity |

**FOUNDATIONAL RULE: Most business owners undercharge by 30-50%.** Before cutting costs, test a price increase.
Pricing is the fastest, least risky, highest-leverage profit improvement available.
A 10% price increase on an 80% gross margin product flows almost entirely to profit.

**Rule:** Always consider pricing first. Only cut costs if you've exhausted pricing opportunities.

## Lever 1: Working Capital Optimization (The "2X Cash Flow" Framework)

Working capital is the **fastest lever for cash impact** because it doesn't require time to execute (unlike revenue growth or cost reduction).

### The Working Capital Triangle: AR → AP ← Inventory

Working capital = Accounts Receivable + Inventory - Accounts Payable

Three levers within working capital:

**A. Accounts Receivable (AR) Optimization**

Fastest way to improve: **Reduce Days Sales Outstanding (DSO)** — how many days until customers pay.

**Actions:**
1. **Set clear payment terms upfront** (not informal "net 30")
   - Example: "Invoice due 15 days from invoice date, not from delivery"
   - Automate invoicing (bill same day as delivery/service completion)

2. **Implement automated AR follow-up**
   - Day 15: Automated email reminder
   - Day 25: Second reminder + phone call
   - Day 35: Escalation to finance manager; consider payment plan options

3. **Track DSO monthly** and investigate trends
   - Formula: (Average AR / Annual Revenue) × 365 = DSO
   - Example: If AR is $100K and annual revenue is $600K, DSO = ($100K / $600K) × 365 = 61 days

4. **Offer incentive for early payment** (but only if discount < your cost of capital)
   - 2/10 Net 30 offer (2% discount if paid in 10 days)
   - Cost: 2% / 20 days = 36% APR (only worth it if your cost of capital >30%)

5. **Segment customers by payment reliability**
   - A-tier (consistent on-time): Standard terms (net 30)
   - B-tier (occasionally late): Shorter terms (net 15) or require prepay
   - C-tier (chronically late): COD or progress billing (don't advance credit)

**Impact:** Reducing DSO from 45 days to 30 days frees up 15 days of cash (about 4% of annual revenue).

**B. Inventory Optimization**

Excess inventory is not "savings"; it's **trapped capital**.

**Actions:**
1. **Forecast demand accurately** — Don't overbuy to avoid stockouts
   - Use sales history + seasonality + pipeline visibility
   - Example: If average monthly sales are 100 units and lead time is 30 days, maintain safety stock of 120 units (30 days + buffer), not 300 units

2. **Calculate safety stock scientifically** (not "just in case")
   ```
   Safety Stock = Z-score × Demand Volatility × Lead Time
   Example: Z-score 2.0 (95% confidence), demand volatility 15 units, lead time 15 days
   Safety Stock = 2.0 × 15 × sqrt(15) = 116 units (not 300)
   ```

3. **Manage lead times** — Work backward from sale date
   - If customer buys in May but inventory ships in March (2-month lead), order in Jan
   - For custom manufacturing, negotiate shorter lead times (reduces inventory float)

4. **Dead stock disposal** (move inventory without discounts)
   - Bundle with new products (offer package deal)
   - Sell to liquidators (recover partial cost instead of writing off)
   - Donate for tax credit (only as last resort)
   - **Avoid:** Deep discounts destroy brand value and margin

5. **Track inventory turnover** — How fast does inventory move?
   ```
   Inventory Turnover = Annual COGS / Average Inventory
   Example: COGS $350K, average inventory $100K → 3.5x turnover (inventory sits ~104 days)
   Goal: Higher turnover = less capital tied up
   ```

**Impact:** Reducing inventory 20% frees up ~$100K cash (if inventory is $500K).

**C. Accounts Payable (AP) Extension**

Opposite of AR: Pay slower (but maintain relationships).

**Actions:**
1. **Negotiate terms with all vendors** (not just one-time)
   - Ask for net 45 or 60 (not just net 30)
   - Leverage annual volume: "We spent $1.2M with you last year; can we get net 60?"
   - Don't accept net 30 from vendor unless contractually locked in

2. **Strategic timing of large payments**
   - Align with cash inflow: If you collect $200K payment from customer on 15th, pay vendor on 20th
   - Delay non-critical payments until next week if cash tight
   - **Caution:** Don't destroy vendor relationships; communicate if payment delayed

3. **Evaluate early payment discounts carefully**
   - Vendor offers: "2% discount if paid in 10 days (vs. net 30)"
   - Math: 2% over 20 days = 36% APR
   - Decision: Only take discount if cost of capital >36% (rare for healthy businesses)

4. **Use payment terms as working capital financing**
   - Example: Buy inventory on net 60, sell to customer on net 30 (net 30-day float on their account)
   - You're financing yourself through the gap

**Impact:** Extending payables from net 30 to net 60 (if suppliers allow) adds 30 days of cash float (~$30K if monthly COGS is $1K).

### Working Capital Quick Win Example

```
Current State:
- DSO: 50 days (customers pay slow)
- Inventory: $150K (high relative to $400K annual COGS)
- DPO: 30 days (we pay vendors fast)

Cash Conversion Cycle = DSO + DIO - DPO = 50 + 136 - 30 = 156 days
(Need capital for 156 days of operations; very tight)

Optimized State:
- Reduce DSO to 35 days (better invoicing, faster collection): +15 days cash freed
- Reduce inventory 20% to $120K (better forecasting, shorter lead times): +30 days cash freed
- Extend DPO to 45 days (negotiate with suppliers): +15 days cash freed

New Cash Conversion Cycle = 35 + 108 - 45 = 98 days
Freed capital: ~$200K (60 fewer days × $3.3K daily spend)

Cost to execute: 20 hours of finance time + vendor relationships (zero cash outlay)
ROI: ~$200K freed in 8 weeks (better than most growth initiatives)
```

## Lever 2: Pricing Strategy (Charge More Without Losing Customers)

Most owners undercharge. Test pricing increases before cutting costs.

### Pricing Psychology: The Thresholds That Matter

Prices ending in certain numbers convert differently:

- **$99 vs. $100:** $99 converts 10–15% better (psychological barrier at $100)
- **$9.80 vs. $10:** $9.80 converts 5–8% better (fractional pricing perception)
- **$49 vs. $50:** Similar effect

**Rule:** If raising price 5%, use psychological pricing ($99.95 instead of $100).

### Pricing Architecture: Three-Tier Model

Instead of single price, offer three tiers:

```
GOOD:       $499/month    (basic features, limited support, 100 users)
BETTER:     $1,299/month  (standard features, email support, 500 users) [POPULAR TIER]
BEST:       $3,999/month  (all features, phone support, unlimited users)
```

**Behavior:** 70% of customers pick BETTER (anchored to middle), 20% pick GOOD (price-sensitive), 10% pick BEST (feature-focused).

Shifting 5% from GOOD → BETTER = +$40K annual revenue (assuming 100 customers):
- 5 customers × $800 difference × 12 months = $48K incremental

### Pricing Tests: A/B Testing for Data-Driven Increases

**Run pricing experiments** before committing to new price:

```
Control Group:   Current price $500
Test Group:      New price $550

Run for 30 days, track conversion rate:
- Control: 10% conversion rate
- Test: 8.5% conversion rate (15% drop)

Math:
Control revenue: 100 prospects × 10% × $500 = $5,000
Test revenue: 100 prospects × 8.5% × $550 = $4,675

Result: Price increase reduces revenue. Revert to $500 or test $520 instead.
```

**Don't assume:** Test before changing all customers. A/B testing on new customer cohorts is safer.

### Customer Segmentation: RFM Analysis for Pricing Power

**RFM = Recency, Frequency, Monetary Value**

Segment customers and price differently:

```
VIP Tier (R=recent, F=frequent, M=high):
- Top 10% of customers by revenue
- DON'T discount (they buy regardless)
- Maximize pricing; upsell premium features
- NPS research: What would make you pay more? (Usually: better support, faster delivery)

**NON-NEGOTIABLE RULE:** Your top 20% of customers generate ~70% of revenue. Never discount to them — they buy regardless.
Discounts on your best customers destroy margins on your most profitable segment. Save discounts for reactivating lapsed customers only.

Core Tier (R=moderate, F=moderate, M=moderate):
- Middle 60% of customers
- Standard pricing; some negotiation acceptable
- Focus on retention; monitor churn

Marginal Tier (R=old, F=rare, M=low):
- Bottom 30% of customers
- Low pricing (already squeezed); focus on cost to serve
- Consider: Are they worth retaining? (Sometimes better to let them go)
```

**Key insight:** Top 20% of customers typically represent 70% of revenue. Don't discount them; they're your profit engine.

## Lever 3: Cost Control & Efficiency

Cut costs **only after exhausting pricing opportunities**. But when you do cut, be smart.

### The Profitability Framework: What to Cut, What to Invest

**NEVER cut:**
- Quality below customer expectations (leads to churn, returns)
- Growth/marketing spend (if ROI is positive; scale it, don't kill it)
- R&D (if keeping pace with competition)

**ALWAYS optimize:**
- Overhead (rent, software subscriptions, administrative costs)
- Vendor costs (negotiate terms, consolidate suppliers)
- Labor efficiency (eliminate low-value tasks, automate)

**Cut strategically:**
- Products/customers with negative unit economics (if losing money per sale, cut them)
- Underutilized capacity (if you have 30% spare capacity, consolidate operations)

### Cost Categories & Typical Optimization

**COGS (Highest Opportunity)**
- Negotiate supplier pricing (volume discounts, annual commitments)
- Consolidate suppliers (one vendor instead of five = leverage)
- Improve manufacturing/fulfillment efficiency (waste reduction)
- Optimize product mix (higher-margin products get more mindshare)

**Labor (Usually 25–40% of OpEx)**
- Eliminate low-value tasks (admin, data entry, reconciliation → automate)
- Improve utilization (target: 80% billable for service firms)
- Increase headcount productivity (tools, training, process improvement)
- Staffing vs. pricing: Often better to raise price than hire more (less complexity)

**Rent & Facilities (Usually 5–10% of OpEx)**
- Negotiate renewal (rent typically rises 2–3%/year; push back)
- Downsize if over-provisioned
- Co-locate/share space with other teams (if possible)

**Software & Subscriptions (Usually 3–5% of OpEx)**
- Audit all subscriptions quarterly (many unused tools hiding)
- Consolidate (e.g., single CRM instead of three different systems)
- Negotiate annual prepay discount (typically 10–15%)

**Marketing & Sales (Usually 5–15% of OpEx)**
- Cut unprofitable channels (if CAC >60% of LTV, kill the channel)
- Optimize profitable channels (if CAC $50 and LTV $200, scale it)
- Test before scaling (A/B test new channels on small budget first)

### Operational Efficiency: Key Metrics to Track

**Labor Efficiency (for service businesses):**
```
Billable Utilization = Billable Hours / Total Hours Available
Target: 80% (20% admin, training, other)

Example: 5 consultants × 40 hours/week × 50 weeks = 10,000 total hours
Billable hours: 8,000 = 80% utilization

If utilization dropping to 60%, either: cut staff, increase prices, or improve sales (get more clients)
```

**Before hiring: check if you have a pricing problem disguised as a capacity problem.**
If utilization drops below 70%, the fix is usually RAISING PRICES (to filter low-value work), not hiring more staff.
Raising prices > hiring more staff for most service businesses under $5M.

**Marketing Efficiency:**
```
CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost) = Marketing Spend / New Customers Acquired
LTV (Customer Lifetime Value) = Average Revenue per Customer × Gross Margin × Customer Lifespan

Profitability threshold: LTV > 3× CAC
Example: LTV $600, CAC $150 → 4x ratio (healthy)
Example: LTV $300, CAC $150 → 2x ratio (barely profitable; cut spend or improve LTV)
```

**Marketing efficiency rule:** If CAC < gross margin per customer, MAXIMIZE spend on that channel (it's profitable).
If CAC > gross margin, optimize before cutting. Don't cut growth spend that's working — advertising drives revenue.
Only kill a channel if CAC exceeds 2x gross margin after 90 days of optimization.

**Inventory Efficiency:**
```
Days Inventory Outstanding (DIO) = (Average Inventory / Annual COGS) × 365

Lower is better (faster inventory moves, less working capital tied up)
Example: Average inventory $100K, COGS $500K → DIO = 73 days
Goal: Reduce DIO to 60 days (frees ~$15K cash)
```

## Unit Economics: The Foundation of Profit Optimization

Unit economics = profit per customer / unit sold.

**Formula:**
```
Unit Economics = Gross Margin per Unit - CAC / Lifespan
```

**Example (SaaS):**
```
Monthly Subscription: $100
Gross Margin: 75% ($75/month)
CAC: $500
Customer Lifespan: 12 months (annual churn rate: 25%)

Unit Economics = ($75 × 12) - $500 = $400 profit per customer acquired
Payback period: $500 CAC / $75 monthly margin = 6.7 months
```

**Red flag:** If unit economics negative (paying more to acquire than customer generates), business is unsustainable. Fix before scaling.

## Key Principles

1. **Pricing before costs.** Most owners leave 20–30% on the table through underpricing. Fix pricing first.

2. **Working capital before hiring.** Improving AR/AP/inventory frees 4–8 weeks of cash with zero headcount. Do it before raising debt or equity.

3. **Unit economics inform strategy.** Never scale a product/channel with negative unit economics. Fix the unit first, then scale.

4. **Specialization enables pricing power.** Niche expertise (e.g., "fractional CFO for e-commerce") justifies 30–50% price premium vs. generalist.

5. **Profitability is a choice.** You can grow unprofitably or profitably. Set profit targets upfront; optimize levers to achieve them.

6. **Second-order effects matter.** Cost cuts often ripple (lower quality → churn). Model second-order before cutting.

7. **Leverage is multiplication.** Combine levers (better pricing + working capital improvement + labor efficiency) for compounding impact.
