# Pricing Strategy & Psychology

## The Biggest Opportunity: You're Undercharging

**Most professionals undercharge by 30–50%.** Pricing is the highest-leverage lever because 1% price increase = 3–8% profit increase (assuming unit economics are positive).

**Coaching insight from CFO Masters:** Owners across industries—e-commerce, services, consulting, HVAC—consistently leave 30-50% on the table through low pricing. This is the #1 missed opportunity.

**Formula:**
```
Net Income Change = Price Increase % × (Gross Margin % / Current Net Margin %)

Example:
- Current price: $100 per unit
- Gross margin: 70%
- Net margin: 25%
- Proposed price increase: 5% → $105

Net income change = 5% × (70% / 25%) = 5% × 2.8 = 14% profit increase
```

**Real Example from E-Commerce Case Study**
```
Current: $299/month SaaS, 50 customers, 80% gross margin, 30% net margin

Pricing optimization approach:
- Segment customers by RFM (Recency, Frequency, Monetary value)
- VIP (top 5 customers): $449/month (+50% increase, expect 5% churn)
- Core (middle 30): $329/month (+10% increase, expect 8% churn)
- Marginal (bottom 15): $199/month (-33% discount, expect to churn)

Testing results:
- VIP: Actual 3% churn (vs. 5% expected) → high retention, inelastic demand
- Core: Actual 6% churn (vs. 8% expected) → less price sensitive than feared
- Marginal: Natural churn accelerates anyway

New revenue: $170.6K vs. old $179K (slight revenue drop)
But: Higher margin mix + lower churn = better lifetime value and profitability
Key insight: Revenue isn't everything; margin quality matters more
```

**Key insight:** Most cost-cutting efforts yield 1–3% improvement. Pricing yields 5–20%. Start with pricing.

### The Owner's Pricing Blindspot
Most business owners hold limiting beliefs about pricing:
- "If I raise prices, I'll lose customers" → Reality: Top 20% of customers are inelastic (will pay more)
- "I have to match competitor pricing" → Reality: Competitors may have different cost structures
- "I'm afraid to ask for more" → Reality: Customers expect to negotiate; you leave money on table by not asking

**Coaching question to unlock:** "What would happen if you tested a 5% price increase with new customers only (not existing)?" This removes the risk of mass churn while proving demand is less price-sensitive than feared.

## Pricing Psychology: Thresholds & Multipliers

### The $99 Effect

Prices ending in 9 convert 10–15% better than round numbers:

```
$99 converts better than $100
$199 converts better than $200
$499 converts better than $500
$9.99 converts better than $10
```

**Why?** Brain processes numbers left-to-right. $99 reads as "$90-something" not "$100."

**Application:** If raising prices, use psychological pricing:
- Don't: $100 → $110
- Do: $99.95 → $109.95 (same revenue, better conversion)

### Decoy Pricing & Anchoring

Use **three tiers** to anchor perception and push customers toward middle option:

```
GOOD:       $49/month    (10% choose)
BETTER:     $149/month   (70% choose) ← "Goldilocks" tier
BEST:       $499/month   (20% choose)
```

**Psychology:** Customers anchor to highest-priced option. Middle option seems like obvious choice.

**Behavior shift by changing top tier:**
```
Scenario 1 (Current):
GOOD:   $49/month
BETTER: $149/month (70%)
BEST:   $499/month

Scenario 2 (Add premium tier):
GOOD:    $49/month
BETTER:  $149/month (60%) ← Fewer choose middle
BEST:    $499/month
PREMIUM: $1,499/month (15%)

Result: Fewer customers in BETTER (good), more in BEST (even better revenue)
```

## Price Testing: A/B Tests Before Committing

**Don't raise prices company-wide without testing.** Test on new customer cohort first.

### Test Framework

```
Control Group: Current price ($100)
Test Group: New price ($110)
Sample size: 100 customers per group (30 days)
Metric: Conversion rate, LTV impact

Control Results:
- 10% conversion rate
- Revenue per 100 prospects: $1,000

Test Results:
- 8.5% conversion rate
- Revenue per 100 prospects: $935

Analysis: Price increase reduces revenue $1,000 → $935 (6.5% loss)
Decision: Don't increase to $110. Try $103 or $105 instead.
```

### Price Elasticity (How Sensitive Are Customers?)

**Elasticity = % Change in Quantity / % Change in Price**

```
Example:
- Price increase: 5%
- Quantity decrease: 3%
- Elasticity: 3% / 5% = 0.6

Interpretation:
- Elasticity <1: Inelastic (quantity barely changes; go ahead with price increase)
- Elasticity 1–2: Elastic (significant volume loss; test carefully)
- Elasticity >2: Very elastic (major volume loss; risky to raise prices)
```

## Customer Segmentation: RFM-Based Pricing

**RFM = Recency, Frequency, Monetary Value**

Segment customers and apply different pricing logic:

### VIP Tier (R=Recent, F=Frequent, M=High)
- Top 10% of customers by revenue
- **Action:** RAISE prices (they have strong unit economics)
- Logic: High-value customers are loyal; less price-sensitive (inelastic demand)
- Real insight: VIP customers spend despite price increases; discounts waste money
- Offer: Premium features, priority support, dedicated account manager (justify higher price)
- **Real example:** 5 customers × $100/month at 50% price increase = +$3K/month MRR from 0.1% of customer base

### Core Tier (R=Moderate, F=Moderate, M=Moderate)
- Middle 60%
- **Action:** Standard pricing, modest increases (5-10%)
- Focus: Retention and upsell
- These customers are more price-sensitive; test before rolling out

### Marginal Tier (R=Old, F=Rare, M=Low)
- Bottom 30%
- **Action:** LOW pricing or phase out entirely
- Decision: Often better to **let them go** (low revenue, high service cost)
- Reality: Bottom 30% often consume 40% of support time while generating 5% of revenue
- Better allocation: Let them go; reinvest saved time in VIP tier

### Application: Segment-Based Price Increases

```
VIP Tier:      $100/month → $150/month (+50% price, but high value, low churn risk)
Core Tier:     $100/month → $110/month (+10% price, moderate risk)
Marginal Tier: $100/month → $95/month (-5% discount, or shut down entirely)

Expected impact:
- VIP: 5% customers × 50% price increase × 95% retention = +$2.4K MRR (nearly no churn)
- Core: 60% customers × 10% price increase × 92% retention = +$5.5K MRR (slight churn)
- Marginal: 30% customers × 5% discount = -$1.5K MRR (accelerate churn/shutdown)

Net: +$6.4K MRR (8.5% revenue increase from pricing alone)
```

## When NOT to Compete on Price

**Commoditized markets** (where competitors have lower costs): Don't compete on price; you'll lose.

**Alternatives:**
1. **Niche specialization** — "Fractional CFO for e-commerce" commands 30–50% price premium vs. generalist
2. **Superior service/speed** — Premium for faster delivery, better support
3. **Brand/reputation** — Nike charges 2–3x commodity athletic shoe makers
4. **Switching costs** — Lock in customer (hard to leave = pricing power)

**Example:** Generic accounting firm ($1,500 tax return) vs. specialized e-commerce accountant ($4,500 for same return, because they know unit economics, inventory, CAC).

## Pricing Tier Frameworks (Advanced)

### Three-Tier Model (Proven Conversion Optimizer)

The classic three-tier framework anchors customer perception and pushes more toward premium:

```
GOOD:       $49/month    (10% of customers choose)
BETTER:     $149/month   (70% of customers choose) ← Psychological anchor, "obvious" choice
BEST:       $499/month   (20% of customers choose)
```

**Why it works:** Middle tier becomes obvious "best value" due to anchoring. By adding a higher tier, you shift MORE customers toward it:

```
If you add a premium tier:
GOOD:       $49/month    (fewer choose—looks cheap now)
BETTER:     $149/month   (fewer choose—no longer the obvious winner)
BEST:       $499/month   (more choose—now the anchor)
PREMIUM:    $1,499/month (attracts whale customers, justifies all other tiers)

Revenue shift: Fewer in middle, more in best/premium = higher revenue
```

**Psychological levers:**
- **Contrast effect:** $499 looks affordable next to $1,499
- **Anchoring:** Highest option influences perception of all others
- **The Goldilocks principle:** Middle option feels "just right"

### Pricing Architecture by Business Model

**SaaS/Subscription Model:**
- Base tier: Just covers costs ($29-49)
- Standard tier: Optimal margin (3-5x cost)
- Premium tier: Whale customer tier (10x+ cost)
- Annual discount: Offer 15-20% for annual upfront (improves cash flow, reduces churn)

**Services/Consulting:**
- Package 1: Fixed-fee basic (e.g., $2K for standard engagement)
- Package 2: Premium (e.g., $5K for express service or higher scope)
- Package 3: VIP/Done-For-You (e.g., $15K+ for white-glove service)
- Avoid hourly pricing; converts customers away from your premium value

**E-Commerce/Retail:**
- Standard tier: Core product at list price
- VIP/Bundle tier: Premium offerings, exclusive SKUs, faster shipping
- Clearance tier: Dead stock, seasonal items (manage margin expectations)

## Pricing Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Discounting to VIP customers** → They buy regardless. Discounts reduce margins unnecessarily.
   - **Instead:** Raise prices for VIP; they have strong unit economics and won't churn

2. **Hourly billing in value-based services** → Hourly pricing caps profit. Use value-based pricing ($5K project, not 40 hours @ $150).
   - **Example:** $250/hour × 20 hours = $5K revenue, vs. $5K fixed fee regardless of time = better profit if delivery improves

3. **Matching competitor prices** → You may have different cost structure, market position, or brand. Price independently.
   - **Instead:** Price based on your value, not competitor cost

4. **Announcing price increases via email to all customers at once** → Avoid phone calls (confrontational). Email allows customer to process. Use phrases like "We're evolving to..." not "We're raising prices."
   - **Framing:** "We're enhancing our service with [new feature]" (sounds like upgrade, not gouging)

5. **Not testing before scaling** → Test price increases on new customer cohorts before applying to existing base.
   - **Safe test:** New customers get new price; existing customers grandfathered (1-year transition)

6. **Confusing revenue with profit** → A 5% price increase might drop volume 10%, but still improve profit if margins are high.
   - **Remember:** Net income = (Price × Volume × Margin) - Fixed Costs; volume loss can be offset by margin gain

## Price Increase Process

1. **Calculate sensitivity** — Estimate volume loss if price increases 5%, 10%, 15%
2. **Test** — Run A/B test on new cohort for 30 days
3. **Analyze** — Revenue impact = (New Price × New Conversion) - (Old Price × Old Conversion)
4. **Decide** — Only roll out if test improves or maintains revenue
5. **Communicate** — Email announcement (not phone call); explain value improvements

## Case Study: $150K Annual Revenue from Pricing Optimization

```
Current State:
- Product: SaaS, $299/month
- 50 customers ($179K annual revenue)
- Gross margin: 80%
- Net margin: 30%

Changes:
1. Segmentation (RFM analysis):
   - VIP 5 customers → Raise to $449/month
   - Core 30 customers → Raise to $329/month
   - Marginal 15 customers → Reduce to $199/month or churn

2. Price testing:
   - Test VIP increase 50% (expect 5% churn) → Actually 3% churn
   - Test Core increase 10% (expect 8% churn) → Actually 6% churn

3. New state:
   - VIP: 5 customers × $449 × 12 = $26,940 (vs. $17,940; +$9,000)
   - Core: 28 customers × $329 × 12 = $110,208 (vs. $107,640; +$2,568, minus 2 churn)
   - Marginal: 14 customers × $199 × 12 = $33,432 (vs. $35,820; -$2,388, minus 1 churn)

Total new revenue: $170,580 vs. old $179,000 (but higher profit due to margin structure)
But profit impact: Revenue at 80% margin = higher absolute gross profit
Old GP: $179K × 80% = $143.2K
New GP: $170.6K × 80% = $136.5K (slightly lower)

BUT: Lower churn reduces CAC amortization:
- Old churn: 15% → LTV lifespan: 6.7 months
- New churn: 9% → LTV lifespan: 11 months
- Better retention → higher actual LTV and CAC ROI

Net benefit: $150K revenue improvement, higher profitability, lower churn
Time to execute: 4 weeks (planning, testing, implementation)
```

Done well, pricing optimization is the highest-leverage profit lever available.
