# Profit Optimization

## Core Principle

Every business's profit comes from **three levers**: pricing, costs, and productivity. Most business owners undercharge by 30-50%. Before cutting costs, test a price increase. A 10% price increase on an 80% gross margin product flows almost entirely to profit.

**Rule of thumb:** Consider pricing first. Only cut costs after exhausting pricing opportunities.

---

## The Profit Tree Framework

Visual hierarchy of every lever driving profit. Use during monthly reviews to pinpoint the branch with the biggest opportunity.

```
Profit
  |-- Revenue
  |     |-- Price (value-based, anchoring, decoy)
  |     |-- Volume (acquisition, retention, reactivation)
  |     |-- Mix (shift toward higher-margin products/services)
  |-- COGS
  |     |-- Materials (procurement, vendor negotiation)
  |     |-- Labor (loaded cost, productivity)
  |     |-- Overhead (step-fixed costs, utilization)
  |-- SG&A
        |-- Fixed (facilities, tech stack, admin)
        |-- Variable (marketing, commissions, travel)
```

**How to use:** Walk the tree top-down. At each branch, ask: "Can we move this 5-10%?" The branch with the highest dollar impact and lowest execution risk is your first lever.

## Lever 1: Pricing Strategy

Pricing is the #1 overlooked lever that creates immediate ROI.

### The Pricing Spectrum

```
HIGH END: Value-Based Pricing
  (Charge just below the value you create)

SWEET SPOT: Intersection of Cost + Affordability + Value

LOW END: Cost-Plus Markup
  (Cover expenses + desired profit, minimum 3x markup on cost)

DANGER ZONES:
  Too Low: Insufficient profit, cannot scale, attracts bad clients, burnout
  Too High: Dissatisfied clients, churn, potential losses
```

### Pricing Tactics

| Tactic | How It Works | When to Use |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| **Value-based** | Price on outcomes delivered, not time/inputs | Differentiated offerings, strong demand |
| **Anchoring** | Set high reference price so actual feels reasonable | Proposals, tiered offerings |
| **Decoy pricing** | Three tiers; middle option steered by extremes (SaaS menu model) | Subscription, service packages |
| **Price testing** | A/B test prices on small segments | Any business with volume |

**Implementation:** Start with a 5-10% increase on your least price-sensitive segment. Measure churn over 60 days. If churn is under 5%, the increase was underpriced. Repeat.

## Lever 2: Cost Reduction

### Fixed vs. Variable Cost Management

| Type | Examples | Management Approach |
|------|----------|-------------------|
| **Fixed** | Rent, salaries, insurance | Renegotiate periodically; right-size before scaling |
| **Variable** | Materials, commissions, shipping | Optimize unit economics; procurement leverage |
| **Step-fixed** | Warehouse space, management layers | Plan for threshold triggers; avoid premature scaling |

### Strategic Procurement

Go beyond price negotiation:
- **Consolidate vendors** for volume leverage
- **Commit volume** in exchange for unit savings
- **Negotiate every 6 months** with all major vendors (see working-capital skill)
- **Project annual spend** to demonstrate buying power

### The Credit Card Trick (Finding Hidden Waste)

Issue a **new credit card number** to the business. Route all new charges to it. The old card balance reveals hidden recurring charges and subscriptions. Simple way to find $500-$5,000/month in waste.

## Lever 3: Productivity

### Key Metrics

| Metric | Formula | What It Shows |
|--------|---------|---------------|
| **Revenue per employee** | Total Revenue / FTE count | Efficiency of human capital |
| **Profit per employee** | Net Income / FTE count | True productivity measure |
| **Loaded labor cost** | Salary + benefits + taxes + overhead per employee | Real cost of headcount |

### Improvement Levers

1. **Time studies** -- identify where hours go vs. where they create value
2. **Technology audits** -- automate repetitive tasks (AP, invoicing, reporting)
3. **Process mapping** -- find bottlenecks and redundant steps
4. **Utilization rate** -- for services businesses, track billable vs. total hours

## Revenue Growth Strategies

### Revenue Diversification

- Avoid over-reliance on a few clients or a single product line
- Build an "anti-fragile" business with multiple revenue streams
- Apply Porter's Five Forces to identify new opportunities (see forecasting-strategy)

### Customer Lifetime Value (CLV)

Focus on **retention over acquisition**. Increasing retention by 5% can increase profits by 25-95%.

### RFM Analysis (Recency, Frequency, Monetary)

Segment customers by three dimensions:

| Segment | Recency | Frequency | Monetary | Action |
|---------|---------|-----------|----------|--------|
| **VIP / Champions** | Recent | Often | High spend | Premium experiences, upsells -- never discount |
| **Loyal** | Recent | Often | Moderate | Cross-sell, reward program |
| **At-risk** | Lapsed | Was frequent | Was high | Re-engagement campaign, personal outreach |
| **Low-value** | Varies | Infrequent | Low | Automate outreach; focus human effort on top performers (80/20) |

**Key insight:** High-value customers get VIP treatment and premium offers, NOT discounts. Discounts train them to wait for sales.

### Revenue Tactics

- **Cross-sells:** Complementary products at point of sale
- **Upsells:** Premium version of what they already buy
- **Downsells:** Lower-priced alternative when they hesitate (keep them in the ecosystem)
- Build a company-wide **revenue culture** where all teams contribute to growth

> See `references/profit-levers.md` for CLV framework, RFM implementation, and revenue culture.

## Commercial Negotiation

Negotiation is a core profit lever. Every dollar saved in negotiation flows directly to profit.

### Core Principles (Leikauf, synthesizing Chris Voss)

| Technique | How It Works |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Mutual gain** | Both sides should feel they won |
| **Tactical empathy** | Understand the other side's perspective and pressures |
| **Mirroring** | Repeat last 2-3 words; encourages them to elaborate |
| **Labeling** | Name the emotion: "It seems like you're concerned about..." |
| **Strategic silence** | After your offer, stop talking. People fill silence with information |
| **Accusation audit** | Disarm objections before they arise: "You're probably thinking..." |

### Negotiation Rules

- Avoid "why" questions (sound accusatory) -- use "how" and "what" instead
- Aim for "that's right" (signals genuine agreement), not "you're right" (signals dismissal)
- In contracts: focus on deliverables, payment terms, failure clauses
- Use redlines for larger deals
- Always finalize: confirm terms, document, follow up

## Best Owner Theory

Every business has a "best owner" -- the entity that can extract maximum value from the firm. A CFO should help the client understand:
1. Whether they are currently the best owner
2. What changes would maximize firm value for ANY owner
3. How this affects capital allocation and exit planning

## Time Value of Money

A dollar today is worth more than a dollar tomorrow. Use the "lemonade stand" analogy for clients: "If I give you $100 today, you can buy lemons and make lemonade this week. If I give it to you next year, you missed a year of lemonade sales."

This underpins:
- Capital allocation decisions
- Financing choices (see financing-strategy)
- Project evaluation and prioritization

## Profit Opportunity Identification Process

1. **Analyze financials** -- Run vertical + horizontal analysis (see financial-diagnostics)
2. **Segment profitability** -- By product, customer, location, channel
3. **Walk the profit tree** -- Identify which branch has the biggest dollar opportunity
4. **Score by impact and effort** -- Quick wins first, then medium-term, then strategic
5. **Quantify the prize** -- "A 5% price increase on your top product = $X/year to the bottom line"
6. **Build accountability** -- Assign owners, set timelines, track in monthly call (see advisory-communication)

### Pitfalls to Avoid

- Cost-cutting without strategy (destroys capability)
- Lack of team buy-in (changes fail without culture)
- No measurement system (cannot improve what you do not track)
- Working capital mismanagement can reduce a business's sale price during M&A (see working-capital)
