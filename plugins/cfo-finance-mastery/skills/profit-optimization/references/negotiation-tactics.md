# Negotiation Tactics Reference

## Core Philosophy (Leikauf)

Negotiation is a **profit lever**, not a soft skill. Every dollar saved in vendor negotiation or gained in pricing negotiation flows directly to the bottom line with zero additional cost of goods.

**Mindset:** Mutual gain. Both sides should feel they won. Zero-sum thinking destroys long-term relationships and future leverage.

## Tactical Empathy Framework (Leikauf, synthesizing Chris Voss)

### The Five Core Techniques

#### 1. Mirroring

**What:** Repeat the last 2-3 words the other person said.

**Why:** Triggers the other person to elaborate, correct, or expand. Gives you more information without asking a direct question.

**Example:**
- Vendor: "We really cannot go lower than that price."
- You: "Cannot go lower?"
- Vendor: "Well, the raw material costs have gone up 12% this quarter..."
- (Now you understand their constraint and can propose alternatives)

#### 2. Labeling

**What:** Name the emotion or concern you observe.

**Format:** "It seems like..." / "It sounds like..." / "It looks like..."

**Why:** Validates the other person's position. Defuses defensiveness. Creates psychological safety.

**Example:**
- "It seems like you are concerned about locking in a lower rate for the full year."
- "It sounds like your team is under pressure to hit quarterly targets."
- "It looks like flexibility on timing matters more to you than the total price."

**Rule:** Never use "I understand" (sounds dismissive). Use "It seems like..." instead.

#### 3. Strategic Silence

**What:** After making your offer or asking a question, stop talking completely.

**Why:** Most people are uncomfortable with silence and will fill it -- often with concessions, additional information, or counter-offers that reveal their true position.

**Implementation:**
- Make your statement or ask your question
- Close your mouth
- Count to 10 silently if needed
- Wait for them to speak first
- The first person who talks after a silence loses leverage

#### 4. Accusation Audit

**What:** Preemptively address the worst things the other side might think about you or your position.

**Format:** "You are probably thinking..." / "This might seem like..."

**Why:** Disarms objections before they form. Takes the emotional sting out of concerns.

**Example:**
- "You are probably thinking we are just trying to squeeze your margins."
- "This might seem aggressive, but let me explain the reasoning."
- "You might feel like we are not valuing your partnership."

**Rule:** List 3-5 accusations before the negotiation begins. Address them proactively in the first 5 minutes.

#### 5. Calibrated Questions

**What:** Open-ended questions using "how" and "what" (never "why").

**Why:** "Why" sounds accusatory and triggers defensiveness. "How" and "what" feel collaborative and invite problem-solving.

**Examples:**
- Instead of "Why is your price so high?" use "How did you arrive at this pricing?"
- Instead of "Why can't you offer better terms?" use "What would it take to improve the terms?"
- "How can we make this work for both of us?"
- "What is the biggest challenge you face in offering better pricing?"

## The Negotiation Process

### Phase 1: Preparation (Before the Conversation)

1. **Know your numbers** -- Total annual spend with this vendor, alternative vendor pricing, your BATNA (best alternative)
2. **Know their numbers** -- Their margins, their competition, their contract renewal timing
3. **Set your range** -- Target price, walkaway price, and your opening position
4. **Prepare your accusation audit** -- List the 3-5 worst things they might think
5. **Project your annual spend** -- Demonstrate buying power (see working-capital: vendor spend projection)

### Phase 2: Opening (First 10 Minutes)

1. **Build rapport** -- Small talk, genuine interest in their business
2. **Run accusation audit** -- Disarm concerns preemptively
3. **State your goal** -- "We want to find something that works for both of us"
4. **Listen first** -- Let them talk. Gather information. Mirror and label.

### Phase 3: Negotiation (The Exchange)

1. **Present your position with data** -- Not just "we want a lower price" but "we have reviewed alternatives and believe $X is fair because..."
2. **Use silence after key points**
3. **Label their concerns as they arise**
4. **Ask calibrated questions** to understand constraints
5. **Propose creative solutions** -- Payment timing, volume commitments, longer terms, bundled services

### Phase 4: Closing (Lock It Down)

1. **Summarize agreed terms** -- Get a "that's right" (not "you're right")
2. **Document immediately** -- Written confirmation within 24 hours
3. **Confirm implementation** -- Who does what, by when
4. **Schedule review** -- Set the next negotiation date (every 6 months per Leikauf)

## Vendor Negotiation Specific Tactics

### The 6-Month Cadence (Leikauf)

Renegotiate terms with ALL major vendors every 6 months:
- Review current pricing vs. market alternatives
- Aggregate projected annual spend for leverage
- Present data-driven case for improvement
- If vendor refuses: request value-adds (faster delivery, extended terms, dedicated support)

### What to Negotiate Beyond Price

| Lever | How to Request |
|-------|---------------|
| **Payment terms** | "We need Net 60 instead of Net 30 to match our cash cycle" |
| **Volume discounts** | "We project $X spend this year. What tier does that unlock?" |
| **Early-pay discount** | Only accept if annualized rate > 30% (see working-capital) |
| **Delivery timing** | "Can we get priority shipping at the same rate?" |
| **Service level** | "What does dedicated account management look like?" |
| **Contract length** | Offer longer commitment in exchange for lower rate |
| **Exclusivity** | "If we consolidate all spend to you, what pricing can you offer?" |

### Negotiation Leverage Sources

1. **Volume** -- Project annual spend; bigger numbers = more leverage
2. **Alternatives** -- Have at least 2-3 competing quotes
3. **Timing** -- Negotiate at vendor's quarter-end or slow season
4. **Relationship** -- Long-term partners get better terms than transactional buyers
5. **Data** -- Market pricing, competitor quotes, historical spend trends

## Contract Focus Areas (Leikauf)

When reviewing or negotiating contracts, focus on:

### Must-Have Clauses
1. **Deliverables** -- Specific, measurable, time-bound
2. **Payment terms** -- When, how much, conditions for payment
3. **Failure clauses** -- What happens if deliverables are not met
4. **Termination provisions** -- How either party can exit, notice period, penalties
5. **Scope changes** -- Process for handling changes to the original agreement

### Red Lines for Larger Deals
- Use formal redline process (track changes in document)
- Involve legal counsel for contracts over $50K or multi-year terms
- Never sign same day on large deals (sleep on it)
- Pay attention to auto-renewal clauses (calendar reminders for termination windows)

## Negotiation Anti-Patterns

| Mistake | Why It Fails | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| Accepting first offer | Leaves value on the table | Always counter, even if offer is fair |
| Negotiating over email | Loses tone, rapport, real-time reading | Pick up the phone for substantive negotiations |
| Revealing your budget | Gives them your ceiling | Ask them to propose first |
| Using "why" questions | Sounds accusatory, triggers defense | Use "how" and "what" instead |
| Talking after your offer | Fills your own silence with concessions | Count to 10, wait for their response |
| Focusing only on price | Misses other valuable concessions | Negotiate terms, timing, service, volume |
| No walkaway point | Negotiates without a floor | Set a walkaway number before the conversation |

## "That's Right" vs. "You're Right" (Voss via Leikauf)

The two most important phrases in negotiation:

- **"That's right"** = They genuinely agree with your summary of their position. This signals real alignment and breakthrough.
- **"You're right"** = They are dismissing you. It means "I want you to stop talking." This signals no real agreement.

**Goal:** Always aim for "that's right." Get there by accurately labeling their emotions and summarizing their position so well they cannot disagree.
