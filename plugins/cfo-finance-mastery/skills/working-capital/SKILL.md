# Working Capital Management

## Core Concept

Working capital is the **fastest lever for cash impact** because it does not require time to grow revenue or cut costs -- it unlocks cash already trapped in the business.

```
Working Capital = Current Assets - Current Liabilities
```

**Goal:** Positive working capital that is actively managed, not passively accumulated.

---

## Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC)

The CCC measures how fast a dollar moves through the business and turns into more dollars. Leikauf calls it his **favorite financial ratio**.

```
CCC = DSO + DIO - DPO
```

| Component | What It Measures | Goal |
|-----------|-----------------|------|
| **DSO** (Days Sales Outstanding) | How fast you collect from customers | Lower is better |
| **DIO** (Days Inventory Outstanding) | How long inventory sits before selling | Lower is better |
| **DPO** (Days Payable Outstanding) | How long you take to pay suppliers | Higher is better |

### CCC Formulas

```
DSO = (Average Accounts Receivable / Annual Revenue) x 365
DIO = (Average Inventory / Annual COGS) x 365
DPO = (Average Accounts Payable / Annual COGS) x 365
```

### CCC Optimization Target

**Minimize CCC.** The lower the CCC, the faster cash circulates and the less external financing you need.

| CCC Range | Assessment | Action |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| Negative | Excellent -- you collect before you pay (Amazon model) | Maintain and reinvest |
| 0-30 days | Healthy | Monitor monthly |
| 30-60 days | Acceptable but improvable | Target one component |
| 60+ days | Cash is trapped | Urgent optimization needed |

### CCC Improvement Levers

Each day of CCC reduction frees approximately (Annual Revenue / 365) in cash.

**Example:** $3.6M revenue business, CCC = 45 days. Each day of reduction frees ~$10K in cash. Reducing CCC by 15 days unlocks ~$150K.

## Accounts Receivable Management

### Payment Terms Strategy

| Tactic | Implementation | Impact |
|--------|---------------|--------|
| **Clear terms upfront** | Written in contract AND on every invoice | Prevents "I didn't know" disputes |
| **Net 15 for new clients** | Default to shorter terms, extend as trust builds | Faster initial collection |
| **Progress billing** | For large projects, bill at milestones | Cash flows during delivery, not after |
| **Retainer/upfront model** | Collect before work begins (services) | Eliminates DSO entirely |
| **E-invoicing** | Invoice same day as delivery/service | Removes billing delay |

### The 28-Day Billing Cycle (Leikauf -- Contrarian)

**Standard practice:** Bill monthly (12 times per year).
**Leikauf's recommendation:** Bill every 28 days (13 times per year).

**Why:** 365 / 28 = 13.04 billing cycles vs. 12. That extra cycle generates approximately 8.3% more billing frequency per year.

**Implementation:**
- Set invoice dates on a 28-day rolling calendar
- Communicate to clients as "every 4 weeks" (sounds routine)
- Automate with billing software
- Works especially well for retainer and subscription businesses

### AR Follow-Up System

| Day | Action | Channel |
|-----|--------|---------|
| Day 0 | Invoice sent (same day as delivery) | Email |
| Day 7 | Friendly reminder | Automated email |
| Day 15 | Second reminder | Email + phone call |
| Day 25 | Escalation notice | Phone call from finance |
| Day 35 | Final notice + payment plan offer | Phone + formal letter |
| Day 45+ | Collections process or factoring decision | Depends on relationship |

### Customer Payment Segmentation

| Tier | Behavior | Terms | Management |
|------|----------|-------|-----------|
| **A-tier** | Always on time | Standard (Net 30) | Light touch |
| **B-tier** | Occasionally late | Shorter terms (Net 15) | Automated reminders |
| **C-tier** | Chronically late | COD or prepay | No credit extension |

### Factoring Receivables

When you need immediate cash from outstanding AR:
- Sell receivables to a factor at a discount (typically 2-5% of face value)
- Factor advances 80-90% of invoice value immediately
- Use as a **bridge**, not a permanent strategy
- Compare factoring cost against your cost of capital
- Best for: seasonal businesses, rapid growth with capital constraints

## Accounts Payable Management

### Vendor Negotiation Cadence (Leikauf)

**Rule:** Renegotiate terms with ALL major vendors every 6 months.

**Process:**
1. Project your annual spend with each vendor (volume = leverage)
2. Research 2-3 alternative vendors with pricing
3. Schedule a call (not email) with your sales rep
4. Present your projected spend and request improved terms
5. Negotiate beyond price: terms, delivery, service levels, volume tiers

### Early-Pay Discount Decision (Leikauf -- Contrarian)

**Standard practice:** Take early-pay discounts (e.g., 2/10 Net 30) whenever offered.

**Leikauf's position:** Skip early-pay discounts unless the annualized rate exceeds ~30%.

**The Math:**
```
2/10 Net 30 offer:
  You save 2% by paying 20 days early (day 10 instead of day 30)
  Annualized cost: (2% / 20 days) x 365 = 36.5% APR

Decision rule:
  If your cost of capital < 30%: DO NOT take the discount
  Keep the cash invested or working in the business
  Only take discounts that exceed your opportunity cost
```

**Why this is contrarian:** Most CFO advice says "always take early-pay discounts." Leikauf argues the math only works if the annualized rate beats what you could earn deploying that cash elsewhere.

### AP Optimization Tactics

| Tactic | How | Benefit |
|--------|-----|---------|
| **Pay on time, never early** | Automate payments for the due date | Maximum float |
| **Centralize purchasing** | One department manages all vendor relationships | Leverage and visibility |
| **Leverage trade credit** | Request Net 60 or Net 90 from key vendors | Free short-term financing |
| **Automated payments** | Schedule payments to land on due date | No late fees, no early payments |
| **Consolidate vendors** | Fewer vendors = larger spend per vendor = more leverage | Better pricing and terms |

## Inventory Management

### ABC Analysis (Leikauf)

Classify all inventory items by value contribution:

| Class | % of Items | % of Value | Management Level |
|-------|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| **A items** | ~20% | ~80% | Tight control, frequent review, safety stock calculation |
| **B items** | ~30% | ~15% | Moderate attention, periodic review |
| **C items** | ~50% | ~5% | Minimal effort, reorder when depleted |

**Rule:** Spend 80% of inventory management effort on A items. Do not over-manage C items.

### Inventory Optimization Tactics

| Tactic | When to Use | Benefit |
|--------|------------|---------|
| **JIT (Just-in-Time)** | Reliable suppliers, predictable demand | Minimizes carrying cost |
| **Phased payments** | Large bulk orders | Breaks payments into smaller cash events |
| **Dropshipping** | E-commerce, low-touch products | Eliminates inventory entirely |
| **VMI (Vendor-Managed Inventory)** | Strong vendor relationships | Vendor carries the cost until you sell |
| **Consignment** | New products, testing demand | No cash outlay until sale |
| **IoT tracking** | Complex supply chains | Real-time visibility, automated reorder |

### Inventory Carrying Cost

Excess inventory is not "savings" -- it is **trapped capital**.

```
Carrying Cost = (Storage + Insurance + Depreciation + Opportunity Cost) / Average Inventory Value
```

Typical carrying cost: 20-30% of inventory value per year. $100K in excess inventory costs $20-30K/year in hidden expenses.

## Payroll Frequency Impact (Leikauf -- Contrarian)

**Standard practice:** Many small businesses pay weekly.

**Leikauf's position:** Bi-weekly payroll preserves liquidity vs. weekly.

| Frequency | Pay Periods/Year | Cash Flow Impact |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Weekly | 52 | Constant cash outflow; tight float |
| **Bi-weekly** | 26 | Extra week of float; smoother cash planning |
| Semi-monthly | 24 | Fixed dates, easier to forecast |
| Monthly | 12 | Maximum float but employee dissatisfaction |

**Recommendation:** Bi-weekly balances employee satisfaction with cash flow management. Weekly payroll is unnecessarily tight on cash for most businesses.

## Anti-Profit-First Cash Management (Leikauf -- Contrarian)

**Profit First method:** Bucket cash into separate accounts (Profit, Owner Pay, Tax, Operating).

**Leikauf's position:** Do not just bucket cash. Forecast bills week by week to stay in true control.

**Why:** Bucketing creates a false sense of security. You might have $50K in your "operating" bucket but $60K in bills due next week. The bucket system does not tell you this.

**Alternative approach:**
1. Forecast all payables for the next 4-8 weeks
2. Map cash inflows against outflows by week
3. Identify cash gaps BEFORE they happen
4. Use a 13-week cash flow forecast (rolling) for precision
5. Reserve cash based on forecasted needs, not arbitrary percentages

## Working Capital Impact on M&A Valuation (Leikauf)

Working capital mismanagement can **reduce a business's sale price** during M&A:
- Buyers adjust purchase price for working capital normalization
- Excess AR (slow collection) reduces enterprise value
- Excess inventory reduces perceived efficiency
- Poor DPO (paying too fast) leaves cash on the table
- Clean CCC signals an efficient, well-managed business

## Projecting Vendor Spend for Leverage (Leikauf)

**Tactic:** Project your big annual spend numbers to negotiate better pricing, terms, and payment timing.

**Process:**
1. Total up all spend with each vendor over the past 12 months
2. Project forward 12 months (include growth assumptions)
3. Present projected annual spend in vendor negotiations
4. Use phrases like: "We expect to spend $X with you this year. Given that volume, what can you offer?"
5. Volume = leverage. Vendors will negotiate harder to keep a large account.
