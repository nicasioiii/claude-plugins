# Cold Call Frameworks

## 30MPC Three-Step Framework

### Step 1: Context-First Opener

Two openers that work. Both lead with context about the prospect -- by definition they cannot be canned.

#### Opener 1: Heard the Name Tossed Around (11.25% success)

**Structure:**
1. Lead with familiar context -- competitors, peers in their region, companies sharing same investor
2. Introduce yourself -- after the context, as an afterthought
3. Ask assumptively -- "Have you heard the name tossed around?"

**Script:**
"Hey [Name], we work with a few other [peers/partners] in the [location/portfolio/industry]. It's [Your Name] from [Company]. Have you heard the name tossed around?"

**If NO (most common):**
"Ha! Guess I'm not as popular as I thought. Well, the reason for my call is..." [roll into problem proposition]

**Or pivot to permission:**
"I guess I'm not as popular as I thought. Do you mind if I take 30 seconds to tell you how I work with some of the other [peers]? And then you can totally hang up on me if it doesn't make sense."

**If YES:**
"Oh! Well, we'd normally be working with someone like you by now. I guess, what'd you hear?"

**Key insight:** It does NOT matter if they have heard your name. It's about acting like they should have. This prevents them from treating you like a random telemarketer.

**Context examples:**
- Investor: "We work with a few other Sequoia portfolio companies"
- Same situation: "We work with a few other multifamily homeowners in Redwood City"
- Same space: "We work with a few other sales tech companies like Gong and Outreach"
- Combination (most effective): "We work with a few other compensation leaders in the Sequoia portfolio"

**Tone:** "Feet up" -- familiar, comfortable, like calling a friend's colleague. Context delivery with familiar laugh. Introduction as afterthought, neutral tone. Question is assumptive.

#### Opener 2: Tailored Permission Opener (11.18% success)

**Structure:**
1. Lead with context -- something about them connected to the problem you solve
2. Own the cold call -- be disarmingly honest
3. Get permission -- tell them how long you need, give perceived control

**Script:**
"[Name], I just finished reading your press release about the new office opening. I'm going to be honest, this is a cold call, but it is a well-researched one. And I'm wondering if I can get half a minute to share why that press release had prompted me to call you. You can totally hang up on me if it doesn't make sense from there."

**Critical rule:** The context CANNOT be random. It must connect to the problem your product solves. "I saw you went to USC" then billing software = bad. "I saw you just won a big case" then billing software = good.

**Context selection examples:**
- Billing software for law firms: Look for firms that just won a big case (they need to bill)
- Sponsorships: Look for companies launching new products (they want to advertise)
- Compensation software: Look for companies posting Head of Compensation roles

**If they say NO:**
1. Get to real objection: "Just so nobody from my team calls you again, is it that I caught you at a horrible time? Or you already know what we do and this isn't a priority?"
2. Pivot: "I promise I did some prep for this one. This isn't a random spam dial. Can I get 30 seconds?"

**Tone:** Professional, measured. Calm confidence. Not begging, not aggressive. Matter-of-fact honesty.

#### Opener 3: Untailored Permission (Backup Only)

"[Name], this is [Your Name] with [Company]. I'm going to be honest, this is a cold call, but it is a fairly well-researched one. And I'm wondering if I can get half a minute to share why I reached out to you specifically."

Use ONLY when you cannot find anything about the prospect. Higher rejection rate.

### Step 2: Problem Proposition

#### Why Traditional Value Props Fail
1. Vague buzzwords ("all-in-one platform", "single source of truth") = same as every telemarketer
2. Pitch benefits instead of problems = no context in prospect's mind
3. Humans are more motivated to eliminate pain than achieve benefits (Advil vs. multivitamin)

#### Problem Proposition Structure

**Part 1: Triggering Problem**
Describe so specifically it triggers a memory and creates a visual.

Layer descriptors to sharpen:
- **Persona:** "Most CFOs of insurance defense law firms..."
- **Annoyances:** "...those deductions, rejections, and appeals..."
- **Scenery:** "...after winning a big case..."
- **Emotion:** "...think it's ridiculous that they have to deal with..."

**Simple solution (one big problem):**
"Most talent teams find it mind-numbing to sift through 2,000 inbound applications just to figure out which five candidates you should even talk to."

**Complex solution (three-part problem, chronological):**
"Most tutoring teams are bogged down in minutia with scheduling all the sessions, tracking how many each student has left, and then making sure the tutors get paid."

**The Hairy Lollipop Test:** Drop a lollipop on a shag rug. That visceral disgust and specificity is what your problem needs. If you cannot see the problem, it's not specific enough.

**Chunk Up for Senior Personas:**
- Manager: "During Q1 merit season, most compensation teams are buried under a mountain of spreadsheets with 17,000 VLookups."
- CHRO: "Most people leaders freak out when they see their team is buried under spreadsheets because it becomes really hard to ensure managers are making equitable compensation decisions."

**Part 2: One-Sentence Solution**
"We do X so that the problem goes away" where X = your single biggest differentiator.

"Pave integrates your HRIS, your equity, and your comp data into one place so you literally never have to run merit cycles in spreadsheets again."

**Part 3: Interest-Based CTA**
Do NOT ask for a meeting. Validate interest only.

- "I know I totally cold called you out of the blue, but would you be open to learning more sometime?"
- "Would you be open to taking a peek at this thing, maybe not when I'm cold calling you out of the blue?"
- "Did any of that even sound moderately interesting?"

Rule: Only ask your prospect to make one decision or answer one question at a time.

## Longden's 7-Step Framework

### Step 1: Opening Statement / Intro
- Identify who you are, where you're calling from
- Be direct, no small talk ("How are you today?" is forbidden)
- Position yourself as expert through tone
- "Hi John, it's Sean from Company."

### Step 2: Why You Reached Out
- Deliver value proposition
- Include case study if available: "We recently helped XYZ company..."

### Step 3: Identify a Need
- Ask question tied to value prop that's virtually impossible to say no to
- "Is generating new leads and increasing revenue a priority for you?"
- Then LISTEN

### Step 4: Qualify the Prospect
- Company revenue, size, case studies, decision-making authority
- If not qualified: "I don't think this would be a good fit" (explain why)
- Never waste time on unqualified prospects

### Step 5: What You Can Do for Them
- Outline exactly what you would do, step by step
- Include expected ROI
- Minimize what's expected of them (make it easy)

### Step 6: CTA / Close
- "Could we arrange a time for a demo?"
- "Can we schedule a convenient time to talk?"
- If prospect is clearly interested, can close on first call

### Step 7: Handle Objections
- Address using value proposition
- Loop back to Step 6 after handling each objection
- All objections reduce to 3 categories: Financial, Trust, Hesitation

## Longden's Value Proposition Framework

### 5-Step Crafting Process
1. Research your audience -- problems, pain points, language
2. Create ideal buyer persona -- industry, role, revenue, case studies
3. Research competitors -- become their prospective customer, attend webinars
4. Determine primary benefits -- save time? Save money? Make money? All three?
5. Condense into one easy sentence -- must make sense to a 5-year-old

### Template
"We help [target market] [achieve outcome] using [method/approach]"

### Example
"We help marketing agencies get on the phone with their ideal clients every month using personalized email, LinkedIn and cold call outreach."

## Two-Call Framework (Longden, for Appointment Setters)

### First Decision Maker Call (3 Objectives)
1. Identify a need
2. Qualify them
3. Get agreement to second call

Before hanging up: "If you love the strategy and want to work with us, who makes that decision?" If someone else, get them on the second call too.

Must obtain: Mobile number, email address, qualifying answers, decision-making process.

### Second Decision Maker Call (3 Objectives)
1. Present offer using value proposition
2. Push ROI hard -- "Your LTV is $100K. We're charging 1% of that per lead."
3. Close

---

## Close in Silence (Longden)

1. Tell them EXACTLY what you'll do (step by step)
2. Tell them what's expected of them (minimize this)
3. State the price: "So John, I'll do all of this for $10,000 a month."
4. **GO SILENT.** Do not say another word. Even if 30 seconds pass.

Anything said after stating price sounds like justification. Silence = confidence.

## Warming Prospects Before Calling

Cold calls are interruptions. Making yourself familiar increases success.

### 3 Pre-Call Warming Actions
1. Send them an email
2. Connect on LinkedIn
3. Engage with their content (like, comment, share -- NO pitching)

Leverages the Baader-Meinhof Effect (frequency illusion) -- the more they see you, the more familiar you become.

### Warm Calling Priority
| Scenario | Priority |
|----------|----------|
| Prospect replies asking for info | HIGHEST -- call immediately, no longer cold |
| Opened emails / accepted LinkedIn | HIGH -- assign lead score, call highest first |
| Engaged on socials but haven't replied | MEDIUM -- reference engagement when calling |
