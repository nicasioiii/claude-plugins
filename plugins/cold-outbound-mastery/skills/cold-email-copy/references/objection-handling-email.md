# Email Objection Handling

## The Pricing Objection (Cold Email Wizard)

### Why This Is a Trap
"How much do you charge?" is a hidden objection, not a genuine question. 99.9% of the time, whatever number you give will be "too expensive" because they do not know your value yet. NEVER answer with a price via email.

### The 5-Part Framework
1. **Acknowledge:** "I'm happy to discuss pricing with you"
2. **Justify not answering:** "I'd be wasting both our time if I threw a random number at you via email"
3. **Explain why a call is needed:** "Our work is both custom and dynamic enough that your question warrants a quick phone call discussion" (only if genuinely true)
4. **Back off / build trust:** "No matter our pricing, we may not be a fit, so I might suggest a call to determine what, if any, ways that we can help"
5. **Preempt frustration:** "I know it's probably not the answer you're looking for, but hope this helps!"

### Alternative Version (Ecom/Service Businesses)
```
It really depends on your specific preferences and needs.

Which is why I always default to getting on a 20-minute intro call.

That way, we can talk through it all and create a custom plan
that makes sense for your business.

Otherwise, I'd be wasting your time and mine by giving you
unrealistic expectations of pricing.

I will say that our clients have all seen ROI positive results
after working with us.

I know it's probably not the answer you're looking for,
but I hope this helps!
```

**Critical note:** Only use "our work is custom and dynamic" if genuinely true for your offer. Do NOT use this line if your pricing is standardized.

## The "We May Not Be a Fit" Reframe (Cold Email Wizard)

This is HONEST positioning -- you are exploring fit, not selling:
- "We don't know if we can help you until we could see what you're currently doing. First off, you might be doing a fantastic job and we might not be able to help you."
- "No matter our pricing, we may not be a fit, so I might suggest a call to determine what, if any, ways that we can help."

## Budget / No Money Objection

### For Performance-Based Models
```
Ohhh don't worry {Name}, we've got you covered. My agency is a
100% performance-based model, so you only pay when we deliver
profitable results.

Would you like to jump on a call this week or next and talk about
how we can scale your {service} (without any upfront payments)?
```

### For Standard Pricing
Use the ROI math approach:
- "If you were spending $3000 for 10 calls, closed 2 retainer clients at $2500/mo, that's $5000/mo in new revenue from a $3000 investment."
- End with fit check: "That being said, the price point needs to make sense for your agency. So if they don't, this probably isn't a fit."

## "Tell Me More Via Email First" Objection

Comply with their request -- provide information. Then re-ask:
- "Let me know if you think this is worth at the very least, a 20-minute phone call to discuss further."
- This often leads to the pricing question, which you handle with the 5-part framework above.

---

## "Not Interested" / Close Rate Concern

Reframe using their sales pipeline data:
- "Our clients typically see a close rate through our outreach that's similar to the close rate they see with their own sales pipeline"
- Use ROI math to justify investment
- End with fit check: "That being said, the price point needs to make sense for your agency."

---

## "Follow Up with Me in {Month}" (Timing)

Agree, but push for a quick intro call NOW:
- "Would it be a terrible idea to grab 15-minutes on calendar to establish the connection?"
- Justify with differentiation: "We pride ourselves in having a more personal touch than other agencies"

## The Referral Strategy (Cold Email Wizard -- Near 100% Booking Rate)

### Why Referrals Are Gold
Most people give up when referred elsewhere. This is the BEST scenario because the chain-of-command principle means an employee is unlikely to refuse a meeting their CEO/boss recommended.

### The Referral Email Framework
```
Subject: Re: {Boss's Name}

Hi {Name}, {Your Name} here from {Company}.

I was speaking with {Boss Name / your colleague} via LinkedIn
in regards to setting up a {call type} call, and he recommended
that I reach out to you to connect.

[ATTACH SCREENSHOT OF LINKEDIN CONVERSATION]

Reply back to let me know some times that work for you.
```

### Critical Tactics
- ALWAYS attach the LinkedIn screenshot -- prevents the email from looking like a bluff
- Frame the referral as being "in regards to setting up a call" even if the original person only said "connect"
- The subject line "Re: {Boss's Name}" creates urgency -- prospect thinks it is from their boss
- This works "100% of the time" per the instructor

### When Referral Leads to an Assistant
- Work with the assistant professionally
- Provide calendar link: "To make your job as easy as possible, please use the link below to schedule a time"
- Be accommodating with scheduling adjustments

## Call Justification Techniques (Cold Email Wizard)

When a prospect needs MORE reason to take a call:

### "Visual/Technical Work Requires a Call"
- "The work we do is very visual. So it'd be best if we could be on a Zoom call."
- "The right solution is dynamic enough that it requires a non-salesy exploratory conversation."

### "Custom Plan" Justification
- "It really depends on your specific preferences and needs. Which is why I always default to getting on a 20-minute intro call. That way, we can talk through it all and create a custom plan that makes sense for your business."

### "Keep It in Your Back Pocket" Soft Sell
- "Completely understand we may not be a good fit at this point, but would love to stay in touch... And if it's not a terrible idea, would love to have a quick intro call to give you an overview about how things work on our end so that you can keep it in your back pocket."

## Calendar Link Rules

### When NOT to Send Calendar Links
- Initial cold email (hurts deliverability, lands in spam)
- People have an "ego-tistical grudge against calendar links"
- Better: "Reply back to let me know some times that work for you"

### When Calendar Links Are OK
- After prospect says "yes" to a call
- In follow-up emails (not initial outreach)
- When prospect specifically asks for easy scheduling
- When working with an assistant

### Providing Available Times
- Be specific: "I have availability tomorrow from 9:10am EST to 7:10pm EST."
- Offer multiple days: "Tomorrow @ 7am EST or 5:30pm EST, Thursday @ 7-11am EST"
- Always include timezone
- Ask for confirmation: "Just let me know which of these times works best for you."

## Assuming the Close (Cold Email Wizard)

After building rapport through several emails:
- "Looking forward to connecting shortly and learning more about your business"
- Assumes they WILL book without saying it explicitly
- Only use after interaction, never in the initial email
