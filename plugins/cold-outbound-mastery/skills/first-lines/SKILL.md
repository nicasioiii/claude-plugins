# First Lines & Personalization Mastery

## Core Principle

**A first line should be an individually personalized compliment** - something genuine, specific, and deep that you would actually say to someone when meeting them for the first time.

First lines are the single most important factor in cold outreach response rates across ALL channels (email, LinkedIn, DMs, cold calls). They determine whether a prospect reads your full message or deletes it.

**Key Insight**: Custom first lines increase response rates by **10x** (1% generic → 10% custom).

## The 8 Core First Line Formulas

### Formula 1: Specific Accomplishment + Deep Thought
```
[Specific accomplishment/thing you noticed] - [deep/genuine observation about it].
```
**Example**: "congrats on being recognized by Clutch as a top SEO & marketing agency in Boston - heavy competition."

**When to use**: They have a major win, recognition, or accomplishment you can celebrate with deeper insight.

---

### Formula 2: Direct Work Mention + Specific Detail + Assessment
```
[Your specific work] [specific detail about it] - [genuine, deep compliment].
```
**Example**: "your Searching trailers you helped supervise are epic - that movie was ahead of its time."

**When to use**: You've seen their specific work product directly (portfolio piece, video, case study).

---

### Formula 3: Product/Collection + Multiple Observations
```
[the/your] [specific product/collection] [verb] [assessment]. [Specific detail about mix/approach] [observation about how well it works].
```
**Example**: "the new Kokoro collection you put out is stunning. The grimy New York/creative & bright Japanese mix go together really well."

**When to use**: They've launched a product or collection with a unique approach worth analyzing.

---

### Formula 4: Metric + Context + Real-World Impact
```
your [metric] for [specific client/context] [assessment] - [real-world implication].
```
**Example**: "your 15% boost in conversion rate for an online university means a lot of new students - online classes must've skyrocketed since COVID."

**When to use**: You have access to metrics showing their impact (case studies, client results).

---

### Formula 5: Testimonial/Evidence + Personal Comment
```
saw a [source] [saying something about them] - [personal, relatable observation].
```
**Example**: "saw a client raving about how you've helped them for a decade - that's a longer relationship than most couples I know :)"

**When to use**: You found third-party validation (client testimonial, review, endorsement).

---

### Formula 6: Specific Info from Website + Expert Question
```
your site says [specific detail] - [deep observation]. [Open-ended question]?
```
**Example**: "your site says you've hired for everything from Snapchat to the UFC - that versatility takes tons of experience. How does recruiting change depending on the market?"

**When to use**: Their website reveals something noteworthy about their experience or positioning.

---

### Formula 7: Published Work + Deep Analysis + Current Event
```
your [published work type] on [topic] [assessment] - [specific insight from piece], & [current event that validates it].
```
**Example**: "your Forbes feature on LeBron & halo hiring was on-point - getting that one top tier talent usually brings in more talent, & look how well that worked out with LeBron and the Lakers winning a ring!"

**When to use**: They've published thought leadership and recent events validate their insight.

---

### Formula 8: Content Review + General Truth
```
your [content type] on [topic] [assessment] - [observation about why this matters in their field].
```
**Example**: "your blog post on creating a story was super interesting - having a great career story separates you from the competition."

**When to use**: They've created content that reveals industry insight or philosophy.

## Writing Standards & Guidelines

### Punctuation Rules
- **Exclamation Points**: Max 1 per first line, use sparingly (only when it fits naturally)
- **Dashes & Ellipses**: Use dashes (—) or three dots (...) to connect ideas instead of commas
- **Contractions**: Use "you're" instead of "you are" for natural flow
- **Abbreviations**: Use "&" instead of "and", abbreviate company names naturally

### Length Standards
- **Maximum**: 2 sentences, period
- **Principle**: Concise beats long. Cut filler words: that, those, very, really, maybe, absolutely, definitely, the
- **Shorten Words**: "biz" instead of "business", "vid" instead of "video"

### Tone & Voice
- **Golden Rule**: Read it out loud 3x - would you say this when meeting them for the first time?
- **Sound**: Casual & professional, like a conversation between people in the same industry
- **Personality**: Fun, upbeat, genuine - but professional enough for CEOs

### Depth & Research Standards
- **Research Rule**: Make compliments about something DEEP: case studies, recent wins, podcast, interview, blog post, podcast appearances
- **Avoid Shallow**: DON'T compliment how long they've been in industry, college they went to, endorsement count on LinkedIn, website design alone
- **Evidence**: Must show you researched THIS SPECIFIC PERSON - if you can copy/paste to 5 others just changing the name, it's too generic

## What Makes a First Line "God-Level"

**Structural Qualities**:
✓ Uber-specific - mentions exact projects, products, details
✓ Doesn't start with "I"
✓ Starts with "Your" or specific detail about their work
✓ Very specific - mentions something the recipient worked on directly
✓ No redundant words, filler language, or unnecessary articles
✓ Uses dashes strategically to separate ideas and improve flow
✓ Right length - not too long, not too short

**Writing Quality**:
✓ Great spelling & grammar (use Grammarly before sending)
✓ Flows really well - natural conversation
✓ Conversational & professional balance
✓ No more than one filler word per line ("really", "very", "so", "super")
✓ Strategic punctuation (dashes, three dots, one exclamation point)

**Personalization & Depth**:
✓ Genuine, deep, & true - not surface-level
✓ Demonstrates deep industry knowledge
✓ Recognizes a big win & compliments without going over the top
✓ Includes a genuine observation or second thought
✓ Explains exactly what you're referring to so recipient understands
✓ Makes the recipient feel like someone actually researched them

**Why They Work**:
- Prove you actually researched the specific person (not mass-sent)
- So specific you couldn't copy/paste to someone else
- Show genuine interest in their work
- Stand out because they're not generic templates
- Acknowledge something real about their business/work

## Pre-Send Quality Checklist

Before sending ANY first line, go through this checklist:

- [ ] Read it out loud 2-3 times - does it sound natural?
- [ ] Make it MORE concise - cut any unnecessary words
- [ ] Fix spelling/spacing/capitalization errors
- [ ] Improve flow - does it connect ideas smoothly?
- [ ] Replace redundant words
- [ ] Check filler words - max 1 per line of "really/very/so/super"
- [ ] Verify it's fun, upbeat, & genuine
- [ ] Run through Grammarly - zero grammar errors
- [ ] Double-check no double spaces (looks unprofessional)
- [ ] Verify research - is this specific to THEIR work or generic?
- [ ] Can someone else use this first line? If yes, make it more specific
- [ ] Check punctuation - max 1 exclamation point, dashes used strategically
- [ ] Verify open-ended questions only (never yes/no questions)

## Research Methods for Personalization

### Where to Research
1. **Case Studies** - Their own case studies (on website or LinkedIn)
2. **Client Testimonials** - Real proof of results
3. **Podcast Appearances** - Deep insights into their thinking
4. **Interviews** - Published interviews where they discuss their work
5. **Blog Posts** - Their published thought leadership
6. **LinkedIn Posts** - Recent announcements, wins, insights
7. **Website** - Company accomplishments, client list, positioning
8. **YouTube Videos** - If they've created content
9. **Third-party Features** - Inc, Forbes, Clutch, G2 reviews

### What to Look For
- Specific clients they've worked with
- Quantifiable results (revenue increase, conversion improvements)
- Unique approach or methodology they've developed
- Recent wins or company milestones
- Speaking engagements or thought leadership
- Company culture or values they've publicly shared
- Industry recognition or awards
- Interesting hiring/team building strategies

See `references/research-methods.md` for complete research framework.

## Quality Scoring System

### God-Level First Line (9-10/10)
- Uber-specific to their exact work
- Includes deep observation or insight
- Flows naturally & conversationally
- Proves genuine research
- No grammatical errors
- Couldn't copy/paste to anyone else

### Great First Line (7-8/10)
- Specific to their work
- Includes genuine compliment
- Good flow and tone
- Obvious research was done
- Minor grammar/polish issues
- Could work for a few similar people but not many

### Mediocre First Line (5-6/10)
- Generic enough to work for multiple people
- Compliment without deep observation
- Decent flow but could improve
- Research evident but not deep
- Some grammar/punctuation issues
- Too short or too wordy

### Poor First Line (3-4/10)
- Could apply to many people (too generic)
- No genuine depth
- Robotic or unnatural language
- Spelling/grammar errors present
- Too many exclamation points
- Doesn't prove research

### Spam-Tier First Line (1-2/10)
- Generic template language
- Spelling/grammar disasters
- Unnatural or robotic
- Multiple exclamation points
- Clearly mass-sent
- Triggers spam filters

## Channel-Specific Guidance

### Email First Lines
- Standard application of all formulas
- Can be 1-2 sentences
- Must be above the body content
- Subject line coordination optional but powerful

### LinkedIn DM First Lines
- Same formulas apply
- Slightly more casual acceptable
- Leverage "creep factor" awareness (don't mention too-personal details)
- Can use more personality

### Cold Call Opening Lines
- Same principle: personalized compliment first
- Shorter & punchier (spoken language)
- "Hey [Name], I saw you [specific accomplishment] - that's impressive"
- Show research in first 10 seconds

### SMS/Text First Lines
- Extreme brevity required
- Single sentence maximum
- Still must show research
- "Saw your Clutch recognition for SEO in Boston - well deserved"

## Industry-Specific First Line Approaches

### B2B Services (Marketing, SEO, Design)
- Focus on case studies and client results
- Reference Clutch, G2, or industry recognition
- Mention specific metrics from their work

### SaaS/Software Companies
- Reference product features or UX approach
- Look for funding news or customer wins
- Comment on their product roadmap or strategy

### Consultants/Coaches
- Reference their published content (books, courses)
- Mention podcast appearances
- Comment on their unique methodology

### E-commerce/Founders
- Reference company growth or funding
- Mention product line or collection launches
- Comment on their business model or approach

## Key Takeaways

1. **Research is non-negotiable** - Spend 60% of time researching, 40% writing
2. **Specificity > Volume** - One god-level first line beats 10 generic ones
3. **Read out loud** - If it sounds unnatural, rewrite it
4. **Cut words ruthlessly** - Shorter & punchier always wins
5. **Deep compliments only** - Surface-level observations don't work
6. **One formula at a time** - Master one formula before mixing approaches
7. **Use dashes strategically** - They improve flow and readability
8. **Grammar matters** - One error loses trust
9. **Never ask yes/no questions** - Only open-ended questions
10. **This applies to ALL channels** - Same principle: research, compliment, insight

## Quick Start: 5-Minute First Line Process

1. **Research (2-3 minutes)**: Find something specific they accomplished
2. **Select Formula (30 seconds)**: Choose the best formula for this insight
3. **Write (1-1.5 minutes)**: Draft your line using the formula
4. **Edit (30 seconds)**: Read out loud, cut words, fix grammar
5. **Final Check (30 seconds)**: Verify specificity, depth, and tone
