# First Line Anti-Patterns: 20 Critical Mistakes to Avoid

Complete reference of every mistake, bad pattern, and thing to avoid when writing first lines.

---

## Pattern 1: Generic, Unspecific Compliments

### The Mistake
```
BAD: "you make amazing sites.... nice."
```

### Why It Fails
- Unspecific and could be sent to any web designer
- Could copy/paste this to 5+ other prospects just changing their name
- Doesn't prove you did ANY research
- "amazing" and "nice" are meaningless without expansion

### Red Flag
**If you can copy/paste your first line to 5 other people just changing their name, it's too generic.**

### How to Fix It
Be specific to THEIR exact work and what makes their sites unique.

```
BETTER: "the rebranding you did for the health tech startup is stunning - going from startup-y to enterprise-ready without losing the human touch is rare."
```

### What Changed
- Names THEIR specific type of work (rebranding for health tech startup)
- Specific detail about what makes it impressive (startup → enterprise while keeping human touch)
- Shows understanding of the tradeoff (hard to do both)

## Pattern 2: Excessive Exclamation Points

### The Mistake
```
BAD: "Love your website!! So cool! Can't wait to work with you!"
```

### Why It Fails
- 3+ exclamation points = desperate/unprofessional
- Looks like you can't control your enthusiasm
- May trigger spam filters
- Sounds like teenager texting, not professional

### The Rule
**Maximum 1 exclamation point per first line. Use sparingly (only when it fits naturally).**

### How to Fix It
Remove extras. Keep one only if you said something clever/funny.

```
BETTER: "the rebranding you did for the health tech company is stunning."
```

Or with one exclamation point if justified:
```
"your approach to founder mental health is refreshing - most founder content is all 'grind harder' and you're doing the real talk!"
```

## Pattern 3: Surface-Level Compliments Without Depth

### The Mistake
```
BAD: "Your PPC strategies are really cool. Case study on Better Beauty is awesome."
```

### Why It Fails
- "really cool" and "awesome" are meaningless without explanation
- No substance or insight
- Doesn't show you understand the work
- Could be said about any PPC agency

### How to Fix It
Provide a specific observation about WHY something is good. Add a deeper thought.

```
BETTER: "your Better Beauty case study shows 340% ROI improvement - that scale of optimization takes serious ad account structure work."
```

### What Changed
- Specific metric (340% ROI)
- Observation about what that requires (account structure expertise)
- Shows business understanding, not just fan enthusiasm

## Pattern 4: Robotic, Unnatural Language

### The Mistake
```
BAD: "your song 'Gettin' Jingle With It' has a nice mix of hip-hop and Christmas music that could grab people's attention on holiday promos."
```

### Why It Fails
- Super robotic and formal
- Uses quotes around song name (odd)
- Uninteresting and bland
- Doesn't sound like how humans actually talk
- Sounds like a template or AI-generated

### How to Fix It
Make it conversational, natural, less formal. Add personality and something clever.

```
BETTER: "huge fan of 'Gettin' Jingle With It' - putting hip-hop and Christmas together is such a fun mix, holiday playlists are sleeping on this one."
```

### What Changed
- Conversational opening ("huge fan of")
- Casual language ("fun mix", "sleeping on")
- Sounds like something a real person would say
- More personality and warmth

## Pattern 5: Spelling & Grammar Errors

### The Mistake
```
BAD: "your webinar on amazon ppc was realy great. i loved it. thats awesome."
```

### Why It Fails
- Misspellings ("realy" instead of "really")
- Lowercase "i" (grammar error)
- "thats" (missing apostrophe)
- Looks unprofessional and sloppy
- Like you don't care or aren't a native English speaker
- May trigger spam filters

### Red Flag
**Double spaces are unprofessional - use Grammarly before sending.**

### How to Fix It
Proofread. Use Grammarly. Check spelling, capitalization, and grammar.

```
BETTER: "your Amazon PPC webinar was valuable - most people miss how bid strategy changes during peak season."
```

### What Changed
- Proper spelling ("Amazon", "your")
- Proper grammar and capitalization
- Grammarly clean
- Sounds professional

## Pattern 6: Very Short & Shallow

### The Mistake
```
BAD: "your branding strategy is iconic."
```

### Why It Fails
- Too short (one statement, nothing else)
- "Iconic" is loaded but unexplained
- No deep thought or observation
- If you say something loaded like "iconic" you MUST explain why
- Comes across as not having thought about it deeply

### The Rule
**If you make a big claim, explain WHY you believe it.**

### How to Fix It
Add deeper thought and explain your compliment.

```
BETTER: "your branding strategy is iconic - most agencies play it safe, but you commit fully to a POV and make it work."
```

### What Changed
- Added deeper observation (most agencies play it safe)
- Explained WHY it's impressive (committing to a POV)
- Now it's a complete thought

## Pattern 7: Ungrammatical or Non-Native English

### The Mistake
```
BAD: "your 2K basketball gam just brought amazing tips and insight in UK."
```

### Why It Fails
- Misspelled ("gam" instead of "game")
- Doesn't make sense ("insight in UK")
- Obviously not written by native English speaker
- Unprofessional
- Lack of clarity

### How to Fix It
Rewrite entirely to flow naturally. Use contractions. Sound conversational.

```
BETTER: "huge fan of your 2K basketball tips - your strategy breakdowns explain things most YouTubers skip over."
```

### What Changed
- Natural English flow
- Makes sense grammatically
- Conversational tone
- Clear and professional

## Pattern 8: Mentioning Where You Found Them (Especially LinkedIn)

### The Mistake
```
BAD: "I found you on LinkedIn and I love what you're doing."
```

### Why It Fails
- Who cares that you found them on LinkedIn?
- Every cold email is "found" on LinkedIn
- Wastes valuable first line real estate
- Proves nothing about your research
- "I found you" = everyone does this

### How to Fix It
Skip the "I found you" or "I came across your" angle. Jump straight to what you found interesting.

```
BETTER: "your LinkedIn article on supply chain resilience was prescient - you warned about over-dependency on single suppliers, and look what happened when China shut down."
```

### What Changed
- Jumps straight to what you found interesting
- Doesn't waste space on HOW you found them
- Shows deeper research (not just LinkedIn stalking)
- Proves you actually consumed their content

## Pattern 9: Fake Genuineness / Inauthentic Claims

### The Mistake
```
BAD: "I was really looking forward to learning more about your services from you."
```

### Why It Fails
- You're not actually looking to learn - you're pitching
- They know this
- Inauthentic and transparent
- Doesn't feel genuine
- Wastes their time

### How to Fix It
Be authentic in your compliments and observations. Don't claim to be learning when you're actually pitching.

```
BETTER: "your 3-step onboarding process reduces time-to-value better than anyone I've seen - that matters because most new customers churn in the first 30 days."
```

### What Changed
- Authentic observation about their work
- Shows you understand the business impact
- Not pretending something you're not doing
- Direct and genuine

## Pattern 10: No Filler Word Control

### The Mistake
```
BAD: "I really like your site, it's very sleek and really professional looking."
```

### Why It Fails
- "really" appears twice
- "very" weakens the compliment
- Filler words make lines longer without adding value
- Sound less professional and confident
- Unprofessional repetition

### Filler Words to Avoid
Remove or limit to MAX 1 per first line: "really", "very", "so", "super", "absolutely", "definitely", "actually", "honestly"

### How to Fix It
Remove filler words. Be direct.

```
BETTER: "your site's design makes your process transparent in a way competitors don't - case studies showing before/after/results upfront is rare."
```

### What Changed
- No filler words
- Specific observation about design strategy
- More professional and confident
- Shorter while saying more

---

## Pattern 11: Excessive Parentheses or Odd Punctuation

### The Mistake
```
BAD: "loved your team's article on Amazon PPC ads (it DID answer a lot of my own questions!)"
```

### Why It Fails
- Parenthetical section adds nothing
- "DID" in caps is weird
- Parentheses section doesn't connect to context
- Breaks flow significantly
- Looks like you ran out of ideas

### How to Fix It
Remove parenthetical asides. Replace with deeper thought instead.

```
BETTER: "loved your team's Amazon Ads blog post - most people focus on keywords, but you emphasized budget allocation strategy which is where real savings come from."
```

### What Changed
- No weird parentheses
- Replaced side comment with deeper insight
- Better flow
- More professional

## Pattern 12: Repeating Prospect's Name Unnecessarily

### The Mistake
```
BAD: "Hey Sarah, not everyone has what it takes to be a true SEO master, Sarah."
```

### Why It Fails
- Results in awkward repetition: "Hey Sarah... Sarah"
- Sounds weird and unnatural
- Email already says "Hey Sarah" so repeating it is redundant
- Looks like a template error where name personalization failed
- Creepy (using name multiple times)

### How to Fix It
Remove name from first line entirely. Let the "Hey [Name]" in greeting be enough.

```
BETTER: "not everyone scales an agency to manage $500K/month in ad spend - that growth rate takes serious operational discipline."
```

### What Changed
- No name repetition
- More specific (mentions the metric)
- Sounds natural
- Professional

## Pattern 13: Asking Yes/No Questions

### The Mistake
```
BAD: "Is client communication something you're passionate about?"
```

### Why It Fails
- Obviously the answer is yes (wastes space)
- Doesn't engage them intellectually
- They can't say "no, I don't care about communication"
- Shows you're asking something you already know
- Weak engagement strategy

### The Rule
**Never ask yes/no questions. Only ask open-ended questions that require real thought.**

### How to Fix It
Ask open-ended questions that start with "how", "what", "why", or "when" - questions requiring real thought.

```
BETTER: "all your clients rave about your communication clarity - how do you train your team to explain complex work so simply?"
```

### What Changed
- Compliment before the question (builds credibility)
- Open-ended question ("how")
- Requires real thought to answer
- Engages them intellectually

## Pattern 14: Uncontrolled Smiley Faces

### The Mistake
```
BAD: "Love your work :) Really impressive :) Can't wait to connect :) Your stuff is amazing :)"
```

### Why It Fails
- Too many smiley faces (4 in one first line)
- Unprofessional
- Looks like you use a smiley face for every thought
- Overshoots the "friendly" goal into "unprofessional territory"

### The Rule
**Only use 1 smiley face per 5-7 emails maximum. Only when you've said something clever, fun, or genuinely friendly.**

### How to Fix It
Use smiley faces rarely and strategically.

```
BETTER: "saw a client raving about how you've helped them for a decade - that's a longer relationship than most couples I know :)"
```

### What Changed
- Only one smiley face (and it's warranted because the line is clever)
- Still friendly but professional
- Smiley face emphasizes the clever observation

## Pattern 15: Being Too Casual/Unprofessional

### The Mistake
```
BAD: "yo, your marketing is dope and super chill. Y'all are crushing it! Dollar dollar bills around these guys."
```

### Why It Fails
- "yo", "dope", "y'all", "dollar dollar bills" - too casual
- These are still CEOs of companies (need some professionalism)
- Loses credibility
- Sounds like you don't respect them
- Unprofessional balance

### The Rule
**Be casual but maintain professionalism. Remember: these are often C-suite executives.**

### Examples of Balance
- "dope" = on the cusp (risky, usually avoid)
- "crushing it" = borderline (acceptable only if very specific)
- "y'all" = too casual for professional context
- "yo" = too casual for CEO

### How to Fix It
Keep personality but maintain professional tone.

```
BETTER: "your marketing strategy balances data rigor with creative boldness - most agencies choose one, you do both."
```

### What Changed
- Casual ("boldness" instead of "aggressive")
- But still professional (explains your observation)
- Shows respect
- Credible tone

## Pattern 16: Treating Website Review Like It's Substantive

### The Mistake
```
BAD: "your website is so clean & easy on the eyes."
```

### Why It Fails
- Surface-level compliment
- Can be said about anyone's modern website
- Doesn't show research into their BUSINESS
- Shows you spent 10 seconds on their site
- Website design is explicitly forbidden as shallow

### How to Fix It
Find something more substantive (case study, interview, podcast, specific business aspect).

```
BETTER: "your case studies show a systematic process - the before/during/after approach makes it easy to see your actual impact."
```

### What Changed
- Focuses on BUSINESS (case study format)
- Shows you researched deeper than surface
- Specific observation (before/during/after structure)
- Proves you understand their work

## Pattern 17: Using Spam-Trigger Words

### The Mistake
```
BAD: "your amazing services are incredible and fantastic - I'm excited to partner with you!"
```

### Why It Fails
- "Amazing" triggers spam filters (Gmail auto-flags it sometimes)
- Multiple spam words together compound the problem
- Makes email look like marketing spam
- Low deliverability

### Spam Trigger Words to Avoid
- "amazing" (worst)
- "fantastic"
- "incredible"
- "wonderful"
- "wonderful opportunity"

### How to Fix It
Replace "amazing" with more specific adjectives.

```
BETTER: "your process for onboarding new clients is impressive - showing the ROI upfront from day one builds trust most agencies don't."
```

### What Changed
- Replaced "amazing" with specific observation
- Uses "impressive" instead of spam-trigger words
- More credible and specific
- Better deliverability

### The Mistake

BAD: "your online marketing optimization for the web is really good."

- "online marketing optimization for the web" is vague/redundant

- Sounds like you don't understand their space

### What Changed

## Pattern 19: Misaligned Industry Comments

### The Mistake
```
BAD: Email to a PPC agency, but: "your web design work is beautiful and minimalist."
```

### Why It Fails
- If emailing PPC company, commenting on web design is irrelevant
- Shows you didn't research what THEY specialize in
- They do PPC, not design (or they do both but you're complimenting the wrong thing)
- Comment on what they do in YOUR industry/service area, not tangential work

### The Rule
**If emailing PPC agency, comment on their PPC case studies and work. If emailing design company, comment on their design work.**

### How to Fix It
Focus on their PRIMARY service that matters to you.

```
BETTER: "your PPC case study for the e-commerce client shows systematic keyword expansion - most teams stall at 50 keywords, you're hitting 200+ profitably."
```

### What Changed
- Comments on their PPC work (primary service)
- Shows research into relevant area
- Specific observation
- Aligned with their actual business

## Pattern 20: Common Typos & Grammar Issues

### The Mistake
```
BAD: "the movie was ahead of it's time."
```

### Why It Fails
- "it's" = "it is" (contraction)
- "its" = possessive (what's correct here)
- Common mistake that looks unprofessional
- Grammar errors lose trust
- One typo suggests you don't care

### Common Typo Patterns to Watch
- "it's" vs "its" (its = possessive, it's = it is)
- "there" vs "their" vs "they're"
- "to" vs "too"
- Spacing issues (double spaces)
- Capitalization inconsistencies

### How to Fix It
Use Grammarly. Double-check.

```
CORRECT: "the movie was ahead of its time."
```

### Pro Tip
**Always run your first line through Grammarly before sending. Zero grammar errors = professional.**

## Bonus Pattern 21: Not Enough Research Investment

### The Mistake
```
BAD: "You seem like a great person and I'd love to work with you."
```

### Why It Fails
- Zero research evident
- Could be sent to anyone
- No specific details about them
- Wastes their time
- Shows you spent zero effort

### The Rule
**Spend 60% of time researching, 40% writing. Research is non-negotiable.**

### How to Fix It
Do the research. Find something specific.

Research checklist:
- [ ] Visited their website
- [ ] Read 1-2 case studies
- [ ] Listened to/read published content if available
- [ ] Found 1-2 specific accomplishments worth mentioning
- [ ] Understand their industry and position in it
- [ ] Could explain their business in one sentence

### Example of Good Research
```
"your case study on DTC brand launch shows you understand product/market fit - getting to 7 figures in first year usually means you nailed your positioning from day one."
```

Shows you:
- Read their case study (DTC brand launch)
- Understand the metric (7 figures, first year)
- Know the business implication (product/market fit, positioning)
- Can articulate WHY the result matters

## Bonus Pattern 22: Not Matching Channel to First Line Style

### The Mistake
```
EMAILS: "Your Forbes feature on remote work was insightful. How did you develop your perspective?"
LINKEDIN DM: "Your Forbes feature on remote work was insightful. How did you develop your perspective?"
```

### Why It Fails
- Email and LinkedIn DM have different social norms
- DM can be more casual
- Email needs more formality
- Not adjusting to channel shows lack of channel awareness

### Channel Adjustments
**Email**: Slightly more formal, professional

**LinkedIn DM**: Can be slightly more casual, more personality acceptable

**Cold Call**: Punchier, spoken language, shorter

**SMS**: Single sentence maximum

### How to Fix It
Adjust tone slightly per channel while keeping research depth.

## The Anti-Pattern Test

Before sending, ask:
- [ ] Could this be copied to someone else (just changing name)?
- [ ] Does it have spelling/grammar errors?
- [ ] Do I explain my compliments with deeper thought?
- [ ] Is the language natural and conversational?
- [ ] Did I avoid all 20 patterns above?
- [ ] Would I say this when meeting them in person?

If any answer is "no" or "unsure", rewrite it.
