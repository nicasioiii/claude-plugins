# First Line Examples Library

Complete reference of good, mediocre, and god-level examples with analysis.

---

## GOD-LEVEL EXAMPLES (9-10/10)

These first lines demonstrate all the principles of excellence: specificity, depth, natural tone, research proof, and emotional resonance.

### Example 1: Clutch Recognition
```
"congrats on being recognized by Clutch as a top SEO & marketing agency in Boston - heavy competition."
```
**Score**: 9.5/10
**Formula**: Formula 1 (Accomplishment + Deep Thought)
**What Makes It God-Level**:
- SPECIFIC: Names exact award (Clutch), exact category (top SEO & marketing), exact location (Boston)
- DEEP THOUGHT: "heavy competition" shows understanding that Boston is saturated, making the achievement more impressive
- CONVERSATIONAL: Would naturally say this when meeting
- UNPASTEABLE: This only works for this specific prospect
- SHORT: One sentence, crystal clear
- NO FILLER: Every word matters
- DASH USAGE: Dash perfectly separates the accomplishment from the deeper insight

### Example 2: Client Relationship Longevity
```
"saw a client raving about how you've helped them for a decade - that's a longer relationship than most couples I know :)"
```
**Score**: 9.2/10
**Formula**: Formula 5 (Testimonial + Personal Comment)
**What Makes It God-Level**:
- SOCIAL PROOF: Found actual client testimonial
- HUMOR: The "longer than couples" joke is relatable and genuine
- OBSERVATION: Shows you understand the rarity of 10-year relationships in B2B
- TONE: Light, authentic, fun without being unprofessional
- STRATEGIC EMOJI: One smiley face used appropriately (clever line warrants it)
- CONVERSATIONAL: Sounds like someone who respects loyalty

### Example 3: Movie Impact
```
"your Searching trailers you helped supervise are epic - that movie was ahead of its time."
```
**Score**: 9.3/10
**Formula**: Formula 2 (Direct Work Mention + Detail + Assessment)

- IMMEDIATE IMPACT: "epic" is strong but supported by the next sentence
- DEEPER INSIGHT: "ahead of its time" shows you understand the cultural moment
- CONVERSATIONAL: "epic" is how people actually talk about great work
- GROUNDED: Not over-the-top, just genuine appreciation
- CORRECT GRAMMAR: "its" not "it's" (common mistake avoided)

## GREAT FIRST LINES (7-8/10)

These lines are strong and effective but have minor room for improvement in specificity or depth.

### Example 1: General Award Recognition
```
"congrats on the Inc 5000 recognition - that's a meaningful accomplishment."
```
**Score**: 7.2/10
**Issues**:
- "meaningful accomplishment" is generic (could say about any award)
- Missing the specific insight about what it means
- Could be more specific about what they do

**Better Version**:
```
"congrats on the Inc 5000 recognition - growing 300% over 3 years in a saturated market is impressive."
```

### Example 2: Blog Post Mention
```
"your blog post on customer retention was really helpful - customer retention is everything for SaaS."
```
**Score**: 7.3/10
**Issues**:
- "really helpful" is vague filler
- Missing specific insight from the post
- Using "really" (filler word)

**Better Version**:
```
"your blog post on customer retention strategy broke down the retention math - most SaaS companies just chase new customers and ignore it."
```

### Example 3: Funding News
```
"saw you raised Series A funding! That's awesome - excited to see what you build next."
```
**Score**: 7.0/10
**Issues**:
- Generic excitement ("awesome", "excited")
- No specific insight about the funding
- Double exclamation point (too many)
- "excited to see what you build" is inauthentic (you don't actually know what they'll build)

**Better Version**:
```
"saw you raised a $5M Series A - that capital must enable serious experimentation with your product roadmap."
```

### Example 4: Testimonial Basic
```
"one of your clients left a review saying you're 'outstanding' - you clearly do great work."
```
**Score**: 7.1/10
**Issues**:
- Using their word ("outstanding") in quotes is awkward
- "clearly do great work" doesn't add value
- Too generic

**Better Version**:
```
"saw a client say you transformed their entire hiring process - that kind of impact usually means you've cracked something most miss."
```

### Example 5: Product Launch Simple
```
"your new product looks amazing - I think it's going to be successful."
```
**Score**: 6.9/10
**Issues**:
- "amazing" is overused and can trigger spam filters
- "I think it's going to be successful" is speculation, not observation
- No specific detail about WHY it's good

**Better Version**:
```
"your new product solves the exact problem your customers kept asking for - that kind of market-fit clarity doesn't happen by accident."
```

### Example 6: Service Quality
```
"your team seems to have really great quality standards - I'm impressed."
```
**Score**: 7.0/10
**Issues**:
- "seems to have" is weak (not definitive)
- "really great" is filler + weak descriptor
- "I'm impressed" is vague

**Better Version**:
```
"your case studies show 95%+ client satisfaction scores - that consistency means your quality bar is non-negotiable."
```

### Example 7: LinkedIn Recognition
```
"I noticed your LinkedIn post about remote work got a lot of engagement - you clearly know how to share valuable insights."
```
**Score**: 7.1/10
**Issues**:
- Starting with "I noticed" (should skip the "I")
- "clearly know how to share" is vague
- Engagement metric without depth

**Better Version**:
```
"your LinkedIn post on asynchronous communication timing nailed something most executives miss - that's why it resonated."
```

## MEDIOCRE FIRST LINES (5-6/10)

These lines aren't bad, but they're too generic and lack depth. They could be sent to multiple prospects.

### Example 1: Surface-Level Compliment
```
"your company is amazing and you're doing great things."
```
**Score**: 2/10
**Problems**:
- Generic template (works for anyone)
- No specificity whatsoever
- "amazing" and "great" are filler
- Doesn't prove any research
- Could be copy-pasted to 100 people

### Example 2: Vague Content Praise
```
"I really liked your recent blog post - it was really good and has lots of useful information."
```
**Score**: 5/10
**Problems**:
- Doesn't name the specific post
- "really good" twice in one sentence (repetition)
- "useful information" is vague
- No specific insight from the content
- Could be sent to any blogger

**Better**:
```
"your post on email subject line psychology was valuable - most people miss that the preview text gets read first."
```

### Example 3: Generic Service Compliment

## WHAT TO AVOID: Anti-Examples

### Anti-Example 1: Excessive Exclamation Points
```
"Your work is amazing!! I'm so impressed!! You've really done incredible things!! Can't wait to chat!!"
```
**Score**: 1/10
**Problems**:
- 4 exclamation points (rule: max 1)
- Multiple filler words ("amazing", "incredible")
- Screaming/desperate energy
- Unprofessional
- Will trigger spam filters ("amazing" and multiple !!!)

### Anti-Example 2: Spelling & Grammar Disasters
```
"your webinar on amazon ppc was realy great and i loved it. so intresting and helpfull."
```
**Score**: 1/10
**Problems**:
- "realy" (misspelling)
- Lowercase "i" (grammar)
- "intresting" and "helpfull" (misspellings)
- No capitalization of Amazon PPC
- Unprofessional
- Doesn't prove native English speaker

### Anti-Example 3: Fake Genuineness
```
"I was really looking forward to learn more about your amazing services and I'm so excited to see what you do. Your company is the best!"
```
**Score**: 2/10
**Problems**:
- "looking forward to learn more" is inauthentic (you're pitching, not learning)
- "amazing" (overused, spam trigger)
- "the best" is over-the-top
- Doesn't prove any research
- Obviously a template

### Anti-Example 4: Mentioning Where You Found Them
```
"I followed you on LinkedIn and I think your business looks really good. Your updates are always interesting."
```
**Score**: 2/10
**Problems**:
- "I followed you on LinkedIn" (who cares?)
- No specific reference to work
- "really good" is vague filler
- "always interesting" is generic
- Proves no research beyond LinkedIn

### Anti-Example 5: Yes/No Question
```
"Your client communication is really important to your clients, isn't it? I was wondering if client communication is something you're passionate about?"
```
**Score**: 2/10
**Problems**:
- Two yes/no questions (should only ask open-ended)
- Obviously the answer is yes (wastes space)
- "really important" is filler
- Doesn't engage, just wastes their time

### Anti-Example 6: Unnatural Language
```
"I would hereby like to inform you that your company's systematic approach to digital marketing infrastructure presents a comprehensive and integrated solution to the modern business landscape challenges."
```
**Score**: 1.5/10
**Problems**:
- Robotic corporate language (who talks like this?)
- Would never say this when meeting someone
- Overly formal and stuffy
- No human personality
- Sounds like a template

### Anti-Example 7: Name Repetition
```
"hey John, I saw your great work on the SEO case study, John. Not everyone can master SEO like you have, John."
```
**Score**: 1/10
**Problems**:
- Name repeated 3 times in first line (and email already says "hey John")
- Awkward and unnatural
- Comes across as trying too hard
- Looks like a template mistake

### Anti-Example 8: Surface-Level + Wrong Terminology
```
"your website design is amazing and your web development team is really skilled at the internet."
```
**Score**: 2/10
**Problems**:
- "website design" is surface-level (forbidden)
- "skilled at the internet" is not real terminology
- Shows lack of industry knowledge
- Amateur language
- No specificity

### Anti-Example 9: Too Many Parenthetical Asides
```
"loved your article on Amazon PPC ads (it DID answer a lot of my questions!) - Amazon ads (which are so important now!) are something (I think) most people overlook."
```
**Score**: 2/10
**Problems**:
- 3 sets of parentheses (excessive and distracting)
- Parentheses don't add value
- Breaks flow significantly
- "DID" in caps is weird
- One exclamation point in parentheses (unprofessional)

### Anti-Example 10: All Filler, No Substance
```
"So I was really thinking about how very awesome and really impressive your super cool work is, and I'm really excited about it."
```
**Score**: 1.5/10
**Problems**:
- "really" appears 3 times
- "awesome", "impressive", "cool" are all filler
- No specificity whatsoever
- No actual content
- Sounds like someone who has nothing real to say

## Reference: Quick Quality Check

**God-Level (9-10)**: You could only send this to this one person. It's that specific.

**Great (7-8)**: Specific enough they'd know you researched, but could work for 2-3 similar prospects.

**Mediocre (5-6)**: Generic enough to send to multiple people with minor changes.

**Poor (3-4)**: Template language that shows no research.

**Spam Tier (1-2)**: Errors, excessive punctuation, or clearly AI-generated.

---

## The Specificity Test

To check if your first line is god-level:

1. Read it out loud
2. Ask: "Could I send this exact line (just changing the name) to someone else in the same industry?"
3. If YES → Too generic, make it more specific
4. If NO → You're on the right track

Example:
- GENERIC: "your marketing content is really good" (could send to 100 marketers)
- SPECIFIC: "your LinkedIn article breaking down the 3C framework for agency positioning nailed it - positioning clarity is everything" (only for this person)
