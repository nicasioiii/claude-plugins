# First Line Formulas & Examples

## The 6 God-Level Formulas (Detailed)

### Formula 1: Specific Achievement + Understated Comment

**Structure:** [specific achievement/recognition] - [understated/cool comment]

**God-Level examples:**
- "congrats on being recognized by Clutch as a top SEO & marketing agency in Boston. Heavy competition."
- "your Forbes feature on LeBron & halo hiring was on-point - getting that one top tier talent usually brings in more talent, & look how well that worked out with LeBron and the Lakers winning a ring!"

**Why it works:** The understated second thought ("heavy competition") shows you understand the context, not just the headline. It proves you thought about what the achievement means, not just that it happened.

**Where to find material:** Awards pages, press releases, LinkedIn announcements, company news sections, Google News alerts.

### Formula 2: Specific Content + Deep Industry Insight

**Structure:** [reference specific content they created] - [demonstrate deep knowledge of their industry]

**God-Level examples:**
- "the all-access training camp episodes this year are sooo well edited - nice work. Hopefully with Donovan & your talent maturing you can make noise this year."
- "your podcast episode on creator economy monetization was sharp - the shift from CPM to community-based revenue changes everything for mid-tier creators."

**Why it works:** Referencing specific content they made is personal. Adding an industry insight proves you consumed it and thought about it -- not just skimmed the title.

**Where to find material:** YouTube channels, podcasts, company blogs, social media video content, conference talks.

### Formula 3: Case Study Stat + Real-World Connection

**Structure:** your [specific stat] for [specific client] means [real-world implication] - [connect to trend/event]

**God-Level examples:**
- "your 15% boost in conversion rate for an online university means a lot of new students - online classes must've skyrocketed since COVID."
- "helping a SaaS cut churn by 30% in 6 months is no joke - retention is everything when CAC keeps climbing."

**Why it works:** You take their specific result and explain what it means in the real world. This shows business understanding, not just data reading.

**Where to find material:** Case studies page, portfolio, testimonials section, LinkedIn posts about client wins.

### Formula 4: Specific Reference + Humor/Cleverness

**Structure:** [specific thing you noticed] - [funny or clever observation]

**God-Level examples:**
- "saw a client raving about how you've helped them for a decade - that's a longer relationship than most couples I know :)"
- "your team page shows 4 dogs in the office - at that ratio you're basically running a rescue with a side hustle."

**Why it works:** Humor disarms. A clever observation signals intelligence and personality. It makes you memorable.

**Warning:** Stay casual but professional. "Dollar dollar bills around" = too corny. The humor should make a colleague smile, not cringe.

**Where to find material:** About pages, team pages, testimonials, social media stories, quirky company culture details.

### Formula 5: Specific Info + Genuine Question

**Structure:** [something specific from their site/profile] - [genuine question you want answered]

**God-Level examples:**
- "your site says you've hired for everything from Snapchat to the UFC - that versatility takes tons of experience. How does recruiting change depending on the market?"
- "saw you scaled from 3 to 40 employees in 18 months - curious how you kept culture intact at that speed?"

**Why it works:** A genuine question invites a reply. It shows you are interested in THEM, not just pitching. Questions that touch on expertise or hard-won knowledge are especially effective.

**Where to find material:** About page, LinkedIn experience section, interviews, company milestones.

### Formula 6: Specific Blog/Podcast + Genuine Deeper Thought

**Structure:** your [type] on [topic] was [adjective] - [genuine additional thought/observation]

**God-Level examples:**
- "your blog post on creating a career story was super interesting - having a great story separates you from the comp."
- "your LinkedIn post on sales comp plans was spot-on - the misalignment between AE and SDR incentives kills pipeline quality."

**Why it works:** You consumed their content and added your own thought. This creates the feeling of a peer conversation, not a sales pitch.

**Where to find material:** Blog posts, LinkedIn articles, Medium posts, guest columns, Twitter threads.

## Quality Spectrum: Concrete Examples

### BAD First Lines (Grade: 1-3/10)

| Example | Problem |
|---------|---------|
| "You make amazing sites!" | Unspecific + "amazing" is a spam trigger + could be sent to anyone |
| "I love your company and what you do!" | Generic flattery, zero specificity |
| "I had to reach out because I love your work!" | "I had to reach out" is a dead pattern + no specificity |
| "I see you've been in marketing for 15 years!!" | Shallow observation + double exclamation = desperate |
| "Your LinkedIn has so many endorsements - impressive!" | Meaningless metric, zero insight |
| "I noticed you are the CEO of a great company" | Could be sent to literally every CEO on earth |

### MEDIOCRE First Lines (Grade: 4-6/10)

| Example | Problem | Fix |
|---------|---------|-----|
| "Great post on inbound marketing" | Missing WHERE (LinkedIn? Blog?) and missing second thought | "great LinkedIn post on inbound marketing - the stat about organic vs paid ROI was eye-opening" |
| "Your recent article on AI in recruiting was excellent" | "Article" is dated (use "blog post"), "excellent" without explanation is shallow | "your blog post on AI in recruiting raised a solid point - the bias problem isn't going away with bigger models" |
| "John, I saw your company grew by 8.8x" | Says the name (redundant after "Hey John"), filler "by" before the number | "growing 8.8x in 2 years is wild - that kind of scale usually breaks ops before it breaks product" |
| "I really loved your software for LinkedIn scaling" | Too wordy, "really loved" is generic | "your LinkedIn scaling software is clever - automating the boring parts so reps focus on conversations" |

### GOD-LEVEL First Lines (Grade: 7-10/10)

| Example | Why It Works |
|---------|-------------|
| "congrats on being recognized by Clutch as a top SEO & marketing agency in Boston. Heavy competition." | Specific award + location + understated second thought |
| "the all-access training camp episodes this year are sooo well edited - nice work. Hopefully with Donovan & your talent maturing you can make noise this year." | Specific content + deep industry knowledge + casual tone |
| "your 15% boost in conversion rate for an online university means a lot of new students - online classes must've skyrocketed since COVID." | Case study stat + real-world meaning + timely connection |
| "saw a client raving about how you've helped them for a decade - that's a longer relationship than most couples I know :)" | Specific testimonial + genuinely funny + appropriate emoji use |
| "your site says you've hired for everything from Snapchat to the UFC - that versatility takes tons of experience. How does recruiting change depending on the market?" | Specific info + source cited + genuine question |

## Fix Patterns (Common Corrections)

### Pattern: Remove Filler Words

| Before | After | Rule |
|--------|-------|------|
| "by 8.8x" | "8.8x" | "by" before numbers is filler (it is implied) |
| "plus" after numbers | Remove | Reader mentally adds "plus" |
| "software for LinkedIn scaling" | "LinkedIn scaling software" | Reorder for concision |
| "that is a really great" | "that's great" | Cut "really" and "is a" |

### Pattern: Be Specific About Source

| Before | After |
|--------|-------|
| "great post on inbound marketing" | "great LinkedIn post on email marketing" |
| "saw your article about growth" | "your blog post on product-led growth was sharp" |
| "loved your content about sales" | "your podcast episode on cold outreach stood out" |

Always name the platform: blog post, LinkedIn post, podcast, Twitter thread, YouTube video, Forbes feature.

### Pattern: Remove Self-References

| Before | After |
|--------|-------|
| "my wifi is so spotty, I need to check them out" | Remove entirely -- cold email is about the PROSPECT |
| "I've been following your work and I think" | "your work on [specific thing] is [observation]" |
| "I was impressed when I saw" | "the [specific thing] was impressive" |

### Pattern: Professionalism Threshold

| Too Casual | Just Right |
|-----------|------------|
| "dollar dollar bills around" | "the revenue numbers you posted are wild" |
| "lol that's sick bro" | "that's a clever approach :)" |
| "OMG your company is blowing up!!" | "growing that fast in this market is no joke" |

### Pattern: Native English Flow

| Non-Native Sound | Natural Sound |
|-----------------|---------------|
| "your former" | "you played pro ball" or "back when you were at [company]" |
| "article" | "blog post" (more modern) |
| "website" | "site" (more conversational) |
| "you are" | "you're" (always use contractions) |
| "it is" | "it's" |
| "I am" | "I'm" |

## Alex Berman's 9 Ways to Write Custom First Lines

1. Reference a specific case study from their website
2. Mention a recent award or recognition
3. Comment on a podcast episode they appeared on
4. Reference a blog post they wrote
5. Mention a conference talk or webinar
6. Note a specific company milestone (funding, expansion)
7. Reference a specific product or feature launch
8. Comment on their company culture (from team page, social)
9. Mention a mutual connection or shared experience

**Impact data:** Custom first lines deliver a 10x boost on reply rates compared to no personalization.

## Cold Email Wizard First Line Patterns (from 60+ Real Emails)

Patterns observed across 60+ successful email conversations:

- Reference specific achievements: "Can't believe you started your first profit-turning business at 18"
- Reference company milestones: "Read that Defiance Ventures invested in [Company]"
- Reference product quality: "Very impressed by the customer reviews on the [Company] website"
- Reference publications/press: "It's remarkable the different publications you've been featured on"
- Reference company mission: "Really admire [Company] for their commitment to quality service"

**Key insight:** Even mediocre first lines can work when paired with strong qualifying questions. The first line earns the right to ask -- the question does the selling.

## Anti-Patterns Across All Sources

1. Never use "amazing" in any email (Gmail spam trigger)
2. Maximum 1 exclamation point per first line
3. Never spend more than 5 minutes on a single first line (diminishing returns)
4. Never make the first line about yourself ("I noticed" -> "your")
5. Never use shallow compliments ("love your website" is meaningless)
6. Never use more than 2 sentences
7. Never start with "I" -- start with "your," "congrats," "the," or the specific reference
8. Never include filler like "I had to reach out" or "I came across your profile"
