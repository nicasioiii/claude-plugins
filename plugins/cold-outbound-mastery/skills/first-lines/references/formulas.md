# First Line Formulas Library

Complete reference of every first line formula with structure, principles, and examples.

---

## Formula 1: Specific Accomplishment + Deep Thought

### Structure
```
[Specific accomplishment/thing you noticed] - [deep/genuine observation about it].
```

### Components
- **Component 1**: Name a specific accomplishment or win they've achieved
- **Component 2**: Add a dash (or three dots)
- **Component 3**: Include a genuine, deep observation about the accomplishment

### Principles
- The dash creates a natural pause and emphasizes the second thought
- The deep thought proves you understand their industry and impact
- This formula works best when you have a specific, recent win to reference

### Examples
- "congrats on being recognized by Clutch as a top SEO & marketing agency in Boston - heavy competition."
- "saw your $50M Series B funding announcement - that capital enables some serious scaling."
- "your case study on the DTC brand hitting 7-figure revenue in year one - getting distribution right from day one is everything."
- "impressed by how you scaled from 10 to 50 employees in 2 years - that's growth most companies dream about."

### When to Use
- They have a major milestone (funding, award, recognition)
- You found a case study showing impressive results
- They've achieved industry recognition or certification
- Recent win is mentioned on their website or LinkedIn

### Pro Tips
- Make the accomplishment VERY specific (numbers, exact outcome)
- The deep thought should show industry understanding, not obvious praise
- Use "&" instead of "and" to keep it concise
- Don't start with "congratulations" if it sounds forced

## Formula 2: Direct Work Mention + Specific Detail + Assessment

### Structure
```
[Your specific work] [specific detail about it] - [genuine, deep compliment].
```

### Components
- **Component 1**: Reference their specific work product directly
- **Component 2**: Add a concrete detail about that work (design choice, approach, outcome)
- **Component 3**: Give a genuine compliment that shows understanding

### Principles
- This shows you've actually consumed their work (not just read about it)
- The specific detail proves you didn't just skim
- The compliment must flow naturally after the detail

### Examples
- "your Searching trailers you helped supervise are epic - that movie was ahead of its time."
- "the animations you created for kids with cancer a few years back were powerful - you made a big difference."
- "your podcast episode on founder burnout hit different - most people don't talk about this stuff."
- "the case study on your site about the fitness app launch is excellent - the user acquisition strategy is something most miss."

### When to Use
- You've actually watched, read, or reviewed their work
- Their portfolio shows specific pieces you can reference
- They've published content (blog, video, podcast)
- Case studies on their website are detailed and impressive

### Pro Tips
- Be very specific about WHICH work (which video, episode, case study)
- Mention one specific detail that shows you actually reviewed it
- Compliment the craft or strategy, not just generic "nice job"
- This formula has the highest credibility because they know you did real research

## Formula 3: Product/Collection + Multiple Observations

### Structure
```
[the/your] [specific product/collection] [verb] [assessment]. [Specific detail about approach] [observation about effectiveness].
```

### Components
- **Component 1**: Name the specific product or collection
- **Component 2**: Assess it (is stunning, is brilliant, works beautifully, etc.)
- **Component 3**: Point out a specific detail about the mix or approach
- **Component 4**: Observe how well this approach works

### Principles
- This formula is best for product/design-focused people
- It shows you understand their strategic choices
- The second sentence explains WHY the approach works

### Examples
- "the new Kokoro collection you put out is stunning. The grimy New York/creative & bright Japanese mix go together really well."
- "your product suite solves for both small businesses and enterprises - that's a smart design that keeps everyone happy."
- "the rebranding you did last year is beautiful. Going from corporate to modern without losing credibility is tough to pull off."
- "your mobile app interface is intuitive. The one-tap checkout flow removes friction most competitors still have."

### When to Use
- They've launched a new product or refreshed an existing one
- Their design approach is innovative or distinctive
- They serve multiple customer segments with one product
- Their strategy balances competing needs (simplicity + power, etc.)

### Pro Tips
- Spend time understanding WHAT makes their approach unique
- Explain WHY their choices work, not just that they look good
- Reference the product specifically by name if possible
- This formula works great for founders, designers, and product leaders

## Formula 4: Metric + Context + Real-World Impact

### Structure
```
your [metric] for [specific client/context] [assessment] - [real-world implication].
```

### Components
- **Component 1**: Reference a specific metric they achieved
- **Component 2**: Name the client or context
- **Component 3**: Assess the achievement
- **Component 4**: Explain the real-world impact of that result

### Principles
- Numbers are specific and hard to argue with
- This shows you understand the business impact, not just the tactic
- The real-world implication connects the metric to their prospect's business

### Examples
- "your 15% boost in conversion rate for an online university means a lot of new students - online classes must've skyrocketed since COVID."
- "your client's 40% improvement in customer retention over 6 months - that's revenue stability most companies are chasing."
- "managing over $100K/month in ad spend across Facebook & Google is no joke - that decision-making must be intense."
- "your 8x increase in qualified leads for the B2B SaaS client - that's the kind of volume that scales sales."

### When to Use
- You have access to specific metrics (case study, website, LinkedIn)
- The metric is impressive enough to mention
- You understand what the metric means for their business
- The result is recent (not old data)

### Pro Tips
- Use specific numbers, not ranges ("15%" not "around 15%")
- Make sure the context is clear (which client, which time period)
- The "real-world implication" sentence shows business acumen
- This formula is perfect for service providers and consultants

## Formula 5: Testimonial/Evidence + Personal Comment

### Structure
```
saw a [source] [saying something about them] - [personal, relatable observation].
```

### Components
- **Component 1**: Name where you found the testimonial (LinkedIn review, case study, testimonial page)
- **Component 2**: Reference what someone said about them
- **Component 3**: Add a personal, relatable observation that validates the testimonial

### Principles
- Third-party validation is more credible than self-praise
- Your personal comment should be relatable and genuine
- This formula shows others trust them (social proof)

### Examples
- "saw a client raving about how you've helped them for a decade - that's a longer relationship than most couples I know :)"
- "noticed on a G2 review that someone called you the 'gold standard' for onboarding - consistency like that doesn't happen by accident."
- "read a testimonial from one of your clients about the transformation your work created - that level of impact is rare."
- "saw your team got a 4.9/5 star rating on Trustpilot - that's the kind of consistency that means something."

### When to Use
- You found genuine testimonials or reviews
- A client has publicly praised their work
- Industry platforms (Clutch, G2, Trustpilot) show strong ratings
- They have a case study that includes client testimonials

### Pro Tips
- Be specific about WHERE you found the testimonial
- Your personal comment should show you understand the impact
- A light, genuine tone works better than "wow, you're amazing"
- This formula is great for service providers and agencies

## Formula 6: Specific Info from Website + Expert Question

### Structure
```
your site says [specific detail] - [deep observation]. [Open-ended question]?
```

### Components
- **Component 1**: Reference a specific detail from their website
- **Component 2**: Show you understand what that means
- **Component 3**: Ask an open-ended question that shows interest in their expertise

### Principles
- This proves you visited their actual website (not LinkedIn)
- The observation shows you understand the business implication
- The question invites them to share their thinking (engagement)
- Open-ended questions work better than yes/no questions

### Examples
- "your site says you've hired for everything from Snapchat to the UFC - that versatility takes tons of experience. How does recruiting change depending on the market?"
- "I saw you specialize in both B2B and B2C - managing two different sales motions is complex. What's the biggest difference in how you approach them?"
- "your About page mentions you focus on 'businesses doing $5M-$50M in revenue' - why that specific range? Is there a reason you don't go smaller or larger?"
- "you mention you've worked with 200+ agencies as clients - how do you stay differentiated when everyone has similar problems?"

### When to Use
- Their website reveals something interesting about their positioning
- You want to engage them with a genuine question
- Their experience is broad or specialized in a noteworthy way
- You're genuinely curious about their approach

### Pro Tips
- Ask questions you actually want the answer to (not rhetorical)
- Open-ended questions start with "how", "what", "why" (not yes/no)
- The observation sentence should connect their experience to what it takes
- This formula invites dialogue, not just acceptance

## Formula 7: Published Work + Deep Analysis + Current Event

### Structure
```
your [published work type] on [topic] [assessment] - [specific insight from piece], & [current event that validates it].
```

### Components
- **Component 1**: Reference their published work (Forbes article, LinkedIn post, interview)
- **Component 2**: Assess it (was great, hit on, nailed it, etc.)
- **Component 3**: Mention a specific insight from the piece
- **Component 4**: Connect to a recent event that validates that insight

### Principles
- This shows deep reading and thinking
- Connecting to current events shows business awareness
- Validating their insight with reality proves its value
- This is highest for thought leaders and published authors

### Examples
- "your Forbes feature on LeBron & halo hiring was on-point - getting that one top tier talent usually brings in more talent, & look how well that worked out with LeBron and the Lakers winning a ring!"
- "your LinkedIn post on supply chain resilience was prescient - you warned about over-dependency on single suppliers, & look what happened when China shut down."
- "your article on 'the future of remote work' predicted this shift perfectly - you nailed how it would change company culture."
- "your podcast episode about founders hiring their first salesperson was valuable - you emphasized culture fit over resume pedigree, and that's exactly what I see winning."

### When to Use
- They've published thought leadership (article, LinkedIn post, interview)
- You can connect their insight to a recent business event
- Their prediction or observation has been validated by reality
- They're a recognized expert in their field

### Pro Tips
- Be very specific about the insight (not just "it was good")
- Current event validation must be recent and relevant
- This formula is excellent for positioning as someone who reads and thinks
- Works best for consultants, coaches, and industry experts

## Formula 8: Content Review + General Truth

### Structure
```
your [content type] on [topic] [assessment] - [observation about why this matters in their field].
```

### Components
- **Component 1**: Reference specific content they created
- **Component 2**: Assess it (was insightful, nailed it, breaks it down, etc.)
- **Component 3**: Add an observation about WHY this matters in their industry

### Principles
- This works when you don't have a current event to tie to
- The second part shows business acumen and industry knowledge
- It's a "what" and "so what" formula

### Examples
- "your blog post on creating a story was super interesting - having a great career story separates you from the competition."
- "your YouTube video on email subject line psychology was valuable - most people overlook this because they focus on body copy."
- "your LinkedIn article on hiring remote contractors was eye-opening - international teams are becoming standard, not exception."
- "your guide to customer retention strategies was comprehensive - most companies focus on acquisition and forget keeping the ones they have costs less."

### When to Use
- They've created helpful educational content
- The content reflects their expertise
- You want to compliment their thinking without needing a specific validation event
- Their perspective on a common problem is valuable

### Pro Tips
- Pick content that shows their unique perspective
- The "why it matters" sentence should show industry understanding
- This is less impressive than Formula 7 (no current event tie-in) but still effective
- Perfect for educational creators and consultants

## Formula 9: Problem Observation + Their Solution

### Structure (Alex Berman's "9 Ways")
```
[Problem you noticed in their space] - [how they solve it differently/better].
```

### Components
- **Component 1**: Observe a common problem in their industry
- **Component 2**: Show how they've solved it in a unique way
- **Component 3**: Compliment the approach

### Principles
- This shows you understand their market and competition
- You're positioning them as the solution, not just one vendor
- It's about insight into their business model

### Examples
- "most agencies promise speed but deliver chaos - your documented processes actually make it work."
- "most consultants talk about strategy but can't execute - you actually have a team that builds what you recommend."
- "retention is the biggest problem in SaaS - your onboarding product solves what everyone complains about."
- "founders usually choose between fast growth and profitability - your model shows you can have both."

### When to Use
- You understand the core problems in their industry
- They have a unique solution or approach
- You want to position them as different from competitors
- Their business model or process is innovative

### Pro Tips
- The problem should be real and acknowledged by the industry
- Your observation of their solution must be specific (not generic)
- This formula positions you as someone who understands their space
- Great for B2B companies and service providers

## Formula 10: Contrarian Observation + Their Evidence

### Structure (Advanced)
```
[Contrarian belief most don't get] - [how they prove/model it].
```

### Components
- **Component 1**: State a belief that goes against the grain
- **Component 2**: Show how they embody or prove this belief
- **Component 3**: Compliment their commitment to it

### Principles
- This shows you think differently
- You're not just praising them, you're validating their worldview
- It's about philosophy and approach, not just output

### Examples
- "most people think you need a huge team to do excellent work - your 5-person team's output proves that's wrong."
- "conventional wisdom says bootstrapped companies can't compete with VCs - your growth numbers prove otherwise."
- "everyone says you need fancy tech to deliver great service - your low-tech, high-touch approach is working better than the automation vendors."
- "most think organic growth is dead - your company's engagement proves audiences care about authenticity."

### When to Use
- They have a clear philosophy or worldview
- Their results prove their approach right
- You respect their contrarian stance
- You want to connect on values/beliefs, not just tactics

### Pro Tips
- The contrarian belief should be something real (not strawman)
- Prove it through their actual results, not just your opinion
- This is more sophisticated and higher-risk (can backfire if wrong)
- Best for founders, leaders, and thought leaders

## Alex Berman's "9 Ways to Write Custom First Lines"

These are alternative angles to approach personalization:

### Way 1: Compliment Their Results
Focus on metrics, outcomes, and business impact.
```
"your agency manages $X/month in ad spend across Y clients - that's serious scale."
```

### Way 2: Compliment Their Content
Reference their blog posts, videos, podcasts, interviews.
```
"your blog post on [specific topic] was the best breakdown I've seen - you explained [specific insight]."
```

### Way 3: Compliment Their Approach
Comment on their methodology, philosophy, or business model.
```
"most [industry] do [common thing], but you [unique approach] - that's better."
```

### Way 4: Compliment Their Growth
Reference company milestones: funding, hiring, expansion, new products.
```
"saw you raised a Series A - that capital must enable some interesting experiments."
```

### Way 5: Compliment Their Specialization
Observe they've chosen a specific niche or market.
```
"you focus exclusively on [niche] - most consultants try to serve everyone, but you go deep."
```

### Way 6: Compliment Their Innovation
Notice something they do that's different or cutting-edge.
```
"you're one of the few [type of company] who [unique thing] - most still [old way]."
```

### Way 7: Compliment Third-Party Validation
Reference reviews, testimonials, industry recognition, awards.
```
"saw you got [award] from [reputable source] - [brief observation about what that means]."
```

### Way 8: Compliment Their Values/Culture
Notice their public commitments to values, causes, or people.
```
"your commitment to [specific value] shows in how you [specific action] - that's rare."
```

### Way 9: Compliment Their Thinking
Notice their unique perspective or hot take on their industry.
```
"your take on [topic] is contrarian - most think [conventional wisdom], but you're saying [their view] and proving it."
```

## Formula Selection Guide

**Use Formula 1** (Accomplishment + Deep Thought) when:
- They have a recent major win
- You found impressive metrics or recognition
- You want to celebrate them

**Use Formula 2** (Work Mention + Detail + Assessment) when:
- You've actually reviewed their work
- They have a strong portfolio
- You want maximum credibility

**Use Formula 3** (Product + Observations) when:
- They're a designer, founder, or product person
- Their product launch is recent
- You understand their strategic choices

**Use Formula 4** (Metric + Context + Impact) when:
- You have access to specific numbers
- The metric tells a business story
- You understand the industry impact

**Use Formula 5** (Testimonial + Comment) when:
- You found genuine social proof
- They have strong reviews or testimonials
- Third-party validation matters

**Use Formula 6** (Website + Question) when:
- You want to engage them with a question
- Their positioning is interesting
- You're genuinely curious about their approach

**Use Formula 7** (Published Work + Analysis + Current Event) when:
- They've published thought leadership
- You can tie it to a recent event
- You want to position as a thinking peer

**Use Formula 8** (Content + Why It Matters) when:
- They've created educational content
- You want to compliment their thinking
- You understand the industry implication

**Use Formula 9** (Problem + Solution) when:
- You understand their market well
- They solve a common problem uniquely
- You want to position them as different

**Use Formula 10** (Contrarian + Evidence) when:
- They embody an interesting philosophy
- You respect their worldview
- You want to connect on values

## Combining Formulas

You can combine formulas for more power:

**Formula 2 + 5**: Work mention + testimonial validation
```
"your case study on the DTC brand hitting 7-figure revenue is impressive - even better that your client raved about the process taking only 6 months."
```

**Formula 1 + 8**: Accomplishment + content insight
```
"congrats on the Clutch recognition - your blog post on scaling agencies explains why that matters, most miss that the real work is in the people."
```

**Formula 7 + 9**: Published work + problem observation
```
"your Forbes piece on remote work predictions was right - you pointed out that most companies hire for presence, but you've built a model around impact."
```

The key: Don't overcomplicate. One strong formula beats multiple weak ones.
