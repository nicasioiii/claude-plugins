# Research Methods for Personalization

Complete framework for researching prospects to find the deep, specific information needed for god-level first lines.

---

## Core Research Principle

**Spend 60% of time researching, 40% of time writing.**

The difference between a mediocre first line and a god-level one is research depth. You can't write a specific compliment without doing the research first.

---

## The Research Hierarchy

### Tier 1: Easy Access (Start Here)
- Their company website
- LinkedIn profile
- Recent LinkedIn posts

### Tier 2: Medium Access (Go Here If Tier 1 Isn't Enough)
- Case studies
- Blog posts
- Company press releases
- Their own published content

### Tier 3: Deep Access (Go Here for God-Level)
- Podcast appearances
- Interview transcripts
- Industry publications featuring them
- Video content
- Webinars or speaking engagements
- Third-party reviews (Clutch, G2, etc.)

### Tier 4: Context (Understanding Their Industry)

- Competitor analysis

- Recent news in their space

## Where to Research: Complete List

### 1. Company Website
**What to look for**:
- About page (company story, mission, values)
- Case studies (specific client results, metrics)
- Testimonials/reviews page
- Blog posts (thought leadership, company perspective)
- Leadership bios
- Client list or "As Seen In" logos
- Press/news section
- Specific accomplishments or recognition

**Time investment**: 10-15 minutes

**Example insights**:
- "Your About page mentions you've worked with brands from Snapchat to UFC"
- "Your case study shows a 340% ROI improvement"
- "Your site lists Clutch recognition as a top agency in Boston"

### 2. LinkedIn Profile
**What to look for**:
- Current role and company
- Past roles (career trajectory)
- Endorsements (what they're known for)
- Recommendations (what clients say)
- Recent posts (what they're thinking about)
- Speaking engagements
- Publications
- "Featured" section (links to their work)

**Time investment**: 5-10 minutes

**Example insights**:
- "Saw you were promoted to VP of Growth at [Company] - that's scaled responsibility"
- "Your recent LinkedIn post on founder burnout has 2000+ comments - people connect with your honesty"
- "Your featured section links to a podcast appearance where you discuss supply chain"

**Pro Tip**: Look at their recent posts more than their old ones. Recent activity shows current thinking.

### 3. Personal/Company LinkedIn Posts
**What to look for**:
- Their own posts (thoughts, takes, insights)
- Comments they're making (what they're engaged in)
- Shares with their commentary
- Company announcements (funding, hiring, milestones)
- Engagement metrics (if public)

**Time investment**: 5-10 minutes

**Example insights**:
- "Your post on 'why most founders fail' nailed the psychological piece - most articles focus on mechanics"
- "Noticed your company announced $5M Series B funding"
- "You've been commenting on supply chain articles - that's clearly a focus area"

### 4. Case Studies
**What to look for**:
- Specific client results (metrics, percentages)
- Client industry (what types they work with)
- Challenge → Solution → Results structure
- Specific tactics or methodology
- Client testimonial in the case study
- Time frame of the project

**Time investment**: 10-15 minutes per case study

**Example insights**:
- "Your case study for an e-commerce client shows 40% improvement in customer retention"
- "You work with both B2B and B2C companies - managing two sales motions is complex"
- "Your case studies emphasize your process transparency"

**Pro Tip**: Look at 2-3 case studies to see if there's a pattern in their approach or type of clients.

### 5. Blog Posts
**What to look for**:
- Topics they cover (what matters to them)
- Depth of insight (surface-level vs. deep)
- Unique perspective (is this different from competitors?)
- Data/research they reference
- Call to action (what do they want readers to do?)
- Recent posts vs. old posts

**Time investment**: 15-20 minutes to read 1-2 posts

**Example insights**:
- "Your blog post on 'hiring your first salesperson' emphasizes culture fit over resume pedigree - that's a specific take"
- "Your 5-part series on e-commerce strategy breaks down something most articles oversimplify"
- "You haven't posted in 6 months - your focus has shifted from content to ops"

### 6. Podcast Appearances
**What to look for**:
- Topics they discuss
- Their perspective on industry issues
- Stories they tell (reveals values)
- Problems they see in their space
- Future predictions or vision
- How they speak (tone, language, personality)

**Time investment**: 30-60 minutes to listen/skim transcript

**Example insights**:
- "In your podcast appearance, you talked about founder burnout being taboo - that's a brave, honest take"
- "You mentioned supply chain resilience as the #1 challenge facing manufacturers right now"
- "You shared how you grew from 5 to 50 employees in 2 years and what that taught you"

**Pro Tip**: Podcasts are goldmines for personality and deep perspectives. Skim transcripts if full listen is too long.

### 7. Interviews & Featured Articles
**What to look for**:
- Media outlets featuring them (Inc, Forbes, TechCrunch, etc.)
- What the article focuses on (their story, their business, their take)
- Quotes and specific insights
- Recognition value (being featured in reputable publications)
- Angle of the feature (why they're newsworthy)

**Time investment**: 10-15 minutes to read

**Example insights**:
- "You were featured in Forbes for 'halo hiring strategy' - getting one top tier talent brings in more talent"
- "Inc magazine featured your story about bootstrapping to $10M without taking VC"
- "TechCrunch covered your product launch - that validation from a major publication is significant"

### 8. YouTube/Video Content
**What to look for**:
- Webinars (what are they teaching?)
- Demo videos (product strategy)
- Speaking engagement videos
- YouTube channel content (if applicable)
- Production quality (shows investment in content)
- View counts and engagement

**Time investment**: 15-30 minutes

**Example insights**:
- "Your YouTube tutorial on Amazon PPC setup has 50K views - people are learning from you"
- "Your speaking engagement video at [Conference] shows your thought leadership"
- "The production quality of your webinars is professional - you invest in content"

### 9. Third-Party Reviews & Validation
**What to look for**:
- Clutch (agency/service provider directory)
- G2 (software reviews)
- Trustpilot (customer reviews)
- Industry awards (Webby Awards, Shorty Awards, etc.)
- Recognition in industry rankings
- LinkedIn endorsements for specific skills

**Time investment**: 5-10 minutes

**Example insights**:
- "You have 4.8/5 stars on Clutch with 50+ reviews - that consistency is earned"
- "G2 named you 'Leader' in your category"
- "You won 'Best Startup of the Year' from [reputable source]"

### 10. Company Press Releases & News
**What to look for**:
- Major milestones (funding, hiring, expansions)
- New product launches
- Partnership announcements
- Awards and recognition
- Company culture initiatives
- Leadership changes

**Time investment**: 10 minutes

**Example insights**:
- "Your company announced $5M Series A - that capital timeline is interesting"
- "You hired a new VP of Sales - that's strategic positioning for next phase"
- "You opened an office in Singapore - expansion into new markets shows confidence"

## Research Process: Step-by-Step

### For a Cold Email First Line (30 minutes total)

**Step 1: Website Scan (5 minutes)**
- Go to their homepage
- Skim About page
- Look at case studies or featured work

**Step 2: LinkedIn Profile Review (5 minutes)**
- Look at current role and company
- Skim recent posts (last 3-6 months)
- Check for speaking engagements or featured section

**Step 3: Deep Dive: Choose Your Best Angle (15 minutes)**
- Read 1 detailed case study, OR
- Watch/listen to recent podcast or speaking engagement, OR
- Read 2-3 recent blog posts or LinkedIn articles, OR
- Read one recent interview or feature

**Step 4: Identify Your First Line Angle (5 minutes)**
- What was most impressive or interesting?
- Which formula fits best?
- What's the deepest insight you can make?
- Write your first line

### For a High-Value Prospect (60 minutes total)

**Step 1: Website Deep Dive (10 minutes)**
- Read About page entirely
- Review 2-3 case studies in detail
- Look at testimonials
- Check press/news section

**Step 2: LinkedIn Deep Dive (10 minutes)**
- Full profile review
- Last 6 months of posts
- Comments and engagement
- Featured section

**Step 3: Content Deep Dive (20 minutes)**
- Read 2-3 recent blog posts, OR
- Skim podcast transcript or listen to episode, OR
- Read recent interview or feature, OR
- Combination of above

**Step 4: Industry Context (10 minutes)**
- What's happening in their space right now?
- Who are they competing against?
- What trends are affecting them?
- Recent news in their industry?

**Step 5: Angle Selection & Writing (10 minutes)**
- Synthesize: What's most impressive?
- Connect to: What's happening in their industry?
- Formula selection: Which formula best fits?
- Write your god-level first line

## Research Red Flags: What NOT to Use

### Don't Compliment...
- ❌ How long they've been in the industry (shallow)
- ❌ College they went to (surface-level)
- ❌ LinkedIn endorsement count (surface)
- ❌ Website design alone (expressly forbidden)
- ❌ Generic "you work hard" observations
- ❌ Obvious things they should be proud of ("you have employees!")
- ❌ Their speaking ability (unless they're a professional speaker)
- ❌ Generic professional attributes ("you're professional!")

### Don't Research...
- ❌ Personal details not publicly shared (family, home life, etc.)
- ❌ Negative information (bankruptcies, lawsuits - unless relevant)
- ❌ Health issues or personal struggles
- ❌ Anything you'd need to stalk for (too creepy)

## Finding the DEEP Insight

The difference between a good first line and a god-level first line is finding the DEEP insight.

### Good Insight
```
"Your agency is recognized by Clutch"
```
Shows you found: A certification/award

### Deep Insight
```
"You're recognized by Clutch as a top SEO agency in Boston - that market is saturated"
```
Shows you found: The certification AND why it matters (market saturation)

### God-Level Insight
```
"Being recognized by Clutch as a top SEO agency in Boston means navigating one of the most competitive agency markets in the country"
```
Shows you: Understand the competitive context AND the rarity of achieving it

### How to Find Deep Insights
1. **Read what they did** (case study, blog post, project)
2. **Ask: "Why does this matter?"** (business implication)
3. **Ask: "What does this take?"** (skills, discipline, resources required)
4. **Ask: "How is this different from competitors?"** (differentiation)
5. **Synthesize into one observation** (your deep insight)

## Research Examples by Industry

### B2B Service Providers (Marketing, SEO, Design Agencies)

**Where to Research**:
- Case studies (specific client results)
- Clutch or G2 reviews (social proof)
- Blog posts (methodology and take)
- Industry recognition (awards, publications)

**What to Look For**:
- Specific metrics from case studies (% improvement, ROI)
- Niche specialization (do they focus on a specific type of client?)
- Unique methodology or process
- Third-party validation
- Client retention or case study consistency

**Example First Line**:
```
"Your case studies show you consistently deliver 40%+ conversion improvements for e-commerce clients - that consistency usually means you've built repeatable process."
```

### SaaS / Software Companies

**Where to Research**:
- Product pages (feature set, positioning)
- Recent funding news (growth trajectory)
- Case studies or customer success stories
- LinkedIn posts (product updates, company milestones)
- Product Hunt or similar (if applicable)

**What to Look For**:
- Product differentiation (what makes it unique?)
- Recent feature launches
- Customer growth or retention metrics
- Founding story (why they built this)
- Competitive positioning

**Example First Line**:
```
"Your product handles both enterprise workflows and SMB simplicity - most tools choose one, that's hard to execute."
```

### Consultants / Coaches / Solo Practitioners

**Where to Research**:
- Published content (courses, books, articles)
- Podcast appearances
- Speaking engagements
- LinkedIn posts and audience engagement
- Case studies or client results
- Their unique philosophy or methodology

**What to Look For**:
- Unique perspective on their field
- Specialized niche or ideal client
- Proven methodology or framework
- Audience they've built
- Recognition in their space
- Contrarian beliefs they hold

**Example First Line**:
```
"Your podcast episode on founder psychology nailed the tension most don't discuss - scaling forces you to change, but change feels like losing your identity."
```

### E-Commerce / Founders / Product Companies

**Where to Research**:
- Company growth metrics
- Press coverage and features
- LinkedIn company page and posts
- Product launches and iterations
- Funding news and investor profiles
- Customer testimonials or reviews
- Founder's personal brand/LinkedIn

**What to Look For**:
- Revenue growth or scaling metrics
- Product innovation or unique approach
- Customer acquisition strategy
- Team growth trajectory
- Business model creativity
- Market expansion

**Example First Line**:

```
"Your DTC brand hit $1M in first year revenue - that speed usually means you nailed your positioning, audience, and acquisition from day one."
```

### Corporate / Large Company Decision Makers

**Where to Research**:
- Company website (strategy, vision)
- LinkedIn profile (role, background)
- LinkedIn company page (recent news, strategic announcements)
- Press releases and news coverage
- Industry publications coverage
- Speaking engagements

**What to Look For**:
- Strategic initiatives they're leading

## The Quality Check: Can You Explain It?

Once you've done your research, ask yourself:

**Can you explain their business in one sentence without sounding generic?**

If YES: You've done enough research
If NO: Keep researching

Good explanations (specific):
- "They help e-commerce brands improve customer retention through behavior-based email"
- "They're a boutique PPC agency focused on high-ticket B2B companies"
- "She coaches bootstrapped SaaS founders on unit economics and scaling"

Bad explanations (too generic):
- "They help companies with marketing"
- "They do consulting work"
- "They're a digital agency"

## Time Management: How Long Should Research Take?

**Cold Email Campaign** (low-value research):
- 3-5 minutes per prospect
- Focus: Website + 1 case study OR LinkedIn posts

**Prospect Follows Up** (medium-value):
- 10-15 minutes per prospect
- Focus: Website deep dive + one piece of content

**High-Value Enterprise Deal** (high-value):
- 30-60 minutes per prospect
- Focus: Comprehensive research + industry context
- Justifies: More personalization effort for high-ticket deal

**Rule**: Don't spend more than 60 minutes on research for ANY first line. Diminishing returns kick in.

---

## The Research Shortcut: If You're Short on Time

**5-Minute Research Only**:
1. Visit their LinkedIn profile
2. Read their last 3 posts
3. Skim their company website About page
4. That's it

You can still write a good first line with just this, just aim for Tier 1 formulas (simpler compliments based on posts or basic company info).

**Result**: 7/10 quality first line (good but not god-level)

For god-level, you need the 15-30 minute deep dive.

---

## The Ultimate Research Question

Before writing your first line, ask yourself:

**"Could someone else write this same first line without doing the research I just did?"**

If YES → You haven't done enough research
If NO → You're ready to write your god-level first line

This is your quality check. If your first line is so specific they KNOW you researched them, you've done it right.
