# Research Process for First Lines

## Research Order for Any Prospect

### Quick Research Protocol (3-5 Minutes Per Lead)

**Step 1: Google (30 seconds)**
- Search "[name] [company]"
- Scan first page results for press, interviews, awards, case studies
- If rich results: stop here, you have material

**Step 2: LinkedIn Profile (60 seconds)**
- Check recent posts (opinions, thoughts, industry commentary)
- Check About section (career story, what they care about)
- Check Experience section (career path, transitions, achievements)
- Check Activity tab (what they have been engaging with)

**Step 3: Twitter/X (30 seconds)**
- Check recent tweets for opinions, hot takes, industry thoughts
- Retweets and likes reveal what they find interesting

**Step 4: Podcasts/Interviews (60 seconds if found)**
- Google "[name] podcast" or "[name] interview"
- Rich personality and opinion material
- Often includes specific stories and insights not found elsewhere

**Step 5: Company Website (60 seconds)**
- Case studies page (stats, named clients, measurable results)
- Blog (their authored posts)
- About page (founder story, mission, culture details)
- Team page (personal details, bios, photos)

**Step 6: Other Social Media (30 seconds)**
- YouTube, Instagram, TikTok channels
- Conference talks or webinar recordings

**Step 7: Awards and Press (30 seconds)**
- Google "[company] award" or "[company] featured"
- Industry association recognition, Top 10 lists, press coverage

### When You Cannot Find Anything on the Individual

This happens with lower-profile people (managers, directors at larger companies):

1. Research their COMPANY instead, then make a comment related to their role
2. Example: Music marketing director at Nike -> comment on the music choice in a recent Nike campaign
3. Look for team-level wins or department initiatives
4. Check if they contributed to a company blog post or case study

### Time Budget

| Lead Tier | Time Per Lead | Research Depth |
|-----------|--------------|----------------|
| A Tier (problem NOW) | 4-5 minutes | Full research protocol, god-level line |
| B Tier (problem) | 3-4 minutes | Steps 1-3, good line |
| C Tier (ICP match) | 2-3 minutes | Steps 1-2, acceptable line |
| Volume/outsourced | 3-5 minutes (freelancer) | Steps 1-2, 7/10 minimum quality |

## Trigger Hierarchy (30MPC)

### Hard Triggers (Highest Priority -- Timing-Based)

These indicate the prospect has the problem RIGHT NOW:
- Recent funding round (need to deploy capital, scale operations)
- New executive hire (new leader = new initiatives = new budgets)
- Product launch (need marketing, sales, operational support)
- Earnings call mention of a specific initiative
- Strategic initiative announced (press release, blog post)
- Job posting for a role your product replaces or supports

### Soft Triggers (Medium Priority -- Situational)

These indicate the prospect likely has the problem:
- Industry trend affecting their segment
- Pervasive problem in their company size/stage
- Technology stack indicates a gap you fill
- Competitor just made a move they need to respond to

### Activity-Based Triggers (Lower Priority but Warm)

These indicate the prospect is showing interest:
- Opened your email 5+ times
- Visited your website (RB2B data)
- Engaged with your LinkedIn content
- Downloaded a resource or attended a webinar
- Replied with an objection (still engaged)

### The First-Best Principle

For each ICP segment, rank your top 5 triggers. When researching a prospect:
1. Check for Trigger 1 first
2. If found, write the first line around it. Stop searching.
3. If not found, check Trigger 2, then 3, and so on
4. Once you find ANY good trigger, write and move on

Do not over-research. The marginal value of the 6th minute of research is near zero.

## The Simplest Research Formula (30MPC)

Two questions that cut through everything:

1. **Why did someone buy last time? What situation were they in?**
2. **What is public proof that this prospect is in that situation?**

Example for Pave (compensation software):
- Why did they buy? They were doing merit cycles in spreadsheets and it was a nightmare.
- Public proof? They just posted a Head of Compensation job opening.

That is your first line: "saw you're hiring a Head of Comp - merit season in spreadsheets with 17,000 VLookups must be getting old."

## AI Research Workflow (30MPC)

### The Prompt-Dig-Summarize Framework

**Step 1: Prompt**
Feed AI a company URL or prospect's LinkedIn and ask:
- "What are the top 3 challenges this company is likely facing based on their recent activity?"
- "What recent events, hires, or announcements signal they might need [your solution]?"

**Step 2: Dig**
Take the AI's output and verify. Check if the triggers it identified are:
- Actually recent (not 2 years old)
- Actually relevant (not tangential)
- Actually specific (not generic industry stuff)

**Step 3: Summarize**
Write the first line based on verified findings. The AI assists research speed but the final line should sound human -- not AI-generated.

### AI Enrichment Tools

- **Clay:** Automatically gathers prospect data at scale (school, B2B/B2C classification, pricing model, recent achievements, job changes)
- **ChatGPT/Claude:** Feed company info, ask for trigger analysis
- **LinkedIn Sales Insights:** Company growth data, hiring trends

### Danger: AI-Generated First Lines

AI can research quickly but writes generic-sounding lines. Always:
1. Use AI for research (gathering facts and triggers)
2. Write the actual first line yourself (or have a trained human write it)
3. Run the Read-Aloud Test -- if it sounds like AI wrote it, rewrite it

## VOC Mining Techniques (Bree Weber)

Voice of Customer (VOC) research enriches first lines and email copy with the prospect's own language.

### 12 VOC Sources

1. **Social media posts** -- Their own content reveals priorities and challenges
2. **Email campaigns** -- Subscribe to their list to see their messaging
3. **Google alerts** -- Set up for each high-priority prospect
4. **Podcasts** -- Both their own and guest appearances
5. **Webinars** they host or speak at
6. **Blog posts** they author
7. **Groups** they participate in (LinkedIn, Facebook, Slack)
8. **Facebook Lives** and video content
9. **Press releases** and media coverage
10. **Interviews** in publications or on other people's platforms
11. **Their website** -- About page, features, blog
12. **Conversations their audience has** about them on social media

### Using VOC in First Lines

The most powerful first lines use the prospect's own language:
- If they describe their goal as "aggressive growth," use "aggressive growth" (not "fast scaling")
- If they call their audience "digital-first customers," use exactly that phrase
- If they describe a pain as "buried under spreadsheets," reference spreadsheets

### Reverse Engineering Problems (Bree Weber)

When researching a prospect, ask:
1. How is their goal similar to other clients' goals I have worked on?
2. How does my solution achieve that goal faster, easier, or better?
3. How can I help them avoid pitfalls along the way?
4. What were the "before state" complaints of similar past clients?

These answers inform both first lines and full pitch content.

## Platform-Specific Research Tips

### LinkedIn Research

- Recent posts reveal current priorities (last 2-4 weeks of activity)
- "Featured" section often contains their best work
- Recommendations received reveal what others value about them
- Activity tab shows what content they engage with (reveals interests)

### Twitter/X Research

- Pinned tweet = what they want you to see first
- Hot takes and opinions = personality and values
- Quote tweets = what they agree/disagree with
- Threads = deep expertise topics

### Podcast Research

- Guest appearances: look for quotes, stories, specific opinions
- Their own podcast: reveals what topics they care about most
- Episode descriptions often summarize key points (time-saver)

### Company Website Research

- Case studies: the single best source for specific, complimentable data
- Blog: thought leadership, recent initiatives, strategic direction
- About page: founder story, mission, values, team culture
- Careers page: what roles they are hiring for (signals priorities and gaps)
