# ICP & Qualification Frameworks

## The Golden Goose Framework (Berman)

### Revenue Sweet Spot: $5M-$150M

| Company Size | Characteristic | Cold Outbound Fit |
|-------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Under $1M | Bootstrapping, no budget | Poor -- cannot afford services |
| $1M-$5M | Growing, but tight budgets | Fair -- selective spending |
| **$5M-$150M** | **Decision speed of small biz + enterprise budgets** | **Ideal -- the Golden Goose** |
| $150M-$500M | More bureaucracy, procurement processes | Moderate -- longer sales cycles |
| $500M+ | Enterprise -- layers of gatekeepers, legal review | Difficult -- needs enterprise sales motion |

### Why $5M-$150M Works for Cold Outbound

- Fast enough to decide (often one conversation with CEO = deal)
- Large enough to have real budget ($8K-$12K/month retainers are feasible)
- Small enough that the CEO still reads their own email
- In companies this size, people sit near each other -- a warm introduction from the CEO cascades

### Filtering by Revenue in Lead Databases

- **UpLead:** Revenue filter built into search
- **Apollo:** Annual revenue filter + employee count proxy
- **Sales Navigator:** Employee count as proxy (no revenue filter directly)

**Employee count to revenue proxies:**
- 10-50 employees: typically $1M-$10M revenue
- 50-200 employees: typically $5M-$50M revenue
- 200-500 employees: typically $20M-$150M revenue

These vary dramatically by industry. SaaS companies may have $50M revenue with 100 employees. Manufacturing companies may have $50M revenue with 500 employees. Always cross-reference.

## Bree Weber's 4-Question Qualification Framework

### Question 1: Who Do You WANT to Work With?

This is about work style, not demographics:
- Do you prefer 1:1 collaboration or team environments?
- Do you work best with founders, marketing directors, or enterprise teams?
- What personality types energize you vs. drain you?
- Remote-first or in-person meetings expected?

**Why this matters:** Bad-fit clients produce bad case studies, bad referrals, and burnout -- even if they pay well.

### Question 2: Who Has the Types of Problems You Can Solve?

Map your skills to actual business problems:
- What specific pain points does your work address?
- What does "before" look like for companies that need you?
- What symptoms can you spot from the outside? (website gaps, missing features, underperforming metrics)
- What triggers indicate they need help NOW vs. eventually?

### Question 3: Who Has the Capital to Invest?

Budget qualification before outreach saves everyone's time:
- What is your minimum engagement value?
- What company size typically has that budget?
- Are there funding signals (recent raise, revenue growth)?
- What indicators suggest they are investing in solutions like yours?

### Question 4: Who Has the Authority to Decide?

The decision maker is not always who you think:
- For startups: Usually the CEO or co-founder
- For mid-market: Usually VP or Director level in the relevant department
- For enterprise: Could be Director, VP, or even Manager level depending on deal size

**Finding the right person:**
- When torn between multiple decision makers, choose the one who is most VISIBLE in the company (more research material for personalization)
- For co-founders: pick the one on the operations/marketing side with a more public persona
- Check who is signing off on similar initiatives at competitor companies

## Decision Maker Targeting Strategy

### The Top-Down Approach (Berman)

1. **Always email the CEO first** (in Golden Goose companies)
2. If no response after 2 weeks: email the next level down (VP, Director)
3. Continue descending one title every 2 weeks
4. Better to get passed DOWN from above than try to navigate from within

**For large companies (Coca-Cola level):**
- Email the CEO of the relevant DIVISION (e.g., CEO of Vitamin Water), not the parent org CEO
- Look for division heads, brand managers, or product leads

### The Multi-Stakeholder Approach (30MPC)

Tier your contacts within each company:
- **Above the line:** VP, SVP, C-Level (strategic conversations)
- **At the line:** Director level (owns the initiative)
- **Below the line:** Manager level (uses the product daily)

**Contact rules:**
- Be bold calling high -- old-school execs are best reached by cold call since their inbox is guarded
- Do not go too low -- an analyst might take a meeting but rarely influences purchases
- Never email two people from the same company simultaneously
- Wait 2 weeks between contacts at the same company

### Finding Email Addresses (Bree Weber)

| Source | Best For | Notes |
|--------|----------|-------|
| LinkedIn profiles | Direct email from About section | After connecting |
| Website contact pages | General company contact | Less personalized |
| Mailing lists / lead magnets | Primary personal email | Subscribe to get it |
| Social media About pages | Facebook especially for SMBs | Often listed publicly |
| Press releases | PR coordinator (often marketing-adjacent) | Good secondary contact |
| hunter.io | Finding emails by domain | Bree's favorite -- transparent data policy |

## Account Tiering (30MPC)

### Building Your Trigger List

For each ICP, identify your top 5 buying triggers that indicate a prospect has your problem:

**Example triggers for Pave (compensation software):**
1. Upcoming compensation review cycle (Problem NOW)
2. Fundraised within the last 3 months (Problem NOW)
3. Hiring a Head of Compensation (Problem NOW)
4. Growing over 10% per year (Problem)
5. References "generous equity compensation" in job openings (Problem)

### Tiering Logic

| Tier | Definition | Signal Type | Extra Touches |
|------|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| A | Problem NOW (timing-based triggers) | Hard triggers | +2 extra dials, priority outreach |
| B | Problem (situational match) | Soft triggers | +1 extra dial, standard outreach |
| C | Anyone else in ICP | No specific trigger | Standard cadence only |

### Practical Implementation

1. Pull a qualified list from your lead database
2. For each account, check against your trigger list
3. Assign A/B/C tier
4. Sort your daily outreach: A tier first, then B, then C
5. Allocate more creative personalization to A tier (first lines, custom research)

## Company Size Targeting by Channel

Different channels work better for different company sizes:

| Channel | Best Company Size | Why |
|---------|-------------------|-----|
| Cold email | $5M-$150M (Golden Goose) | CEOs read their own email |
| Cold calling | $50M+ | Larger companies have receptionists/gatekeepers; calling breaks through |
| LinkedIn DMs | $1M-$50M | Founders and small-team leaders are active on LinkedIn |
| Instagram DMs | Under $5M | Solopreneurs, coaches, consultants live on Instagram |
| Facebook DMs | Under $5M | Small business owners active in Facebook groups |

### Volume Guidelines by Channel

| Channel | Daily Volume | Monthly List Size Needed |
|---------|-------------|--------------------------|
| Cold email | 30-40/day per domain | 600-800/month per domain |
| Cold calling | 50-200 dials/day | 200-800/month |
| LinkedIn DMs | 40 connection requests/day | 800/month |
| Instagram DMs | 100 new/day + 50-100 follow-ups | 2,000+/month |
| Facebook DMs | 20-50 new/day + 100 follow-ups | 400-1,000/month |
