# Trigger Research & AI Workflows

## THE RESEARCH TRIGGER HIERARCHY

### Tier 1: Hard Triggers (Timing-Based) -- HIGHEST PRIORITY

Hard triggers signal the prospect has a problem RIGHT NOW. These convert at the highest rate because urgency is built in.

| Hard Trigger | Where to Find It | Why It Works |
|---|---|---|
| Recent funding (within 3 months) | Crunchbase, press releases, LinkedIn announcements | Capital = new projects, hiring, infrastructure spend |
| New executive hire in buyer's department | LinkedIn job changes, company announcements | New leaders bring new initiatives and new budgets |
| Product launch or expansion | Company blog, press releases, Product Hunt | Expansion creates operational gaps and scaling pains |
| Earnings call mention of relevant initiative | Seeking Alpha transcripts, investor presentations | Public commitment to a priority = budget allocated |
| Strategic initiative announcement | Press releases, annual reports, conference presentations | Stated priorities mean active problem-solving |
| Job posting for a role your product replaces or supports | LinkedIn Jobs, Indeed, company careers page | If they are hiring for it, they have the problem |
| Competitor acquisition or departure | Industry news, LinkedIn | Vendor changes create windows of opportunity |

### Tier 2: Soft Triggers (Situational)

Soft triggers indicate the prospect likely has the problem but may not feel urgency yet.

| Soft Trigger | Where to Find It | Why It Works |
|---|---|---|
| Industry trends affecting their segment | Industry reports, trade publications, analyst briefings | Everyone in the segment faces it; the question is timing |
| Pervasive problems at their company stage | Size/stage-based pattern matching from your customer base | Startups post-Series B have different pains than pre-seed |
| Company growing >10% year-over-year | LinkedIn company data, news, job posting volume | Growth creates operational strain |
| Regulatory changes in their industry | Government announcements, compliance publications | Forced action creates urgency whether they like it or not |
| Competitor moves in their space | Industry news, press releases | Competitive pressure motivates action |

### Tier 3: Activity-Based Triggers

These signal interest or attention -- use to prioritize within existing pipeline.

| Activity Trigger | What It Means | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Opened your email 5+ times | Interested but hesitant; considering your message | Call them immediately; reference the email |
| Visited your pricing page | Evaluating cost; near decision | Priority outreach within 24 hours |
| Engaged with your LinkedIn content | Aware of you; warm | Use the post they engaged with as conversation opener |
| Replied with an objection | Still engaged; the objection is not rejection | Handle the objection -- they gave you an opening |
| Viewed your LinkedIn profile | Demonstrated curiosity about you personally | Reach out ASAP (highest intent short of inbound) |

## THE FIRST-BEST PRINCIPLE

**Rule: Rank your top 5 triggers for each major problem. Run the list top to bottom. Once you find one, write the outreach. Stop searching.**

The first good trigger is the best trigger because:
1. Speed to outreach matters more than perfect personalization
2. Diminishing returns on additional research past the first strong signal
3. The prospect does not know what you did NOT find
4. Perfectionism in research is procrastination in disguise

### Example Trigger Ranking (Pave -- Compensation Software)

| Rank | Trigger | Category |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Upcoming compensation review (Q1/Q3 cycle) | Problem NOW |
| 2 | Fundraised within the last 3 months | Problem NOW |
| 3 | Hiring a compensation person (job posting) | Problem NOW |
| 4 | Growing over 10% per year (headcount expansion) | Problem |
| 5 | References "generous equity compensation" in job openings | Problem |

If you find trigger #1, stop. Write the email. Do not keep looking for triggers #2-5.

### The Simplest Research Formula
1. Why did someone buy last time? What situation were they in?
2. What is public proof that they are in that situation?

## RESEARCH SOURCES BY CHANNEL

### For Cold Email
| Source | What to Extract | Time |
|---|---|---|
| Company website (careers, blog, about) | Strategic priorities, product launches, team structure | 3-5 min |
| LinkedIn (prospect profile) | Role tenure, recent posts, career moves | 2-3 min |
| Press releases / news | Funding, partnerships, milestones | 2-3 min |
| Job postings | What they are hiring for (reveals gaps) | 1-2 min |
| Earnings calls (enterprise only) | CEO/CFO stated priorities and problems | 10-15 min |

### For Cold Calls
| Source | What to Extract | How to Use |
|---|---|---|
| Competitor intelligence | Which competitor they likely use | Trap questions in objection handling |
| LinkedIn content | Context for tailored permission opener | "I saw your press release about..." |
| Job postings | Trigger for problem proposition | "Most companies posting for X role are dealing with..." |
| Industry trends | Pervasive problem for untailored opener | "Not sure if you're running into this, but..." |

### For LinkedIn DMs
| Source | What to Extract | How to Use |
|---|---|---|
| LinkedIn posts and activity | Opinions, interests, achievements | Restrained compliment + authority-building observation |
| Company page | Service offering, client types | Rephrase their offer as a question to pry pain |
| Mutual connections | Shared network | Social proof through association |
| Sales Navigator triggers | Profile views, job changes, content engagement | Three DM triggers (connect, view, engage) |

### Three LinkedIn DM Triggers (Ty Frankel)
1. **People who connect with you** -- check followers for quality connections
2. **Profile viewers** -- highest demonstrated interest short of inbound; reach out ASAP
3. **Content engagement** (likes and comments) -- warmest outbound prospects

## VOC MINING (Bree Weber's 12 Sources)

Voice of Customer is the actual language your prospect uses -- not your interpretation, their literal words and phrases.

### Where to Mine VOC

| Source | What to Extract | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Social media posts | Goals, frustrations, celebrations | "Aggressive growth goals" -- their exact phrase |
| Facebook Lives / videos | Unscripted pain points, personality | "Blizzard brain" -- how they describe being overwhelmed |
| Blog posts they write | Thought leadership, priorities, challenges | Strategic direction in their own words |
| Podcast episodes (hosted or guested) | Deep views on problems, aspirations, philosophy | Most unfiltered research source |
| Interviews (press, webinars) | Public commitments, strategic direction | "We're focused on..." = stated priority |
| Email newsletters they send | How they communicate with their audience | Tone, language, values |
| About pages and website copy | Self-description, mission, values | "Fast-growing disruptors" -- how they see themselves |
| Community/group posts (Facebook, Slack, Reddit) | Peer-to-peer discussions, unfiltered complaints | Raw pain language not filtered for press |
| Public conversations on Twitter/X | Real-time reactions, opinions, hot takes | Current mood and priorities |
| Press releases | Official milestones, strategic messages | Formal language around priorities |
| Earnings call transcripts | Financial priorities, strategic bets, worries | "We're investing heavily in..." = budget signal |
| Product reviews (G2, Capterra) | What users love/hate about current solutions | Competitor weakness = your opportunity |

### How to Use VOC in Outreach

1. **Mirror their language for goals.** If they say "aggressive growth," you say "aggressive growth" -- not "rapid scaling."
2. **Use their self-descriptors.** If they call themselves "fast-growing disruptors," use that phrase in your intro.
3. **Pull pain point language verbatim.** Their words are more persuasive than yours.
4. **Reference specific details.** Pet names, children's names, reading lists, hobbies from their about page -- for high-value future pacing.
5. **Pair audience objections with product features.** Mine VOC to find doubts, then show which feature resolves each doubt.
6. **Reflect brand values back.** Use their own mission language to show alignment.

## AI RESEARCH WORKFLOW (Prompt-Dig-Summarize)

### Step 1: PROMPT -- Educate the AI

Feed the AI:
- Your messaging matrix (personas, priorities, current solutions, problems, aspirations)
- Your top 5 triggers per problem
- What specifically to look for ("Find evidence they are in situation X")
- The account name and key contacts

**Example prompt structure:**
"My company sells [X] to [persona]. Our top triggers are [1, 2, 3, 4, 5]. Research [Company Name] and look for evidence of any of these triggers. Focus on publicly available reports, website content, recent news, job postings, and LinkedIn activity."

### Step 2: DIG -- Direct Where to Look

Tell the AI which sources to check:
- Publicly available reports (strategic plans, budgets, annual reports)
- Company website (about, careers, blog, product pages)
- News articles (Google News, industry publications)
- Earnings call transcripts (Seeking Alpha -- for public companies)
- LinkedIn content from key executives
- Quarterly reports and investor presentations
- Job postings (LinkedIn, Indeed, Greenhouse/Lever)

### Step 3: SUMMARIZE -- Make It Actionable

Ask the AI to output:
1. Which triggers were found (ranked by strength)
2. Key quotes or evidence for each trigger
3. Recommended messaging angle based on the messaging matrix
4. Suggested first line or problem proposition
5. Any red flags or disqualifying signals

### Key Principle
AI is only as smart as the person using it. The manual work is defining the messaging matrix and trigger hierarchy. Once those are locked, AI can apply them at scale.

## ACCOUNT TIERING BY TRIGGER

| Tier | Criteria | Sequence Type | Research Time |
|---|---|---|---|
| **A Tier** | Hard trigger found (problem NOW) | High-effort: all manual calls, personalized emails, LinkedIn touches, full triple/double tap | 15-30+ min |
| **B Tier** | Soft trigger found (problem) | Standard: personalized emails, targeted calls for engaged prospects | 10-15 min |
| **C Tier** | In ICP, no specific trigger | Low-effort: automated/templated emails, call people who open, pervasive problem angles | 4-6 min |

### A/B Tier Gets Extra Dials
Add two extra phone dial attempts to A and B tier accounts. Same number of total dials, 13x the meetings -- the difference is calling the right people at the right time.

## RESEARCH TIMELINES

### High-Value Prospect / A-Tier Account (Bree Weber Standard)
- Day 1: Initial research across all sources, build messaging matrix for this specific prospect
- Day 2: Draft the outreach (email, DM, or call framework)
- Day 3: Edit, refine, get feedback, then send
- Total: 1-3 days for a single high-value prospect

### Standard B-Tier Account
- 15-30 minutes total
- Quick LinkedIn scan, trigger check, one pain angle identified
- Write and send same session

### Volume C-Tier Outreach
- 4-6 minutes per prospect
- First-best trigger scan only
- Pervasive problem angles, templated structure with light personalization

### AI-Assisted at Scale
- 2-4 minutes per prospect after initial AI setup
- Prompt-Dig-Summarize workflow with pre-built trigger list
- Human reviews and adjusts AI output before sending

## REVERSE ENGINEERING PROBLEMS (Bree Weber)

When you cannot find an obvious trigger, reverse engineer from your past work:

1. How is this prospect's goal similar to other clients' goals I have worked on?
2. How does my solution achieve that goal faster, easier, or better?
3. How can I help them avoid pitfalls and mistakes along the way?
4. What were the "before state" complaints of similar past clients?

This creates empathy-driven research angles even without hard triggers.

## PERSONALIZATION PLACEMENT RULE

The personalization MUST show up in the first 5-10 words of the first line (for email preview text).

**Wrong:** "Frank, with the recent approval of the FAPI study..."
**Right:** "With the recent approval of the FAPI study, Frank..."

Flip sentence structure to lead with the personalization before you say their name. The preview text in the inbox is your 3-second hook -- do not waste it on a name.

---

## PERSONALIZATION DANGERS (Acquisition X)

**Safe to use (stable data):**
- First name (never changes)
- Recent achievements (verified timing)
- Recent posts/content (time-stamped)
- Product launches (publicly dated)

**Dangerous to use (stale quickly):**
- Industry (people change industries)
- Job title (people change roles frequently)
- Location (people relocate)

In personalization, more is NOT always better. Using outdated job titles, industries, or locations causes prospects to mark you as spam because the email is irrelevant to their current situation. Verify data freshness before using AI-generated personalization.
