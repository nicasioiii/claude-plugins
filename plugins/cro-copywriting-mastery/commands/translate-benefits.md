# Translate Benefits Command

## What This Command Does

Takes a list of product features and transforms each into a three-tier benefit chain: Functional > Dimensionalized > Emotional. Produces a complete FAB (Feature-Advantage-Benefit) chart ready for use in landing pages, ads, emails, and sales pages.

---

## Step 1: Gather Context

Before translating benefits, I need:

1. **What is the product/service?** (Brief description)
2. **Who is the target customer?** (Their role, situation, pain points)
3. **List all features** (Everything the product does, includes, or offers -- raw list is fine)
4. **What is the core transformation?** (Where do they start vs. where do they end up?)
5. **What awareness stage is the audience?** (Determines which benefit tier to lead with)

---

## Step 2: Build the Three-Tier Benefit Chain

For each feature, I produce three tiers:

### Tier 1: Functional Benefit
The typical, surface-level benefit. What the feature DOES for the user.
- Uses the "So What?" test once: Feature > "So what?" > Functional benefit
- Example: Soft grip pencil > Easier to hold

### Tier 2: Dimensionalized Benefit
What the functional benefit looks like in the real life of the prospect. Paints a picture with specificity.
- Uses the "So What?" test again: Functional benefit > "So what?" > Dimensionalized benefit
- Must be vivid enough to create a mental picture
- Must be universal enough that the majority of the audience can insert themselves
- Example: Easier to hold > "You can write for hours every day with no pain in your hand or wrist"

### Tier 3: Emotional Benefit
The emotional impact of the dimensionalized benefit. How it makes them FEEL.
- Uses the "So What?" test one final time: Dimensionalized benefit > "So what?" > Emotional benefit
- Connects to one of the Four Core Human Drivers: Self-Preservation, Romance, Money, Recognition
- Example: No pain when writing > "Feel great knowing you can write to your heart's content without ever worrying about being in pain again"

---

## Step 3: Apply the Faux Benefit Check

Every benefit gets checked for the "faux benefit" anti-pattern:

**What is a faux benefit?** A statement that FEELS like a benefit but is at least one step removed from what the prospect actually wants.

**Faux benefit examples:**
- "Flush fat from your intestinal tract" (so what? that is a feature of the mechanism, not a benefit)
- "Your arteries will instantly relax" (so what? how does that change my life?)
- "Make your ads more attractive" (so what? what does that mean for me?)

**Fix with bridge phrases:**
- "So you can..."
- "So you will..."
- "Which means you..."
- "...and that means..."

If a benefit cannot survive the bridge phrase test, it is still a feature in disguise.

---

## Step 4: Apply Universal Picture Painting

For dimensionalized benefits, I check that they are:
- **Specific enough** to trigger emotion and create mental pictures
- **Universal enough** that the majority of the audience can insert themselves

**BAD (too specific):** "Imagine you jump on your MacBook... sip your morning coffee... login to your Gmail account and see dozens of sales."

**GOOD (universalized):** "Imagine you jump on your computer... sip your favorite morning beverage... login to check your sales stats... and see more sales than you ever thought possible in a single day."

The key is giving them a framework they fill in with their own details while providing enough dimensionalization to trigger emotion.

---

## Step 5: Overlay the Big 4 Feelings Check

Every emotional benefit gets tagged with which of the Big 4 Feelings (Kyle Milligan / Agora) it activates:

1. **New/Only** -- Makes them feel they are getting something nobody else has
2. **Easy/Anybody** -- Makes them feel confidence that they can succeed
3. **Safe/Predictable** -- Makes them feel secure in the outcome
4. **Big/Fast** -- Makes them feel excited about the magnitude and speed of results

A strong benefit set should activate all four across different features.

## Step 7: Expert Audience Exception

**When features ARE benefits:** For expert/enthusiast audiences, the feature itself IS what they care about. Camera enthusiasts want specs, not "capture special moments." Know your audience's expertise level.

If the audience is expert-level, I flag features that should be presented AS features rather than translated into emotional benefits. The FAB chart still gets built, but the recommendation shifts to leading with Tier 1 (functional) rather than Tier 3 (emotional).

---

## Step 8: Recommend Which Tier to Lead With

Based on awareness stage:

| Awareness Stage | Lead With | Because |
|----------------|-----------|---------|
| Unaware | Emotional (Tier 3) | They need to feel the pain/desire before they care about the solution |
| Problem Aware | Emotional (Tier 3) + Dimensionalized (Tier 2) | They know the problem; show them the transformed life |
| Solution Aware | Dimensionalized (Tier 2) | They know solutions exist; show them what YOUR solution looks like in practice |
| Product Aware | Functional (Tier 1) + Dimensionalized (Tier 2) | They are comparing options; specific functional advantages matter |
| Most Aware | Functional (Tier 1) | They know you; give them the specs and the deal |

## Bonus: The "So What?" Chain Worked Example

**Product:** Online course on email marketing
**Feature:** 47 pre-written email templates

**So What Chain:**
1. Feature: "47 pre-written email templates"
2. "So what?" > Functional: "You don't have to write emails from scratch"
3. "So what?" > Dimensionalized: "Open your email tool, pick a template, customize 3 lines, and hit send -- your entire week of emails done in 20 minutes instead of 4 hours"
4. "So what?" > Emotional: "Feel the relief of never staring at a blank screen again, knowing your emails are proven to convert -- and spend those reclaimed hours growing your business or being with your family"

**Copy-ready versions by awareness stage:**
- Problem Aware: "Never stare at a blank email screen again. Our 47 proven templates let you send your entire week of emails in 20 minutes."
- Product Aware: "47 pre-written, proven email templates. Customize 3 lines, hit send. Your entire week of emails done in 20 minutes instead of 4 hours."
- Most Aware: "Includes 47 email templates. Plug, customize, send."
