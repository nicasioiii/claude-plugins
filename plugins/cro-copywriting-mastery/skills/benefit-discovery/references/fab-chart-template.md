# FAB Chart Template & Examples

## The Complete FAB Framework

FAB stands for Features → Advantages → Benefits.

This is your working document for translating product features into customer benefits that actually sell.

---

## How to Read the Chart

| FEATURE | ADVANTAGE | BENEFIT |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| What does it do? | So what? What does that enable? | How does that make them feel? Why do they care? |

## Complete Examples by Product Type

### EXAMPLE 1: Project Management Software

| FEATURE | ADVANTAGE | BENEFIT |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Drag-and-drop task reorganization | You can rearrange priorities in seconds without learning a new system | Feel in control of your day; adjust on the fly without stress |
| Automated deadline reminders | You never miss a deadline or commitment | Sleep peacefully knowing you'll never let your team down; feel reliable |
| Time-tracking integration | You see exactly where your hours went each day | Feel proud at day's end knowing your time went to what matters; end stress-free |
| Team collaboration dashboard | Everyone sees the same updated project status in real-time | Stop coordinating via email; feel aligned with your team; trust they're on the same page |
| Mobile access | You can update tasks from anywhere, not just at your desk | Feel flexible and responsive; never miss an update even when mobile |

**How to use this:** When writing copy, you don't mention all the features. You pick the 2-3 most compelling BENEFITS and emphasize those.

## How to Fill Out Your Own FAB Chart

### Step 1: List Your Features

Write down every major feature of your product/service. Don't edit—just list them.

**Examples:**
- Software features
- Service processes
- Product attributes
- Guarantees/policies

### Step 2: For Each Feature, Write the Advantage

Ask: "So what? What does that enable?"

This bridges from WHAT to WHAT IT ALLOWS.

**Example:**
- Feature: "Mobile app"
- Question: "So what? What does mobile access enable?"
- Advantage: "You can manage tasks from anywhere, not just a desk"

### Step 3: For Each Advantage, Write the Benefit

Ask: "How does that make them feel? Why do they care?"

This is where emotion lives.

**Example:**
- Advantage: "Manage tasks from anywhere"
- Question: "How does that make them feel?"
- Benefit: "Feel flexible and responsive; adapt to life instead of being chained to a desk"

### Step 4: Test with "So You Can..."

Does your benefit pass this test?

- Feature: "Mobile app"
- So you can... "manage tasks from anywhere"? (That's just restating the feature)
- So you can... "feel flexible and in control of your schedule"? (Yes, that's a real benefit)

If it doesn't pass, your benefit is too close to the feature.

## Common FAB Chart Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Benefit Is Too Close to Feature

**Wrong:**
- Feature: "Dashboard with metrics"
- Benefit: "See your metrics on a dashboard"
(This just repeats the feature in different words)

**Right:**
- Feature: "Dashboard with metrics"
- Benefit: "Stop wasting 6 hours weekly gathering data, so you can focus on strategy"
(Now it's about them, not the product)

### Mistake 2: Benefit Is Too Generic

**Wrong:**
- Benefit: "Feel good about your results"
(Too vague)

**Right:**
- Benefit: "Feel confident in your diet choices; take control of your body"
(Specific emotion + specific benefit)

### Mistake 3: Benefit Doesn't Connect to Customer's World

**Wrong:**
- Benefit: "Uses AI-powered algorithms"
(Technically true, but customer doesn't care about algorithms)

**Right:**
- Benefit: "Spot opportunities that others miss; stay ahead of market changes"
(Now it's about them, not the technology)

## Using Your FAB Chart for Copy

Once you've filled out your FAB chart, you have everything for compelling copy.

### In Headlines

Use benefits, not features:
- Bad: "Project management software with mobile access"
- Good: "Manage your day from anywhere; never miss an update"

### In Body Copy

Expand on top 2-3 benefits:
- Pick your strongest benefits
- Describe them with sensory language
- Paint before/after picture
- Use emotional language

### In Social Proof

Let customers speak about benefits (not features):
- Customer says: "I feel so much more in control"
- Bad testimonial: "The mobile app works well"
- Good testimonial: "Finally feel like I have control of my schedule instead of my schedule controlling me"

### In Objection Section

Address feature concerns with benefits:
- Objection: "Is it hard to set up?"
- Feature answer: "No, it takes 5 minutes"
- Benefit answer: "Get started in 5 minutes, so you can start feeling the relief immediately"

## Quick FAB Exercise (30 minutes)

If you're short on time:

1. **List your top 3 features** (5 min)
2. **For each, write 1 advantage** (5 min)
3. **For each, write 1 benefit** (5 min)
4. **Test with "So you can..."** (5 min)
5. **Pick strongest benefit for headline** (5 min)

You now have your top benefit ready for copy.

## FAB Chart by Product Type

### SaaS / Software

Focus on:
- Time saved (benefit: peace/control)
- Effort reduced (benefit: ease/freedom)
- Accuracy improved (benefit: confidence/trust)
- Integration (benefit: organization/clarity)

### Physical Products

Focus on:
- Quality/durability (benefit: confidence/pride)
- Aesthetics (benefit: self-image/status)
- Function (benefit: ease/relief)
- Value (benefit: smart/savvy)

### Services

Focus on:
- Expertise (benefit: confidence/trust)
- Time savings (benefit: freedom/peace)
- Results (benefit: achievement/pride)
- Support (benefit: care/belonging)

### Digital Products

Focus on:
- Learning (benefit: empowerment/growth)
- Implementation (benefit: ease/speed)
- Community (benefit: belonging/support)
- Results (benefit: transformation/pride)

## FAB Chart as Swipe File

Keep your FAB chart handy when writing. Reference it constantly.

**When stuck on copy:**
- Go back to your FAB chart
- Pick a benefit you haven't emphasized
- Write from that angle
- Test how it lands

This is how you keep copy fresh and benefit-focused.
