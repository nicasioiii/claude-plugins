# Dimensionalized Benefits -- Before & After Examples

## What Dimensionalization Means

A dimensionalized benefit takes a flat, functional claim and paints a specific picture in the prospect's real life. It moves from abstract to concrete, from logical to emotional, from forgettable to unforgettable.

**Three tiers of benefits** (Source: Todd Brown):
1. **Functional Benefit:** The typical, superficial benefit. (e.g., soft grip = easier to hold)
2. **Dimensionalized Benefit:** What the functional benefit looks like in the real life of the prospect. Paint the picture. (e.g., "Write for hours with no pain in your hand or wrist.")
3. **Emotional Benefit:** How the dimensionalized benefit makes them FEEL. (e.g., "Feel great knowing you can write to your heart's content without ever worrying about pain again.")

**Why this works:** Vague, general benefits are processed logically. Specific, dimensionalized benefits trigger emotion because they create pictures in the mind. You are the director of the movie playing in the prospect's mind. (Source: Todd Brown)

---

## THE TRANSLATION PATTERN

**Flat (Functional)** -> **Dimensionalized (Vivid, Specific, Emotional)**

The dimensionalized version always does three things:
1. Places the reader in a specific scenario
2. Uses sensory or experiential language
3. Connects to an emotional outcome

## THE BIG 4 FEELINGS APPLIED TO BENEFITS

Every dimensionalized benefit should trigger at least one of the Big 4 Feelings (Source: Kyle Milligan / Agora, cited by Todd Brown):

### 1. New/Only -- "I am getting something nobody else has"

**Flat:** "Our proprietary formula."
**Dimensionalized with New/Only:** "This is the exact formula used by 3 Olympic athletes -- and until last month, it was not available outside their training facility. You are among the first 500 people to access it."

**Flat:** "Unique methodology."
**Dimensionalized with New/Only:** "While your competitors are still using the same playbook from 2019, you will be running a system that 97% of the market does not even know exists yet."

### 2. Easy/Anybody -- "I can actually do this"

**Flat:** "Simple to use."
**Dimensionalized with Easy/Anybody:** "If you can send an email, you can use this. No tech skills. No learning curve. One of our customers is a 68-year-old retired teacher who set up her entire system in a single afternoon."

**Flat:** "Beginner-friendly program."
**Dimensionalized with Easy/Anybody:** "Start with zero experience. Follow the step-by-step walkthrough. By day 3, you will have your first campaign live. Not planned -- live and running."

### 3. Safe/Predictable -- "This will work for me"

**Flat:** "Proven results."
**Dimensionalized with Safe/Predictable:** "This is the same system used by 4,200 businesses across 47 industries. The average result is a 34% increase in the first 90 days. Not a promise -- an average across tracked clients."

**Flat:** "Money-back guarantee."
**Dimensionalized with Safe/Predictable:** "Try it for 60 days. If you do not see results, email us one sentence and get every penny back within 48 hours. We have processed 11 refunds out of 2,300 customers. That is how confident we are."

### 4. Big/Fast -- "The results will be massive and quick"

**Flat:** "Fast results."
**Dimensionalized with Big/Fast:** "Your first lead comes in within 72 hours of launching. Not a trickle -- our median customer gets 14 qualified leads in their first week."

**Flat:** "Significant revenue increase."
**Dimensionalized with Big/Fast:** "One headline change on a $10M product page was worth $3M over the testing period. That is the scale of impact we are talking about. Not 5% tweaks -- multiples."

## FUTURE PACING EXAMPLES

Future pacing is a specific technique where you describe the customer's future state in vivid detail. It is one of the most powerful forms of dimensionalization. (Source: Arman Assadi, Eddie Shleyner)

**Trigger phrases for future pacing:** "Imagine...", "Picture this...", "Stop...", "Start...", "6 months from now...", "In one year..."

### Example 1: Copywriting Course
"Stop feeling like an imposter. Start thinking, writing, and selling like a professional conversion copywriter."
(Source: Eddie Shleyner -- subheadline example)

### Example 2: Business Coaching
"6 months from now, you are not scrambling for clients. You have a waitlist. You are choosing who you work with. You raised your prices last month and nobody blinked."

### Example 3: Health Product
"In 12 weeks, you catch your reflection in a store window and do a double-take. Not because you look like a model -- because you look like the version of yourself you have been carrying in your head for years."

### Example 4: SaaS Product
"Picture this: it is Friday at 4pm. Your dashboard is green. Every deliverable shipped. Your team is wrapping up. You close your laptop knowing Monday will not be a scramble."

### Example 5: Financial Planning
"Imagine your 60th birthday. You are not worried about whether you saved enough. You are deciding between Portugal and Costa Rica for next winter."

### Example 6: Trading Education (Arman Assadi's Timothy Sykes example)
The core transformation is not "learn to trade stocks." The underlying value is FREEDOM: leaving your job, leaving the rat race, dictating your own terms, your own schedule, working a few hours a day to earn more than most people do in a year. Go LAYERS deep -- not just the surface benefit.

## THE DIMENSIONALIZATION CHECKLIST

For every benefit in your copy, check these boxes:

- [ ] Does it place the reader in a specific scenario? (Not abstract)
- [ ] Does it include sensory detail? (Sight, sound, touch, taste, smell)
- [ ] Does it include a time reference? (When will they experience this?)
- [ ] Does it include an emotional outcome? (How will they feel?)
- [ ] Is it universal enough for 80%+ of the audience to self-insert?
- [ ] Would reading this make someone say "I want THAT" (not just "that sounds nice")?
- [ ] Does it pass the So What test? (No further "so what?" questions possible)
- [ ] Does it trigger at least one of the Big 4 Feelings? (New/Only, Easy/Anybody, Safe/Predictable, Big/Fast)

If any check fails, the benefit needs more work.

## UNIVERSAL PICTURE PAINTING RULES

When dimensionalizing benefits, keep them universal enough so the majority of the audience can insert themselves. (Source: Todd Brown)

**BAD (too specific):** "Imagine you jump on your MacBook... sip your morning coffee... login to your Gmail account and see dozens of sales."
**GOOD (universalized):** "Imagine you jump on your computer... sip your favorite morning beverage... login to check your sales stats... and you see more sales than you ever thought you would in a single day."

The key is giving them a framework they can fill in with their own details while still providing enough dimensionalization to trigger emotion.

## COMMON DIMENSIONALIZATION MISTAKES

1. **Too specific** -- "Open your MacBook Air in your Pottery Barn home office" excludes too many people. Use "Open your computer in your workspace."

2. **Too vague** -- "Feel better about your life." About WHAT specifically? WHEN? In what scenario?

3. **All logic, no emotion** -- "Save 10 hours per week." Add the emotional payoff: "Use those 10 hours for the projects (or the people) you have been neglecting."

4. **Describing the product, not the life** -- "Our AI-powered engine processes data in real-time." That is a feature. Show what life looks like BECAUSE of that feature.

5. **Forgetting the contrast** -- The most powerful dimensionalized benefits show BEFORE and AFTER. "No more [old pain]. Instead, [new experience]."

6. **Stopping at functional** -- "Easier to use than competitors." That is an advantage, not a dimensionalized benefit. Keep asking "So what?" until you reach a life scenario and an emotion.

7. **Using faux benefits** -- "Flush fat from your intestinal tract" sounds like a benefit but is at least one step removed from what the prospect actually wants. Bridge it: "Flush fat from your intestinal tract so you can finally see definition in your midsection when you look in the mirror." (Source: Todd Brown)
