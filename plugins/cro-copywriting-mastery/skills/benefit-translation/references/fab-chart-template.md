# FAB Chart Template -- Features, Advantages, Benefits

## How to Use This Template

Build a FAB chart for every product before writing any copy. The chart forces you to translate every feature through all benefit tiers. Most copy fails because the writer stopped at the feature or advantage level and never reached the emotional benefit.

**Source framework:** Todd Brown (Three Tiers of Benefits), Arman Assadi (F.A.B. Principle), Copywriting Made Simple (So-What Test), Kyle Milligan/Agora (Big 4 Feelings).

---

## THE 4-COLUMN FAB CHART

| Feature | Advantage | Benefit (Dimensionalized) | Emotional Benefit |
|---------|-----------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| What it IS or DOES | Why that is HELPFUL | What it looks like in THEIR LIFE | How it makes them FEEL |

---

## THE "SO WHAT?" CHAIN PROCESS

Before filling in the chart, master the So-What Chain. For each feature, ask "So what?" repeatedly until you reach an emotional end state. This is how you move from functional to dimensionalized to emotional.

### Chain Example 1: SaaS Tool

Feature: "Automated email sequences"
-> So what? "You do not have to manually send follow-up emails"
-> So what? "Your leads get nurtured even while you sleep or take vacation"
-> So what? "You wake up to new sales without lifting a finger"
-> Emotional: "Feel free to actually live your life while your business grows"

### Chain Example 2: Physical Product

Feature: "Phone has location tracking"
-> So what? "You can find out its location whenever you want"
-> So what? "You always know where your daughter is, day or night"
-> Emotional: "Feel secure knowing your family is safe without hovering"

### Chain Example 3: Info Product

Feature: "12 downloadable worksheets"
-> So what? "You do not start from a blank page"
-> So what? "Each exercise takes 15 minutes instead of 2 hours of guesswork"
-> So what? "You finish the entire program in half the time"
-> Emotional: "Feel accomplished instead of overwhelmed -- this actually gets done"

### Chain Example 4: Agency Service

Feature: "Dedicated account manager"
-> So what? "One person knows your entire account history"
-> So what? "You never have to re-explain your brand to a new person"
-> So what? "Requests get handled in hours, not days"
-> Emotional: "Feel like you have a trusted partner, not a vendor you have to manage"

**Rule:** If a reader could say "So what?" to your benefit and you have an answer, that answer is the REAL benefit. Use it instead.

## IDENTIFYING FAUX BENEFITS

A faux benefit is a statement that FEELS like a benefit but is at least one step removed from what the prospect actually wants. (Source: Todd Brown)

**Examples of faux benefits:**
- "Flush fat from your intestinal tract." (So what? What does that MEAN for the person?)
- "Your arteries and blood vessels will instantly relax." (So what? How does that change their day?)
- "Make your ads more attractive." (So what? What happens because of that?)

**Fix faux benefits with bridge phrases:**
- "So you can..."
- "So you will..."
- "Which means you..."
- "...and that means..."

Always ask: what does this MEAN for the prospect? What is the ultimate result they will experience in their real life?

## THE FAB BUILDING PROCESS

### Step 1: List Every Feature

Be exhaustive. Include features that seem obvious or minor. Even "small" features can produce powerful emotional benefits when translated fully.

### Step 2: Apply the "So What?" Chain to Each

For each feature, ask "So what?" repeatedly until you reach an emotional end state. Use the chain examples above as your model.

### Step 3: Check for Faux Benefits

Review every benefit in column 3. Apply the faux benefit test:
- If a reader said "So what?" to your benefit, what would you say?
- If you have an answer, that answer is the REAL benefit -- use it instead
- Use bridge phrases ("so you can...", "which means...") to push deeper

### Step 4: Universalize the Picture

Keep dimensionalized benefits universal enough so the majority of the audience can insert themselves.
- BAD (too specific): "Imagine you jump on your MacBook... sip your morning coffee... login to your Gmail account"
- GOOD (universalized): "Imagine you jump on your computer... sip your favorite morning beverage... login to check your sales stats"
- The key is giving them a framework they can fill in with their own details while still providing enough detail to trigger emotion. (Source: Todd Brown)

### Step 5: Identify the Top 3

Not all benefits are created equal. Identify the 3 most powerful emotional benefits. These become:
- Your headline angle (the strongest one)
- Your subhead
- Your primary body copy theme

### Step 6: Match to Big 4 Feelings

For each top benefit, check which of the Big 4 it triggers (Source: Kyle Milligan / Agora):
1. **New/Only** -- makes them feel they are getting something nobody else has
2. **Easy/Anybody** -- makes them feel confidence that they can succeed
3. **Safe/Predictable** -- makes them feel secure in the outcome
4. **Big/Fast** -- makes them feel excited about the magnitude and speed of results

If a top benefit triggers none, it is not emotionally resonant enough. Rework until it triggers at least one.

**The two biggest factors in solution attractiveness** (Todd Brown): Easy/quick to implement + fast results. These are probably the single two biggest factors. If your FAB chart does not surface benefits in these categories, look harder.

## FAB CHART FOR EXPERT AUDIENCES

When your audience is highly technical or expert, the standard chart inverts. Features LEAD. Benefits support. (Source: Copywriting Made Simple)

**Why this happens:** For expert/enthusiast audiences, the feature IS what they care about. Camera enthusiasts want specs, not "capture special moments." Developers want API response times, not "build faster." Know your audience's expertise level.

| Feature (LEAD) | Technical Advantage | Practical Outcome | Emotional (SECONDARY) |
|----------------|-------------------|-------------------|----------------------|
| 99.99% uptime SLA | Redundant architecture across 3 regions | Your application stays live even during regional outages | Sleep through the night without pager anxiety |
| Sub-10ms API latency | Optimized query engine with edge caching | Real-time user experiences that keep engagement high | Feel proud of the technical excellence your team delivers |
| SOC 2 Type II certified | Annual third-party security audits with published reports | Pass enterprise security reviews without a 6-month procurement cycle | Feel confident putting your reputation on the line with this vendor |
| Open-source SDK | Full source code access, community-maintained plugins | Customize integrations without waiting on vendor roadmap | Feel in control of your technical stack, not locked in |

**For expert audiences:** Features lead. Benefits support. Reverse the usual order. But do NOT skip the emotional column entirely -- even experts are humans who feel relief, pride, and confidence.

## QUICK REFERENCE: BRIDGE PHRASES FOR DEEPENING BENEFITS

When you are stuck at a shallow benefit level, use these phrases to push one level deeper:

- "So you can..."
- "Which means..."
- "So you will..."
- "And that means..."
- "Imagine..."
- "Picture this..."
- "What that looks like in your life is..."
- "The result? You..."

Each phrase forces you to answer from the prospect's perspective, not the product's.

## COMMON FAB CHART MISTAKES

1. **Stopping at the advantage column.** Most marketers write advantages and call them benefits. "Save time" is an advantage. "Reclaim your Thursday afternoons for the deep work that actually gets you promoted" is a benefit.

2. **Making every emotional benefit about "feeling confident."** Dig deeper. Confidence, relief, pride, excitement, calm, freedom, belonging -- the emotional palette is wide. Vary it.

3. **Writing benefits the product cannot deliver.** Every benefit must trace back to a real feature. If you cannot draw the line from Feature -> Advantage -> Benefit, the benefit is aspirational fiction.

4. **Ignoring "negative" emotional benefits.** Sometimes the most powerful benefit is the absence of a bad feeling: "No more Sunday night dread." "Stop dreading tax season." Relief from pain is just as motivating as pursuit of pleasure.

5. **Copying competitor benefits instead of deriving your own.** Your FAB chart must come from YOUR product's features. Competitors may have different features that produce different benefits. Starting from their copy creates faux benefits that you cannot prove.

6. **Skipping the chart for "simple" products.** Even a commodity product (t-shirts, coffee, notebooks) has features that translate to emotional benefits when you push hard enough. The EVO Planner example (copper-gilded pages -> feeling like someone who takes goals seriously) proves this.

7. **Not involving customer-facing team members.** Customer care reps, salespeople, and community managers know the customer's language better than marketers in a vacuum. Include them in the FAB chart exercise. (Source: Ryan Deiss)
