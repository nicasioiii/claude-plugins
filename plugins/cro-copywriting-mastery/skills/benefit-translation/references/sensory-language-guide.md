# Sensory Language Guide -- Word Banks & Application Rules

## Why Sensory Language Works

Neuroscience research confirms that sensory language activates brain areas beyond basic language processing:

- **Tactile words** ("leathery," "velvet," "sharp") activate the somatosensory cortex
- **Aromatic words** ("perfume," "coffee," "pine") light up the olfactory cortex in MRI studies
- **Motion words** ("kicked," "sprinted," "grasped") engage the motor cortex

When these brain areas activate, the reader EXPERIENCES the copy rather than merely PROCESSING it. Experience creates emotion. Emotion drives action.

Facts make the prospect WORK. Sensory language makes the prospect EXPERIENCE.

### Texture Words
smooth, rough, silky, gritty, velvety, coarse, satin, grainy, polished, fuzzy, slick, bumpy, crisp, fluffy, prickly, supple, wiry, feathery, plush, rugged

### Temperature Words
warm, cool, icy, scorching, lukewarm, frigid, toasty, chilling, blazing, tepid, frosty, searing, brisk, blistering

### Pressure/Weight Words
heavy, light, crushing, feathery, dense, airy, solid, hollow, weighty, delicate, sturdy, flimsy, firm, tender, rigid

### Application Examples

**Generic:** "Our software has a clean interface."
**Tactile:** "Our interface feels crisp and polished -- every click responds instantly, every transition is smooth."

**Generic:** "The mattress is comfortable."
**Tactile:** "Sink into the plush surface as it molds around your body. Feel the cool fabric against your skin."

**Generic:** "A well-built product."
**Tactile:** "Pick it up. Feel the solid weight in your hand. Run your thumb across the brushed aluminum edge. This is not flimsy -- it is built to last a decade."

## APPLICATION RULES

### Rule 1: Replace ONE Generic Word Per Sentence

Do not overload copy with sensory language. One vivid word per sentence is enough. More than that becomes purple prose.

**Overloaded (BAD):** "Feel the silky, smooth, velvety, luxurious fabric cascade across your warm, glowing skin."

**Balanced (GOOD):** "Feel the silky fabric glide across your skin."

### Rule 2: Match Sensory Type to Product

Choose the sensory category most relevant to your product:

| Product Type | Primary Sensory Category |
|-------------|------------------------|
| Physical products | Tactile (how it feels in their hands/on their body) |
| Food/beverage | Aromatic + Tactile (taste + smell + texture) |
| Software/digital | Motion + Visual (speed, flow, clarity) |
| Services | Motion (progress, momentum, results flowing) |
| Experiences | All categories (immersive description) |

### Rule 3: Use Sensory Language in Key Positions

Sensory words have the most impact in:
- **Dimensionalized benefits** -- painting the "after" picture
- **Future pacing sections** -- "imagine" scenarios
- **Product descriptions** -- making intangible products feel tangible
- **Testimonial pull-quotes** -- amplifying customer language
- **Headlines and subheads** -- stopping the scroll with vivid imagery

Sensory words have LESS impact in:
- Technical specifications
- Pricing sections
- Legal/guarantee copy
- CTA buttons

### Rule 4: Test Sensory Language Against Plain Language

Sometimes sensory language obscures meaning. "Sharp-edged surveys" sounds vivid but "faster surveys" might convert better. Use sensory language intentionally and test it.

The Copy Hackers contrarian insight: "Sometimes you can play with language even if meaning is not immediately clear. But do this intentionally and sparsely."

### Rule 5: Borrow Sensory Language from Customer Research

The strongest sensory copy comes from your prospects' own words. In review mining and interviews, flag any sensory language they use naturally:
- "It felt like a weight was lifted off my shoulders" (tactile + motion)
- "I could finally see the light at the end of the tunnel" (visual)
- "It was like a breath of fresh air" (aromatic)

Use their language. It is already calibrated to resonate.

## THE SPANISH RESEARCHER TEST

Spanish researchers using fMRI found that metaphorical language ("velvet voice," "leathery hands") activated the sensory cortex, while plain language ("potent voice," "strong hands") triggered NO sensory activation.

**Application:** When writing metaphors and descriptions, choose words that carry sensory weight. "A smooth process" is better than "a good process." "A sharp increase" is better than "a large increase." The sensory word does extra work in the reader's brain.

## SENSORY LANGUAGE IN DIMENSIONALIZED BENEFITS

The highest-impact use of sensory language is in dimensionalized benefits -- the second tier of the three-tier benefit model (Todd Brown):

1. **Functional Benefit** (plain): "The pencil has a soft grip."
2. **Dimensionalized Benefit** (sensory): "Write for hours without feeling your fingers cramp or your wrist ache. The grip molds to your hand like warm clay."
3. **Emotional Benefit** (feeling): "Feel confident knowing you can write to your heart's content without pain stopping you."

Sensory language lives in tier 2 and bridges the prospect from understanding to feeling. Without sensory dimensionalization, benefits stay abstract. With it, the prospect physically experiences the outcome before purchasing.
