# Hook Development Process

## How Hooks Are Created

Hooks are DEVELOPED, not discovered. They come from looking for new or unusual combinations and connections. Lots of ideas come from lots of input. Only write copy for hooks you are wildly excited about.

---

## THE HOOK FOUNDATION

**Promise + Curiosity = Hook Foundation**

Every good hook has two simultaneous qualities:
1. **Emotionally compelling** = big bold promise + unique mechanism (creates hope)
2. **Intellectually interesting** = storyline or angle they have never heard before (creates curiosity)

If it has promise but no curiosity, it is a flat claim. If it has curiosity but no promise, it is entertainment with no conversion power.

---

## FINDING SEEDS OF HOOKS

### The Input Method

Hooks grow from seeds. Seeds come from broad consumption of information:

- News and current events in your market
- Scientific research and studies
- Historical analogies and parallels
- Customer stories and testimonials
- Competitor weaknesses and gaps
- Cultural trends and shifts
- Books, podcasts, and conferences outside your niche
- Conversations with customers

**Dedicated thinking time:** Spend at least one hour weekly on pure idea generation. No writing, no editing -- just generating hook seeds.

### The Correlation Technique (Andrew Milligan)

Relate a personal lesson to your audience through something odd, relevant, or something only you know. Connect two unrelated events to provide a paradigm shift.

**Examples:**
- **Golf analogy:** Most people learn to drive the ball far, but the winning move is learning to putt. Same with business -- the small 20% drives the 80%.
- **Wayne Gretzky:** "Skate where the puck is going to be." You are skating to where customers currently ARE. You need to go where they are GOING to be.
- **Stone in shoe:** You deal with a tiny stone in your shoe immediately. But massive problems (debt, broken business) you put off. Why?

**Sources for correlations:**
- Events in your life
- News and controversial topics
- Sports
- Books
- Conflicting views
- Be ridiculously relevant to what prospects want or need to believe

**Goal:** Be able to connect literally ANYTHING that happens in your life to a lesson for your market. This is a muscle that gets stronger with practice.

### The Amazon R&D Method (Todd Brown)

Mine best-selling book subtitles for hook angles:
1. Go to Amazon and search your topic
2. Look at the top 20 best-selling books
3. Read the subtitles (not just titles)
4. Subtitles often contain proven hook angles that publishers have validated through sales

### The Imitation Game Method (Todd Brown)

Study winning hooks from other niches and adapt them to yours:
1. Collect winning ads, sales pages, and campaigns from OUTSIDE your industry
2. Identify the underlying hook pattern
3. Apply the same pattern to your mechanism and promise
4. This avoids copying within your niche (which the market will recognize) while borrowing proven structures

## THE EXTRAORDINARY PRESENTATION OF THE ORDINARY

When you cannot find something absolutely new and different, take the ordinary and present it in an extraordinary way.

### How to Do This

1. **Find the hidden angle.** Every product has a story, origin, or detail that the market has never heard. Dig for it.

2. **Change the framing.** Instead of "we improve your email open rates," try "the forgotten inbox principle that turns your emails from invisible to irresistible."

3. **Change the protagonist.** Instead of your product being the hero, make the customer the hero who discovers your product.

4. **Change the timeline.** Start at the end (results first), start in the middle (crisis moment), or start with a seemingly unrelated event that connects.

5. **Change the comparison.** Compare your solution to something unexpected rather than the obvious competitor.

### Examples

**Ordinary:** "Our accounting software saves you time on bookkeeping."
**Extraordinary:** "Why a NASA scheduling algorithm is now keeping small business books balanced -- and saving owners 11 hours a week."

**Ordinary:** "A fitness program for people over 40."
**Extraordinary:** "The training method that 82-year-old Japanese fishermen use to stay stronger than most American 30-year-olds."

**Ordinary:** "Email marketing templates."
**Extraordinary:** "The 9 email patterns that a Stanford behavioral scientist discovered work on 94% of humans -- regardless of what you sell."

### Step 3: Apply the Hook Checklist

For each seed, score against the Hook Checklist:

- [ ] Follows Rule of One (one narrative, one promise, one emotion)
- [ ] Big, believable promise of result
- [ ] New and fresh (timely)
- [ ] Specific, concrete, tangible
- [ ] Intellectually interesting (creates curiosity)
- [ ] Easy to grasp (no complexity barrier)
- [ ] Prevents categorization (the Categorical Imperative)
- [ ] Appropriate for the target awareness stage

### Step 4: Develop Top 3 Seeds into Full Hooks

Take your 3 highest-scoring seeds and develop each into a complete hook:

**Hook = Opening statement + Promise + Curiosity gap**

For each, write:
- The opening line (what stops them)
- The promise embedded in the hook
- The curiosity element (what makes them need to know more)

### Step 5: Test

- Read each hook to 3 people in your target market
- Watch their reaction in the first 3 seconds
- Ask: "Would you want to know more?"
- The hook that generates the strongest "tell me more" reaction wins

## THE "EVEN IF" TECHNIQUE

After establishing your hook, use "even if" introductions to expand the promise and overcome objections simultaneously:

**Formula:** "[Primary promise] even if [objection/limitation]"

**Examples:**
- "...even if you have never written a line of code"
- "...even if you are starting from zero"
- "...even if you have tried everything else and failed"
- "...even if you only have 30 minutes a day"

Each "even if" statement simultaneously:
1. Expands who the offer is for (scoopability)
2. Overcomes a specific objection
3. Increases the impressiveness of the promise

## THE FOUR QUICKSTART FORMULAS (Arman Assadi)

For when you need a hook structure fast:

### 1. Yes, And
Confirm something the audience believes, then extend it further:
"Yes, carbs are making you fat... AND they can cause diabetes."

### 2. Yes, But
Confirm a belief, but present an exception:
"Yes, cutting fat can be healthy... BUT not if you cut out the good fats."

### 3. No, And
Shatter a belief and make it worse:
"No, fatty foods do NOT make people fat... AND the right fats cause weight loss."

### 4. No, But
Redirect a wrong belief in a new direction:
"No, high-fat diets are NOT dangerous... BUT you have to plan meals correctly."

Choose the formula based on whether you want to confirm or challenge the audience's existing beliefs.
