# Naming Mechanisms -- Patterns, Rules & Examples

## The One Rule That Matters

Do NOT name your mechanism something that sounds benefit-driven or commercial. Name it something that sounds scientific, official, like it could appear in a news article or college textbook. Sound legitimate, not commercialized.

**Why this matters:** A commercial-sounding name triggers the prospect's "I'm being sold to" defense. A scientific or official-sounding name triggers "this is a real thing worth learning about."

## NAMING PATTERNS THAT WORK

### Pattern 1: The Method/Protocol/System

Structure: [Descriptor] + [Method/Protocol/System/Framework]

**Examples:**
- The E5 Method
- The Metabolic Reset Protocol
- The Authority Cascade System
- The Recovery-First Framework
- The Validate-First Method

**When to use:** When your mechanism is a multi-step process or structured approach.

### Pattern 2: The Scientific-Sounding Term

Structure: [Technical/Scientific Descriptor] + [Process Word]

**Examples:**
- Pixel Refreshing
- Neural Calm Protocol
- Mitochondrial Optimization
- Biological Age Reset
- Conversion Architecture

**When to use:** When your mechanism has a technical or scientific basis (or can be framed that way).

### Pattern 3: The Alliterative/Memorable Acronym

Structure: Acronym that spells a word or memorable sequence

**Examples:**
- The PAISA Story Formula (Problem, Agitate, Invalidate, Solve, Action)
- The CPB Chunk (Claim, Proof, Benefit)
- The PMS Formula (Paradigm, Mechanism, Sell)
- The VAA Framework (Value, Access, Action)
- The IRS Method (Intriguing, Riveting, Satisfying)

**When to use:** When your mechanism has distinct steps that can be abbreviated memorably.

### Pattern 4: The Numbered System

Structure: [Number] + [Descriptor]

**Examples:**
- The 7-Step Copy Framework
- The 12-Point Core Story Framework
- The 5-Phase Launch System
- The 3-Tier Benefit Framework

**When to use:** When the specific number of steps or components IS the differentiator.

### Pattern 5: The Origin Story Name

Structure: Named after a person, place, discovery, or event

**Examples:**
- The Gretzky Principle (named after the insight)
- The Stockholm Method (named after where it was discovered/developed)
- The Schwartz Scale (named after the originator)

**When to use:** When the origin story of the mechanism is compelling and adds credibility.

### Pattern 6: The Metaphor Name

Structure: [Vivid Metaphor] + [Process Word]

**Examples:**
- The Slippery Slide Technique
- The Domino Strategy
- The Flywheel Framework
- The Compounding Engine

**When to use:** When a metaphor captures how the mechanism works more intuitively than a technical description.

## NAMING PATTERNS THAT FAIL

### Anti-Pattern 1: The Hype Name

**Bad examples:**
- The Ultimate Fat Blaster System
- The Money Machine Formula
- The Dream Body Blueprint
- The Wealth Explosion Method

**Why they fail:** They sound like infomercials. They trigger skepticism. They promise a benefit in the NAME instead of in the copy.

### Anti-Pattern 2: The Generic Name

**Bad examples:**
- The Social Media Strategy
- The Marketing Plan
- The Weight Loss Program
- The Business Growth System

**Why they fail:** They are commodities with names. They fail the Google Slap test instantly. Nothing prevents categorization.

### Anti-Pattern 3: The Acronym That Means Nothing

**Bad examples:**
- The XQR System
- The NLP Framework (unless it actually IS NLP)
- The ABC Method (too generic)

**Why they fail:** Meaningless acronyms create no imagery, no curiosity, and no memorability.

### Anti-Pattern 4: The Too-Long Name

**Bad examples:**
- The Complete Automated Revenue-Generating Client Acquisition System
- The Science-Based Progressive Overload Metabolic Training Protocol

**Why they fail:** If it takes more than 5-6 words, it is too complex. People cannot remember it or repeat it.

## THE NAMING PROCESS

### Step 1: Do NOT Name It First

Interrogate the mechanism thoroughly BEFORE naming. Understand what makes it unique, different, and superior. The name should REFLECT the mechanism's core differentiator, not invent one.

### Step 2: Identify the Core Differentiator

What single word or concept captures what makes this mechanism DIFFERENT?

- Is it the ORDER of steps? (Name around sequence: "First-Step," "Validate-First")
- Is it the SPEED of results? (Name around speed: "Rapid," "Accelerated," "Flash")
- Is it the EASE of implementation? (Name around simplicity: "Simple," "One-Step," "Auto-")
- Is it the SAFETY or low risk? (Name around protection: "Safe," "Secure," "Recovery-First")
- Is it a REVERSAL of conventional wisdom? (Name around the reversal: "Inverse," "Reverse," "Counter-")
- Is it PRECISION or specificity? (Name around accuracy: "Precision," "Targeted," "Calibrated")

### Step 3: Generate 10 Name Candidates

Combine the core differentiator with naming patterns from above. Generate at least 10 options.

### Step 4: Apply the Naming Checklist

For each candidate:

- [ ] Does it sound scientific, official, or legitimate? (Not commercial)
- [ ] Would it fit in a news article or textbook title?
- [ ] Does it PREVENT categorization? (Cannot be filed into a known bucket)
- [ ] Is it 2-5 words maximum?
- [ ] Is it easy to say aloud?
- [ ] Is it easy to remember after hearing it once?
- [ ] Does it pass the Google Slap test? (Googling it leads only to you)
- [ ] Does it hint at HOW the mechanism works without giving it away?
- [ ] Does it create curiosity? ("What is that? Tell me more")

### Step 5: The Bar Test

Imagine telling someone at a bar: "We use the [mechanism name] to [primary promise]." If they would say "What's that?" with genuine curiosity, you have a winner. If they say "Oh, like [competitor]?" you have a commodity name.

## CLAIMING AND PROTECTING YOUR NAME

### Establishing Ownership

- Use the name consistently across ALL marketing materials
- Create a dedicated explainer page or video about the mechanism
- Register the domain if available (not required but helpful)
- Use the TM symbol (trademark) next to the name in marketing materials
- The more you use it, the more it becomes associated with you

### The Google Slap Prevention Check

Before finalizing your name:

1. Google the exact name in quotes
2. If results show competitors, generic information, or Wikipedia articles, choose a different name
3. The ideal result: zero relevant results (or only YOUR content appears)
4. After launching, monitor for competitors copying your name

### Name Uniqueness in Your Market

Your name does not need to be globally unique. It needs to be unique within YOUR market. "The Compound Effect" works fine for a fitness brand even though Darren Hardy wrote a book with that title -- different markets, different audiences.

But within your specific competitive landscape, the name must be yours alone.
