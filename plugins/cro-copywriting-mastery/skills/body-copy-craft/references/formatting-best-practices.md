# Formatting Best Practices

People do not read web copy — they scan. Your formatting must serve scanners FIRST and readers SECOND. A beautifully written paragraph that looks like a wall of text will never be read. A well-formatted mediocre paragraph will get at least a scan.

---

## RULE 1: WHITE SPACE IS YOUR MOST POWERFUL FORMATTING TOOL

White space reduces intimidation, increases readability, and makes the next sentence feel effortless to begin.

### Paragraph Length Rules
- **Maximum:** 2 sentences per paragraph in sales copy
- **Ideal:** 1 sentence per paragraph for most body copy
- **Exception:** Story sections can use 2-3 sentence paragraphs for narrative flow
- **Never:** 4+ sentence paragraphs in any digital copy

### Line Spacing
- Use generous line height (1.5-1.8x font size for web)
- Double-space between paragraphs (not just single)
- Add extra space before and after subheads

### The "Greased Chute" Test
If your copy looks like a greased chute — smooth, inviting, impossible to resist sliding down — you have enough white space. If it looks like a textbook, you have too little.

---

## RULE 2: ONE-LINE PARAGRAPHS ARE YOUR DEFAULT

This is not an essay. This is a conversation.

Each line should feel like a spoken sentence — something you would actually say to a person across a table.

**Example — Before (Essay Style):**
```
Our software helps businesses generate more leads by automating their outreach process. It connects with your existing CRM and sends personalized messages to prospects based on their behavior. This means you spend less time on manual tasks and more time closing deals.
```

**Example — After (Binary/One-Line Style):**
```
Our software generates leads on autopilot.

It plugs into your CRM in under 10 minutes.

Then it sends personalized messages based on what your prospects actually do.

Which means you stop chasing and start closing.
```

## RULE 3: SUBHEAD RULES

Subheads serve two critical functions:
1. They re-engage skimmers who are scrolling
2. They break long sections into digestible chunks

### Subhead Frequency
- Place a subhead every 3-5 paragraphs (every 200-400 words)
- Never let a section run longer than 5 paragraphs without visual interruption

### Subhead Content Rules
- **Subheads must TEASE, not summarize.** A subhead that gives away the content below it removes the reason to read.
- BAD: "Our Product Features" (summary — no reason to read further)
- GOOD: "The Hidden Feature Nobody Talks About" (tease — must read to learn)
- BAD: "Client Testimonials" (summary)
- GOOD: "What 347 Clients Said When We Asked Them One Question" (tease)

### Subhead Formatting
- Make subheads visually distinct from body text (larger, bolder, or different color)
- Use sentence case or title case consistently
- Leave extra white space above subheads (more than below)
- Subheads should make sense even if the reader reads ONLY the subheads

### The Subhead-Only Test
Read only the subheads of your page, top to bottom. Do they tell a compelling story on their own? Do they create curiosity? Do they logically progress toward the offer? If yes, your subheads are working. If no, rewrite them.

## RULE 4: BOLD TEXT RULES

Bold is for emphasis, not decoration. Overuse of bold makes nothing stand out.

### What to Bold
- The single most important phrase in each section (the key takeaway)
- Benefits that the reader would want to remember
- Numbers, data points, and specific results
- The product or mechanism name on first mention
- Key terms they need to understand

### What NOT to Bold
- Entire sentences (defeats the purpose)
- Generic words (do not bold "the," "and," "also")
- More than 15% of any paragraph
- Headers or subheads (they are already prominent)

### The Bold-Scan Test
If a reader scans ONLY the bolded text on your page, do they get the core message? Do they understand the benefit, the mechanism, and the offer? If yes, your bold strategy works.

## RULE 5: BULLET POINT RULES

Bullets make information scannable, digestible, and memorable. But poorly written bullets are worse than no bullets at all.

### When to Use Bullets
- Lists of 3 or more items
- Feature/benefit lists
- "What you'll learn" or "What's included" sections
- Fascinations (benefit teasers)
- Objection-busting points

### Bullet Formatting Rules
1. **Always use odd numbers:** 3, 5, 7, or 9 bullets. Odd numbers feel more credible and specific than even numbers.
2. **Parallel structure:** Every bullet must start with the same grammatical form (all verbs, all nouns, all "How to..." etc.)
3. **One idea per bullet:** Never combine two distinct thoughts in one bullet
4. **End with "And so much more..."** as the 3rd or 5th bullet. This signals abundance without requiring you to list everything.
5. **Front-load the benefit:** The first 3-5 words of each bullet should convey the value. Readers often only read the beginning of bullets.

### Bullet Writing Formulas
- **The "How to" Bullet:** "How to [achieve result] without [common pain]"
- **The "Why" Bullet:** "Why [surprising thing] is actually [beneficial]"
- **The "Secret" Bullet:** "The little-known [thing] that [impressive result]"
- **The "Number" Bullet:** "The [number] [things] that [result]"
- **The "Mistake" Bullet:** "The #1 mistake [audience] makes when [activity] — and how to avoid it"
- **The "What" Bullet:** "What [authority] never told you about [topic]"

### Bullet Point Examples — Before and After

**Before (Weak):**
- Includes email templates
- Has a training video
- Access to the community
- Bonus PDF download
- Calendar tool

**After (Strong):**
- 47 plug-and-play email templates proven to generate 30%+ open rates
- A 90-minute deep-dive training that walks you through the entire system step by step
- Lifetime access to our private community of 2,300+ active marketers
- The "Quick-Start" blueprint (a 12-page PDF that gets you results in your first 48 hours)
- And so much more...

## RULE 6: VISUAL HIERARCHY

The eye follows a predictable path. Design your formatting to guide it.

### The F-Pattern
Readers scan web pages in an F-pattern:
1. First, they read horizontally across the top (headline area)
2. Then they move down and read a shorter horizontal line (subhead area)
3. Then they scan vertically down the left side (looking for visual anchors)

**Implication:** Place your most important content at the top, left-align all text, and use visual anchors (bold, bullets, subheads) along the left edge.

### Visual Anchors (In Order of Stopping Power)
1. Images and screenshots
2. Subheads
3. Bold text
4. Bullet points
5. Pull quotes or callout boxes
6. Numbered lists
7. Colored or highlighted text

### Callout Boxes
Use sparingly for:
- Key statistics or data points
- Critical warnings or important notes
- Testimonial pull-quotes
- "Key Takeaway" summaries
- Guarantee statements

## RULE 7: SALES PAGE SPECIFIC FORMATTING

### Price Section Formatting
- Strikethrough the higher "value" price
- Make the actual price visually prominent (larger font)
- Use clear visual separation between price options (if multiple)
- Place the CTA button immediately after the price — no gap

### Testimonial Formatting
- Name, photo, and title/company for credibility
- Pull the most powerful sentence as a large pull-quote
- Keep individual testimonials to 2-4 sentences maximum
- Group testimonials by theme (results, ease, support)

### CTA Button Formatting
- High contrast color (stands out from everything else on the page)
- Generous padding (button should be large enough to feel inviting)
- First-person text: "Start My Free Trial" not "Start Your Free Trial"
- Reassurance text directly below the button: "No credit card required" or "Cancel anytime"

### The "Scroll Depth" Rule
Place CTAs at natural decision points:
- After the hero section (top)
- After the main benefit section
- After testimonials
- After the price reveal
- At the very bottom

## RULE 8: EMAIL FORMATTING

### Email-Specific Rules
- Shorter lines than web copy (40-60 characters per line ideal)
- Even more white space than sales pages
- No more than 2 sentences per paragraph
- Hyperlink text + raw URL (give both in sales emails)
- Use P.S. as a second subject line — many readers skip to it first

### Email Readability Checklist
- [ ] Every paragraph is 1-2 sentences max
- [ ] No paragraph exceeds 3 lines on mobile
- [ ] Links are clearly visible and clickable
- [ ] Subject line is under 50 characters
- [ ] Preview text complements (not repeats) the subject line
- [ ] P.S. contains the key CTA or benefit

---

## RULE 9: THE FORMATTING AUDIT CHECKLIST

Before publishing any piece of copy, run this audit:

### Structure
- [ ] No paragraph exceeds 2 sentences
- [ ] Subheads appear every 3-5 paragraphs
- [ ] Subheads tease, not summarize
- [ ] Bullet points use odd numbers
- [ ] Bullet points have parallel structure

### Visual
- [ ] Bold text highlights only key takeaways (under 15% of text)
- [ ] Adequate white space between all sections
- [ ] CTA buttons are visually prominent
- [ ] Testimonials include name + photo
- [ ] Price section uses strikethrough for anchor price

### Scannability
- [ ] Bold-scan test passes (bolded text tells the core story)
- [ ] Subhead-only test passes (subheads create a compelling narrative)
- [ ] F-pattern alignment (critical content at top and left)
- [ ] Multiple visual anchors per screen (no "text deserts")

### Mobile
- [ ] Checked on mobile viewport (50%+ of readers are mobile)
- [ ] No lines extend beyond mobile screen width
- [ ] Buttons are large enough for thumb tap
- [ ] Images scale properly
