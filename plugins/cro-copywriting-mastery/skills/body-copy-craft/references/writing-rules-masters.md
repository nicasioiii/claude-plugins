# Writing Rules from the Masters

These rules come from the greatest writers in literature and direct response copywriting. They have been tested across billions of dollars in sales copy and millions of published words. Treat them as laws, not suggestions — with one exception: Orwell's Rule 6.

---

## GEORGE ORWELL'S 6 RULES FOR WRITING

From "Politics and the English Language" (1946). These remain the gold standard for clear, powerful prose.

### Rule 1: Never use a metaphor, simile, or figure of speech which you are used to seeing in print.
**What it means:** Kill every cliche. "At the end of the day," "low-hanging fruit," "think outside the box," "game-changer," "take it to the next level," "move the needle" — all dead. If you have heard it before, your reader has heard it a thousand times. It registers as background noise.

**Application:** When you catch yourself writing a common phrase, stop. Ask: "How would I say this if the phrase didn't exist?" That answer is your copy.

**Cliches to eliminate from copy immediately:**
- "At the end of the day..."
- "It's not rocket science"
- "Take your business to the next level"
- "Game-changing"
- "Revolutionary" (unless it literally involves a revolution)
- "World-class"
- "Best-in-class"
- "Cutting-edge"
- "State-of-the-art"
- "Synergy"
- "Leverage" (as a verb)
- "Move the needle"
- "Low-hanging fruit"
- "Deep dive"
- "Paradigm shift" (ironic — was fresh when first coined)
- "Think outside the box"
- "Hit the ground running"

### Rule 2: Never use a long word where a short one will do.
**What it means:** "Utilize" → "Use." "Commence" → "Start." "Facilitate" → "Help." "Endeavor" → "Try." "Subsequently" → "Then." "Approximately" → "About."

**Bill Bonner at Agora Publishing** would physically strike out every big vocabulary word and replace it with a simple one. His reasoning: "No one says that in conversation." He helped build Agora to over $1 billion in revenue with this principle.

**Common offenders and their fixes:**
| Long Word | Short Replacement |
|-----------|------------------|
| Utilize | Use |
| Commence | Start, Begin |
| Facilitate | Help |
| Endeavor | Try |
| Subsequently | Then |
| Approximately | About |
| Implement | Do, Run |
| Demonstrate | Show |
| Acquire | Get |
| Sufficient | Enough |
| Terminate | End |
| Numerous | Many |
| Ascertain | Find out |
| Regarding | About |
| In order to | To |
| At this point in time | Now |
| In the event that | If |
| Due to the fact that | Because |

### Rule 3: If it is possible to cut a word out, always cut it out.
**What it means:** Every word must earn its place. If removing a word does not change the meaning or weaken the impact, remove it.

**Common words to cut:**
- "Very" (almost always unnecessary)
- "Really" (same)
- "Quite" (same)
- "Just" (usually filler)
- "Actually" (usually filler)
- "Basically" (always filler)
- "Literally" (unless literal)
- "In my opinion" (of course it is — you wrote it)
- "I think" (same)
- "Sort of" / "Kind of" (commit or don't)
- "That" (remove and re-read; if it still makes sense, leave it out)

**Example:**
- Before: "I really think that this is actually quite a very good opportunity for you."
- After: "This is a good opportunity for you."
- Even better: "This is the opportunity you've been waiting for."

### Rule 4: Never use the passive where you can use the active.
**What it means:** Active voice = the subject performs the action. Passive voice = the subject receives the action.

- Passive: "The report was written by the team."
- Active: "The team wrote the report."
- Passive: "Results will be delivered to your inbox."
- Active: "We deliver results to your inbox."

Active voice is more direct, more engaging, and more energetic. It puts the reader in the driver's seat.

### Rule 5: Never use a foreign phrase, a scientific word, or a jargon word if you can think of an everyday English equivalent.
**What it means:** Your reader should never need a dictionary, a Google search, or industry knowledge to understand your copy.

- "ROI" → "return on investment" → even better: "the money you get back"
- "Optimize" → "improve" or "make better"
- "Leverage" → "use" or "take advantage of"
- "Bandwidth" → "time" or "capacity"
- "Alignment" → "agreement"

**Exception:** If your audience IS the expert community and the jargon is their everyday language, use it. Camera spec enthusiasts want "ISO 6400" not "captures great photos in low light." Know your audience.

### Rule 6: Break any of these rules sooner than say anything outright barbarous.
**What it means:** Rules serve clarity and impact. If following a rule produces awkward, unnatural, or barbarous-sounding prose, break it. The rules exist for the reader's experience, not for their own sake.

## KURT VONNEGUT'S 8 RULES FOR WRITING

From "Bagombo Snuff Box" (1999). Vonnegut was a master of economy and engagement.

### Rule 1: Use the time of a total stranger in such a way that he or she will not feel the time was wasted.
**Copy application:** Every paragraph must deliver value. If the reader finishes your page feeling it was a waste, you have failed regardless of whether they bought. Respect their time by being useful, entertaining, or both.

### Rule 2: Give the reader at least one character to root for.
**Copy application:** In sales copy, the character to root for is the READER. They are the hero of the story. You are the guide. Make them feel like the protagonist of a transformation.

### Rule 3: Every character should want something, even if it is only a glass of water.
**Copy application:** State the reader's desire clearly and early. What do they want? What are they trying to achieve? Name it explicitly within the first 10% of the page.

### Rule 4: Every sentence must do one of two things — reveal character or advance the action.
**Copy application:** In copywriting, every sentence must do one of three things: advance the argument, deepen the emotion, or create curiosity. If it does none, delete it.

### Rule 5: Start as close to the end as possible.
**Copy application:** Do not build up slowly. Drop the reader into the middle of the action, the result, or the transformation. The backstory can come later if needed. Start with the payoff.

### Rule 6: Be a sadist. Make awful things happen to your characters so the reader can see what they are made of.
**Copy application:** Do not shy away from the pain. Agitate the problem. Make the reader feel the full weight of what happens if they do NOT act. Then offer the resolution.

### Rule 7: Write to please just one person.
**Copy application:** Write to your One Reader. Not the entire market. Not the broadest possible audience. One specific person with one specific problem seeking one specific result.

### Rule 8: Give your readers as much information as possible as soon as possible.
**Copy application:** Do not withhold critical information for the sake of mystery. Curiosity gaps are for the MECHANISM reveal, not for basic information the reader needs to understand what you are offering. Clarity always.

## DONALD MILLER'S RULES FOR WRITING

From "Building a StoryBrand" and his writing practice.

### Rule 1: Love your reader.
Think of writing as serving others and adding value to their lives. Every sentence should be written with the reader's benefit in mind, not your ego.

### Rule 2: Remove "that" from your writing.
Overuse of "that" makes writing sound weak and cluttered. Delete every "that" you can. Re-read the sentence — if it still makes sense without "that," leave it out.

- Before: "The system that we built that generates leads that convert..."
- After: "The system we built generates leads which convert..."

### Rule 3: Use verbs instead of adjectives.
Show, do not tell. But do not go too far with flowery descriptions either. The goal is action and clarity.

- Telling (adjectives): "The detective was dedicated."
- Showing (verbs): "The detective bit grooves into his eyeglass earpiece from thinking at crime scenes." (Michael Connelly)

### Rule 4: Test your ideas on social media before committing to long-form.
Use short posts to test headlines, hooks, and angles. Engagement data on a 280-character tweet can save you from writing a 3,000-word sales page nobody cares about.

### Rule 5: Keep yourself motivated.
Join writing groups. Surround yourself with encouraging people. Writing is a muscle that requires consistent exercise. The writers who produce the most also improve the fastest.

## BILL BONNER'S WRITING RULES (Agora Publishing)

Bill Bonner co-founded Agora, which grew to over $1 billion in revenue largely on the strength of direct response copy.

### Rule 1: Write in spoken language.
If you would not say it out loud in a conversation, do not write it. Read every sentence aloud. If it sounds stilted, rewrite it.

### Rule 2: Strike every big vocabulary word.
Replace with the simplest possible alternative. "Utilize" → "Use." Always.

### Rule 3: No one reads — they scan.
Format for scanning first. Short paragraphs, subheads, bold, bullets.

## EUGENE SCHWARTZ'S WRITING RULES

From "Breakthrough Advertising" and his personal writing practice.

### The 33-Minute Rule
Set a kitchen timer for 33 minutes and 33 seconds. Sit at your computer. You may only write or stare at the screen — no email, no browsing, no phone. When the timer goes off, take a break. Reset and repeat.

**Why it works:** It forces writing momentum. The hardest part is starting. Once you start, the words come. The timer removes the pressure of "write for hours" and replaces it with "just survive 33 minutes."

### The Slippery Slide
"The job of the headline is to make someone read the first line of the body copy. The job of the first line is to make someone read the second line. And so on."

Every element exists only to propel the reader to the next element.

## PRACTICAL WRITING HABITS FROM MASTERS

### Read Aloud (Mark Twain Method)
Mark Twain would read his work to his family, watching their reactions. Bored faces = cut that section. Excited faces = expand that section. Sandy Franks and Addison Wiggin at Agora sat together reading copy aloud, editing in real time.

**Application:** Read every piece of copy aloud before publishing. You will catch:
- Flawed transitions
- Tongue-twisters
- Unnatural rhythm
- Sentences that are too long
- Words that do not sound like YOU

### Write One Page a Day (John Steinbeck)
Do not focus on the end goal. Gain momentum through daily output. One page a day = 365 pages a year. That is more than most people write in a lifetime.

### Stop While Going Well (Ernest Hemingway)
Walk away when the writing is flowing. Come back fresh the next day. You will know exactly where to pick up, and the momentum carries forward.

### Copywork Practice
Rewrite good copy, novels, and poems by hand. This forces you to absorb the tone, rhythm, and technique at a subconscious level. Gary Halbert famously assigned this exercise to every student.

**How to do it:**
1. Find a piece of copy that converted well (check swiped.co for annotated examples)
2. Copy it by hand, word for word
3. As you write, notice: sentence length, word choice, transitions, rhythm
4. Do this for 30 minutes daily for 30 days
5. Your natural writing will improve dramatically

### Write Regularly, Inspiration Follows (Mario Vargas Llosa)
Do not wait for inspiration. Inspiration comes FROM the regular effort, not before it.

## THE MASTER RULES — SYNTHESIZED CHECKLIST

Before publishing any copy, check every line against:

- [ ] **Cliche-free?** No phrases you have seen a thousand times (Orwell Rule 1)
- [ ] **Simple words?** No long words where short ones work (Orwell Rule 2)
- [ ] **Tight?** Every word earns its place; nothing can be cut (Orwell Rule 3)
- [ ] **Active voice?** Subject performs the action (Orwell Rule 4)
- [ ] **Plain English?** No jargon or foreign phrases (Orwell Rule 5)
- [ ] **Advances the argument?** Every sentence moves toward the sale (Vonnegut Rule 4)
- [ ] **Starts close to the end?** No slow buildup (Vonnegut Rule 5)
- [ ] **One Reader?** Written for one specific person (Vonnegut Rule 7)
- [ ] **Spoken language?** You would say this out loud (Bonner)
- [ ] **Verbs over adjectives?** Showing, not telling (Miller Rule 3)
- [ ] **Read aloud?** Sounds natural and flows (Twain)
