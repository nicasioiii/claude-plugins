# Calls to Action, Closing & Conversion Elements

## Core Principle: CTAs Are Nudges, Not Magic Fixes

CTAs are the gateway from passive (reading) to active (doing). They should be a gentle nudge for someone already pretty much ready to act -- NOT a magic fix for weak copy. If your CTA is not converting, the problem is almost always upstream: weak proof, unclear benefits, or unhandled objections.

**Fundamental truth:** Even if your reader completely agrees with you, you are still asking them to make a change. Most people resist change even when beneficial. Your job: remove every obstacle between agreement and action.

## 1. CTA Copy Formula Toolkit

### Formula 1: The "I Want" Button
Write button copy from the visitor's perspective:
- "I want to [blank]"
- "I want you to [blank]"
- "Show me my [blank]"
- "Give me [blank]"

**Rule:** Keep first-person "me/my" in the copy. It feels awkward to write but tests well consistently.

### Formula 2: The "Get" Button
"Get" as the first word on a button is magic. Low friction, suggests good things coming:
- "Get my free guide"
- "Get instant access"
- "Get started now"
- "Get your copy"

### Formula 3: The RAD Formula
Before finalizing any CTA, check three things:
- **R -- Require:** Does the visitor have all the information needed to click?
- **A -- Acquire:** Is the button easy to find and click? (Size, placement, contrast)
- **D -- Desire:** Does the visitor want what the button promises?

If any answer is "no," fix that element before launching.

### Formula 4: CPSC Button Checklist
- **C -- Copy:** Is the text clear about what happens next?
- **P -- Placement:** Is the button where eyes naturally go?
- **S -- Size:** Is it large enough to be an easy-to-acquire target?
- **C -- Color:** Does it contrast with the surrounding page?

### Formula 5: Hook-Line-Sinker
Three components in one CTA:
**Command verb + Offer + Urgency**
- "Download our whitepaper today"
- "Start your free trial before midnight"
- "Claim your spot -- only 12 left"

### Formula 6: Value-First CTA
Instead of action words, lead with value:
- "Generate more leads" beats "Download ebook"
- "Start saving 8 hours a week" beats "Sign up"
- "See your personalized plan" beats "Submit"

**Exception:** When the prospect is very hot (most aware, high intent), stick to direct action words: "Buy now," "Add to cart," "Complete purchase."

## 2. Button Design Principles

### Contrast Is King
There is no universally best button color. The rule is contrast -- the button must visually pop against its surrounding elements. Orange on a blue page. Green on a white page. Whatever creates maximum contrast.

### Size Matters
Easy-to-acquire targets convert better. Make buttons slightly larger than feels natural. A bigger button is almost never a mistake. A too-small button always is.

### Reassuring Text Below Buttons
Reduce anxiety with micro-copy directly below the CTA button:
- "Next: tell us where to ship it" (tells them what happens)
- "No credit card required" (removes risk)
- "Cancel anytime" (reduces commitment fear)
- "Takes less than 60 seconds" (reduces effort perception)
- "No salesperson will call" (removes social pressure)
- "100% money-back guarantee" (removes financial risk)

**Rule:** Reassuring text should never suggest work is about to begin. "Next: fill out a quick form" is bad. "Next: see your results" is good.

### Button Punctuation
Never put a period on headlines or CTA buttons. Periods are stop signals. You want forward momentum.

## 3. CTA Placement Strategy

### By Page Type

| Page Type | CTA Placement Strategy |
|-----------|----------------------|
| **Landing page** | First CTA above the fold, second after social proof, third after offer details, final after FAQ |
| **Homepage** | Primary CTA above the fold, secondary ghost button for alternative path, bottom CTA restating primary action |
| **Sales page** | After lead section, after marketing argument, after offer presentation, after guarantee, after P.S. |
| **Email** | One primary CTA, link 2-3 times (top, middle, end), always link the P.S. |

### The Contrarian Hero Section Rule
Hero sections do NOT always need a CTA button. Sometimes the hero's only job is to grab attention and pull people down the page. Forcing a button there can hurt if the visitor is not ready to act (problem-aware or unaware visitors).

**Test:** Try removing the hero CTA on pages targeting problem-aware audiences. If scroll depth increases without harming conversions, keep it removed.

### Multiple CTAs, One Action
You can place the same CTA multiple times on a page, but every CTA should drive the SAME action. No backup offers. No timid second option as a text link under the button. One page, one offer, one action.

## 4. The Close: Three-Part Closing Process

Every offer requires a close -- even free ebooks. The close is where you ask for the sale directly.

### Part 1: Re-Summarize
Recap the key benefits and what they are getting. Use bullet points for scanners:
- "Here's everything you're getting today:"
- Bullet 1: Main product + key benefit
- Bullet 2: Bonus 1 + its value
- Bullet 3: Bonus 2 + its value
- Bullet 4: Guarantee statement
- "Total value: $X,XXX -- Yours today for just $XXX"

### Part 2: The Closing Formula
Use one of these closing structures:

**Elements-of-the-Offer Close (Copy Hackers):**
1. Here is what you are going to get
2. Establish the value
3. Offer a (conditional) bonus
4. State the price
5. Trivialize the price
6. State the guarantee
7. Reverse the risk
8. Add scarcity

**Consequences-of-Not-Acting Close:**
After the final CTA, add a section answering: "What is the consequence of NOT acting today?" Paint the picture of continued struggle, missed opportunity, or ongoing loss.

**The Takeaway Close (Todd Brown):**
- "This is not for you if..."
- "You should pass on this offer, unless you..."
- "...if you qualify"
Creates scarcity and exclusivity by reversing the dynamic. The seller becomes the gatekeeper.

### Part 3: First-Person CTA
End with a CTA button in first person:
- "Yes, I want [specific result]"
- "Give me instant access"
- "Start my transformation today"

## 5. The Re-Close and P.S.

### The Re-Close (Adil Amarsi)
After the main close, add a second closing section for those who scrolled past:
- Restate the primary promise in different words
- Add one more testimonial (strongest available)
- Restate the guarantee
- Final CTA button

### P.S. Formulas
The P.S. is one of the most-read parts of any sales letter or email.

**P.S. Formula 1: Restate the offer**
"P.S. Remember, when you grab [product] today, you get [key items]. And your investment is protected by our [guarantee]. Click here to get started."

**P.S. Formula 2: Add urgency**
"P.S. This offer expires at midnight on [date]. After that, the price goes up to $X. Don't wait."

**P.S. Formula 3: Address the #1 objection**
"P.S. If you're wondering whether this will work for [specific concern], here's what [Name] said: [testimonial addressing that concern]."

**P.S. Formula 4: Future pace**
"P.S. Imagine where you'll be 90 days from now. [Vivid description of after state.] It starts with this one decision."

## 6. Nonchalant Selling

The invitational frame: instead of hard-selling, invite. This works especially well for sophisticated audiences who resist traditional sales pressure.

**Nonchalant language patterns:**
- "If this sounds like something you'd find useful..."
- "Whenever you're ready..."
- "No rush -- this will be here when you need it"
- "Take a look and see if it's right for you"

**When to use nonchalant selling:**
- Sophisticated markets (stage 4-5)
- High-trust audiences (existing customers, warm lists)
- Premium products where exclusivity matters
- Audiences that react negatively to urgency tactics

**When NOT to use it:**
- Cold traffic from paid ads
- Time-sensitive promotions with real deadlines
- Low-price impulse purchases

## 7. Attention Management Rules

### Single Focus
- NO global navigation on landing pages (distracting links lead prospects astray)
- Nothing on the page that is not directly tied to the conversion goal
- One page, one offer, one CTA action

### Decision Management
Fewer choices = happier customers and more sales.

**The Iyengar Jam Study:** 6 jams = 30% purchase rate. 24 jams = 3% purchase rate. Fewer options always wins.

**The Rule of Two (Todd Brown):** Present exactly two choices, even if one is "No, I'm not interested in [benefit]." A conscious decision is always better than an unconscious page close.

**Practical rules:**
- No more than 3 pricing tiers
- Copy blocks should look distinct enough not to blur together
- Group related information so visitors can focus on what matters
- Pre-select the recommended option

## 8. Homepage CTA Hierarchy

### Don't Propose Marriage on the First Date
Homepage visitors are often early in the awareness journey. Don't ask for the sale immediately.

**CTA hierarchy by visitor awareness:**
- **Unaware/Problem-aware:** Soft CTA -- "Learn more," "See how it works," free resource
- **Solution-aware:** Medium CTA -- "See plans," "Request demo," "Start free trial"
- **Product-aware:** Direct CTA -- "Get started," "Buy now," "Add to cart"
- **Most-aware:** Deal CTA -- Price, discount, "Upgrade now"

### Downsell CTA Concept
Offer a secondary, lower-commitment CTA for those not ready for the primary action:
- Primary: "Start your free trial"
- Downsell (ghost button): "Watch a 2-minute demo"

**Three incentive types for CTAs (Ryan Deiss):**
1. **Moral** -- "Help others by joining..."
2. **Social** -- "Your peers are already using..."
3. **Economic** -- "Save $X by acting today..."

## 9. Checkout and Order Form Optimization

### Strip to Skeleton (Todd Brown)
When click-through from the sales page is strong but order form abandonment is high:
1. Strip the order form to bare minimum: product name, price, payment fields only
2. Remove ALL extra elements (testimonials, bonus reminders, additional offers)
3. Test skeleton vs. current form
4. If skeleton wins, add back one element at a time and test each

**Why this works:** Extra elements on the order form can TALK PEOPLE OUT of the sale. Like a car salesman handing you a brochure after you already decided to buy.

### Gumroad Case Study: 20% Lift
Five changes that produced a 20% lift in paid conversions:
1. Thick glowing border around primary CTA button
2. Changed "Estimate Shipping" button to "Fast, affordable shipping" (verb-free)
3. Button copy in first person: "I'm ready to check out"
4. Made button slightly bigger (easy-to-acquire target)
5. Added testimonial click triggers near the button

### Coupon Code Fields
Coupon code fields INCREASE cart abandonment. Visitors see the empty field, think "I should find a coupon first," leave to search, and never return. If you do not actively promote coupons, hide or remove the field entirely.

## 10. DO/DON'T Button Word Lists

### DO Use These Words on Buttons
- Get, Start, Claim, Join, Discover, See, Try, Access, Unlock, Save, Create, Build, Download, Watch, Learn, Show me, Give me, Yes

### DON'T Use These Words on Buttons
- Submit, Enter, Click here, Continue, Proceed, Register, Sign up (alone without benefit), Buy (for cold traffic), Order (sounds like work), Pay
