# Customer Avatar & Audience Research

## Why Avatar Work Comes First

Most copywriters skip research and jump to writing. This is why their copy fails. You cannot write compelling copy for people you don't understand. Avatar work isn't a nice-to-have—it's the foundation everything else rests on.

**The principle:** Understanding your reader's world, problems, and desires determines whether your copy lands or misses.

---

## The Three-Layer Research Framework

Avatar research has three distinct layers. Each layer goes deeper.

### Layer 1: Demographics (The Surface)
*What can you observe about them?*

- **Age range** (e.g., 28-42)
- **Role/Title** (e.g., marketing manager, solopreneur, stay-at-home parent)
- **Income level** (relevant? price sensitivity)
- **Education** (impacts language complexity)
- **Location** (urban/rural, influences context)
- **Industry** (if B2B)

**Exercise (5 minutes):** Write down 4-5 demographic facts about your ideal customer. Don't overthink—surface level is fine here.

**Key insight:** Demographics alone tell you almost nothing about motivation. Move to Layer 2.

---

### Layer 2: Frustrations & Fears (The Moving Away)
*What problems are they experiencing right now?*

This layer captures the pain they want to move away from.

**Categories to explore:**

1. **Daily frustrations** — What annoys them regularly?
   - Example: "Spending 3 hours daily on email vs. actual work"
   - Example: "Constantly switching between tools"

2. **Unfulfilled needs** — What do they lack?
   - Example: "No time for exercise"
   - Example: "Lack of technical knowledge"

3. **Recurring failures** — What systems keep breaking?
   - Example: "CRM tool always glitches during key sales moments"
   - Example: "Diets always fail by week 3"

4. **Systemic problems** — What's broken about their current solution?
   - Example: "Our current platform charges per user, killing budget flexibility"
   - Example: "Existing coaches don't understand our market"

5. **Emotional costs** — How do these problems make them feel?
   - Not "sad" or "frustrated" (too generic)
   - Specific: "Embarrassed when presenting unpolished reports"
   - Specific: "Anxious about missing deadlines"
   - Specific: "Resentful toward teammates who don't carry weight"

**Exercise (5-10 minutes):** Use post-it notes. Write one frustration per post-it. Aim for 10-15. Don't edit—just brain dump.

**Key insight:** You'll use these exact frustrations in your headlines, problem sections, and benefit language.

---

### Layer 3: Aspirations & Desires (The Moving Toward)
*What do they want for themselves?*

This layer captures what they're reaching for.

**Categories to explore:**

1. **Aspirational identity** — Who do they want to become?
   - Example: "A trusted authority in their field"
   - Example: "A parent who's present, not stressed"
   - Example: "Someone who's mastered their craft"

2. **Concrete goals** — What do they want to achieve?
   - Example: "Launch a course within 6 months"
   - Example: "Increase revenue 50% YoY"
   - Example: "Run a 5K without stopping"

3. **Lifestyle desires** — How do they want their life to feel?
   - Example: "Freedom to work from anywhere"
   - Example: "Time to pursue creative interests"
   - Example: "Predictable, peaceful mornings"

4. **Status/Recognition** — What do they want to be recognized for?
   - Example: "Known as the go-to expert"
   - Example: "Appreciated by their team"
   - Example: "Able to provide for family"

5. **Secret desires** — What do they rarely admit wanting?
   - Example: "Prove doubters wrong"
   - Example: "Make more than their boss"
   - Example: "Be the most knowledgeable person in the room"

**Exercise (5-10 minutes):** Go deeper. These aren't surface desires. Ask "Why?" for each goal. A goal of "more income" becomes "I want to prove I made the right career choice" or "I want to stop worrying about bills."

**Key insight:** Aspirations are THE most powerful element in copy. Changing how someone sees themselves (identity) is more powerful than just giving them a capability.

## The Before-After Grid (Most Valuable Exercise)

Spend 1-2 hours on this. It's the single most useful document for copy development.

### How to Fill It

**Rows (5 dimensions):**

1. **HAVE** — What tangible things, tools, or resources?
   - Before: "Excel spreadsheets for everything"
   - After: "Integrated dashboard with real-time data"

2. **FEEL** — Specific emotional state (not generic)
   - Before: "Overwhelmed by competing priorities"
   - After: "Calm knowing what matters most"

3. **AVERAGE DAY** — What's one day like?
   - Before: "Wakes up checking email, worries all day about forgotten tasks, ends day stressed"
   - After: "Wakes up with clarity, focuses on high-impact work, ends day satisfied"

4. **STATUS** — How does their standing change?
   - Before: "Seen as reactive, always putting out fires"
   - After: "Seen as strategic, thinking ahead"

5. **GOOD VS EVIL** — What philosophical battle are they waging?
   - Before: "Fighting between ambition and burnout"
   - After: "Living the balance they've always wanted"

**Most missed element:** Status/identity change. This is where the deepest motivation lives.

### Template Format

```
| Dimension | BEFORE | AFTER |
|-----------|--------|-------|
| HAVE | [tools, resources, situation] | [what changes] |
| FEEL | [specific emotions] | [emotional shift] |
| AVERAGE DAY | [daily experience] | [how it changes] |
| STATUS | [how others see them] | [new perception] |
| GOOD VS EVIL | [internal battle] | [resolution] |
```

### Why This Matters for Copy

Each row becomes a different angle in your copy. Your headline might focus on FEEL ("Calm mornings instead of chaos"). Your benefits might paint the AVERAGE DAY. Your opening story might show the STATUS change.

## Audience Awareness Lifecycle

Your customer exists somewhere on a spectrum from completely unaware to hyper-aware of their problem and your solution.

### Five Awareness Stages

**1. UNAWARE**
- Don't know they have a problem
- Example: Someone in an inefficient business process who doesn't realize better exists
- Your job: Show the problem exists
- Tone: Educational, almost journalistic

**2. PROBLEM AWARE**
- Know they have a problem
- Don't know solutions exist
- Example: "I'm struggling with productivity but have no idea what to do"
- Your job: Show that solutions exist, validate their pain
- Tone: Hopeful, empathetic
- Headline approach: "This might be your issue..."

**3. SOLUTION AWARE**
- Know solutions exist
- Don't know yours specifically
- Know about competitors
- Example: "I know productivity software exists, but there are 100 options"
- Your job: Differentiate why yours is better/different
- Tone: Clear, specific to why you're superior
- Headline approach: "The X way to solve productivity..."

**4. PRODUCT AWARE**
- Know YOUR product exists
- Deciding if right for them
- Example: "I've heard of your tool, now I need to know if it's for me"
- Your job: Show how it works, if it matches their specific situation
- Tone: Detailed, specific to their use case
- Headline approach: Product demo, features focus

**5. MOST AWARE**
- Already decided they want it
- Debating price/timing
- Example: "I want this, when can I get it?"
- Your job: Remove final friction, justify price
- Tone: Friendly, professional
- Headline approach: Rarely needed, mostly product details

### Why This Matters

Copy for a PROBLEM AWARE audience is completely different from copy for a SOLUTION AWARE audience.

**Ryan Deiss insight:** Most businesses target the wrong awareness level. They admire big brands (Most Aware) and try to write like them. But their audience is Problem Aware.

- Apple in "Most Aware" mode: "Where work happens" (assumes you know MacBooks)
- Apple when Problem Aware: "Your computer keeps crashing. Here's why..." (explains the problem)

## How to Research Your Avatar (Beyond Guessing)

### Method 1: Customer Interviews (Best)

Conduct 5-10 conversations with actual customers (or ideal customers).

**Interview framework (20-30 minutes):**

1. **Opening** — "Tell me about your [area of problem]"
2. **Problem depth** — "What's the hardest part about that?"
3. **Current solution** — "How are you handling that now?"
4. **Frustrations** — "What's frustrating about your current approach?"
5. **Aspirations** — "What would ideal look like?"
6. **Hesitations** — "What made you hesitant before buying?"
7. **Language** — "How would you describe this to a friend?"

**Gold nuggets to listen for:**
- Exact phrases they use (steal these for copy)
- Emotional language (how they FEEL about problems)
- Stories they tell (use in your own origin story)
- Objections they mention (address preemptively in copy)

### Method 2: Community Research

Join communities where your ideal customer hangs out.

**Places to look:**
- Reddit (subreddits for your niche)
- Facebook Groups (interest-specific, industry-specific)
- Industry forums
- Slack communities
- LinkedIn groups

**What to extract:**
- Common complaints (most-upvoted complaints)
- Language they use (exact words, phrases, slang)
- Fears they express
- Aspirations mentioned
- Objections raised
- Quick wins they celebrate

**Process:** Read 20-30 posts without posting. Just listen. Document themes.

### Method 3: Competitive Research

Study how competitors describe your avatar.

- What customer challenges do competitor websites highlight?
- What language do they use?
- What before-after scenarios do they paint?
- What objections do they address?

**Note:** You're not copying them—you're understanding the conversation already happening in your market.

## Avatar Anti-Patterns (What NOT to Do)

**Don't:** Create a fictional person with a name and photo
- This is a persona, not useful research
- You'll start writing for the name, not the actual customer

**Don't:** Use only demographics
- You'll write generic copy that applies to thousands of people
- Demographics alone reveal nothing about motivation

**Don't:** Assume you know your customer
- "I think my customer wants X"
- You're guessing. Guessing = wasted copy

**Don't:** Create one "average" customer
- If you have multiple audience segments, they need separate copies
- The Rule of One: One customer, one message, one offer

**Don't:** Skip the emotional layer
- "They want faster software" (feature focus)
- "They want to stop feeling like they're always behind" (emotional/aspirational)
- Emotion sells, logic justifies

## Quick Avatar Brief (If You're Short on Time)

If you can't do the full framework, do this minimal version:

1. **Who is one ideal customer?** (Name them, make them real)
2. **What's their biggest frustration?** (The pain that wakes them at 3am)
3. **What do they aspire to?** (Identity shift, not just capability)
4. **What language do they use?** (How do they describe their problem?)
5. **What objections might they have?** (What's their hesitation?)

This 5-point brief is better than nothing. But the full framework is worth the time investment.
