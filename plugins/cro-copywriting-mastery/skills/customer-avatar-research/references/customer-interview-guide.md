# Customer Interview Guide

## Why Interviews Matter

Five customer interviews will teach you more about your market than six months of guessing. You'll discover language, objections, and aspirations you never would have invented.

Real customer words are copywriting gold.

---

## Pre-Interview Setup

### Recruit the Right People

**Ideal interview subjects:**
- Existing customers (they solved the problem already)
- People you know (easier, more honest)
- People in your target market (even if not customers yet)

**Who to contact:**
- Email recent customers: "I'm doing customer research, would you spend 20 minutes on a call?"
- Offer a small incentive if needed ($25 gift card, discount, etc.)
- Aim for 5-10 interviews

**Screening question (before interview):**
"Can you briefly tell me about your [area of problem]? I want to make sure this is relevant to what we're studying."

This ensures you get people actually dealing with the problem.

### Prepare the Environment

- **One-on-one calls** (video or phone, not group)
- **20-30 minutes** (respect their time)
- **Record it** (get permission first: "Mind if I record so I can focus on our conversation?")
- **Quiet location** (no dogs barking, office noise)
- **No script** (use questions as guide, not rigid script)

---

## The Interview Framework

This structure takes someone from broad problem → specific objections → language. It's designed to go deep without feeling like an interrogation.

### Opening (2-3 minutes)

**Tone:** Friendly, conversational, not salesy

"Thanks for taking time for this. I'm researching how people [in your field] handle [problem area]. I want to understand the real challenges you face. There are no right answers—I just want to hear your honest perspective. Cool?"

**Why this opening:**
- Sets expectation (you're learning, not selling)
- Gives permission to be honest
- Feels like a conversation, not a survey

---

### Section 1: Problem Discovery (5-7 minutes)

Start broad, then get specific.

**Question 1: "Tell me about your [area of problem]"**

Example questions:
- "Tell me about your productivity challenges"
- "How do you currently manage your email?"
- "Walk me through how your team handles customer communication"

**Listen for:**
- What comes out naturally (not what you expect)
- Specific examples they mention
- Frustration level in their voice
- Pain points they mention without prompting

**If they're vague, ask:** "Can you give me a specific example of that?"

---

**Question 2: "What's the hardest part about [their problem]?"**

This pinpoints the core frustration, not surface-level annoyance.

Example:
- "What's the hardest part about managing those 200 daily emails?"
- Response: "It's not the volume, it's not knowing which ones need my attention vs. which I can delegate"

**Insight:** The real problem (not knowing what to prioritize) is different from the surface complaint (too many emails).

**Listen for:**
- Core frustration vs. surface symptom
- What wakes them up at night
- What costs them the most (time, money, reputation)

---

**Question 3: "How long has this been an issue?"**

This tells you if it's chronic frustration or recent problem.

Chronic = higher motivation to solve.

---

### Section 2: Current Solutions & Frustrations (5-7 minutes)

**Question 4: "How are you handling that right now?"**

They'll describe their current system (manual, another tool, workaround, nothing).

**Listen for:**
- Workarounds they've created
- Tools they're using (competitive intel)
- How much manual work it requires
- Where the current solution breaks down

**Follow-up:** "What's frustrating about that approach?"

This is where you'll hear the strongest objections. They're living with a solution they hate.

---

**Question 5: "Have you tried other ways to solve this?"**

Get them to talk about what didn't work.

- "Have you tried [competitor]?"
- "Have you tried [obvious workaround]?"

**Insight:** Understanding why they rejected solutions tells you what objections you must address in your copy.

Example: If they say "We tried Slack but it became a black hole of information," now you know: speed of finding info matters to them. A solution must solve "quick retrieval," not just "unified communication."

---

### Section 3: Aspirations & Impact (4-5 minutes)

**Question 6: "What would ideal look like?"**

This isn't asking for features. It's asking for their vision of solved.

- "If this problem was completely solved, what would be different?"
- "What would your day look like?"
- "How would you feel different?"

**Listen for:**
- Emotional language (how they feel, not just what they do)
- Identity shift (who they become: "I'd feel like I actually have control")
- Status change (how others perceive them: "My team would respect my leadership")
- Time reclaimed (what they'd do instead)

**Gold:** "I'd have time to actually think strategically instead of just reacting" = they want to be seen as strategic, not reactive.

---

**Question 7: "What would change for you?"**

Get specific about the transformation.

- Before state: "Chaotic, always firefighting"
- After state: "Peaceful, planning ahead"

This becomes your before-after narrative in copy.

---

### Section 4: Hesitations & Objections (3-5 minutes)

**Question 8: "Before you tried [solution], what made you hesitant?"**

Or if they haven't tried anything: "What would hold you back from trying a solution like [your product type]?"

**Listen for:**
- Fear about change
- Trust issues
- Cost objections
- Time investment concerns
- Skepticism (("another tool that won't solve it")

These objections MUST be addressed in your copy.

---

**Question 9: "What would need to be true for you to solve this?"**

This asks: What's the minimum bar for you to say yes?

- "The solution would need to integrate with X"
- "It would need to cost less than $Y"
- "It would need to work without training"

These are your deal-breakers and key positioning points.

---

### Section 5: Language & Closing (3-4 minutes)

**Question 10: "How would you describe this problem to a friend?"**

Listen to the exact words they use. Steal this language for your copy.

- If they say "productivity black hole," use that phrase
- If they say "email vortex," use that phrase
- If they say "constant chaos," use that phrase

This is how your customer talks about the problem. This is how your copy should talk about it.

---

**Question 11: "What would I be missing if I didn't talk to customers like you?"**

Open-ended invitation for anything else they want to share.

Often reveals unexpected insights.

---

**Closing:** "This was incredibly helpful. Is it okay if I reach out if I have a follow-up question?"

Leaves door open for quick clarifications.

## After the Interview: Extraction Process

### Immediate (while fresh)

**Within 2 hours of interview, document:**

1. **Top 3 frustrations they mentioned**
2. **How they described the problem** (exact words)
3. **One specific story they told**
4. **Their aspiration** (what would solved look like)
5. **One objection they expressed**

Don't try to transcribe everything. Extract the key insights.

### Pattern Recognition (after 5+ interviews)

After 5-10 interviews, look for patterns:

- **Common frustrations** (mentioned by 3+ people)
- **Repeated language** (words used by multiple people)
- **Consistent objections** (same hesitations across people)
- **Universal aspirations** (what most people want)

These patterns become:
- Headline angles (based on common frustrations)
- Body copy language (use their words)
- Objection section (address repeated concerns)
- Benefits (based on aspirations)

### Interview Notes Template

```
Customer Name: [Name]
Date: [Date]
Industry/Role: [Their situation]

TOP FRUSTRATION:
[The #1 pain point they mentioned]

CURRENT SOLUTION:
[How they're currently handling it]

OBJECTIONS:
[What makes them hesitant to change]

LANGUAGE THEY USED:
[Exact phrases: "black hole," "chaos," etc.]

ASPIRATION:
[What solved looks like for them]

STORY THEY TOLD:
[One specific example that stuck with you]

DEAL-BREAKER/REQUIREMENT:
[Something that's non-negotiable for them]
```

Keep these templates. Refer back to them when writing copy.

## Interview Tips & Tricks

### Avoid These Mistakes

**Don't pitch during the interview**
- You're researching, not selling
- If you sell, they'll tell you what they think you want to hear
- Save pitch for after they understand

**Don't ask leading questions**
- Bad: "Don't you hate how slow email is?"
- Good: "How do you feel about email as a tool?"
- Leading questions get agreement, not truth

**Don't interrupt**
- Let them finish, even if they go off on tangents
- The tangents often reveal unexpected insights
- Comfortable silences are okay (let them think)

**Don't make it about your solution**
- You're asking about their world, not your product
- "How might you solve this?" not "Would you use X?"
- They'll tell you what they need

### Get Them Talking

**Techniques that work:**

1. **Specific, not general**
   - "Tell me about the last time this happened"
   - Not: "Do you experience this often?"

2. **Examples**
   - "Can you walk me through how you handle this?"
   - Not: "Is this difficult?"

3. **Stories**
   - "Tell me a story about when this went wrong"
   - Not: "What problems have you had?"

4. **Emotional**
   - "How did that make you feel?"
   - Not: "Was that bad?"

5. **Curiosity**
   - "That's interesting, tell me more about that"
   - Not: "Okay, moving on..."

## What You'll Get From 5-10 Interviews

1. **Validated frustrations** — Your assumptions confirmed (or shattered)
2. **Real language** — Exact words your customer uses (for copy)
3. **Objection roadmap** — What you must address before they buy
4. **Aspiration insights** — What transformation looks like to them
5. **Stories** — Real examples you can use in your origin story or social proof
6. **Confidence** — You're no longer guessing

This is why interviews are worth the effort.

---

## Follow-Up: From Interviews to Copy

Once you've done interviews, you have everything you need for compelling copy:

- **Headlines:** Based on their #1 frustration
- **Problem section:** Using their language
- **Objection section:** Addressing what they hesitated about
- **Benefits:** Painting their aspiration
- **Social proof:** Using their language and transformation

You've interviewed them. Now write to them.
