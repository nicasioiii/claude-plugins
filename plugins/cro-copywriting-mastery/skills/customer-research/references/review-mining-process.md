# Review Mining Process -- Complete Guide

## Why Review Mining Works

When you mirror prospects' own words on the page, they see themselves and connect. Review mining is the fastest way to gather authentic, emotionally charged customer language without scheduling a single interview.

Proven results from review mining:
- "If you think you need rehab, you do" (Amazon book review) = 20%+ more leads for a rehab center = tens of thousands of dollars monthly
- "Big bum, thick waist, not super perky boobs" (forum language) = 125% more clicks to start a trial
- "Eliminate up to 99% of paper" (customer testimonial) = winning headline when all others failed

## THE 7-STEP REVIEW MINING PROCESS

### Step 1: Identify Your Sources (30 minutes)

Build a source list of 5-10 places where your prospects and competitors' customers leave reviews, comments, and feedback.

**Universal Sources:**
- Amazon book reviews (search for books in your niche -- 3-5 star reviews are most useful)
- Reddit threads in relevant subreddits
- Quora answers on relevant questions
- Facebook groups in your niche
- YouTube video comments on competitor/niche content

**B2C Sources:**
- Yelp reviews of competitors
- TripAdvisor (travel/hospitality)
- Google Business reviews
- App Store / Play Store reviews
- Trustpilot

**B2B Sources:**
- G2 reviews of competitors
- Capterra reviews
- TrustRadius
- LinkedIn posts and comments in industry groups
- Industry-specific forums

**Niche Sources:**
- Specialized forums (bodybuilding.com for fitness, Bogleheads for finance)
- Niche subreddits
- Stack Overflow / Stack Exchange (technical products)
- Course review sites (for education products)
- Indie Hackers (for SaaS/tech entrepreneurship)

### Step 2: Start With Amazon Book Reviews (1-2 hours)

Amazon book reviews are the single richest source because people write long, detailed reviews explaining their problems, what they tried, what worked, and how they feel.

**Process:**
1. Search Amazon for 3-5 top-selling books in your niche
2. Read the 3-star, 4-star, and 5-star reviews (1-star and 2-star are usually about shipping/format issues)
3. Look specifically for reviews that describe:
   - The problem the reader was experiencing before buying
   - What they tried that did not work
   - The specific language they use to describe their frustration
   - What result or change they experienced
   - What surprised them

**Example search terms:**
- For a fitness product: Search "weight loss," "home workout," "meal prep" books
- For a SaaS tool: Search "project management," "productivity," "team communication" books
- For a coaching offer: Search "business growth," "freelancing," "career change" books

### Step 3: Mine Competitor Reviews (1-2 hours)

Read 50-100 reviews of your top 3 competitors. Focus on:

**What to extract from POSITIVE reviews:**
- Language describing the transformation
- Specific results mentioned (numbers, timelines)
- Emotional language ("finally," "at last," "I can't believe")
- Unexpected benefits the customer discovered
- How they describe the product to others

**What to extract from NEGATIVE reviews:**
- Unmet expectations (these are objections you must address)
- Missing features or capabilities
- Frustrations with the experience
- What they wished the product did differently
- Language expressing disappointment or anger

### Step 4: Mine Forums and Reddit (1-2 hours)

Forums and Reddit threads contain the rawest, most unfiltered customer language because people are talking to peers, not brands.

**Search strategy:**
- Reddit: Search `site:reddit.com [your niche] + [problem keywords]`
- Look for threads with 50+ comments (more data to mine)
- Focus on "help me" threads, "what do you recommend" threads, and "I tried X and it didn't work" threads

**What to look for:**
- How they describe the problem in their own words
- What solutions they have already tried (and why they failed)
- What they are specifically asking for help with
- Emotional language embedded in their posts
- Recurring themes across multiple threads

### Step 5: Document Verbatim (Critical)

**THE GOLDEN RULE: Listen WITHOUT interpreting or summarizing. Document verbatim.**

Create a spreadsheet with these columns:
| Source | Verbatim Quote | Category | Emotional Intensity (1-5) | Headline Candidate? |

**Categories:**
- **Pain Language:** How they describe the problem and its effects
- **Desire Language:** How they describe what they want
- **Objection Language:** Concerns, skepticism, reasons not to buy
- **Transformation Language:** How they describe the change after using a solution
- **Trigger Language:** What made them start looking for a solution
- **Identity Language:** How they describe themselves or who they want to become
- **Competitor Language:** How they describe competing products

### Step 6: Identify Patterns (30 minutes)

After documenting 75-150 verbatim quotes:

1. Sort by category
2. Look for phrases or themes that appear 3+ times across different sources
3. Rank by emotional intensity
4. Highlight the top 5 phrases per category
5. Note any surprising or counterintuitive findings

**Frequency signals priority:**
- A phrase appearing in 5+ reviews across multiple sources = primary messaging candidate
- A phrase appearing in 3-4 reviews = secondary messaging candidate
- A unique but intensely emotional phrase appearing once = potential headline hook

### Step 7: Map to Copy Elements (30 minutes)

| Copy Element | Source Category | How to Use |
|-------------|----------------|------------|
| **Headlines** | Pain Language + Desire Language | Use the most vivid, specific phrases as headline starters |
| **Subheads** | Transformation Language | Contrast before/after using their words |
| **Opening hook** | Trigger Language | Start copy with the moment they started looking |
| **Objection handling** | Objection Language | Address their exact concerns in their exact words |
| **Social proof** | Transformation Language | Match testimonials to the transformations they describe |
| **CTA framing** | Desire Language | Frame the action in terms of what they want |
| **Guarantee** | Objection Language | Reverse their specific fears |

## REVIEW MINING TEMPLATES

### Quick-Mine Template (2 Hours)

For when you need fast results:

1. Pick ONE Amazon book + ONE competitor review source + ONE Reddit thread
2. Read 30 reviews from each (90 total)
3. Document the top 20 verbatim phrases
4. Map to headlines, objections, and transformation copy

### Deep-Mine Template (6-8 Hours)

For a thorough research round:

1. Identify 8-10 sources across all categories above
2. Read 200+ reviews total
3. Document 100+ verbatim phrases
4. Categorize, pattern-match, and rank
5. Produce a full copy brief with messaging hierarchy

### Ongoing Mining Habit

Set a recurring weekly task:
- Spend 30 minutes reading new reviews, forum posts, and Reddit threads
- Add 5-10 new phrases to your running swipe file
- Flag anything that changes or contradicts your existing messaging assumptions
- VOC data has a ~2-year shelf life -- refresh regularly

## COMPETITOR CONTENT AUDIT

Before mining individual reviews, audit the competitor's overall messaging:

1. **Visit their homepage.** Write down their headline, subhead, and primary CTA.
2. **Visit their sales page.** Write down their primary promise, unique mechanism, and proof elements.
3. **Read their ads.** Use Facebook Ad Library to see their current campaigns.
4. **Subscribe to their email list.** Document their welcome sequence messaging.
5. **Note what they do NOT address.** Gaps in their messaging = your opportunity to differentiate.

The competitor content audit tells you what the market has already heard. Review mining tells you how customers actually respond to it.

## ADVANCED TECHNIQUES

### The Contrast Mine

When you find a positive and a negative review on the SAME platform about the SAME product, you have a natural before/after contrast. Document both side by side.

### The Language Escalation Mine

Look for reviews where the person's language escalates in emotional intensity. The most intense moment often contains the most persuasive copy language.

**Example escalation:**
"I was frustrated" (moderate) > "I was at my wit's end" (strong) > "I was ready to throw my laptop out the window" (vivid, copy-ready)

Use the most vivid version.

### The Unexpected Benefit Mine

Look specifically for reviews where someone mentions a benefit they did NOT expect. Unexpected benefits are often the most compelling in copy because they feel authentic and surprising.

**Example:** "I bought this for the workout plans but honestly the biggest change was my energy levels at work. I'm more productive than I've been in years."

The unexpected benefit (productivity) may be a stronger hook than the expected benefit (fitness).
