# 7-Email Welcome Sequence — Complete Templates

Both Long and Short versions follow the same structural arc. The Long Version is more personal and story-driven. The Short Version is more direct and case-study focused. Choose based on your brand voice and audience.

---

## EMAIL 1: THE WELCOME EMAIL (Both Versions)

**Purpose:** Set the tone of the entire relationship. This is the most powerful and important email in the sequence.

### Template Structure
```
Subject: [Pop culture reference / welcome theme]
Preview: [What they opted in for]

[Headline delivering what they opted in for]

[Greeting]

[Introduction to you — who you are, personality]

[Numbered list of 5-6 points/instructions:]
  1. Something personal about you (interests, quirks)
  2. What you'll be sending them (frequency, content types)
  3. Ask them to reply + whitelist your email
  4. Ask them to create a dedicated folder for your emails
  5. Ask them to set an automated rule to move emails there
  6. Invite audience participation / questions

[Remind them of why they opted in]

[Be truthful that you will sell them stuff — give honest reason]

[Reference your offer / product / service]

[Give them the free guide/download again]

[Sign off]

P.S. — Recap the key action items
```

### Critical Rules
- Links go to TWO places only: your offer OR your download
- If you do NOT have an offer, send them to your social media
- Be upfront about selling — explain why
- The folder creation request trains them to value your emails
- The whitelist/reply request improves deliverability immediately
- This email sets the ENTIRE tone — personality matters more here than anywhere

### Example Subject Lines
- "Welcome To The Party, Pal!" (Die Hard reference)
- "You're in. Here's what happens next."
- "Welcome aboard — and your first gift"

## EMAIL 2: THE BUILD / WINE & DINE

### Long Version Template (The Build — Stories Sell Everything)
```
Subject: [Overarching theme summated in a few words]
Preview: [Downloadable guide from yesterday]

[Greeting]

[Obvious statement about yourself that connects to the theme]

[Lead into content around the subject line]

[Link the idea to their end goal]

[Disrupt their thinking by introducing a new parable or concept]

[Expand and explain the disruption]

[Give a specific example]

[Combine the example back to your original opt-in/guide/reason
 for being on your list]

[Link to your download]

[Sign off]
```

### Short Version Template (Wine & Dine Your Reader)
```
Subject: [Bold claim or philosophical statement]
Preview: [Tease or callback to download]

[Greeting]

[Brief reminder of who you are and what you do]

[Share ONE key insight or philosophy that defines your approach]

[Give a quick example or anecdote]

[Connect it to their goal/pain]

[Link to download or content]

[Sign off]
```

### Key Insight
Market leaders always tell a story and sell a narrative. Smart ones reinvent themselves every few years. Align your story with the image your audience wants to see.

## EMAIL 3: THE ONE-TWO SETUP (Part 1) — Both Versions

**Purpose:** Content email that sets up Email 4. Can be standalone content OR a two-parter that creates the perfect payoff.

### Template Structure
```
Subject: [Ominous subject line — Part 1]
Preview: [Immediately make a joke about how it's not as serious]

[Greeting]

[Drop suspense immediately and get into the meat]

[Challenge beliefs and misconceptions]

[Use a 3rd party quote — if paraphrasing, don't hide that fact]

[Question the quote]

[Explain its importance]

[Give them a reason to click on a link to your site]

[Link]

[Sell the link in bullet points]
  - Bullet 1
  - Bullet 2
  - "And so much more..." as the 3rd or 5th bullet point

[Final point]

[Link again]

[Sign off / signature]
```

### Tactical Notes
- End bullet lists with "And so much more" always as the 3rd or 5th bullet (odd numbers)
- The ominous subject line creates urgency to open
- The preview text defuses with humor — creates pattern interrupt
- This email should deliver genuine value even without Email 4

### Example Subject Lines
- "Not having this = death part 1"
- "The mistake that's costing you everything (part 1)"
- "What nobody's telling you about [topic] — part 1"

## EMAIL 4: A CALL TO ARMS (Part 2) — Both Versions

**Purpose:** Promotional payoff. Wraps up Email 3 as the "One-Two Punch." Teaches how to write a subtle pitch into email stories.

### Template Structure
```
Subject: [Continued subject line — similar but with "Part 2"]
Preview: [Explanation of why there's a Part 2]

[Greeting]

[Reason why you created the 2nd piece of content]

[Self-deprecating humor / monologue moment if desired]

[Give instruction on timeline to get result]

[Link]

[Sell the link with bullets]

[Last point]

[Signature]

P.S.: [Summation of the email]
P.P.S.: [Goes into an offer + reminds them of the P.S. from
         Email 1 with reasons why]
```

### Key Technique
The P.P.S. in Email 4 calls back to the offer mentioned in Email 1. This creates continuity and a second touch on the sales message without being the primary focus.

### Example Subject Lines
- "Not having this equals death part 2"
- "The solution to yesterday's problem (part 2)"
- "Here's how to fix it — part 2"

## EMAIL 5: TESTIMONIAL EMAIL

**Purpose:** Use testimonials to bridge content. Write stories for fun and profit.

### Long Version Template
```
Subject: [From X to Y] or [Name of person's journey]...
Preview: [Announce what you're sending them]

[Greeting]

[Set expectation that this email is different]

[Get them to check out previous content if they missed it]

[Set when they can view that content but keep them hooked HERE]

[Go into story about a client/customer you've helped]

[Put their testimonial in the email — in their words]

[Have their name]

[Talk about something the testimonial leaves out — context]

[Set up a segue into contemplation]

[Give them something to contemplate — ask probing questions:]
  - Are you emailing your list as often as you should?
  - Do they know who you REALLY are?
  - Are you showing up in the best possible way?
  - Is your audience entertained?

[If you want help... link to content/services/sales page]

[Sign off / signature]

P.S.: [A way to get in touch with you via social media]
```

### Short Version Template (Case Study Focus)
```
Subject: [[Name]'s journey | Shocking result | Something
          seemingly "abnormal"]
Preview: [Curiosity or tease — optional]

[Greeting]

[Expand on subject line — why you're emailing + set up premise]

[Show the testimonial or share what they said]

[Talk about how simple the case study is and how common]

[Give insight into how the case study is different + how simple
 it can be to use your service/product]

[Solid call to action]

[Sign off]

P.S.: [Optional — highlight a bonus or advantage with CTA]
```

### The Contemplation Technique
End testimonial emails with probing self-assessment questions. These force the reader to evaluate their own situation — which creates desire for the solution.

### Example Subject Lines
- "from burn out to so fresh, so clean..."
- "[Client name]'s journey from $0 to $10K/month"
- "How [name] did it in 90 days"

## EMAIL 6: PHILOSOPHY / CASE STUDIES

### Long Version Template (Philosophy Through Content)
```
Subject: [A to-the-point phrase lifted from conversation]
Preview: [Optional]

[Greeting]

[Explain the subject line with what you've been doing]

[Expand on the situation]

[Relate it to a story about you and your past]

[Ensure your story highlights your key points]

[Summate your point in 1 sentence]

[Give a guideline if they follow your advice]

[Sign off]
```

### Short Version (Case Studies)
Uses case studies to drive home points and trigger desire. Similar structure to the short testimonial email but with more depth on the specific methods used.

### Purpose
Instill your philosophy through content. Build community and shared worldview. This email is about WHO YOU ARE and WHAT YOU BELIEVE.

### Example Subject Lines
- "self-esteem plays a big part..."
- "How many emails?!"
- "The one thing I refuse to compromise on"

## EMAIL 7: THE SALES EMAIL

**Purpose:** Direct sales pitch wrapped in content value.

### Template Structure
```
Subject: [Summary of email or expectation of what's inside]
Preview: [Headline that offers click while expanding on headline]

[Greeting]

[Immediate admittance you're doing something different]

[Bait emotion by showing a statistic or claim]

[Go into content — pick ONE focus point if you made 3, 5, or 7]

[Explain the importance of your content]

[Focus on the one big point in depth]

[Hyperlink to sales page/video]

[Expand on main point — give enough to act on but need MORE clarity]

["If you want to [xyz result], then go watch/read this
[video/letter] here"]

[RAW link to the URL]

[Sign off]

[Name]

P.S.: [Direction to do something: "I'd suggest you take notes /
      go for a walk / listen while running / don't watch this
      while driving" etc.]

[Hyperlink text with call to action]
[Raw link]
```

### Key Principle
Provide the sales pitch within genuine content. The email must have standalone value even if they never click the link.

### The 3 Reasons Emails Die
1. You don't connect with your audience
2. Your subject lines are bad
3. You don't know what to write about

Use your story as the treasure trove of content ideas.

### Example Subject Lines
- "3 reasons why emails die..."
- "The email that changed everything"
- "I don't usually do this, but..."

## BEYOND EMAIL 7: THE CYCLICAL PATTERN

After Email 7, repeat the cycle with fresh content:

| Email 8 | Same as Email 1 type — reintroduction/value delivery |
| Email 9 | Same as Email 2 type — philosophy/bond building |
| Email 10 | Same as Email 3 type — content setup (Part 1) |
| Email 11 | Same as Email 4 type — promotional payoff (Part 2) |
| Email 12 | Same as Email 5 type — testimonial/case study |
| Email 13 | Same as Email 6 type — philosophy/deeper case study |
| Email 14 | Same as Email 7 type — sales email |

One student (Tom) used this to write a 14-email sequence in 6 hours.

## TIMING RECOMMENDATIONS

| Email | Send Timing |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Immediately upon opt-in |
| 2 | Day 2 (next day) |
| 3 | Day 4 |
| 4 | Day 5 (next day after Part 1) |
| 5 | Day 7 |
| 6 | Day 9 |
| 7 | Day 11 |

Adjust based on your audience and engagement data. The key is consistency — do not send Email 3 and Email 4 more than 24-48 hours apart or the "One-Two Punch" loses its power.
