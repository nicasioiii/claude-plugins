# Headlines, Hooks & Attention Engineering

## Core Principle: The Headline IS the Ad

David Ogilvy found that five times as many people read the headline as read the body copy. The headline might be your only chance to get your message across. Ogilvy: 80 cents of every dollar in a promotion goes toward the headline.

**The Slippery Slide principle (Joe Sugarman):** The job of the headline is to make someone read the first line. The job of the first line is to make someone read the second line. And so on. Every element exists to pull the reader into the next element.

---

## The 6 U's: Headline Scoring Checklist

Score every headline against the 6 U's. Aim for 4 of 6. Originally 4 U's from Agora/AWAI; Sandy Franks added the final two.

1. **Urgent** -- Creates time pressure or immediacy
2. **Useful** -- Provides clear value to the target audience
3. **Unique** -- Feels different from everything else out there
4. **Ultra-specific** -- Uses precise numbers, dates, amounts
5. **Ultra-relevant** -- Directly relatable to the reader (Sandy's addition)
6. **Curiosity-arousing** -- Makes the reader need to know more (Sandy's addition)

**Benchmark:** Use Advanced Marketing Institute Headline Analyzer. Aim for 30%+ emotional score. 55% is excellent. Sandy scored 54.5% on a test headline.

---

## The 20-25 Headline Iteration Process

Never ship your first headline. The magic happens around headline 20-25 when you have exhausted the obvious.

1. Write at least 20-25 headline variations minimum
2. Each should be slightly different -- improve one word, swap an adjective, try a different formula
3. The first 20 will mostly be mediocre. That is normal and expected.
4. Test the final 3-5 candidates with real people
5. Always A/B test: "control" vs. "challenger"

**Practice method (John Morrow via Brian Clark):** Write 10 headlines a day for 30 days. This produced 200M+ content views.

## The Kernel Sentence Technique (Copy Hackers)

The clearest messages follow "Blank is Blank" structure. When in doubt, simplify to a kernel sentence before expanding.

- **Bad:** "Use Qualaroo to get your visitors to answer short pop-up surveys on your website"
- **Good:** "Qualaroo is a pop-up survey for your website"

The second tells you what it IS. The first overcomplicates. Start with the kernel, then add benefit and specificity.

## The 60-Second Sales Hook (Kevin Rogers)

Derived from standup comedy joke formula. When the stakes are high and you cannot afford to fail, this is the formula.

**Structure:** Identity > Struggle > Discovery > Result > Call to Action

**Kevin Rogers' own example:**
- Identity: "Hi, I'm Kevin Rogers."
- Struggle: "I spent years as a dead broke standup comedian."
- Discovery: "Until I discovered how a simple joke formula can be used as an irresistible sales hook I call the 60 Second Sales Hook"
- Result: "and began teaching marketers how to use it to skyrocket sales and grow their businesses."
- CTA: "If you'd like to discover this powerful four-sentence formula, put it to work in your business, enter your name below."

**Critical rule:** Name your mechanism in the Discovery step. Do NOT say "Then I discovered a way to convert cold traffic" (generic -- they will Google it). DO say "Then I discovered the 60-Second Sales Hook" (unique, Googling only leads back to you).

## Headline Types by Category

### Sandy Franks' 8 Headline Types
1. **How-to** -- "How to [achieve result]"
2. **Newsy** -- "Announcing..." / "Introducing..."
3. **Direct** -- State the benefit plainly
4. **Question** -- "Do you...?" / "Are you...?"
5. **Why** -- "Why [counterintuitive claim]"
6. **Myth/hoax/surprise** -- Challenges a belief
7. **Statement/proclamation** -- Bold declaration
8. **Two-part** -- Sandy's preference for stories

### 7 Headline Approaches (Copywriting Made Simple)
1. **Just Say It** -- State what the product does
2. **Set the Theme** -- Tell readers what you want to discuss
3. **Offer a Benefit** -- Feature one or two benefits at most
4. **Create Intrigue** -- Peak curiosity with indirection
5. **Ask a Question** -- Leading questions invite mental "yes"
6. **Explain Why** -- Promises insight, not just information
7. **Break the News** -- Signal novelty
8. **Give a Command** -- Direct and forceful

## Fascinations: The Art of Intensifying Desire

Fascinations are 1-3 sentence statements that tease what the reader will learn inside content. They promise value but do NOT give it away. Even ONE fascination that hits the right nerve can compel conversion.

### Three-Step Fascination Writing Process
1. **Find the nugget** -- Identify a piece of valuable information inside the content
2. **Tease the value and state the benefit** -- Hint at what the reader gains
3. **Apply a filter** -- Use one of the 18 proven fascination templates

### Top 8 Fascination Filters (Quick Reference)
1. **Mechanism** -- "The [mechanism] that [benefit]"
2. **How** -- "How to [achieve result] without [obstacle]"
3. **What** -- "What [surprising thing] does for [result]"
4. **Why** -- "Why [counterintuitive claim]"
5. **Number** -- "7 [things] that [benefit]"
6. **If-then** -- "If you [situation], then [consequence/benefit]"
7. **Caution** -- "Caution: [warning about common mistake]"
8. **Truth** -- "The truth about [misconception]"

### 9 Rules for Writing Fascinations
1. Write more than you need -- overproduction yields the best material
2. The more specific the benefit, the better
3. Prove it -- back claims with evidence
4. Clarity beats concision
5. Use simple words -- no jargon
6. Use the prospect's vocabulary
7. Avoid transactional words (that feel like selling)
8. Avoid work words (that imply effort)
9. Create variance in sentence structure, length, and rhythm

## Curiosity Gap Creation

Based on George Loewenstein's research at Carnegie Mellon. The gap between information given and information deliberately withheld triggers the brain to want to fill in the missing piece.

**Three factors determining curiosity intensity:**
1. **Importance** -- How much the information matters to the person
2. **Salience** -- How attention is drawn to the gap
3. **Surprise** -- Does the new information contradict expectations?

**Direct curiosity gap phrases:**
- "I'll tell you what this means in just a minute, but first..."
- "More about that in a moment..."
- "Hold on one minute..."

**The Upworthy formula:** Too vague = no click. Too specific = no need to click. Just enough info to create the gap = click.

**Rule:** Every curiosity gap you open MUST be closed. An unclosed gap leaves the reader frustrated.

## Copy Massaging: Strengthening Sentences Progressively

Start with a flat benefit and progressively add layers:

1. MEH: "How to alter your mindset"
2. + INTRIGUE: "How to naturally alter your mindset using nothing but your index finger"
3. + TIME: "How to alter your mindset in 17 minutes using nothing but your index finger"
4. + SPECIFICS: "How to reboot your warrior mindset 97% using nothing but your index finger"
5. + PROOF: "Why Michael Jordan's mindset practice helps reboot your state in as little as 17 minutes"
6. + MECHANISM NAME: "Why Michael Jordan's 'mindflow' practice naturally rebuilds your mindset"
7. + POSITIONING: "Why this Aztec Warrior mindset practice helps reboot your mindset to 97% clarity base"

**Power words for massaging:** "without," "even if," "plus," "in just," "finally"

## Crossheads (Subheads Down the Page)

Crossheads should be at least as engaging as headlines. They are NOT summaries -- they are teasers.

**Rules:**
- Should tease/hint so the reader keeps reading, NOT summarize what follows
- Bad: "What Customers Say" or "FAQs"
- Good: Fascination-style subheads that create curiosity
- Center and bold them, tag as H2
- Trick: put quotation marks around ONE crosshead (not all) to grab the eye

## Subject Line Patterns (Email)

Email subject lines follow headline principles but have unique constraints:

**Key rules:**
- Subject lines are headlines for email -- same principles apply
- Open loops are especially powerful in subject lines
- Personalization (first name, company) increases open rates
- Specificity beats cleverness every time
- Test capitalization: sentence case vs. title case vs. all lowercase

## Primer Copy (Eddie Shleyner)

A line of text ABOVE the headline that provides extreme clarity. Modeled after cereal box descriptors.

**Format:** State what the page is advertising in plain language, end with a colon to push into the headline.

**Example:** "A short 100-year-old book declared essential reading for modern copywriters:"

Primer copy eliminates any chance of confusion before the headline even fires.

## The Hook Foundation (Todd Brown)

**Promise + Curiosity = Hook Foundation**

Every good hook is both EMOTIONALLY COMPELLING and INTELLECTUALLY INTERESTING:
- Emotionally compelling = big bold promise + unique mechanism (creates hope)
- Intellectually interesting = storyline/angle they have never heard before (creates curiosity)

**Hook Checklist:**
- Follows the Rule of One (one narrative, one promise, one emotion)
- Big, believable promise of result
- New and fresh (timely)
- Specific, concrete, tangible
- Intellectually interesting
- Easy to grasp

### The Categorical Imperative (Bill Bonner / Agora)

When humans encounter information, they try to categorize it: "Can I put this into a bucket I already know?" If they CAN, they dismiss it. Your hook must PREVENT this categorization.

---

## 5 Elements of a Strong Hook (OnePeak)

For video ads and social content, hooks have 5 components:

1. **Verbal Hook** -- What you say (the opening line)
2. **Visual Hook** -- What they see (setting, product, person)
3. **Audio Hook** -- Sound design, music, voice quality
4. **Text Hook** -- On-screen text/caption
5. **Caption Hook** -- The ad copy caption below the video

**The Hook-Proof-How Caption Formula:**
1. Hook -- Open with a bold promise that summarizes your offer
2. Proof -- Back up claims with case studies and success stories
3. How -- Highlight key features that deliver on the promise

1. **5-Second Test** -- Show the headline for 5 seconds, ask what they remember
2. **Headline Analyzer** -- Run through AMI Headline Analyzer, aim for 30%+ emotional score
3. **A/B Test** -- Always have a control and a challenger
4. **Test big first** -- Test radically different headlines before tweaking words

## Anti-Patterns: What Kills Headlines

1. **Clever over clear** -- In direct response, "clever" is a bad word. Clarity trumps cleverness.
2. **No periods on headlines or CTAs** -- A period is a stop signal
3. **No exclamation marks** -- The most effective way to write exciting copy is to promise something truly desired, not to add punctuation
4. **Cramming multiple benefits** -- Feature one or two at most; go hard on convincing one
5. **Writing for yourself instead of the reader** -- Use "you" and "your," never "I" in headlines
6. **Generic mechanisms** -- If they can Google the generic term and find competitors, you have lost (the Google Slap test)
7. **Summarizing subheads** -- Crossheads should tease, never summarize
8. **Stopping at headline 5** -- The magic happens at headline 20-25
9. **Skipping the kernel sentence** -- If you cannot say "Blank is Blank," you do not have clarity yet
10. **Leaving curiosity gaps unclosed** -- Every gap opened must eventually be closed
