# Headline Testing Process

**Sources:** Copy Hackers (Joanna Wiebe), Sandy Franks, Eddie Shleyner, Arman Assadi, OnePeak
**Purpose:** Systematically test and validate headlines to find winners, not guess

---

## The Complete Testing Workflow

### Phase 1: Generate (Quantity First)

**Step 1: Write 20-25 headline variations minimum**
- Use the headline formula bank -- write for every applicable formula
- Start with the kernel sentence ("Blank is Blank") for clarity
- Each variation should be slightly different: swap a word, try a different formula, change the angle
- Do NOT self-edit during generation. Quantity produces quality. The first 20 will mostly be mediocre.
- The magic happens around headline 20-25 when you have exhausted the obvious and start finding unexpected angles

**Step 2: Categorize your headlines**
Group them by type:
- How-to headlines
- Question headlines
- Benefit/result headlines
- Story/curiosity headlines
- Objection-busting headlines
- News/announcement headlines

**Step 3: Select your top 5-8 candidates**
- Pick the best from each category
- Aim for variety -- your finalists should NOT all be the same type
- Score each against the 6 U's (aim for 4 of 6)

---

### Phase 2: Validate (Before Going Live)

#### The 5-Second Test
**What it is:** Show the headline (and optionally the hero section) for exactly 5 seconds, then hide it.

**How to run it:**
1. Find 5-10 people who match your target audience
2. Show the headline for 5 seconds on screen
3. Hide it
4. Ask: "What do you think this is about?" and "Would you want to learn more?"
5. If they cannot articulate what the page offers or why they should care, the headline failed
6. Repeat with each finalist

**What to look for:**
- Can they describe the offer in their own words?
- Do they express interest or curiosity?
- Do they mention a benefit or result?
- Are they confused about anything?

**Tools:** UsabilityHub (now Lyssna), Maze, or simply screen-share and time it manually.

#### The 7-Second Test (Full Hero)
**What it is:** Show the full above-the-fold section for 7 seconds. Answers 3 questions:

1. What is it?
2. Why should I care?
3. What should I do next?

**How to run it:**
1. Set context: tell the reviewer who they should pretend to be (your avatar)
2. Do NOT explain your solution or business
3. Open the page for exactly 7 seconds, then stop
4. Ask the three questions
5. Do not argue with their answers -- document and fix

**Key insight:** 7 seconds in a test is MORE focused attention than real visitors give. Real visitors are multitasking, on mobile, distracted.

### Phase 3: Analyze with Tools

#### Headline Analyzer (Advanced Marketing Institute)
**URL:** aminstitute.com/headline
**How to use:**
1. Enter your headline text
2. Select your industry category
3. Submit
4. Review the Emotional Marketing Value (EMV) score

**Benchmarks:**
- 20-30%: Average headline
- 30-40%: Good headline
- 40-50%: Very strong headline
- 50%+: Exceptional headline (Sandy Franks scored 54.5%)

**What the score measures:** The percentage of emotional/power words in your headline. Higher = more emotionally engaging.

**Limitations:**
- EMV score is one signal, not the final answer
- A clear, low-scoring headline may outperform a high-scoring but confusing one
- Use as a complement to human testing, not a replacement

#### CoSchedule Headline Analyzer
**URL:** coschedule.com/headline-analyzer
**Measures:** Word balance, headline type, reading grade level, sentiment, clarity, and skimmability.
**Target:** 70+ overall score

#### Sharethrough Headline Analyzer
**URL:** headlines.sharethrough.com
**Best for:** Social media and content marketing headlines
**Measures:** Engagement score, impression score, quality score with specific improvement suggestions

### Phase 4: A/B Test (Live Traffic)

#### Setting Up the Test

**Step 1: Define your control**
- Your current best headline is the "control"
- If launching fresh, your best 5-second test winner is the control

**Step 2: Create a challenger**
- Pick one finalist that is meaningfully different from the control
- Do NOT test two similar headlines -- test distinct approaches (e.g., question vs. benefit)

**Step 3: Split traffic evenly**
- 50/50 split between control and challenger
- Both variations must see the same traffic source, time of day, and audience

**Step 4: Run to statistical significance**
- Minimum: 100 conversions per variation (200+ is better)
- Use a significance calculator (optimizely.com/sample-size-calculator)
- Do NOT call the test early based on gut feeling
- Typical test duration: 1-4 weeks depending on traffic volume

**Step 5: Evaluate results**
- Winner becomes the new control
- Create a new challenger and repeat
- Track both conversion rate AND revenue per visitor

#### What to Test (Priority Order)

1. **Big differences first:** Test a how-to headline vs. a question headline vs. a story headline. Different approaches, not word tweaks.
2. **Angles second:** Test different benefits or promises within the same format.
3. **Word tweaks last:** Only after you have found the winning approach and angle do you optimize individual words.

**Eddie Shleyner's philosophy:** When trying to beat a control, create something that looks and reads NOTHING like the original. Big differences are much more likely to create big shifts (2x, 3x conversions). Small tests (headline word, button color) come AFTER you have had a breakthrough.

## Testing Frameworks by Platform

### Landing Page Headlines
- A/B test using: Google Optimize (deprecated), VWO, Optimizely, Convert.com
- Primary metric: Conversion rate (leads or sales)
- Secondary metric: Bounce rate, scroll depth, time on page
- Test duration: 2-4 weeks minimum

### Email Subject Lines
- A/B test using: Built-in ESP tools (Mailchimp, ConvertKit, ActiveCampaign)
- Primary metric: Open rate
- Secondary metric: Click-through rate (opens without clicks = empty curiosity)
- Test method: Send variant A to 20% of list, variant B to 20%, winner to remaining 60%
- Wait time: 2-4 hours before sending winner

### Ad Headlines
- A/B test using: Facebook Ads Manager, Google Ads
- Primary metric: Click-through rate (CTR)
- Secondary metric: Cost per click (CPC), conversion rate on LP
- Test method: Run 3-5 headline variations simultaneously
- Budget: At least $50-100 per variation before making decisions

### Social Media Headlines
- Test using: Post multiple variations at different times
- Primary metric: Engagement rate (likes + comments + shares / impressions)
- Secondary metric: Click-through to link
- Test method: Post variations across different days, measure engagement

## The Headline Swipe File Practice

### Building Your Swipe File
1. Save every headline that stops YOUR scroll
2. Save winning headlines from competitors
3. Archive control headlines from successful campaigns
4. Organize by formula type (how-to, question, story, benefit, etc.)
5. Include the context: where it appeared, what it was selling, why it caught your attention

### Using Your Swipe File for Generation
1. Open your swipe file before writing
2. Pick 5-10 headlines you admire
3. Identify the structural pattern of each
4. Replace the specifics with YOUR product, audience, and benefit
5. This is the Imitation Game method (Todd Brown) -- and it reliably produces 30-100 candidates per session

### Recommended Swipe File Sources
- swiped.co -- Annotated copywriting samples
- goodemailcopy.com -- Email subject line examples
- headlines.sharethrough.com -- Top-performing headlines
- Your own inbox -- Save subject lines that made you open
- Competitor ads -- Use Facebook Ad Library to find active competitor ads

## Sandy Franks' Practice Method

Write 10 headlines a day for 30 days. This is the method John Morrow learned from Brian Clark at CopyBlogger -- and it resulted in 200M+ content views.

**Process:**
1. Pick a topic each day (can be the same product or different ones)
2. Write 10 headlines using different formulas
3. Score each against the 6 U's
4. Run the best one through the Headline Analyzer
5. Save winners to your swipe file
6. After 30 days, you will have written 300 headlines and developed genuine intuition

**Why this works:** Headline writing is a skill built through volume, not theory. After 300 headlines, you start to feel what works before you test it. The testing then confirms your intuition rather than replacing it.
