# Email Subject Line Patterns & Formulas

**Sources:** Adil Amarsi (Extract 07), Sandy Franks (Extract 02), Copy Hackers (Extract 04), OnePeak (Extract 08)
**Purpose:** Write subject lines that get opens. Everything in headline-hook-craft applies to subject lines, but these are the email-specific patterns.

---

## Core Principle: Subject Lines Are Headlines for Email

Every headline principle applies. But email adds unique constraints:
- Character limit: 40-60 characters visible on most devices (30-40 on mobile)
- Preview text: The 50-100 characters after the subject that appear in inbox
- Competition: Your subject line competes against 50-200 other emails in the inbox
- Deliverability: Certain words trigger spam filters
- Context: The "from" name matters as much as the subject line

---

## Subject Line Formula Categories

### Open Loop / Curiosity Gap Subject Lines
The most powerful email subject line technique. Create an incomplete thought that demands opening.

**Patterns:**
- "I made a mistake with [topic]..."
- "This is what happened when I [action]..."
- "I can't believe [surprising thing]"
- "The one thing I'd change about [topic]"
- "I was wrong about [topic]"
- "Something weird happened when [event]..."
- "Don't open this email" (reverse psychology -- use very sparingly)

**Rules:**
- The open loop MUST be closed inside the email
- Do not use clickbait that disappoints -- trust destruction is permanent
- The "payoff" inside the email must be worth the curiosity

---

### Benefit-Driven Subject Lines
Lead with what the reader gets.

**Patterns:**
- "[Result] in [timeframe]"
- "How to [achieve result] without [obstacle]"
- "The [fastest/easiest/simplest] way to [result]"
- "[Number] ways to [benefit] starting today"
- "Get [desirable thing] -- free"

---

### Question Subject Lines
Engage by inviting a mental response.

**Patterns:**
- "Are you making this [topic] mistake?"
- "What would you do with [benefit]?"
- "Is your [asset] working against you?"
- "Quick question about your [topic]..."
- "Have you tried this yet?"

---

### News/Announcement Subject Lines
Leverage novelty and timeliness.

**Patterns:**
- "[New]: [what it is]"
- "Introducing [product/feature]"
- "Breaking: [news relevant to reader]"
- "Just launched: [description]"
- "[Date/Event] update: [what changed]"

---

### Story Subject Lines
Promise a story worth reading.

**Patterns:**
- "The day I almost [dramatic event]"
- "How [person] went from [before] to [after]"
- "The [adjective] story of [topic]"
- "A [identity] walks into a [place]..."
- "What [famous person] taught me about [topic]"

---

### Urgency/Scarcity Subject Lines
Create time pressure for action.

**Patterns:**
- "[Hours/Days] left: [offer]"
- "Last chance: [benefit]"
- "Closing tonight: [offer]"
- "Only [number] spots left"
- "This disappears at midnight"

**Rules:**
- ONLY use urgency when it is genuine
- Fake urgency destroys trust permanently
- If the timer resets, your audience learns to ignore your deadlines

---

### Personal/Conversational Subject Lines
Feel like a message from a friend, not a brand.

**Patterns:**
- "Hey [first name], quick thought"
- "Can I ask you something?"
- "This reminded me of you"
- "I've been thinking about this..."
- "You probably already know this, but..."
- "[First name]" (just the name -- surprisingly effective)

---

### Controversial/Provocative Subject Lines
Challenge beliefs to force engagement.

**Patterns:**
- "Why [common practice] is dead"
- "[Popular opinion] is wrong -- here's proof"
- "Stop [common action] immediately"
- "The lie about [topic] everyone believes"
- "I disagree with [authority/common wisdom]"

## Subject Line Best Practices

### Capitalization Testing
- **Sentence case:** "How to write better headlines" -- feels casual, personal
- **Title Case:** "How To Write Better Headlines" -- feels professional, authoritative
- **ALL CAPS:** "HOW TO WRITE BETTER HEADLINES" -- feels urgent (use very sparingly)
- **lowercase:** "how to write better headlines" -- feels intimate, casual

Test all four. Different audiences respond to different capitalization.

### Personalization
- First name in subject line increases open rates across most lists
- Company name personalization works well in B2B
- Location personalization works for local businesses
- Do NOT over-personalize -- 1 personal element per subject line maximum

### Length Guidelines
- **Short (1-5 words):** Best for intrigue and personal feel. "Quick question" / "Bad news" / "[First name]"
- **Medium (6-10 words):** Best for benefit-driven and how-to. "How to double your conversions this week"
- **Long (11+ words):** Best for story and specificity. "The surprising method a broke comedian used to become a millionaire copywriter"

Test all three lengths. There is no universal winner.

### Preview Text Strategy
The preview text (preheader) is the second subject line. Use it to:
- Complete the thought from the subject line
- Add a benefit the subject line teased
- Create a second curiosity hook
- NEVER let it default to "View this email in your browser" or other system text

**Example pairings:**
- Subject: "I made a mistake..." | Preview: "And it cost me $14,000. Here's what I learned."
- Subject: "Quick question" | Preview: "About your landing page -- I noticed something."
- Subject: "3 words" | Preview: "That doubled our email open rates overnight."

## Subject Line Anti-Patterns

1. **ALL CAPS entire subject line** -- Triggers spam filters and screams unprofessional
2. **Excessive punctuation** -- "!!!!" or "???" triggers spam filters
3. **Spam trigger words** -- "Free!!!," "Act now!!!," "Limited time offer" in combination
4. **Misleading subject lines** -- Clickbait that does not deliver destroys trust permanently
5. **"Newsletter" or "Update"** -- Boring, expected, easy to ignore
6. **"Re:" or "Fwd:" when it is not** -- Deceptive, and audiences learn to distrust
7. **Emojis overuse** -- One emoji can help; three is spam signaling
8. **Generic subjects** -- "Monthly update" or "News from [Company]" invites archiving

## The Sandy Franks Subject Line Benchmark

Sandy Franks achieved 47-49% email open rates on her newsletter headlines. Her formula:
- Two-part headlines with story elements
- Ultra-specific details (names, numbers, dollar amounts)
- Curiosity-arousing structure (the gap between what is revealed and what is withheld)
- 20-30 words maximum

These are not short subject lines. They are story-driven headlines that happened to appear in email. Length is not the enemy -- boredom is.

## Subject Line Testing Method

1. Write 10+ subject line variations
2. Categorize by type (curiosity, benefit, question, story, urgency)
3. Pick the best from each category (5 finalists)
4. A/B test the top 2 against each other
5. Send the winner to the remaining list
6. Track open rates AND click rates (opens without clicks = empty curiosity)
7. Build a personal swipe file of your best-performing subject lines
