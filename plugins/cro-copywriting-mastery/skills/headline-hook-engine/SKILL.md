# Headlines, Hooks & Attention-Grabbers

## THE ONLY JOB OF A HEADLINE

**Rule:** Your headline's only job is to GET ATTENTION. Nothing else.

- Don't try to tell the full story in the headline
- Don't try to include all benefits
- Don't be clever at the expense of clarity
- Just stop them scrolling. That's it.

**Hard truth:** 50-80% of your copy's effectiveness lives in the headline. If it fails, nothing else matters.

---

## WHY HEADLINES MAKE OR BREAK COPY

### The Math
- In email: Subject line determines 50%+ of open rates
- On web: Determines if reader scrolls
- In ads: Only chance to grab attention
- In social: Determines if stop-scroll happens
- In search: Determines if click happens

If headline fails, reader never sees your persuasive copy. The best body copy in the world is worthless without attention first.

### The Psychology
- Brain makes decision in 3-7 seconds whether copy is "for me"
- Specificity triggers "this applies to me" response
- Curiosity creates psychological pull forward
- Benefit clarity removes friction
- Numbers create structure, reducing cognitive load

## THE EIGHT CORE HEADLINE TYPES

### 1. BENEFIT HEADLINES
**Format:** "Get [Specific Benefit]" or "[Benefit] for [Avatar]"

**Why it works:** Direct appeal to self-interest. Reader thinks "This is for me."

**Rules:**
- Benefit must be clear in 5 seconds
- Specific beats vague (30 pounds > weight loss)
- Target must be obvious
- Benefit must be real (not aspirational jargon)

**Examples:**
- "Cut Your Energy Bills in Half"
- "Lose 30 Pounds in 90 Days Without Dieting"
- "Get 50 Qualified Leads Daily for Your Agency"

**When to use:** Strong product, clear problem solved, high awareness audience

---

### 2. CURIOSITY HEADLINES
**Format:** "The [Surprising Thing] About [Topic]" or "What [Question]?"

**Why it works:** Information gap creates pull. Reader thinks "I need to know this."

**Rules:**
- Create gap without answering it
- Must relate to reader's interest
- Don't be clickbait—deliver on promise
- Tease, don't trick

**Examples:**
- "The Weird Trick Doctors Don't Want You to Know"
- "What 47,000+ Entrepreneurs Know About Productivity That You Don't"
- "The Secret Most Copywriters Never Discover"

**When to use:** Cold traffic, competitive markets, when benefit alone isn't enough

---

### 3. PROBLEM/PAIN HEADLINES
**Format:** "Struggling with [Specific Pain]?" or "Tired of [Problem]?"

**Why it works:** Creates immediate identification. Reader thinks "Yes, that's me."

**Rules:**
- Name SPECIFIC problem, not generic one
- Make reader feel seen
- Follow with implied promise of solution
- Don't just complain—hint at answer

**Examples:**
- "Can't Get Your Kids to Sleep Without Fighting?"
- "Exhausted From Your High CPA On Facebook Ads?"
- "Trapped in the Feast-or-Famine Cycle?"

**When to use:** Problem-aware audiences, objection handling, email sequences

---

### 4. NUMBER/SPECIFICITY HEADLINES
**Format:** "[Number] [Ways/Reasons/Secrets] to [Benefit]"

**Why it works:** Numbers create structure; specificity increases credibility.

**Rules:**
- Number must match content inside (don't promise 7, deliver 5)
- Odd numbers slightly better (7, 9, 11, 13)
- Lower numbers work better (3, 5 best; 47 seems padded)
- Specific numbers > round numbers ("7" > "several")

**Examples:**
- "7 Proven Strategies to Lower Your Insurance Premiums"
- "3 Ways to Triple Your Productivity in 30 Days"
- "5 Mistakes Preventing Your Hair Growth"

**When to use:** Always safe; listicles; how-tos; educational content

---

### 5. HOW-TO / WHY HEADLINES
**Format:** "How to [Specific Outcome]" or "Why [Success Indicator]"

**Why it works:** Promise of education/solution; natural pull to keep reading.

**Rules:**
- Outcome must be desirable and specific
- Can stack with benefit or number ("How to Get $10K Months in 60 Days")
- "Why" works for contrarian angles
- Simple, reliable, almost always works

**Examples:**
- "How to Write Sales Copy That Converts 2X Better"
- "How to Get Your First Paying Client in 30 Days"
- "Why 90% of Copywriters Plateau at $50K/Year"

**When to use:** When stuck; educational angles; authority positioning; evergreen content

---

### 6. QUESTION HEADLINES
**Format:** "[Rhetorical Question]?" or "What if [possibility]?"

**Why it works:** Brain naturally tries to answer questions; creates engagement.

**Rules:**
- Must be relevant to reader (not random)
- Presupposes reader's situation
- Avoid obvious yes/no answers
- Open-ended better than closed

**Examples:**
- "What's Keeping You From Your First Six Figures?"
- "What If You Could Write Copy in Half the Time?"
- "Are You Still Using Old-School Sales Tactics?"

**When to use:** Self-awareness campaigns; email openers; blog titles

---

### 7. NEWS/ANNOUNCEMENT HEADLINES
**Format:** "[New/Breakthrough/Revolutionary] [Benefit]" or "Announced: [Big News]"

**Why it works:** Novelty catches attention. Reader thinks "This is fresh."

**Rules:**
- Must feel timely/new (not rehashed)
- Actual news beats artificial newsjacking
- Can be trend-based (iOS changes, algorithm updates)
- Use sparingly (overuse loses power)

**Examples:**
- "New Study Reveals Best Time to Exercise for Fat Loss"
- "Announced: The First AI-Powered Copywriting Tool"
- "Breaking: Facebook's Algorithm Change Affects 47% of Brands"

**When to use:** Actual innovation; trend angles; product launches

---

### 8. COMMAND/IMPERATIVE HEADLINES
**Format:** "[Verb] [Object]" (direct instruction or call)

**Why it works:** Authoritative, action-oriented; creates urgency through tone.

**Rules:**
- Must be congruent with offer
- Can feel pushy if misused
- Works best for known audiences
- Pair with benefit for power

**Examples:**
- "Claim Your Free Report Before We Hit Capacity"
- "Discover the Framework That Changed Our Business"
- "Learn the 3 Copywriting Secrets That Sell"

**When to use:** Email CTAs; action-oriented audiences; existing relationships

## THE RULE OF ONE FOR HOOKS

A strong hook (your headline + opening sentence) must follow ONE rule:

- **ONE narrative** (single storyline, not scattered ideas)
- **ONE promise** (singular focus, not multiple benefits)
- **ONE emotion** (one core feeling you're provoking)
- **ONE audience** (specific person, not everyone)

**Why:** When you try to communicate to two audiences, both feel unaddressed. When you offer two promises, reader is confused and paralyzes into inaction.

---

## PROMISE + CURIOSITY FORMULA

The most reliable hook structure:

**Promise + Curiosity = Attention**

1. **Promise:** "I have something valuable for you"
2. **Curiosity:** "But you have to read to find out what"

This makes reader think: "I have to know more!" / "How can that be?" / "Sounds awesome."

**Examples:**
- "Cut Your Costs in Half (Here's How)"
- "The Copywriting Formula That Generated $2 Billion (And How You Can Use It)"
- "We Tested 47 Headline Angles—This One Won (By a Landslide)"

## HEADLINE TESTING METHODOLOGY

**Rule:** Never bet on one headline.

### The Process
1. **Write 10+ options** (generate volume first)
2. **Narrow to 5 best** (strongest benefit, curiosity, clarity)
3. **Test winners** (A/B test top 3 against current)
4. **Measure performance** (clicks, opens, conversions)
5. **Use winner, test new** (continuous improvement)

### What to Test
- Benefit vs. curiosity approach
- Specific numbers vs. vague
- Pain point vs. solution focus
- Long headlines vs. short
- Different benefit angles
- "You" language vs. "I/We" language

### Statistical Requirement
- Minimum: 100+ opens for email subject lines
- Minimum: 50+ clicks for landing pages
- Run until pattern clear (not one success)

## CONTEXT-SPECIFIC HEADLINES

### EMAIL SUBJECT LINES (Different Rules)
- Create curiosity first (open = success metric)
- Benefit works but less than landing pages
- Preview text extends your message (use it!)
- Personalization (name, segment) increases opens 20-50%
- Urgency language (expires, limited, today only) works but must be authentic

**Strong email subjects:**
- "Your [specific benefit] is waiting"
- "What [surprising thing] about [topic]"
- "[Name], I found something for you"
- "Quick question before the price goes up"

### AD COPY HEADLINES (Character Limits)
- Extremely limited (6-8 words max for paid)
- Must work immediately (no scrolling)
- Image + text combo critical
- Benefit clarity non-negotiable
- Usually shorter format

**Strong ad headlines:**
- "Finally: The [Benefit] You've Been Looking For"
- "Stop [Pain]. Start [Solution]."
- "[Number] [Benefit] Without [Common Fear]"

### SALES PAGE / LANDING PAGE HEADLINES
- Benefit or curiosity both work
- Longer headlines acceptable (more space)
- Subheadline critical for bridge
- Visual hierarchy matters
- Can use question format

### SOCIAL MEDIA HEADLINES
- Thumb-stopping first
- Often curiosity/controversy wins
- Question format works well
- Benefit clear but less important than stop-scroll
- Shorter is better

## COMMON HEADLINE MISTAKES (WHAT NOT TO DO)

### 1. Too Clever
- Wordplay at expense of clarity
- Reader doesn't understand benefit
- Misses opportunity for attention

**Weak:** "We're Over the Moon About Our New Feature"
**Strong:** "Get Your Work Done 3X Faster"

### 2. Too Vague
- Generic benefit claims
- Could apply to competitor
- No specificity to trigger "this is for me"

**Weak:** "Our Product is Amazing"
**Strong:** "Cut Your Email Response Time From 2 Hours to 5 Minutes"

### 3. Too Broad
- Trying to appeal to everyone
- Violates Rule of One
- Reader thinks "This isn't for me"

**Weak:** "The Complete Business Solution"
**Strong:** "For Solopreneurs: Triple Your Income Without Hiring"

### 4. Overpromise
- Promise you can't deliver
- Damages trust when benefit doesn't match copy
- Leads to high refund rates

**Weak:** "Get Rich Overnight"
**Strong:** "How to Build a $100K Business in 12 Months (Based on Real Case Studies)"

### 5. Benefit Buried
- Feature-heavy opening
- Reader doesn't understand "why should I care"
- Loses attention immediately

**Weak:** "Our Software Uses AI-Powered Algorithms"
**Strong:** "Never Spend 3 Hours on Data Entry Again"

## HEADLINE POWER CHECKLIST

Before finalizing any headline:

- [ ] **Rule of One:** Single promise, single emotion, single narrative
- [ ] **Promise + Curiosity:** Both elements present (not always—sometimes benefit alone)
- [ ] **Clarity Test:** Can 5th-grader understand it? (reading level ~6-8)
- [ ] **Benefit Clear:** Reader knows what's in it for them in 5 seconds
- [ ] **Specific:** Numbers, names, details (not vague adjectives)
- [ ] **Relevance:** "This is for me" trigger present
- [ ] **Authentic:** True to offer, not clickbait
- [ ] **Congruent:** Matches body copy promise
- [ ] **Testable:** Concrete, measurable claim
- [ ] **Memorable:** Sticky enough to share? (bonus)

## QUICK START: HEADLINE GENERATOR

**If you're stuck, use this decision tree:**

1. **Is problem obvious to reader?**
   - YES → Use benefit headline ("Get [Benefit]")
   - NO → Use problem headline ("Struggling with [Pain]?")

2. **Do you have a unique angle?**
   - YES → Use curiosity headline ("The [Surprising Thing]")
   - NO → Use how-to headline ("How to [Outcome]")

3. **Do you have surprising data?**
   - YES → Use number headline ("[Number] [Benefit]")
   - NO → Combine benefit + curiosity

4. **Is this for existing audience?**
   - YES → Can be more specific/assumptive
   - NO → More benefit clarity needed

## KEY PRINCIPLES

1. **Attention first, persuasion second.** Your headline's job is to get read. Body copy does persuasion.

2. **Clarity always beats cleverness.** A confused reader is a lost reader.

3. **Specificity is the credibility signal.** Vague = suspicious. Specific = believable.

4. **Test ruthlessly.** Your guess is worth less than data.

5. **One headline can't win every context.** Test multiple angles; different audiences respond differently.
