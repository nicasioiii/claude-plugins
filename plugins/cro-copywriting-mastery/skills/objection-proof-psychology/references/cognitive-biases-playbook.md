# Cognitive Biases Playbook for Copy

**Source:** Todd Brown (7 Figure Marketing Copy) + Copywriting Made Simple + Copy Hackers

**Ethical foundation:** Persuasion (everyone wins) is not manipulation (only you win). Only use these strategies if you genuinely believe in your offer's value. Truthfulness and transparency are both ethical and legal responsibilities.

---

## 1. Affect Heuristic

**What it is:** Current emotional state affects risk/benefit calculations. Positive emotional state = lower perceived risk, higher perceived benefit.

**Copy rules:**
- ALWAYS conclude on a clear positive note before the CTA
- Associate negative emotions with: competitors, other approaches, inaction, the status quo
- Associate positive emotions with: your offer, your community, the after state
- Warning: intensity must match topic magnitude. Don't use doomsday language for a $19 product

**Copy examples:**

*Negative association (competitors):*
"While other tools leave you staring at a blank screen, wondering if your subject line will even get opened..."

*Positive association (your offer):*
"...our AI analyzes 2.3 million high-performing subject lines and suggests three options tailored to your audience. You'll feel confident hitting Send every time."

*Transition pattern:*
[Paint the negative current state] -> [Introduce your solution] -> [Paint the positive after state] -> [CTA while they feel positive]

**Placement:** Use negative emotions early (problem section). Transition to positive by mid-page. CTA should follow a positive emotional peak.

## 2. Loss Aversion / Prospect Theory

**What it is:** The pain of losing registers at 2x the impact of the pleasure of gaining the same amount. People are irrationally motivated to avoid loss.

**The 2-to-1 Rule:** Perceived benefits must more than DOUBLE the perceived risk of purchase. If the product costs $100, the perceived value must exceed $200.

**Copy rules:**
- Reframe benefits as losses avoided: "Stop losing $X every month" beats "Save $X every month"
- Remove coupon fields if you don't offer coupons (eliminates feeling of missing out on a deal)
- Strike-through features on lower tiers to show what they miss
- Add urgency near discounts/bonuses: "This bonus disappears at midnight"
- Use order bumps available ONLY during checkout (creates "now or never")

**Copy examples:**

*Loss framing (stronger):*
"Every day without [product], you're leaving $47 on the table. Over a year, that's $17,155 you'll never get back."

*Gain framing (weaker):*
"With [product], you could save up to $47 per day."

*Staged FAQ reframing:*
"Q: Is it worth the investment?
A: We understand the concern. [Validate.] The average customer sees a 340% return in the first 90 days. [Answer.] The real question isn't whether you can afford to try it -- it's whether you can afford not to. [Reframe as loss.]"

**Language patterns:**
- "How to avoid the [number] mistakes that are costing you [desired result]" > "Learn how to [desired result]"
- "Don't miss out on..." > "Get..."
- "While this offer lasts..." (implies future loss) > "Special offer..." (neutral)

## 3. Choice Overload / Paradox of Choice

**What it is:** Too many options overwhelm decision-making and reduce purchases. The Iyengar jam study: 6 options = 30% purchase rate; 24 options = 3%.

**Copy rules:**
- Offer no more than 3 pricing tiers per product
- Reduce form fields to bare minimum (every field is a decision point)
- Pre-select the recommended option ("Most Popular" badge)
- Narrow the decision to exactly two choices when possible

**The Rule of Two (Todd Brown):**
Present exactly two choices, even if one is declining:
- "Yes, give me instant access" / "No, I don't want to [benefit]"

A conscious decision is always better than an unconscious page close. When visitors make a deliberate choice (even to decline), they engage more deeply than when they simply close the tab.

**Practical applications:**
- TG Store test: simplifying homepage to just 2 decisions (man/woman, cycling/running) = 96.6% more visitors to shop cycling, 104.5% more to shop running (both at 100% confidence)
- Remove unneeded navigation links on landing pages
- Group related features into packages rather than presenting individually
- Use comparison tables with no more than 3 columns

**Copy example -- narrowing choice:**
"There are hundreds of email platforms. We've spent 3 years testing 47 of them. Here's what we learned: you only need to choose between two approaches. [Approach A] if you [condition]. [Approach B] if you [condition]."

## 4. Forer Effect / Barnum Effect

**What it is:** People rate vague, generic personality descriptions as highly accurate to them personally. Named after P.T. Barnum ("there's a sucker born every minute") and psychologist Bertram Forer.

**Copy rules:**
- Use "Barnum statements" with "you" to create perceived personal connection
- Especially powerful in quizzes that segment audiences
- Works best when the statement FEELS specific but is actually broadly applicable

**Copy examples:**

*Barnum statements for copy:*
- "You're the kind of person who does their research before making a decision."
- "You've probably tried a few things that didn't work before landing here."
- "Deep down, you know you're capable of more than your current results show."
- "You sometimes feel like you're the only one struggling with this."
- "You have a knack for spotting opportunities -- you just need the right tool to capitalize on them."

*Quiz-based implementation:*
Create a "What type of [X] are you?" quiz where every result path:
1. Makes them feel uniquely understood
2. Diagnoses a specific problem
3. Recommends your solution as the fix for their "type"

**Warning:** Do not be so vague that the statement becomes meaningless. The art is in statements that feel targeted but apply broadly.

## 5. Framing Effect / Choice Architecture

**What it is:** People decide based on whether options are presented with positive or negative connotations, not on the options themselves.

### 7 Framing Tactics

**Tactic 1: Contextual Savings Frame**
Compare cost to an everyday purchase:
- "For less than a cup of coffee per day, you get access to the entire platform."
- "That's less than your Netflix subscription -- and this actually makes you money."

**Tactic 2: Picking the Right Number**
Good outcomes = use the larger frame. Bad outcomes = use the smaller frame.
- GOOD: "200% growth" (not "2x growth")
- GOOD: "Saves 8 hours every week" (not "saves 1.1 hours per day")
- BAD: "Only 1% of your total budget" (not "$10,000")
- BAD: "A fraction of what agencies charge" (not "$5,000")

**Tactic 3: Savings or Penalty Frame**
Highlight savings AND the risk of not taking advantage:
- "Save $200 when you choose annual billing. But more importantly: if you wait, this price goes up on [date]."

**Tactic 4: Good vs. Evil Frame**
Position your offer as the hero's tool, problems/competition as the villain:
- "While big agencies milk budgets with vague 'strategy sessions,' [product] gives you a concrete plan in 24 hours."

**Tactic 5: Us vs. Everyone Else Frame**
Position purchase as joining a community that stands for positive values:
- "Join 12,000 marketers who refused to accept 'good enough' and built something better."

**Tactic 6: Unique in a Sea of The Same Frame**
Frame as innovative new approach; build argument for why new is better and old leads to disaster:
- "Every CRM looks the same. Works the same. Fails the same way. We rebuilt it from scratch, starting with [unique mechanism]."

**Tactic 7: Because I'm Worth It Frame**
Position in terms of customer's entitlement to benefits:
- "You've worked too hard to settle for tools that slow you down."
- "You deserve better than spreadsheets and guesswork."

> **Read `references/framing-tactics.md`** for extended examples of each tactic with before/after copy.

## 6. Mere Exposure Effect / Familiarity Principle

**What it is:** People develop preference for things simply because they are familiar with them. Repeated exposure builds trust.

**Copy rules:**
- "Staying on message" repeatedly and consistently is the game
- Do not turn off campaigns too soon -- familiarity builds over time
- Repeat the main promise over and over in DIFFERENT ways (repetition is good; being repetitious is not)

**Rhetorical techniques for repetition:**

*Anaphora (repeating opening words):*
"You deserve better tools. You deserve better results. You deserve better support."

*Epistrophe (repeating closing words):*
"Our customers get more leads. Our customers get more sales. Our customers get more freedom."

*Symploce (repeating both):*
"When you need results, we deliver results. When you need speed, we deliver speed."

**Application across touchpoints:** The same core message should appear in ads, landing pages, emails, and sales pages -- phrased differently each time but driving the same core promise.

## 7. Endowment Effect

**What it is:** People value things more once they feel ownership. The mere act of possessing something makes it feel more valuable.

**Copy rules:**
- Use possessive language: "your plan," "your dashboard," "your results"
- Free trials leverage this: once they're using it, losing it feels like a loss
- Configurators and customization tools create psychological ownership before purchase
- "Get started with YOUR [product]" not "Try [product]"

**Copy examples:**
- "Your personalized growth plan is ready" (they feel they already own it)
- "Don't lose the progress you've already made" (trial-to-paid messaging)
- "Your [product] is waiting. Claim it now." (possession before purchase)

---

## 8. Reactance

**What it is:** When people feel their freedom of choice is being taken away, they resist. Hard sells trigger the opposite of the intended effect.

**Copy rules:**
- Never tell the reader what they MUST do -- give them a choice
- Frame the decision as theirs: "You get to decide" not "You need to act now"
- The Rule of Two helps here: presenting two options gives a sense of control
- Takeaway selling works because it triggers reactance in YOUR favor: "This might not be for you" makes them want it more

**Copy example -- triggering positive reactance:**
"Honestly, this program isn't for everyone. If you're not willing to put in at least 30 minutes a day, this isn't the right fit. But if you are..."

(Reactance kicks in: "Wait, I AM willing. I can do 30 minutes. Don't tell me I can't.")

**Warning signs of negative reactance:**
- "You MUST act now" (feels controlling)
- "There's NO other option" (removes choice)
- "You'd be CRAZY not to..." (invalidates their judgment)
- Fake countdown timers that reset (breaks trust permanently)

## Quick-Reference: When to Use Each Bias

| Situation | Primary Bias | Secondary Bias |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| Writing the close section | Loss Aversion | Affect Heuristic |
| Designing pricing page | Choice Overload | Framing Effect |
| Writing quiz/assessment | Forer Effect | Endowment Effect |
| Creating urgency | Loss Aversion | Reactance (takeaway) |
| Building trust over time | Mere Exposure | Affect Heuristic |
| Reframing price | Framing Effect | Loss Aversion |
| Free trial conversion | Endowment Effect | Loss Aversion |
| Handling resistance | Reactance | Forer Effect |
