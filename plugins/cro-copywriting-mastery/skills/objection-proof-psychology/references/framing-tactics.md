# 7 Framing Tactics for Copy

**Source:** Todd Brown (7 Figure Marketing Copy -- Cognitive Biases section)

**Core principle:** People decide based on how options are presented, not on the options themselves. The same fact framed differently produces radically different decisions.

**Classic example:** "90% of people who undergo this surgery are fine" vs. "10% of people who undergo this surgery die on the table." Same fact. Radically different perception.

---

## Tactic 1: Contextual Savings Frame

**What it does:** Compares the cost of your product to an everyday purchase the reader already makes without thinking.

**When to use:** Price objection, making a recurring cost feel small, justifying premium pricing.

**Before (raw price):**
"Our plan costs $97 per month."

**After (contextual savings):**
"For less than what you spend on coffee each week, you get a system that generates [result] on autopilot. That's $3.23 per day -- less than a latte, but it actually makes you money."

**More examples:**
- "For the price of one client lunch per month, you get unlimited access to..."
- "That's less than your Netflix subscription -- and this one pays for itself."
- "Skip one takeout dinner this week. That's all it costs to get started."

**Rules:**
- The comparison must be something they ACTUALLY spend on regularly
- The comparison should be something they don't think of as "valuable" (entertainment, convenience)
- Always follow with what they GET for that money (contrast idle spending with productive investment)

---

## Tactic 2: Picking the Right Number

**What it does:** Uses the larger or smaller version of the same number depending on whether the framing should be positive or negative.

**When to use:** Any time you present a statistic, result, or cost.

**Rule:** Good outcomes = LARGE number. Bad outcomes = SMALL number.

### Good Outcomes -- Use the Bigger Frame

| Small Frame (weaker) | Big Frame (stronger) |
|----------------------|---------------------|
| 2x growth | 200% growth |
| Doubled their revenue | Added $147,000 in revenue |
| Twice as fast | 110% faster |
| Cut time in half | Saved 4.2 hours per week |
| 3x ROI | 300% return on investment |

### Bad Outcomes -- Use the Smaller Frame

| Big Frame (scarier) | Small Frame (less alarming) |
|--------------------|-----------------------------|
| $10,000 loss | Only 1% of annual revenue |
| 30% failure rate | 70% success rate |
| 8 hours of setup | Less than one workday |
| $5,000 investment | $13.70 per day for a year |

**Exception:** When you WANT the bad outcome to feel scary (agitating the problem), use the big frame: "You're losing $10,000 every month to [problem]."

## Tactic 3: Savings or Penalty Frame

**What it does:** Combines the positive (savings) with the negative (penalty for not acting). Leverages both gain and loss motivation simultaneously.

**When to use:** Pricing section, urgency section, close.

**Before (savings only):**
"Save $200 when you choose annual billing."

**After (savings + penalty):**
"Save $200 when you choose annual billing. But more importantly: this rate is locked in for life. If you wait and come back later, you'll pay the new rate -- which has gone up every quarter for the past two years."

**More examples:**
- "Lock in today's price of $97/month. After [date], it increases to $147/month. That's $600/year more for the exact same product."
- "Early-bird members save 40% AND get [bonus]. After launch, the bonus disappears and the price doubles."
- "Your 60-day trial starts now at no cost. If you decide it's not for you, cancel anytime. But if you DO nothing, you lose access to everything you've built."

**Structure:**
1. State the saving/benefit (positive)
2. State the penalty for not acting (negative)
3. Make the penalty feel REAL (specific date, specific dollar amount, specific consequence)

## Tactic 4: Good vs. Evil Frame

**What it does:** Positions your offer as the hero's tool and the problem/competition as the villain. Creates a narrative conflict that makes choosing your product feel like a moral victory.

**When to use:** Brand positioning, competitive differentiation, mechanism superiority arguments.

**Before (neutral comparison):**
"Unlike other project management tools, ours is simpler to use."

**After (Good vs. Evil):**
"Most project management tools are built by engineers who've never managed a real project. They add complexity because complexity looks impressive in a demo. [Product] was built by project managers who were sick of tools that create MORE work. We didn't add features. We removed everything that doesn't directly help you ship."

**More examples:**

*Financial services:*
"Wall Street wants you confused. Confusion keeps you dependent on expensive advisors. [Product] makes your money transparent -- because you should understand exactly where every dollar goes."

*Health:*
"The supplement industry profits from your confusion. 47 ingredients you can't pronounce, dosages that don't match clinical research, and labels designed to impress rather than inform. [Product] has 3 ingredients. Each one backed by peer-reviewed research. That's it."

*Education:*
"Traditional courses charge $5,000 for 40 hours of content you'll never finish. We charge $197 for 3 hours of content you'll implement this week. Because information isn't the problem. Implementation is."

**Rules:**
- The "evil" must be real and recognizable to the reader
- Never attack specific competitors by name (attack the APPROACH)
- Your product must genuinely be different from the "evil" you describe
- The contrast should make the reader feel smart for choosing you

## Tactic 5: Us vs. Everyone Else Frame

**What it does:** Positions the purchase as joining a community that stands for positive values. Creates in-group identity.

**When to use:** Community-driven products, membership programs, brands with strong values.

**Before (neutral CTA):**
"Sign up for our marketing platform."

**After (Us vs. Everyone Else):**
"Join 12,000 marketers who refused to accept 'good enough.' While everyone else chases vanity metrics and copies their competitors, our members build systems that produce real, measurable revenue. You're not buying a tool. You're joining a community that holds itself to a higher standard."

**More examples:**
- "We're the 3% of businesses that measure what actually matters."
- "Our customers don't follow trends. They set them."
- "This isn't for everyone. It's for the marketers who take their craft seriously."
- "Most people will read this page and do nothing. The ones who act are the ones who get results."

**Warning:** The "us" must be aspirational but achievable. If the reader feels they CAN'T be part of the group, this backfires.

---

## Tactic 6: Unique in a Sea of The Same Frame

**What it does:** Frames your product as the innovative new approach in a market of identical commodities. Makes sameness the enemy.

**When to use:** Crowded markets, commodity categories, when you have a genuine unique mechanism.

**Before (standard positioning):**
"Our CRM helps you manage customer relationships."

**After (Unique in a Sea of The Same):**
"Every CRM looks the same. Works the same. Fails the same way: your team stops using it after 3 months because it creates more work than it eliminates. We didn't build another CRM. We built the anti-CRM -- a system that does 80% of the work FOR you, so your team actually wants to use it."

**More examples:**

*Email marketing:*
"There are 200+ email platforms. They all do the same thing: let you send emails and pray someone opens them. [Product] doesn't send emails. It sends the RIGHT email to the RIGHT person at the RIGHT moment -- automatically."

*Fitness:*
"Every fitness app tracks your calories and gives you a plan. Then you quit in 3 weeks because the plan doesn't fit your life. [Product] adapts to YOUR schedule, YOUR preferences, and YOUR energy levels -- daily."

**Structure:**
1. Acknowledge the sameness in the market (validates their frustration)
2. Identify the SPECIFIC way sameness fails them
3. Position your approach as fundamentally different (mechanism)
4. Show why different = better for them (benefit)

---

## Tactic 7: Because I'm Worth It Frame

**What it does:** Positions the purchase in terms of the customer's entitlement to benefits. Taps into pride and self-worth.

**When to use:** Premium products, self-improvement, productivity tools, luxury/comfort purchases.

**Before (feature-focused):**
"Our software automates your reporting."

**After (Because I'm Worth It):**
"You didn't build a business to spend your evenings in spreadsheets. You built it to create something meaningful -- and to have time to enjoy what you've created. [Product] gives you those evenings back. Because your time is worth more than manual data entry."

**More examples:**
- "You've worked too hard to settle for tools that slow you down."
- "Your team deserves better than cobbled-together solutions and midnight troubleshooting."
- "After everything you've invested in getting here, doesn't it make sense to invest in the one thing that accelerates everything else?"
- "You've earned the right to work smarter. This is how."

**Rules:**
- Must feel empowering, not indulgent
- Connect the "worthiness" to something they've already accomplished (hard work, expertise, investment)
- The product should be positioned as the TOOL they deserve, not the reward
- Works especially well with achievement-oriented audiences

## Framing in Price Presentation

### Frame 1: One Payment vs. Payment Plan
"One payment of $997" vs. "4 easy payments of $297"
- Use one payment when emphasizing savings ("Save $191 with a single payment")
- Use payment plan when reducing perceived cost ("Just $297 to get started")

### Frame 2: Annual vs. Monthly
"$1,164 per year" vs. "$97 per month"
- Annual sounds expensive; monthly sounds manageable
- Show annual only as the "savings" frame: "Pay annually and save $200"

### Frame 3: Per Unit vs. Total
"$2.50 per day" vs. "$912 per year"
- Use per-unit for ongoing costs (makes it feel small)
- Use total only when comparing to something MORE expensive (anchoring)

## Quick Decision Guide

| Situation | Best Tactic |
|-----------|-------------|
| Price objection | Contextual Savings (#1) + Savings/Penalty (#3) |
| Competitive market | Good vs. Evil (#4) + Unique (#6) |
| Premium product | Because I'm Worth It (#7) + Right Number (#2) |
| Community product | Us vs. Everyone (#5) + Good vs. Evil (#4) |
| Data/results-heavy | Right Number (#2) + Savings/Penalty (#3) |
| New category/approach | Unique (#6) + Good vs. Evil (#4) |
