# Pricing Psychology Playbook

## The Golden Rule of Pricing Copy

If you just come right out with the price, the reader compares it to zero -- the cost of not buying. Always provide context before revealing the price. Context turns "that's expensive" into "that's a great deal."

---

## TACTIC 1: ANCHORING

### How It Works
Mention a high price before stating your actual price. The first number readers encounter becomes their reference point for evaluating every subsequent number.

### Implementation Patterns

**High-end comparison anchor:**
"While premium [solution type] costs $5,000 or more, [Product] gives you the same results for just $497."

**Industry rate anchor:**
"The average [professional] charges $250/hour. At that rate, doing this yourself would cost you $5,000 in time alone. [Product] gives you the complete system for $297."

**Component value anchor:**
"If you bought each component separately -- the [item 1] ($199), the [item 2] ($149), and the [item 3] ($99) -- you would spend $447. Today you get everything for $197."

**Competitor anchor:**
"Other [solution category] tools charge $99/month -- that is $1,188 per year. [Product] is a one-time investment of $297 with lifetime access."

**Time-cost anchor:**
"Most people spend 10+ hours a week on [task]. At even $30/hour, that is $15,600 a year in lost productivity. [Product] eliminates that waste for a one-time investment of $199."

### Rules
1. The anchor must be CREDIBLE -- do not inflate fictional numbers
2. Use actual market prices, competitor prices, or calculated time-cost values
3. The anchor should be at least 3-5x the actual price for maximum effect
4. Place the anchor BEFORE the price reveal, never after

## TACTIC 2: BIGNESS BIAS

### How It Works
Smaller amounts sound insignificant next to larger amounts. Frame your price against the larger outcome it delivers.

### Implementation Patterns

**ROI framing:**
"Your investment of $997 pays for itself the moment you land your first new client worth $5,000+"

**Risk-of-not-acting framing:**
"The average business loses $2,500/month to [problem]. At $497, [Product] pays for itself in less than a week."

**Lifetime value framing:**
"A single customer acquired through this system is worth $3,000 over their lifetime. The system costs $197."

**Problem cost framing:**
"Companies spend an average of $50,000 per bad hire. Our assessment tool costs $99/month."

### Rules
1. The "big number" must be real and verifiable
2. Frame it as a ratio: investment vs. return
3. Use specific numbers (not "thousands" -- use "$4,327")
4. The bigger the gap between cost and value, the more powerful the effect

## TACTIC 3: PENNIES PER DAY

### How It Works
Divide the total cost into the smallest possible time increment. Daily costs feel negligible even when annual costs feel substantial.

### Implementation Patterns

**Daily breakdown:**
"For less than $2.74 a day -- less than a cup of coffee -- you get the complete [system/tool/program]."

**Per-use breakdown:**
"If you use [Product] just 3 times a week, each use costs you less than $0.50."

**Per-result breakdown:**
"At $297 for the year, that is less than $0.82 per day for unlimited [results/access/support]."

### The Coffee Comparison
The most widely used pennies-per-day anchor is the cup of coffee. It works because:
- Nearly everyone buys coffee daily
- It is considered a small, automatic expense
- It frames your price as equally trivial

**But be careful:** The coffee comparison is overused. Consider alternatives:
- "Less than a parking meter charge"
- "Less than a streaming subscription"
- "Less than the gas to drive to work"

### Rules
1. Always compare the daily cost to something they ALREADY spend without thinking
2. Use odd, specific numbers ($2.74, not $3) for credibility
3. Round DOWN when calculating daily cost (use the lowest legitimate number)
4. Only use this for recurring or higher-ticket items -- it feels silly for $9 products

## TACTIC 4: COMPARISON REFRAMING

### How It Works
Compare your price to something the reader already buys without hesitation. This shifts the mental category from "expensive purchase" to "routine expense."

### Implementation Patterns

**Charitable/emotional:**
"For the price of a cup of coffee each week, you could provide clean drinking water for a family."

**Luxury comparison:**
"The cost of one dinner out per month gives you [complete benefit] all year long."

**Subscription comparison:**
"Less than what you pay for Netflix each month, and this actually makes you money."

**Habit comparison:**
"Skip the drive-through twice a week, and [Product] pays for itself."

### Advanced: Compare to the Wrong Thing

Frame your price against the WRONG category to make it feel like a bargain:
- Compare a $500 course to a $50,000 MBA (education category)
- Compare a $200/month service to a $60,000/year employee (staffing category)
- Compare a $50 tool to a $500 consultant session (professional services category)

### Rules
1. The comparison must be something your specific audience relates to
2. Do not compare to needs -- compare to wants (they feel no guilt about wants)
3. The comparison item should be something they buy without deliberation
4. Frame it as a TRADE: "Instead of X, you could have Y"

## TACTIC 5: SUNK COST LEVERAGE

### How It Works
Remind the reader of money they have already spent that your product will help them get more value from. This makes your product feel like it PROTECTS their existing investment.

### Implementation Patterns

**Existing tool leverage:**
"You have already invested in [tool/platform]. [Product] makes sure you actually get the results it was designed to deliver."

**Existing knowledge leverage:**
"You have spent years learning [skill]. [Product] turns that knowledge into actual revenue."

**Existing asset leverage:**
"Your website is already getting [X] visitors. Without [Product], [Y%] of those visitors leave forever. With it, you capture and convert them."

**Existing time leverage:**
"You have already put in the work to build [asset]. [Product] makes sure that work was not wasted."

### Rules
1. Reference a specific investment they have already made
2. Show how your product MULTIPLIES the value of that investment
3. Frame not buying as WASTING their previous investment
4. This works especially well for add-on products and upgrades

---

## TACTIC 6: WHOLE-LIFE COST REFRAMING

### How It Works
Encourage the reader to think about costs over a longer timeframe rather than the upfront amount. Spreads the perceived cost and emphasizes long-term value.

### Implementation Patterns

**Monthly breakdown of annual price:**
"Your annual investment of $1,200 comes out to just $100/month -- less than most [comparable service] charges for a single session."

**Cost per year of ownership:**
"Built to last 10+ years, your per-year cost comes out to less than $30. Compare that to the $150/year you spend replacing cheap alternatives."

**Cumulative savings framing:**
"Over 3 years, [Product] saves you an average of $12,000 in [cost category]. Your investment today: $997."

### Rules
1. Choose the time frame that makes your price look smallest
2. Always pair with a value or savings number to create contrast
3. This works best for durable goods, annual subscriptions, and high-ticket items
4. Be honest about the timeframe -- do not claim 10-year durability if the product lasts 2

## PRICE PRESENTATION SEQUENCE

Use this sequence when revealing the price in copy:

1. **Anchor** -- Establish a high reference point (competitor cost, DIY cost, component cost)
2. **Value stack** -- Show everything included with individual values
3. **Total value** -- Sum up the total value of everything
4. **Price reveal** -- Show the actual price (contrast with total value)
5. **Trivialize** -- Pennies per day or comparison reframing
6. **ROI proof** -- Show how the price pays for itself (bigness bias)
7. **Guarantee** -- Remove remaining financial risk

**Example flow:**
"Hiring a consultant to build this for you would cost $15,000+ [anchor]. You are getting the Strategy Module ($2,997 value), the Template Library ($997 value), the Community Access ($497 value), and 3 Bonus Masterclasses ($891 value) [value stack] -- a total value of $5,382 [total value]. Your investment today: just $497 [price reveal]. That is less than $1.37 per day [trivialize], and most members recoup their investment within the first 30 days [ROI proof]. Plus, you are covered by our 60-day money-back guarantee [guarantee]."

---

## TACTIC 7: THE PRICE-REVEAL DELAY

### How It Works
Never reveal the price until you have built sufficient perceived value. The longer the prospect engages with benefits, proof, and testimonials before seeing the price, the more reasonable ANY price will feel.

### Implementation Rule
On sales pages and VSLs, the price reveal should come AFTER:
1. The full marketing argument (all CPB chunks)
2. The value stack with individual values assigned
3. The total value summation
4. At least one round of social proof

Revealing the price before establishing value forces the reader to compare against zero. Revealing it after lets them compare against the perceived value you have built.

### Anti-Pattern
"Our course costs $997" placed in the first few paragraphs. The reader has no context for why $997 is reasonable and immediately resists.
