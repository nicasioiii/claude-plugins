# Page Architecture & Template Selection

## Core Principle: Copy Before Design, Always

Every page starts with copy, never with design. Message determines structure. Structure determines layout. Layout determines design. Reversing this order is the single most common reason pages underperform.

**Ryan Deiss:** "Great design will never save bad messaging. But great messaging can overcome bad design."

**The Copy-First Design Workflow:**
1. Complete the customer avatar canvas
2. Write the before-and-after grid
3. Draft 10+ headlines using swipe file templates, narrow to 5
4. Define primary and secondary CTAs
5. Select hero imagery based on desired emotional after state
6. Fill out the wireframe template (spit draft)
7. Find 3-5 design inspiration sites
8. Hand wireframe + copy + imagery direction + design examples to designer

## The Page Type Decision Matrix

There are exactly 6 page types. Choose based on what you sell and who the traffic is.

| Page Type | Best For | Traffic Temperature | Typical Length |
|-----------|----------|-------------------|----------------|
| Digital Product LP | Courses, SaaS, digital downloads | Cold to warm | Long (13 sections) |
| Physical Product LP | E-commerce, physical goods | Warm to hot | Medium (10 sections) |
| Service LP | Agencies, consultants, professionals | Cold to warm | Short (6 sections) |
| Listicle LP | Advertorial-style, editorial disguise | Cold | Medium (8 sections) |
| Editorial LP | Story-driven, trust-first | Cold | Medium-Long |
| Homepage | Brand hub, trust checkpoint | Mixed awareness | Medium (11 sections) |

**Decision rules:**
- Selling a digital product or course? Use Digital Product LP
- Selling physical goods? Use Physical Product LP
- Selling services or booking calls? Use Service LP
- Running paid ads to cold traffic and want editorial feel? Use Listicle LP
- Need story-driven trust building before the pitch? Use Editorial LP
- Building your main brand page? Use Homepage template

## The 10/90 Rule (Copy Hackers)

Divide every landing page into two zones:

**Top 10% = Expectation MATCHING**
- Match the visitor's stage of awareness
- Message-match from the ad/email that brought them here
- Answer the 3 subconscious questions in 7 seconds: What is it? Why should I care? What now?

**Bottom 90% = Expectation EXCEEDING**
- Move them to the next awareness stage
- Make the full argument for your offer
- Structure as a persuasive letter parsed into scannable sections

**Overlay a framework on the bottom 90%:**
- AIDA (Attention > Interest > Desire > Action) -- for product-aware visitors
- PAS (Problem > Agitation > Solution) -- for problem-aware visitors

## The Hero Section: Your 84% Zone

Above-the-fold content gets approximately 84% of all attention. 16% of visitors never scroll. Therefore 84% of your time, budget, and effort should go to the above-the-fold message.

### Hero Section Element Checklist

Not every element is required. Use what serves the message.

| Element | Purpose | Required? |
|---------|---------|-----------|
| Primer copy | Extreme clarity -- cereal box descriptor ending with colon | Optional but powerful |
| Headline | One big beautiful promise | YES |
| Subheadline | Unpack the headline with specificity + future pacing | YES |
| Body copy | 1-2 sentences, 20-30 words answering HOW | Optional |
| CTA button | Crystal clear next step | Depends on awareness stage |
| Hero image/art | Visualize the after state | YES |
| Eyebrow copy | Keyword/message match line above headline | Optional |

### Hero Section Rules

1. Do NOT force all elements into every hero -- sometimes just a headline is enough
2. Do NOT use scrolling/rotating hero banners -- auto-scrolling is universally bad
3. Do NOT pack the hero with multiple buttons -- one obvious CTA outperforms (Crazy Egg: single button = 45% boost)
4. Make headline and subhead prominent -- never compete with auto-playing video
5. Hero image: person's eyes should look TOWARD the CTA, not away from it
6. No hero button is sometimes better than a premature hero button (if visitor is not ready to act)

### Image Quality Hierarchy (Ryan Deiss)

| Rating | Approach |
|--------|----------|
| Best | Show customer in ideal after state |
| Better | Show the product clearly |
| Good | Professional-looking image |
| Bad | Distracting, misaligned, egotistical (founder pointing at camera) |
| Acceptable | No image at all (better than a bad image) |

**Advanced technique:** Black-and-white, overlays, and silhouettes invite the viewer to see THEMSELVES as the subject. Literal photos create observer mode; stylized images create participation mode.

## Message Matching Requirements

**Transitional congruence** is the forgotten conversion principle. When the ad and landing page feel like different conversations, conversions die.

### The 3 Gut-Check Questions (Before Launching Any Ad)

1. Is the subject matter on my landing page consistent with what got them interested in the ad?
2. Is the tone consistent and familiar -- does the page feel like the same conversation?
3. Does the design/flow make sense for the viewer based on what they just saw?

### Message Matching Checklist

- Exact words from the ad appear in the hero headline or eyebrow copy
- Same faces/people from the ad appear on the page
- Same color palette and visual style
- Promise made in ad is the first thing delivered on the page
- Emotional tone is consistent (casual ad = casual page, urgent ad = urgent page)

## The 7-Second Test Protocol

Every page must answer 3 questions in 7 seconds or less:

1. **What is it?** -- What do you do? What value do you provide?
2. **Why should I care?** -- Is this for me? What is the benefit?
3. **What now?** -- What action should I take next?

### How to Run the Test
1. Tell the reviewer who they should pretend to be (your avatar)
2. Do NOT explain your solution or business
3. Have them open the page for exactly 7 seconds, then stop
4. Ask the three questions -- do not argue with their answers
5. Document insights and fix

**Reality check:** The 7 seconds in a test environment is MORE focused attention than real visitors give. Real visitors are multitasking, distracted, on mobile.

## Messaging Hierarchy Principles

The order messages appear on the page is NOT universal. It depends on:
- Your One Reader
- Their stage of awareness
- Their level of intent
- Their unique context

**Rules of thumb by awareness stage:**

| Awareness Stage | Lead With | Then | Close With |
|----------------|-----------|------|------------|
| Unaware | Story or problem others have | Education, curiosity | Soft opt-in |
| Problem Aware | Their problem directly | Agitation, solution exists | Opt-in offer |
| Solution Aware | Unique insight into solutions | Your mechanism, differentiation | Free trial or sale |
| Product Aware | Your value proposition | Proof, comparison, overcome objections | Sale |
| Most Aware | The offer directly | Price, urgency, deal | Buy now |

## Three Types of Homepages

1. **Transactional** -- Has an implicit CTA, designed to elicit initial transaction. THIS is what most businesses should build.
2. **Categorical** -- Gateway when business has two fundamentally different markets (e.g., Uber: drivers vs. riders). Still needs transactional pages behind each category.
3. **Consumption** -- No explicit CTA, goal is to maximize readership (blogs, media sites). NOT a high-converting homepage unless your business IS media.

## The Zero Moment of Truth

Google discovered that before buying, customers navigate BACK to your homepage to research your brand. A great homepage probably will not close the sale, but it can absolutely lose the sale.

**Flow:** See ad > Visit product/sales page > Click home button > Evaluate brand credibility > Return to purchase (or leave)

If the homepage is inconsistent, unprofessional, or gives them pause, they leave. The homepage must build trust at this "zero moment."

## Copy-Heavy Beats Design-Heavy

A text-heavy landing page version performed 25% better than a graphic-heavy version. Over a year, that single copy difference was worth over $2 million. The instructors note this was "disappointing as a former graphic designer" but the results were "too dramatic to ignore."

**Rules:**
- No global navigation on landing pages (Copy Hackers)
- Copy-first, design-second, always
- Strip distractions ruthlessly

## Anti-Patterns: What Kills Pages

1. Modeling most-aware brand homepages when your audience is problem-aware
2. Starting with design before messaging
3. Mega-menu dropdowns in navigation
4. Primary CTA buried below the fold
5. Subheadline that merely repeats the headline
6. "Get Started" or "Learn More" as CTA text (too vague)
7. Hero image that conflicts with messaging emotion
8. Person in hero shot looking AWAY from the CTA
9. Making the blog your homepage (unless you are a media company)
10. Getting bored with clear messaging and switching to clever messaging
11. Missing terms of service / privacy policy links (violates ad platform policies)
12. Breaking transitional congruence between ad and landing page
13. Basing copy on a Frankenstein persona instead of a real person
14. No body copy in hero because writer is trying to keep it "clean"
15. Running small A/B tests before testing a big, transformational difference
