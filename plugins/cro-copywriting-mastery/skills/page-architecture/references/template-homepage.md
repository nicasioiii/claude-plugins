# Homepage Templates

**Sources:** Ryan Deiss - Craft A High-Converting Homepage v2 | Eddie Shleyner - Transformational Landing Pages
**Best for:** Main brand page, trust checkpoint, multi-audience gateway

---

## TEMPLATE 1: RYAN DEISS HIGH-CONVERTING HOMEPAGE (11 Sections)

### The Complete Section Order

**Critical rule:** It is less important to follow this exact sequence than it is to have ALL of these elements present somewhere on the page. They can be reordered based on your audience and awareness stage.

**Essential vs. optional:** Only the above-the-fold elements (headline, subheadline, CTA) are truly essential. Everything else is "nice to have" -- do not delay launching because you lack trust elements.

---

#### Section 1: Top Menu
**Purpose:** Navigation and primary CTA placement

**Rules:**
- Logo far left
- Minimal nav links -- bare essentials only
- Primary CTA button far right (where the Western eye stops)
- Consider engaging labels: "Why [Company]?" instead of "About"
- Never create massive dropdown mega-menus
- "Fill the footer, free the top" -- move administrative links to footer

**Layout:**
```
[Logo]     [Nav 1]  [Nav 2]  [Nav 3]     [PRIMARY CTA BUTTON]
```

---

#### Section 2: Headline + Subheadline
**Purpose:** Core message, above the fold. Must answer: What is it? Why should I care?

**Rules:**
- Center and bold
- Headline: Big promise or clear value proposition
- Subheadline: Expand with specificity, add future pacing
- Never compete against a large auto-playing video
- No exclamation marks
- Clarity over cleverness, always

**Use the 12 Homepage Headline Formulas:**
1. "Transform your [existing asset] into [desired end result]"
2. "[Desired end result] for [avatar]" -- simplest, most reliable
3. "We help you [featured action] that [achieves desired end result]"
4. "We help [market/company type] get more out of [known painful action]"
5. "Get more [desired result] from your [existing asset], starting now"
6. "Turn [something they have] into [desired end result]"
7. "Easily [end result] with [feature]"
8. "Make your [constituent group] better at [core skill/benefit]"
9. "[Accomplish desired end result] without [known roadblock]"
10. "Everyone can now [something simple] and [accomplish desired end result]"
11. "Stop [known pain point]. We'll do it for you."
12. "Tired of [known pain point]? [Product] makes it easy to [overcome pain]"

**Process:** Write a headline for EVERY template (aim for 10+), then narrow to 5 for testing.

---

#### Section 3: Hero Shot
**Purpose:** Primary image reinforcing the desired after state

**Image hierarchy:**
1. Best: Customer in ideal after state
2. Better: Product clearly shown
3. Good: Professional-looking image
4. Bad: Distracting, founder-focused
5. Acceptable: No image (better than a bad image)

**Eye direction rule:** If a person is in the shot, eyes look TOWARD the CTA, not away. Simple fix: flip image horizontally.

**Advanced technique:** Black-and-white, overlays, silhouettes invite participation mode. Literal photos create observer mode. Use stylized images when you want the visitor to see THEMSELVES.

---

#### Section 4: Primary CTA
**Purpose:** Above the fold, crystal clear next step

**CTA hierarchy (ranked from best to acceptable):**
1. Lead magnet opt-in
2. Free gift opt-in
3. Register / join free account
4. Newsletter signup
5. Quiz or assessment
6. Schedule a demo
7. Start a trial

**Rules:**
- "Learn more" is too generic -- avoid unless nothing else fits
- Purchase links rarely belong on homepages (save for product pages)
- Crystal clear what happens when they click
- Bad: "Get Started" (started what?)
- Good: "Get the Free Tools" / "Start Your Free Trial" / "Take the Quiz"
- Button color: CONTRAST is the key, not a specific color

**"Do not propose marriage."** The homepage CTA should get them one step closer to a first date, not ask for a lifetime commitment.

---

#### Section 5: Secondary CTA (Optional)
**Purpose:** Ghost button for visitors not ready for the primary action

**Rules:**
- Ghost button style: background-matching, de-emphasized
- Lower commitment: "See How It Works" or "Watch Demo"
- Never make the secondary CTA compete visually with the primary
- Placement: immediately below or beside the primary CTA

---

#### Section 6: Trust Bar
**Purpose:** Quick credibility boost with logos

**Options:**
- Customer logos: "[Company A] [Company B] [Company C] trust us"
- "As featured in" media mentions
- Partner/integration logos
- Customer count: "Trusted by 7,000+ marketing teams"

**Rules:**
- Do not lie about these -- if you do not have real logos, skip this section
- 4-6 logos is the sweet spot
- Gray/muted logos maintain visual hierarchy (the CTA should still be the star)
- Skip this section if you have no legitimate logos to show

---

#### Section 7: How It Works
**Purpose:** 3-step process or feature walkthrough

**Rules:**
- Simply say "How It Works" -- do not be clever with the headline
- 3 steps is ideal -- keep it simple
- Each step: Number + Title + 1-2 sentence description
- Can include a demo video but ALWAYS accompany with text
- This is a BROAD overview -- a dedicated page goes deeper

---

#### Section 8: Who It's For
**Purpose:** Sub-market segmentation with use cases

**Rules:**
- Should already be implied in headline/imagery above the fold
- This section reinforces for visitors who need more convincing
- Especially valuable when serving multiple sub-markets
- Link each sub-market to specific use cases or products
- Format: "For [segment A] / For [segment B] / For [segment C]"

---

#### Section 9: Trust Builders
**Purpose:** Testimonials, customer stories, integration partners

**Categories to include:**
1. Testimonials -- Include avatar diversity so different segments see themselves
2. Customer stories -- More detailed case studies (2-3 sentences each)
3. Integration/partner logos -- Proves legitimacy through association
4. Customer count -- Specific number for social proof

---

#### Section 10: Bottom CTA
**Purpose:** Restate primary call to action at page bottom

**Rules:**
- Same CTA as the hero section
- Can use slightly different copy for variety
- Include a final reassurance line
- This catches visitors who scrolled the entire page and are now convinced

---

#### Section 11: Footer
**Purpose:** Expanded nav, legal, social links

**Must include:**
- Copyright notice
- Physical address (for trust)
- Contact information
- Terms of service link
- Privacy policy link (required by ad platforms)
- Social media links
- Expanded navigation
- Flagship content links

## TEMPLATE 2: EDDIE SHLEYNER TRANSFORMATIONAL LANDING PAGE (7 Dividers)

### The Four Functions

Every divider serves one of four functions:
- **Clarity** -- Visitor understands what is being offered
- **Desire** -- Want for the product/content is intensified
- **Credibility** -- Trust is earned through proof and authority
- **Action** -- Visitor is compelled to take a specific step

---

### Divider 1: Hero (Clarity)

Six elements in order:

1. **Primer copy** -- Extreme clarity descriptor ending with colon
2. **Headline** -- One big beautiful promise
3. **Subheadline** -- Unpack with specificity + future pacing
4. **Body copy** -- 1-2 sentences, 20-30 words answering HOW
5. **Call to action** -- Clear, specific button
6. **Art/imagery** -- Visualize what they get

**Each element has one tight job.** The goal is the "slippery slide" where each element compels the reader into the next.

---

### Divider 2: Fascinations (Desire)

Fascination statements that tease what they will learn or get:
- 6-10 fascinations using the 18 filter templates
- Each fascination promises a benefit without giving it away
- Format: Bullet list with bold lead-ins
- The only options: convert or keep reading more fascinations (win-win)

---

### Divider 3: Social Proof (Credibility)

Testimonials matched to specific claims:
- 3-5 testimonials with photos and names
- Each testimonial should reinforce a specific fascination or claim
- Include diversity of voices and use cases

---

### Divider 4: Authority (Credibility)

Expert endorsements, media mentions, credentials:
- Author/founder bio with credibility markers
- "As featured in" logos
- Expert endorsements or reviews
- Relevant certifications or awards

---

### Divider 5: Feature/Benefit Walkthrough (Desire)

Detailed explanation of what the product includes:
- Break down each component with benefits
- Use visuals (screenshots, mockups, photos)
- Pair each feature with its corresponding benefit

---

### Divider 6: Objection Handling (Credibility)

Address remaining concerns:
- FAQ format or narrative format
- Target the top 3-5 objections
- Include a testimonial per objection when possible

---

### Divider 7: Final CTA (Action)

Close with urgency and a clear call to action:
- Restate the offer
- Add urgency or scarcity
- Include guarantee
- CTA button with benefit-driven copy
- Final reassurance text below button

## Three Types of Homepages (Ryan Deiss)

### 1. Transactional (Build This)
- Has an implicit CTA
- Designed to elicit initial transaction (opt-in, trial, or purchase)
- This is what 90% of businesses should build

### 2. Categorical (When You Must)
- Gateway when business has two fundamentally different markets
- Example: Uber (drivers vs. riders), marketplace (buyers vs. sellers)
- Still needs transactional pages behind each category
- Each category card has its own CTA leading to a dedicated page

### 3. Consumption (Media Companies Only)
- No explicit CTA
- Goal is to maximize on-page time and readership
- Blogs, news sites, media companies
- NOT a high-converting homepage unless your business IS content/media

## Homepage Awareness Matching

Most businesses should target problem-aware or solution-aware audiences on their homepage. Adjust messaging accordingly.

**Case Study -- Slack's Homepage Evolution:**
- Problem-aware phase: "Be less busy" / "48.6% reduction in internal email"
- Solution-aware phase: "A messaging app for teams"
- Product-aware phase: "So yeah, we tried Slack"
- Most-aware phase: "Where work happens" / featuring new features

Each phase required fundamentally different messaging. Do NOT copy a most-aware brand's homepage when your audience is problem-aware.
