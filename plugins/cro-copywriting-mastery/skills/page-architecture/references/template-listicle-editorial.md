# Listicle & Editorial Landing Page Templates

**Source:** $1M Landing Page Blueprint (OnePeak Creative)
**Best for:** Cold traffic advertorials, editorial-style trust building, paid social
**Key insight:** These formats feel like editorial content, not ads. That editorial disguise builds trust differently than direct product pages.

---

## LISTICLE LANDING PAGE

### What It Is

A landing page formatted as a numbered list article. One of the most powerful formats for doubling conversions because it feels like editorial content rather than an advertisement.

### Why It Works

1. Feels like third-party content, not a sales pitch
2. Numbered format is inherently scannable and easy to consume
3. Each "point" is a mini-argument for the product
4. Reader accumulates buy-in point by point
5. BuzzFeed trained the internet to click on listicles -- leverage that pattern

---

### Listicle V1: Third-Party Feel (Sends to Product Page or Checkout)

**Purpose:** Makes it feel like the content was hosted by a third party, not the brand itself.

**Exact section order:**

#### Section 1: 5-to-15-Word Punchy Headline
- Must feel irresistibly clickable
- Think BuzzFeed-style but for products
- Curiosity-driven: the reader MUST know what the points are
- Use numbers: "5 Reasons..." or "7 Things..."

**Headline templates:**
- "X Reasons [Product/Solution] Is Changing [Industry/Activity]"
- "X Things Nobody Tells You About [Problem] (And How to Fix Them)"
- "We Tested X [Product Category] -- Here's What Actually Worked"
- "X [Surprising Adjective] Ways to [Desired Outcome] This [Season/Year]"
- "The X [Product Type] Secrets [Authority Group] Don't Want You to Know"

#### Section 2: Optional Subtitle
- One or two sentences explaining what the listicle covers
- Test with and without -- sometimes removing the subtitle increases curiosity
- Keep it benefit-focused if included

#### Sections 3-7: Points 1 Through 5

Each point follows the same structure:

```
[Point Number]: [Benefit-Driven Title]

[Photo illustrating the point -- product in use, lifestyle shot, or diagram]

[1-2 paragraphs educating on the product, knocking down an objection,
or building trust. Each point should address a DIFFERENT angle:]

Point 1: The core benefit / main value proposition
Point 2: How it solves a specific pain point
Point 3: Social proof / what others are saying
Point 4: Comparison to alternatives / why this is different
Point 5: The guarantee or risk reversal / why there's nothing to lose
```

**Rules for each point:**
- Include a photo illustrating the point
- Write 100-200 words per point
- Educate, don't sell -- the selling happens through education
- Address one objection per point naturally
- Build trust progressively -- by point 5, the reader should be convinced

#### Section 8: The Offer Section

```
[Product name + photo]

[Brief recap: "Ready to [benefit]?"]

[Price + any discount]
[CTA Button: "Buy Now" or "Get Yours Today"]

[Guarantee statement]
[3 value props as bullet points]
[Any bonus or limited-time offer]
```

**Two CTA strategies to test:**
1. Send to the full product landing page (more info, warmer lead)
2. Send straight to checkout (shorter funnel, higher intent)

Test both. Measure by revenue, not just click-through.

---

### Listicle V2: Brand-Owned Feel

Same structure as V1 but with these differences:
- Include brand logo and navigation at top
- More overtly branded design and colors
- Points can be more explicitly product-focused
- Feels like a "reasons to buy" page from the brand itself

**When to use V1 vs. V2:**
- V1: Cold traffic from paid social where editorial disguise is valuable
- V2: Warm traffic, email traffic, or when brand recognition is an asset
- Always test both if budget allows

## EDITORIAL LANDING PAGE

### What It Is

Sells through story-driven, value-packed content. Feels like a trusted magazine article or expert blog post. The product is positioned as the natural solution discovered through the story.

### Why It Works

1. Stories bypass sales resistance
2. Education-based marketing creates grateful buyers
3. The reader feels informed, not sold to
4. Trust is built before the pitch ever appears
5. Particularly effective for cold traffic and skeptical audiences

---

### 3-Step Editorial Framework

#### Step 1: Use Storytelling and Education to Build Trust BEFORE Making the Pitch

**Opening structure:**
- Hook with a relatable struggle or surprising discovery
- Tell a story that mirrors the reader's situation
- Educate on the problem -- why it is worse than they think
- Introduce the insight or discovery that changes everything
- Transition naturally from education to solution

**Rules:**
- 500-1000 words of pure value before any product mention
- Use first person or third person narrative (never second person selling voice)
- Include data points, research, or expert quotes for credibility
- The story should make the reader think "this is exactly my situation"

#### Step 2: Position the Product as the Natural Solution

**Transition structure:**
- Bridge from the story to the product: "That's when I discovered [Product]..."
- Explain how the product embodies the principles from the story
- Show specific features that deliver on the education
- Include testimonials woven naturally into the narrative

**Rules:**
- The product introduction should feel inevitable, not forced
- The reader should think "of course -- this makes perfect sense"
- Keep the product section shorter than the education section (60/40 ratio)
- Include 2-3 testimonials within the product section

#### Step 3: Keep Readers Entertained and Informed Throughout

**Engagement rules:**
- Headlines that hook (use fascination formulas)
- Stories that sell (use the zigzag technique -- never be predictable)
- Woven-in testimonials and social proof (not in a separate section)
- Powerful CTA at the end (and optionally mid-article)

---

### Editorial LP Elements Checklist

- [ ] Compelling headline that reads like a magazine article title
- [ ] Author byline and date (adds editorial credibility)
- [ ] Opening story or narrative hook (500+ words before product mention)
- [ ] Educational content with data points or expert quotes
- [ ] Natural transition from education to product
- [ ] 2-3 testimonials woven into the narrative
- [ ] Product benefits framed as story conclusions
- [ ] Clear CTA at the end
- [ ] Optional CTA in the middle for eager readers
- [ ] Social proof indicators (comments section, share counts, related articles)

---

### Editorial LP Design Tips

- Use a blog-style layout: narrow column, large text, plenty of white space
- Include article-style images (not product shots) in the story portion
- Add an author bio box for credibility
- Consider a "related articles" sidebar or footer
- No flashy design elements -- the editorial feel IS the design
- Reading time indicator at top ("8 min read") sets expectations

## Listicle Headline Strategies

Write buzzy, curiosity-driven titles that stop the scroll:

**Proven patterns:**
1. "[Number] Reasons [Audience] Is Switching to [Product]"
2. "I Tried [Number] [Product Category] -- Only [Number] Was Worth It"
3. "[Number] Mistakes You're Making With [Activity] (And What to Do Instead)"
4. "The [Number] Best [Category] for [Specific Use Case] in [Year]"
5. "What Happened When I Used [Product] for [Timeframe]"
6. "[Number] Things I Wish I Knew Before [Common Activity]"
7. "[Expert/Celebrity] Swears By This [Product Category] -- Here's Why"
8. "The Hidden Truth About [Industry Problem] (And [Number] Ways to Fix It)"
9. "[Number] [Adjective] [Category] That [Surprising Result]"
10. "Why [Number] [Authority Group] Recommend [Product/Approach]"

**Rules for listicle headlines:**
- Always use a number -- odd numbers tend to outperform even (psychological quirk)
- 5-7 points is the sweet spot (enough to feel substantial, not overwhelming)
- The headline must promise value the reader cannot get by guessing the list items
- Include specificity: "[Year]," "[Industry]," "[Audience]" narrows and targets
