# Price Reveal Techniques

## WHY PRICE REVEAL MATTERS

If you do not set up the price correctly, you scare customers away with sticker shock. The price reveal gives them the "excuse" — the justification they will use when explaining the purchase to their spouse, friend, or themselves.

**Core Rule:** If you just come right out with the price, the reader compares it to ZERO (the cost of not buying). Always anchor first.

---

## THE 6-STEP PRICE REVEAL SEQUENCE

### Step 1: Build Value Through the Entire Page
The price should never appear until the reader understands the full value of what they are getting. Every section before the price reveal builds perceived value.

### Step 2: Stack the Value
Break the offer into components, each with an assigned dollar value:

```
Here's everything you get:

Module 1: The Foundation Blueprint .................. ($497 value)
Module 2: The Traffic Accelerator ................... ($397 value)
Module 3: The Conversion System ..................... ($697 value)
Bonus 1: 47 Plug-and-Play Templates ................ ($297 value)
Bonus 2: Private Community Access ................... ($497 value)
Bonus 3: The Quick-Start Blueprint .................. ($197 value)

Total Value: $2,582
```

### Step 3: Show the Anchor Price (Strikethrough)
Display the total value prominently, then cross it out:

```
Total Value: $2,582  ~~$2,582~~
```

### Step 4: Reveal the Actual Price
```
Your Investment Today: Just $497
```

### Step 5: Justify with Honest Reasoning
```
The reason it's only $497 is simple.

[Honest reason — accessibility, community building, ROI potential,
introductory pricing, belief in the product]
```

### Step 6: Show Comparison
Compare the price to:
- The cost of NOT buying (lost revenue, continued pain)
- The cost of alternatives (hiring, other programs, DIY mistakes)
- Everyday purchases they make without thinking

## PRICING PSYCHOLOGY TACTICS

### 1. Anchoring
Mention a high price before stating the actual price. The first number the reader sees becomes their reference point.

**Example:** "While a high-end marketing consultant charges $5,000-$10,000 for this kind of strategy, you're getting the entire system for just $497."

**Rule:** The anchor must be believable. If the anchor is too high, it triggers skepticism instead of value perception.

### 2. Bigness Bias
Smaller amounts sound less significant when placed next to larger amounts.

**Example:** "Our program costs just $997 — a small investment when a single new client could be worth $10,000 to your business."

### 3. Pennies Per Day
Divide total cost into tiny daily fractions to reduce perceived size.

**Example:** "For less than $1.64 a day — literally less than a cup of coffee — you get access to the entire system."

**Math to prepare:** $497 / 365 = $1.36/day. $997 / 365 = $2.73/day. $1,997 / 365 = $5.47/day.

### 4. Comparison Reframing
Compare price to something the reader already buys without thinking.

**Examples:**
- "For the price of a Netflix subscription, you could be building a real business"
- "You spend more than this on takeout every month"
- "That's less than one dinner out with your partner"

### 5. Sunk Cost Leverage
Position the product as helping them get more value from something they have already invested in.

**Example:** "If you've already invested in building your email list, this training shows you how to actually monetize it. Without it, that list is just sitting there costing you money."

### 6. Whole-Life Cost Reframing
Encourage thinking about costs over a longer timeframe.

**Example:** "Over the next 12 months, this works out to just $41/month. And the skills you learn will generate revenue for the rest of your career."

### 7. The Would-You-Rather Frame (Amarsi)
```
Think about it this way...

Would you rather spend the next 6 months trying to figure out
[their current struggle] on your own?

Or...

Would you rather spend the next 6 months implementing
[proven solution] with a clear roadmap?

The choice is entirely yours.
```

## PRICE JUSTIFICATION TEMPLATES

### Template 1: The Accessibility Story
```
What's the investment?

It's only $[amount].

That's it.

The reason it's only $[amount] is simple.

I could easily charge $[higher amount] for this — and many of my
peers charge far more for less comprehensive programs.

But my mission has always been to make this accessible to
[target audience] who are serious about [desired outcome]
but may not have thousands to invest right now.

So I priced it at a point where you can say "yes" without
overthinking — and still get everything you need to [result].
```

### Template 2: The ROI Frame
```
The investment is $[amount].

Now, I know what you might be thinking.

But consider this: if this program helps you [get just one new client /
make just one additional sale / save just one hour per day],
it will have paid for itself [many times over / in the first week /
before you finish Module 2].

And everything after that is pure profit.
```

### Template 3: The Cost of Inaction
```
The investment is $[amount].

But here's what most people don't think about:
What's the cost of NOT doing this?

If you keep [doing what you're doing], you'll keep
[getting what you're getting].

Another [timeframe] of [painful current state].
Another [timeframe] of [specific negative consequence].

That cost — the cost of staying stuck — is far higher
than $[amount].
```

### Template 4: The Comparison Frame
```
You could hire a [professional] to do this for you.
That would run you $[high price] minimum.

Or you could try to figure it out on your own.
But the cost of mistakes — in time, money, and missed
opportunities — is almost always more than $[high price].

Or... you could invest $[your price] in a proven system
that shows you exactly how to do it right the first time.
```

## PAYMENT PLAN FRAMING

If offering a payment plan, frame it to make monthly payments feel smaller:

```
Option 1: One payment of $997 (Best Value — Save $191)
Option 2: 3 monthly payments of $397
```

**Rules:**
- Always show the single payment as "best value"
- Show the savings explicitly
- Never offer more than 3-4 installments for products under $2,000
- Make the first payment equal to or less than subsequent payments

### Frame Control in Price
Three ways to present the same $1,000 price:
1. "One payment of $1,000"
2. "4 payments of $300" (total $1,200 — payment plan premium)
3. "Save $200 with one payment of only $1,000"

Each creates a different psychological frame.

## HIGH-TICKET PRICE STRATEGY

For offers above $2,000-$3,000, consider NOT revealing the exact price on the page.

**Instead:** Mention a price RANGE to filter leads.
- "Investment starts at $2,500 and depends on the scope of your project"
- "Typical engagement ranges from $3,000 to $10,000"

This scares away people who genuinely cannot afford it and creates a sense of premium positioning for those who can.

**Application:** Use this approach when the next step is booking a call, not a direct purchase.

## THE VALUE STACKING PSYCHOLOGY

### Why Value Stacking Works
The 5-10x rule: perceived value should be 5-10x the actual price. At this ratio, the purchase becomes a "no-brainer" — the same psychology as a clearance sale.

### Value Stacking Process (Assadi)
1. Break core offer into sub-components
2. Assign a dollar value to each (what it would cost to buy separately)
3. Add bonuses and assign dollar values
4. Stack everything together
5. Show the total prominently
6. Cross out the total
7. Reveal the actual price

### Assigning Dollar Values
- Base on what comparable standalone products actually cost in the market
- Do NOT inflate wildly — "$10,000 value!!!" for a $47 product destroys credibility
- Amarsi's rule: Give the TRUE value. Do not stick $10,000 of bonuses in a $10 product.
- Use round numbers for components, specific numbers for total
