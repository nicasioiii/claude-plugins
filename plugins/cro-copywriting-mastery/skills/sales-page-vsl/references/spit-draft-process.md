# The Spit Draft & Letter Writing Process

## WHAT IS A SPIT DRAFT?

Borrowed from TV scripting. A spit draft lays out WHAT you will say, not HOW you will say it. It is a structural blueprint that separates strategy (message) from execution (words).

**Why this matters:** The biggest copywriting mistake is jumping straight to writing. Strategy must come first. The spit draft forces you to think about the message before crafting the language.

---

## THE SPIT DRAFT STEP BY STEP

### Step 1: Complete Your Research
Before writing a single word of the spit draft, you must have:
- One Reader defined (who are you writing for?)
- Their stage of awareness identified
- Their level of intent assessed
- One Big Idea selected
- One Promise crafted
- One Offer determined
- Voice-of-customer data collected

### Step 2: Write the Spit Draft
Describe each section of the page in plain language. Do NOT write polished copy.

**Example spit draft for a cashmere cleaning product:**
```
- Eyebrow copy with exact phrase message match from the ad
- Headline introducing the pain of cleaning cashmere
- Line that agitates the expense of dry cleaning
- Three-line story about problems with at-home cleaning attempts
- Introduction of the idea that home solutions exist
- Introduction of the product by name
- Value prop for a pain-aware prospect
- Social proof: 3 customer quotes about ease of use
- How-it-works section: 3 simple steps
- Before/after visual description
- Second social proof block: results-focused testimonials
- Price comparison to dry cleaning costs over 1 year
- CTA with guarantee reassurance text
- FAQ section addressing top 3 objections
- Final CTA with urgency element
```

### Step 3: Review the Flow
Read the spit draft top to bottom. Ask:
- Does the argument build logically?
- Is there a gap where the reader might disengage?
- Does each section naturally lead to the next?
- Is there a clear progression from problem → solution → offer?

### Step 4: Reorder as Needed
Move sections around until the logic flows perfectly. This is much easier to do with descriptions than with polished copy.

### Step 5: Set Aside 2-3 Days
Walk away. Fresh eyes will catch gaps you miss when close to the work.

### Step 6: Return and Fill Gaps
With fresh perspective, identify:
- Missing proof points
- Unclosed objections
- Logical jumps that need bridging
- Sections that feel thin

### Step 7: Write the First Draft
NOW write actual copy for each section. The spit draft is your roadmap. You know exactly what each section needs to accomplish.

## THE LETTER WRITING TECHNIQUE

For the bottom 90% of the page (everything below the hero section).

### Step 1: Write a Persuasive Letter
Write a letter to your One Reader arguing why they should take you up on your offer. Write it in first person, conversational tone, as if writing to a friend.

Do NOT think about page layout, formatting, or structure. Just make the argument.

### Step 2: Parse Into Bullets
Take the letter and break it into individual messages/arguments. Each becomes a bullet point.

### Step 3: Lay Out as a Spit Draft
Arrange the bullets in the order they should appear on the page. This becomes the backbone of the bottom 90%.

### Step 4: Set Aside 2-3 Days
Walk away.

### Step 5: Find Gaps
Return with fresh eyes. Where does the argument have holes? Where would a skeptical reader push back?

### Step 6: Fill Gaps
Add missing proof, testimonials, or supporting arguments.

### Step 7: Write First Draft
Convert each bullet into polished copy.

## THE 10/90 RULE

Divide every landing page into two parts:

### Top 10% = Expectation MATCHING
- Match the visitor's stage of awareness
- Match the exact message from the ad/email they clicked
- Answer: "Am I in the right place?"

### Bottom 90% = Expectation EXCEEDING
- Move them from their current awareness stage to the NEXT stage
- Build the argument for why they should take your offer
- This is where the spit draft and letter writing technique apply

## BRIEF YOURSELF FIRST

Before starting ANY spit draft, complete a landing page brief:

### Landing Page Brief Template
```
ONE READER: [Who specifically are you writing for?]

AWARENESS STAGE: [Unaware / Pain Aware / Solution Aware / Product Aware / Most Aware]

INTENT LEVEL: [Low / Moderate / High]

ONE BIG IDEA: [The single most compelling benefit]

ONE PROMISE: [Measurable, testable commitment]

ONE OFFER: [What you are offering — NOT an incentive]

TOP 3 OBJECTIONS:
1. [Biggest objection]
2. [Second objection]
3. [Third objection]

KEY PROOF POINTS:
1. [Strongest proof]
2. [Second proof]
3. [Third proof]

DESIRED ACTION: [Exactly what you want them to do]
```

**Rule:** Reviewers and stakeholders should sign off on the brief BEFORE any copy is written. This prevents scope creep and misalignment.

## SPIT DRAFT TEMPLATES BY PAGE TYPE

### Digital Product Sales Page
```
1. Pre-headline calling out audience
2. Headline with primary benefit + mechanism
3. Sub-headline expanding the promise
4. Opening paragraph with open loop
5. Problem agitation (3-5 pain points)
6. Introduction and credibility (2-3 sentences max)
7. Origin story / story of discovery
8. Mechanism introduction (what makes this different)
9. CPB Chunk 1: [Claim about why old way fails]
10. CPB Chunk 2: [Claim about how mechanism works]
11. CPB Chunk 3: [Claim about who has succeeded]
12. Introducing the product
13. Product descriptions with odd-number bullets
14. Social proof block 1 (transformation testimonials)
15. "Who this is for" / "Who this is NOT for"
16. More social proof
17. Value stack (everything included with dollar values)
18. Price reveal with justification
19. The Close (exact steps to buy)
20. Guarantee
21. Bonuses (AFTER guarantee)
22. The Re-Close (summary + second CTA)
23. FAQ (top 5 objections as questions)
24. Final CTA
25. Sign off + P.S. / P.P.S. / P.P.P.S.
```

### Service Sales Page
```
1. Headline with result promise
2. Lead capture form with pre-qualifying questions
3. Problem identification and agitation
4. Credibility (brief — 2 sentences)
5. How it works (3-step process)
6. Case study or client result
7. Social proof block
8. Brand story (short — why you do this)
9. "Who this is for" section
10. CTA (book a call / apply)
11. FAQ
12. Final CTA
```

### VSL Script
```
1. Hook (first 30 seconds — equivalent to headline)
2. Problem identification with vivid pain
3. Credibility (brief)
4. "What if" bridge to solution
5. Mechanism introduction
6. CPB Chunk 1
7. CPB Chunk 2
8. CPB Chunk 3
9. Testimonial montage or case study
10. Transition to offer
11. Product reveal
12. Value stack (everything included)
13. Price reveal with comparison
14. Guarantee
15. Bonuses
16. Final summary and CTA
17. Urgency/scarcity close
```

## COMMON SPIT DRAFT MISTAKES

1. **Writing polished copy too early.** The spit draft is intentionally rough. If you are wordsmithing, you are doing it wrong.
2. **Skipping the brief.** Without the brief, the spit draft has no foundation.
3. **Not setting it aside.** The 2-3 day gap between spit draft and writing is where clarity happens.
4. **Ignoring gaps in logic.** If a skeptical reader would say "wait, what?" at any transition, fill the gap.
5. **Forgetting the One Reader.** Every section of the spit draft should serve ONE person.
