# The 12-Point Core Story Framework (Sandy Franks)

Developed over 35 years at Agora Publishing (helped grow to $1B in revenue). Each step is sequential and builds on the previous. This framework has been used for business origin stories, sales letters, feature articles, email sequences, and brand narratives.

---

## STEP 1: STRONG TITLE / HEADLINE

**Purpose:** Trigger the reptilian brain to stop scanning and pay attention.

### The 6 U's Checklist
Score your headline against these. Aim for 4 of 6:

1. **Urgent** — Creates time pressure or immediacy
2. **Useful** — Provides clear value to the target audience
3. **Unique** — Feels different from everything else
4. **Ultra-specific** — Uses precise numbers, dates, amounts
5. **Ultra-relevant** — Directly relatable to the reader (Sandy's addition)
6. **Curiosity-arousing** — Makes the reader need to know more (Sandy's addition)

### Title Rules
- Length: 20-30 words maximum
- Two-part headlines work best for business origin stories
- Headline Analyzer target: 30%+ emotional score (55% is excellent)
- Write at least 10 headlines before choosing; first attempt is rarely final
- Practice: Write 10 headlines a day for 30 days (John Morrow method = 200M+ content views)

### Headline Types
- How-to headline
- Newsy headline
- Direct headline
- Question headline
- Why headline
- Myth/hoax/surprise headline
- Statement/proclamation headline
- Two-part headline (Sandy's preference for stories)

### Proven Headline Examples with Results
- "The Peculiar Tale of the Black Bear That Saved Lives and The Man Who Turned a $54 Investment Into a 2.2 Billion Fortune" — 49% open rate
- "This Husband and Wife Team Found Their Eureka Moment When They Paired Gasoline With a Necessity All Humans Crave" — 48% open rate
- "He Was Nicknamed Godfather of the Modern Day Cruise Industry Which Is Now Valued at 7.25 Billion Dollars" — 47% open rate

## STEP 2: THE INTRODUCTION / OPENING

**Purpose:** Reward the reader for stopping at the title by delivering an equally engaging opening.

**Core Rule:** Must be the OPPOSITE of ordinary. NEVER start chronologically ("was born in..."). The opening must be as attention-grabbing as the headline.

**Length guideline:** 100-600 words for the intro.

### 11 Opening Techniques

1. **Memorable Statement** — An audacious proclamation that stays in the reader's mind
2. **Action Scene** — Drop the reader into the middle of action ("A soldier sits in battle, bombs exploding...")
3. **Character Description** — Describe what they look like, how they think, feel
4. **Imagery - Sight** — Colors, unique shapes, what the place looks like
5. **Imagery - Smell** — Unusual or evocative scents
6. **Imagery - Sound** — Specific, soothing or jarring sounds
7. **Imagery - Taste** — Food descriptions, visceral taste sensations
8. **Imagery - Touch** — Physical sensations the reader can imagine
9. **Question** — Direct ("Who pulled the trigger?") or indirect ("Why do bad things happen?")
10. **Setting Description** — Paint the scene of where the story takes place
11. **Startling Fact** — A data point that stops the reader cold

**Three places to capture attention after the headline:**
- First sentence
- First paragraph
- First page

If you lose them at any of these three points, they are gone.

## STEP 3: ADD CONTENT WITH CONTEXT

**Content** = new information and story elements from your research
**Context** = meaning and connection for the reader (why this matters to THEM)

### The Pattern
1. Add a piece of content
2. Give it context (what it means for the reader)
3. Add another piece of content
4. Give it context
5. Repeat

**Critical Rule:** The writer does ALL the work of connecting dots. The reader just experiences. Never dump information and expect the reader to figure out why it matters. Never present content without context — it confuses.

**Anti-pattern:** Dumping everything at once. Control the pace of revelation.

---

## STEP 4: CREATE A CURIOSITY GAP

Based on George Loewenstein's research at Carnegie Mellon.

**Definition:** The space between information given and information deliberately withheld. This triggers the brain to want to fill in the missing information.

### Three Intensity Factors (Loewenstein & Goldman)
1. **Importance** — How much the information matters to the person
2. **Salience** — How attention is drawn to the gap
3. **Surprise** — Does the new information contradict expectations?

### Direct Curiosity Gap Phrases
- "I'll tell you what this means in just a minute, dear reader, but first..."
- "Next, but first..."
- "Last but not least..."
- "Hold on one minute..."
- "But wait, there's more..."

### Indirect Method
Use subheads as curiosity gaps. Allude to information without stating it. The reader must continue to find out.

### Upworthy's Formula
Too vague = no engagement. Too specific = no need to continue. Just enough information to create the gap = engagement.

---

## STEP 5: PACING AND TONE

**Short sentences/paragraphs** = faster pace, quick rhythm
**Long sentences/paragraphs** = slower pace, more detailed descriptions

### Pacing Rules
- Mix deliberately based on what you want the reader to FEEL at each moment
- The writer controls: what the reader reads next, how they feel, where they go
- Transitions are the MOST critical element — must flow seamlessly
- Use subheads in stories just like in sales copy to aid transitions
- Dialogue can heighten conflict or urgency in business origin stories

### Reading Aloud Test
Sandy Franks and Addison Wiggin would sit together reading copy aloud, editing in real time. If a transition feels bumpy when spoken, rewrite it.

---

## STEP 6: CONNECT STORY TO TITLE

At some point in the story, you MUST connect back to the title.

### Connection Methods
- **Direct:** Use actual words from the title in the story
- **Indirect:** Allude to the title's promise without repeating exact words
- Connection can appear in beginning, middle, or end

**Why this matters:** The title is what engaged the reader. The connection is their reward for continuing. Without it, the title feels like a broken promise.

---

## STEP 7: CLOSE THE CURIOSITY GAP

The gap opened in Step 4 MUST be closed.

### Closing Rules
- Reveal the withheld information at a strategic point
- Close it AFTER you have delivered additional value between the open and close
- The reader has been waiting for this answer while reading other content
- Never leave a gap unclosed — it frustrates the reader

---

## STEP 8: ADD CONFLICT

Every story needs conflict, struggle, or a problem. Find it in your research — there is always one.

### Six Types of Conflict
1. **Person vs. Person** — Clash between individuals
2. **Person vs. Nature** — Struggle against natural forces
3. **Person vs. Self** — Internal struggle, doubt, bad habits
4. **Person vs. Society** — Fighting institutions, norms, "the system"
5. **Person vs. Technology** — Battling tools, algorithms, machines
6. **Person vs. Supernatural** — Unexplained forces

### Conflict Rules
- Zero in on the SPECIFIC conflict and dramatize it
- Make it relatable to the reader; make it feel BIG
- **WARNING:** Do NOT exaggerate conflict to the point of paralysis. There is a fine line between elevating the problem and making it feel unsolvable.

---

## STEP 9: PROVIDE RESOLUTION

After establishing conflict, offer resolution.

- The resolution shows HOW the problem was overcome
- In copywriting: the product/service IS the resolution
- In business origin stories: how the founder overcame the obstacle
- The resolution must feel earned — not handed on a plate

---

## STEP 10: KEEP READER IN ZONE OF CURIOSITY

After resolution, maintain engagement through to the pivotal moment.

### Three Zones
- **Zone of Relaxation** — Too comfortable, stop reading
- **Zone of Curiosity** — Sweet spot: engaged, wanting more
- **Zone of Anxiety** — Too stressed, stop reading

**Goal:** Keep the reader in the Zone of Curiosity after the resolution. Use additional details, secondary conflicts, or new revelations to maintain interest.

---

## STEP 11: CREATE A PIVOTAL MOMENT

The transformation point in the story. The "aha moment" that changes everything.

- In business stories: the moment the product was created, the breakthrough occurred, the company transformed
- This is the emotional climax of the story
- The reader should feel the weight of this moment

---

## STEP 12: WRAP UP THE STORY

- Tie together all loose ends
- Reader should feel satisfied and complete
- All elements connect back to create a cohesive narrative
- The ending should leave the reader changed in some way

---

## THE 6 RESEARCH QUESTIONS (Apply Repeatedly)

Use these to build rich stories through deep research:

- **WHO:** Central character, supporters, stakeholders, personality traits
- **WHAT:** What happened, reactions, conclusions, what NEVER happened
- **WHERE:** Setting, movements, what the place is similar to
- **WHEN:** Timeline, challenges, will it happen again
- **WHY:** Why this choice, why does it matter, why now
- **HOW:** How is it possible, how do they feel, how could it have been different

**Apply in LAYERS** — answer one question, then ask again to go deeper (Tree Branch Effect). One finding leads to another, which leads to another. This is how rich stories are built.

---

## THE ONE-SHEET METHOD

As you gather research, organize fascinating details on a single sheet of paper. Place details where you think they will go in the story (intro detail here, ending detail there). This creates an informal outline that makes writing flow faster.

---

## IMPLEMENTATION CHECKLIST

Before writing your story, confirm:
- [ ] You have a headline that scores 4+ on the 6 U's
- [ ] Your opening is NOT chronological
- [ ] You have identified the central conflict
- [ ] You know where the curiosity gap will open and close
- [ ] You have enough research for rich, sensory details
- [ ] You know the pivotal moment (transformation point)
- [ ] You have planned the zigzag — at least 2 redirects
- [ ] Your story connects back to the title
- [ ] You have a satisfying ending that ties loose ends
