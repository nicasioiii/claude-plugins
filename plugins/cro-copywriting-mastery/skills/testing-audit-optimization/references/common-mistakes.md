# Common Copy Mistakes by Workflow Stage

Synthesized from all 8 source courses (13+ expert instructors). Organized by the workflow stage where the mistake occurs.

---

## FOUNDATION STAGE MISTAKES

### Mistake: Writing for Everyone Instead of One Reader
**What it looks like:** Vague copy that could apply to any business. "We help companies succeed."
**Why it happens:** Fear of excluding potential customers.
**The fix:** Write for the 20-35% of traffic most likely to be your ideal customer. Multiple landing pages for different segments beats one catch-all page.
**Source:** Copy Hackers (Rule of One)

### Mistake: Skipping Research
**What it looks like:** Copy based on assumptions about what the audience wants. Generic language that doesn't mirror how customers actually talk.
**Why it happens:** Research is the least enjoyable part. Writers want to write, not research.
**The fix:** Spend 60-80% of project time on research. 7 interviews = 500 survey responses worth of insight. Review mine before writing a single word.
**Source:** Todd Brown, Copy Hackers

### Mistake: Modeling Most-Aware Brands for Problem-Aware Audiences
**What it looks like:** Copying Apple, Nike, or Slack's homepage when your audience has never heard of you. Leading with features when they don't even know they have a problem.
**Why it happens:** Aspirational benchmarking without awareness-stage analysis.
**The fix:** Identify your audience's awareness stage first. If they're problem-aware, lead with the problem. Save product-aware messaging for product pages.
**Source:** Ryan Deiss, Eddie Shleyner

### Mistake: Selling a Commodity Without a Unique Mechanism
**What it looks like:** "We do Facebook ads" or "Try our project management tool." The prospect Googles the generic term and finds 1,000 alternatives.
**Why it happens:** Failure to interrogate the method. Naming a process but not understanding WHY it's different.
**The fix:** Every product has something unique. Find it through the mechanism interrogation process. Name it. Prove its superiority.
**Source:** Todd Brown (Google Slap test)

## STRATEGY STAGE MISTAKES

### Mistake: No Campaign Thesis
**What it looks like:** Copy that jumps from problem to offer without establishing WHY the mechanism is the best solution. No clear buying belief.
**Why it happens:** Skipping the 75/25 rule. Going straight to selling without educating.
**The fix:** Write your Campaign Thesis: "The [statement of superiority] way to [primary promise] is with [unique mechanism]." Then spend 75% of the copy proving it.
**Source:** Todd Brown

### Mistake: Multiple Big Ideas on One Page
**What it looks like:** "Save time AND money AND reduce risk AND increase productivity AND..." The page tries to be everything.
**Why it happens:** Fear of leaving benefits on the table.
**The fix:** Pick ONE big idea. Go hard on convincing the reader of that one thing. Other benefits are ancillary.
**Source:** Copy Hackers (Rule of One)

### Mistake: Selling the Product Instead of the Transformation
**What it looks like:** Features and specs dominate. No description of how the customer's life changes.
**Why it happens:** Product knowledge bias. You know the product so well you forget the customer doesn't care about it -- they care about themselves.
**The fix:** Ask: "What does their life look like in 6 months, 1 year, 5 years?" Sell THAT.
**Source:** Arman Assadi

## WRITING STAGE MISTAKES

### Mistake: Adjective-Driven Copy Instead of Verb-Driven Copy
**What it looks like:** "Our amazing, incredible, revolutionary tool provides outstanding results."
**Why it happens:** Adjectives feel like they're adding emphasis. They're not -- they're adding noise.
**The fix:** Replace adverb + weak verb with a single strong verb. "Slowly walk" -> "strut." "Get more leads" -> "Flood your business with leads."
**Source:** Todd Brown

### Mistake: Passive Voice Throughout
**What it looks like:** "Clicks were generated" instead of "The ad attracted clicks." "Passengers were asked to return" instead of "The captain forced passengers back."
**Why it happens:** Academic writing habits. Corporate voice. Playing it safe.
**The fix:** Convert every sentence to active voice. Subject -> Verb -> Object.
**Source:** Todd Brown

### Mistake: Faux Benefits
**What it looks like:** "Flush fat from your intestinal tract." Sounds like a benefit but is at least one step removed from what the prospect actually wants.
**Why it happens:** Stopping at the functional benefit instead of pushing to the emotional benefit.
**The fix:** Add bridge phrases: "So you can..." / "Which means you..." until you reach the real desire.
**Source:** Todd Brown

### Mistake: Reading Level Too High
**What it looks like:** Complex sentences, industry jargon, compound clauses, Flesch-Kincaid score above 10.
**Why it happens:** Trying to sound smart. Writing for peers instead of prospects.
**The fix:** Write at middle-school reading level (FK < 8). Short sentences. Short paragraphs. Simple words.
**Source:** Todd Brown, Arman Assadi

### Mistake: Emoting Instead of Evoking
**What it looks like:** "I'm SO excited to tell you about..." / "We're thrilled to announce..."
**Why it happens:** Confusing YOUR excitement with THEIR excitement.
**The fix:** Evocative, not emotive. Stimulate THEIR emotions, don't share yours. "You'll be thrilled when you see..." not "We're thrilled to show you..."
**Source:** Todd Brown

### Mistake: Cleverness Over Clarity
**What it looks like:** Puns, wordplay, metaphors that obscure the message. "When we experience true breakthrough, it changes everything."
**Why it happens:** Creative instinct overriding conversion instinct.
**The fix:** "Clever is a bad word in direct response." Write clearly. Clarity ALWAYS beats cleverness.
**Source:** Ryan Deiss, Eddie Shleyner, Copy Hackers

## CONVERSION STAGE MISTAKES

### Mistake: Claims Without Proof
**What it looks like:** "Best in class." "Industry-leading." "Revolutionary." No evidence anywhere.
**Why it happens:** Assuming the prospect will take your word for it.
**The fix:** No claim without proof. Use reason-why for mechanism claims, testimonials for product claims. Apply the Proof Power of Three for bold claims.
**Source:** Todd Brown

### Mistake: Generic Testimonials in a Generic Section
**What it looks like:** A "What Customers Say" section with three vague quotes: "Great product!" "Loved it!" "Highly recommend!"
**Why it happens:** Collecting testimonials without a strategy for using them.
**The fix:** Match each testimonial to a specific claim on the page. Place it adjacent to that claim. Only use testimonials with specific results.
**Source:** Todd Brown, Copy Hackers

### Mistake: CTA Says "Submit"
**What it looks like:** Button text that describes the action (Submit, Enter, Click Here) instead of the value.
**Why it happens:** Default form button text. Nobody changed it.
**The fix:** Replace with value-driven, first-person copy: "Get my free guide," "Show me my plan," "Start my trial."
**Source:** Copy Hackers

### Mistake: Coupon Code Fields Visible When No Coupons Are Offered
**What it looks like:** Empty coupon field on the checkout page.
**Why it happens:** Default e-commerce platform settings.
**The fix:** Hide or remove the field. It increases cart abandonment by 8-27%.
**Source:** Copy Hackers

### Mistake: Global Navigation on Landing Pages
**What it looks like:** Full site navigation at the top of a landing page.
**Why it happens:** Using the same template for every page.
**The fix:** Remove all navigation on landing pages. Nothing on the page that is not directly tied to the conversion goal.
**Source:** Copy Hackers

### Mistake: Proposing Marriage on the Homepage
**What it looks like:** Hard "Buy Now" CTA to unaware or problem-aware homepage visitors.
**Why it happens:** Treating the homepage like a sales page.
**The fix:** Match CTA intensity to awareness stage. Unaware visitors need "Learn more," not "Buy now."
**Source:** Ryan Deiss

## TESTING STAGE MISTAKES

### Mistake: Testing Button Colors Before Testing the Offer
**What it looks like:** Running A/B tests on button color when the offer itself is weak.
**Why it happens:** Button tests are easy to set up. Offer tests are hard.
**The fix:** Follow the test priority hierarchy. Test big things first (offer, headline, page type). Micro-tests last.
**Source:** Synthesized

### Mistake: Exposing 100% of Traffic to Untested Changes
**What it looks like:** Redesigning the entire page and launching it to everyone.
**Why it happens:** Confidence in the new design. Impatience with testing.
**The fix:** Split test. Even 80/20 is better than 100/0. Protect your baseline.
**Source:** Copy Hackers

### Mistake: Declaring Winners Too Early
**What it looks like:** Calling a test after 3 days because one variant is "winning."
**Why it happens:** Impatience. Excitement about early results.
**The fix:** Run for minimum 2 weeks. Wait for statistical significance (95% confidence). Account for day-of-week patterns.
**Source:** Copy Hackers

### Mistake: Ignoring Learnings from Losing Tests
**What it looks like:** Deleting losing variants without analysis.
**Why it happens:** Bias toward "wins" over learnings.
**The fix:** Document every test result -- wins AND losses. Ask "Why did this lose?" The answer often reveals something more valuable than the win itself.
**Source:** Copy Hackers

### Mistake: Never Testing at All
**What it looks like:** Running the same page for months or years without any experimentation.
**Why it happens:** Lack of traffic, lack of tools, lack of process, or belief that "it's fine."
**The fix:** Start with qualitative tests (5-second test, 7-second test) if traffic is too low for A/B. Even before/after measurement is better than nothing.
**Source:** Synthesized

## Quick Wins: Fixes That Typically Produce Fast Results

If you only have time for 5 fixes, do these:

1. **Rewrite the headline** to be benefit-driven and specific (not clever)
2. **Add 3 specific testimonials** matched to your top 3 claims
3. **Change CTA to first-person value copy** ("Get my [result]" not "Submit")
4. **Remove global navigation** from landing pages
5. **Add reassuring text below every CTA button** ("No credit card required" / "Cancel anytime")

These five changes alone, applied to an average page, typically produce 15-40% improvement.

## STORYTELLING AND NARRATIVE MISTAKES

### Mistake: Starting the Story Chronologically
**What it looks like:** "John Smith was born in 1975 in a small town..." The reader falls asleep before anything interesting happens.
**Why it happens:** Defaulting to chronological order feels natural.
**The fix:** Start in the middle of the action or at the moment of highest tension. Sandy Franks rule: never start at the beginning.
**Source:** Sandy Franks (Core Story Framework)

### Mistake: Exaggerating Conflict to the Point of Paralysis
**What it looks like:** The problem is painted so catastrophically that the prospect feels frozen and hopeless rather than motivated to act.
**Why it happens:** Writers push pain agitation too far thinking more pain equals more sales.
**The fix:** Elevate the problem to create urgency, but always leave a visible path to resolution. The prospect must feel the pain is solvable, not terminal.
**Source:** Sandy Franks

### Mistake: Content Without Context
**What it looks like:** Dumping facts, stats, or story elements without explaining why they matter to the reader.
**Why it happens:** The writer knows why it matters but does not connect the dots for the reader.
**The fix:** For every piece of content (fact, detail, story point), immediately provide context: what this means for YOU. The writer does ALL the connecting work. The reader just experiences.
**Source:** Sandy Franks (Content + Context principle)

## EMAIL AND SEQUENCE MISTAKES

### Mistake: Not Setting Selling Expectations in Email 1
**What it looks like:** The welcome email delivers the lead magnet but never mentions that future emails will include offers. Then the first sales email feels like a betrayal.
**Why it happens:** Fear that mentioning selling will scare subscribers away.
**The fix:** In the first email, explicitly state: "I will sell you things over the course of our relationship" and explain why. This builds trust through transparency and sets the frame for future sales emails.
**Source:** Adil Amarsi (Transparent Selling Philosophy)

### Mistake: Using Even-Numbered Bullet Lists on Opt-In Pages
**What it looks like:** 4 or 6 bullets describing the lead magnet.
**Why it happens:** Not knowing the rule.
**The fix:** Always use odd numbers (3, 5, or 7 bullets). Odd numbers feel more credible and less "designed." End with "And so much more..." as the final bullet.
**Source:** Adil Amarsi (Opt-In Page Formula)
