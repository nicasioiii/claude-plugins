# 20-Point Copy Audit Checklist

## Overview

This is a **detailed audit of copy quality** independent of design. It evaluates whether the actual words on your page are persuasive, clear, and conversion-focused.

**How to Use:** Go through each of the 20 points. For each, mark it as:
- ✅ **PASS** (meets standard)
- ⚠️ **PARTIAL** (partially meets standard)
- ❌ **FAIL** (doesn't meet standard)

**Scoring:**
- **18-20 PASS:** Excellent copy
- **14-17 PASS:** Good copy with minor issues
- **10-13 PASS:** Adequate copy with significant issues
- **<10 PASS:** Weak copy; major rewrite needed

---

## SECTION A: HEADLINE & OPENING (Points 1-3)

### Point 1: Headline Clarity

**Standard:** The headline answers "What is this?" clearly in 7 seconds

**Criteria:**
- ✅ Visitor immediately knows what you do/offer
- ✅ No jargon or confusing language
- ✅ Specific (not vague)
- ✅ Benefit-focused (not feature-focused)

**Bad Example:** "Innovative Cloud Solutions"
- Vague, unclear what it does, no benefit

**Good Example:** "Get More Leads From Your Website in 30 Days—Guaranteed"
- Clear what it does, specific timeline, benefit stated

**How to Test:**
Show headline to 5 people unfamiliar with your business. Ask: "What does this company do?" If more than 2 people are confused, it fails.

**Status:** ☐ PASS ☐ PARTIAL ☐ FAIL

---

### Point 2: Headline Benefit

**Standard:** The headline includes a clear benefit or result

**Criteria:**
- ✅ Focuses on outcome for customer (not product feature)
- ✅ Speaks to desires or pain points
- ✅ Benefit is specific, not generic ("More" is generic; "$50K" is specific)
- ✅ Benefit is desirable to target customer

**Bad Example:** "New Software Cuts Processing Time"
- Feature-focused; benefits unclear

**Good Example:** "Get 20 Extra Hours Per Week—Automated"
- Clear benefit; speaks to what customer values

**How to Test:**
For every headline, ask: "What does the customer GET as a result?" If you can't answer in one sentence, the benefit isn't clear.

**Status:** ☐ PASS ☐ PARTIAL ☐ FAIL

---

### Point 3: Opening Paragraph Delivery

**Standard:** Opening paragraph delivers on headline promise immediately

**Criteria:**
- ✅ First sentence continues headline message (doesn't start over)
- ✅ Opens with hook/curiosity or direct problem statement
- ✅ Avoids corporate fluff ("At [Company], we believe...")
- ✅ Uses short, punchy sentences (not long, complex ones)
- ✅ Speaks to reader directly ("You" language, not "one might")

**Bad Example:**
"At ABC Company, we pride ourselves on delivering innovative solutions to clients worldwide. With over 20 years in the industry..."
- Generic corporate opening; wastes time before delivering value

**Good Example:**
"You're probably wasting 10+ hours per week on tasks that could be automated. Let's fix that."
- Direct, speaks to reader, immediately relevant

**How to Test:**
Read opening 3 sentences. Do they continue the headline promise or start a new topic? If new topic, it fails.

**Status:** ☐ PASS ☐ PARTIAL ☐ FAIL

---

## SECTION B: CLARITY & MESSAGE (Points 4-7)

### Point 4: Target Customer Definition

**Standard:** It's clear WHO this is for (and implicitly, who it's NOT for)

**Criteria:**
- ✅ Specific audience identified (not "everyone")
- ✅ Avatar characteristics mentioned (role, pain point, goal)
- ✅ Visitor sees themselves in the copy
- ✅ Implicitly tells wrong customers they're not the fit

**Bad Example:** "Solutions for all businesses"
- Too broad; anyone could be target

**Good Example:** "For agency owners generating $1M-$5M revenue struggling to hit margins"
- Specific; clear who benefits and who doesn't

**How to Test:**
Ask: "Who would benefit from this?" If answer is "almost everyone," the targeting fails.

**Status:** ☐ PASS ☐ PARTIAL ☐ FAIL

---

### Point 5: Problem Clarity

**Standard:** The core problem is stated explicitly and viscerally

**Criteria:**
- ✅ Problem is named directly (not vague)
- ✅ Problem is explained with emotional/sensory language (paint a picture)
- ✅ Reader feels the pain ("Yes, that's exactly my problem")
- ✅ Problem speaks to desires or fears (not abstract)

**Bad Example:** "Inefficient workflows reduce productivity"
- Abstract, intellectual; doesn't feel like a problem

**Good Example:** "You're stuck in endless meetings, drowning in emails, and losing track of what actually matters. It's exhausting."
- Visceral, emotional, reader feels it immediately

**How to Test:**
Read the problem statement to someone in your target market. Do they nod and say "Yes, that's exactly my problem"? If not, the problem needs to be more explicit and visceral.

**Status:** ☐ PASS ☐ PARTIAL ☐ FAIL

---

### Point 6: Solution Clarity

**Standard:** What you're selling/offering is crystal clear

**Criteria:**
- ✅ Solution is named specifically (not vague)
- ✅ What it does is explained simply (one sentence summary)
- ✅ How it solves the stated problem is shown
- ✅ Not focused on features; focused on outcome

**Bad Example:** "Our proprietary platform leverages AI to optimize workflow automation"
- Feature-heavy; unclear what problem it solves

**Good Example:** "A system that handles the busywork automatically, freeing you up to do work that matters"
- Clear what it does; directly solves stated problem

**How to Test:**
Can you explain the solution in one plain-English sentence? If not, it's not clear enough.

**Status:** ☐ PASS ☐ PARTIAL ☐ FAIL

---

### Point 7: Feature-to-Benefit Translation

**Standard:** Features are translated to customer benefits

**Criteria:**
- ✅ Every feature followed by "so you can..." or "which means..."
- ✅ Benefit speaks to customer outcome, not product capability
- ✅ Benefits are specific and measurable
- ✅ No features left unexplained (features without benefits)

**Bad Example:**
- Feature: "Automated scheduling system"
- (No benefit stated)

**Good Example:**
- Feature: "Automated scheduling system"
- Benefit: "So you can forget about managing calendars and never double-book a meeting again"

**How to Test:**
List every feature mentioned. For each, ask: "What does the customer GET as a result?" If you can't answer, the translation is missing.

**Status:** ☐ PASS ☐ PARTIAL ☐ FAIL

---

## SECTION C: PERSUASION & PROOF (Points 8-12)

### Point 8: Proof Presence

**Standard:** Claims are backed by proof (proof hierarchy present)

**Criteria:**
- ✅ Main claims have proof (not unsupported assertions)
- ✅ Multiple types of proof (social proof, claim proof, data proof)
- ✅ Proof is specific and credible (numbers, names, sources)
- ✅ Rule of Three applied (3 pieces of evidence = pattern)

**Bad Example:**
"This system works great! Customers love it."
- No proof; just assertion

**Good Example:**
"96% of customers see 50%+ improvement in [metric] within 30 days. John Smith, CEO of ABC Corp, increased revenue by $200K. Independent study by XYZ Institute confirms [claim]."
- Multiple proof types; specific; credible

**How to Test:**
Look for every claim made. Does each have proof? If not, identify which claims need proof added.

**Status:** ☐ PASS ☐ PARTIAL ☐ FAIL

---

### Point 9: Testimonial Specificity

**Standard:** Testimonials include specific results (not vague praise)

**Criteria:**
- ✅ Testimonials mention specific results (numbers, metrics)
- ✅ Include name and credible identifier (title, company)
- ✅ Sound authentic (specific language, natural flow)
- ✅ Multiple testimonials from different customer types

**Bad Example:**
"Great product! Highly recommend!" — John D.
- Vague; no credibility; no results

**Good Example:**
"Implemented the system on Monday, closed my first $50K deal by Friday. Game-changer." — Sarah Chen, Sales Director, TechCorp
- Specific results; credible source; authentic language

**How to Test:**
Read testimonials aloud. Do they sound like real people? Do they include specific results? If answers are no, testimonials need improvement.

**Status:** ☐ PASS ☐ PARTIAL ☐ FAIL

---

### Point 10: Social Proof Diversity

**Standard:** Social proof comes from varied sources (not all same type)

**Criteria:**
- ✅ Different industries/company sizes represented
- ✅ Different use cases shown (beginners, advanced, different applications)
- ✅ Different proof types used (quotes, logos, case studies, data)
- ✅ Social proof embedded throughout (not just at bottom)

**Bad Example:**
Five testimonials from agency owners all saying "Great product."
- Same avatar; same proof type; repetitive

**Good Example:**
- Case study from beginner in freelance services (before-after)
- Logo from Fortune 500 company (social proof)
- Testimonial from advanced user reaching 6-figures (results)
- Statistic: "92% of customers succeed within 90 days" (data proof)

**How to Test:**
Look at all social proof. Are different audiences represented? Different proof types? If mostly one type, diversity is weak.

**Status:** ☐ PASS ☐ PARTIAL ☐ FAIL

---

### Point 11: Objection Preemption

**Standard:** Main objections are addressed in copy BEFORE they kill the sale

**Criteria:**
- ✅ Common objections identified
- ✅ Addressed preemptively (not waiting for customer to ask)
- ✅ Each objection has proof-based answer
- ✅ Objection section feels conversational (not defensive)

**Common Objections to Address:**
- Price/cost
- Time/effort
- Risk/does it really work?
- Relevance/is this for me?
- Legitimacy/trust

**Bad Example:**
No mention of pricing; no mention of how long it takes; no guarantee
- Objections left unaddressed

**Good Example:**
- Q: "Is this too expensive?" A: "At $500, it pays for itself in [timeframe]..."
- Q: "Will I have time for this?" A: "Setup takes 15 minutes..."
- Q: "What if it doesn't work?" A: "60-day money-back guarantee..."

**How to Test:**
Ask: "What concerns might a customer have?" For each, is that concern addressed in the copy? If not, identify missing objection-handling copy.

**Status:** ☐ PASS ☐ PARTIAL ☐ FAIL

---

### Point 12: Guarantee Clarity

**Standard:** Guarantee is clear, specific, and prominent

**Criteria:**
- ✅ Guarantee is stated explicitly (not vague)
- ✅ Terms are specific (timeframe, condition, refund method)
- ✅ Guarantee removes financial risk (money-back preferred)
- ✅ Refund process is easy to understand

**Bad Example:**
"Satisfaction guaranteed"
- Vague; doesn't specify what satisfaction means or how to claim

**Good Example:**
"60-day money-back guarantee. If you don't see 50%+ improvement in [metric], email us and we'll refund 100% within 48 hours. No questions asked."
- Specific condition; timeframe; process; ease

**How to Test:**
Can you explain the guarantee in one sentence? Does it remove financial risk? If not, it needs work.

**Status:** ☐ PASS ☐ PARTIAL ☐ FAIL

---

## SECTION D: STRUCTURE & CTA (Points 13-15)

### Point 13: Argument Structure

**Standard:** Copy follows logical flow (lead → proof → objection handling → close)

**Criteria:**
- ✅ Hook/lead comes first (grab attention)
- ✅ Problem → solution stated early
- ✅ Proof built progressively (light proof early, heavy proof later)
- ✅ Objections handled before final CTA
- ✅ Close is strong and clear

**Bad Example:**
Testimonials first, then headline, then problem, then solution
- Random order; confusing flow

**Good Example:**
1. Headline + problem (grab attention)
2. Solution introduced
3. Benefits explained
4. Proof stacked (light → heavy)
5. Objections addressed
6. Strong CTA

**How to Test:**
Outline the copy's main points in order. Does the flow make logical sense? Does each section lead naturally to the next?

**Status:** ☐ PASS ☐ PARTIAL ☐ FAIL

---

### Point 14: CTA Clarity & Specificity

**Standard:** Call-to-action is specific and benefit-focused (not generic)

**Criteria:**
- ✅ CTA starts with action verb (Get, Start, Claim, etc.)
- ✅ CTA includes specific benefit (not "Learn More")
- ✅ CTA appears multiple times (at least 3x for long pages)
- ✅ CTA is prominent (above fold, mid-page, bottom)

**Bad Example:**
- "Submit"
- "Click Here"
- "Learn More"
(Generic; no benefit; no action clarity)

**Good Example:**
- "Get Your Free Audit"
- "Start Your 30-Day Trial"
- "Claim Your Spot Now"
(Specific; benefit; action verb)

**How to Test:**
Read the CTA button text. Does it tell visitor what they GET? Or does it just tell them to do something generic? If latter, it needs improvement.

**Status:** ☐ PASS ☐ PARTIAL ☐ FAIL

---

### Point 15: CTA Frequency

**Standard:** CTA appears enough times to capture different visitor readiness levels

**Criteria:**
- ✅ Primary CTA appears at least 3 times (above fold, mid, bottom)
- ✅ Secondary CTAs offer alternatives (not competing)
- ✅ No section goes longer than 300 words without CTA option
- ✅ Final CTA includes urgency/scarcity

**Bad Example:**
Only one CTA at bottom of page
- Misses visitors ready to convert earlier

**Good Example:**
- Hero CTA: "Start Your Trial" (above fold)
- Mid-page CTA: "See How It Works" (after benefits)
- Bottom CTA: "Join Before Friday" (with urgency)

**How to Test:**
Count CTAs. Is there one every 300-400 words? If page is 2,000+ words, are there multiple CTAs? If not, frequency needs improvement.

**Status:** ☐ PASS ☐ PARTIAL ☐ FAIL

---

## SECTION E: LANGUAGE & TONE (Points 16-18)

### Point 16: Audience Language Match

**Standard:** Copy uses language and terminology that resonates with target audience

**Criteria:**
- ✅ Speaks in customer's language (not corporate jargon)
- ✅ Uses words target audience actually uses
- ✅ Avoids industry jargon (unless audience is industry-native)
- ✅ Tone matches audience expectations

**Bad Example:**
(For solopreneur audience) "Leverage our proprietary methodology to optimize stakeholder engagement"
- Corporate jargon; doesn't match solopreneur audience

**Good Example:**
(For solopreneur audience) "Stop spending your evenings on emails. Get your time back."
- Plain language; matches audience values

**How to Test:**
Read copy aloud. Would your target customer naturally speak this way? If not, adjust language.

**Status:** ☐ PASS ☐ PARTIAL ☐ FAIL

---

### Point 17: Emotional Resonance

**Standard:** Copy taps into emotions (desires, fears, aspirations)

**Criteria:**
- ✅ Identifies emotional pain points (not just logical ones)
- ✅ Speaks to desires/aspirations (what they want to become)
- ✅ Uses sensory/vivid language (not abstract)
- ✅ Creates emotional connection (prospect feels understood)

**Bad Example:**
"Our system increases efficiency by 40%"
- Logical; no emotion

**Good Example:**
"Imagine having your evenings and weekends back. No more emails at midnight. No more Sunday dread. You're in control of your time again."
- Emotional; speaks to desire and fear relief

**How to Test:**
Read copy. Does it make you FEEL something? Or does it just inform? If latter, add more emotional language.

**Status:** ☐ PASS ☐ PARTIAL ☐ FAIL

---

### Point 18: Specificity & Concreteness

**Standard:** Claims and benefits are specific, not vague

**Criteria:**
- ✅ Numbers are specific (not "many," "some," "a lot")
- ✅ Timeframes are specific (not "soon," "quickly")
- ✅ Results are concrete (not "better," "improve")
- ✅ Examples are detailed (not generic illustrations)

**Bad Example:**
"This system will help you save time and make more money. Many customers report significant improvements."
- Vague; no specifics

**Good Example:**
"Our customers save 12 hours per week and generate an average of $5,000 additional monthly revenue in the first 90 days. Sarah went from 5 clients to 15 clients in 6 months."
- Specific; concrete; measurable

**How to Test:**
Circle every vague word (many, some, better, improve, soon, etc.). Replace with specific numbers, timeframes, and metrics.

**Status:** ☐ PASS ☐ PARTIAL ☐ FAIL

---

## SECTION F: OVERALL COPY QUALITY (Points 19-20)

### Point 19: Scannability & Formatting

**Standard:** Copy is easy to scan (not wall of text)

**Criteria:**
- ✅ Sections have clear headlines
- ✅ Bullet points used for lists (not paragraph form)
- ✅ Short paragraphs (2-3 sentences max in body)
- ✅ Whitespace used effectively (not cramped)
- ✅ Bold/italic used to highlight key points (not overdone)

**Bad Example:**
Page of solid text with no breaks, no sections, no bullets
- Hard to scan; skimming is difficult

**Good Example:**
- Sections with clear headlines
- Key points bulleted
- Paragraphs broken into 1-2 sentences
- Important phrases in bold
- Whitespace between sections

**How to Test:**
Can someone scan the page and understand main points in 30 seconds? If not, formatting needs improvement.

**Status:** ☐ PASS ☐ PARTIAL ☐ FAIL

---

### Point 20: Authenticity & Credibility

**Standard:** Copy feels authentic, not salesy or manipulative

**Criteria:**
- ✅ Tone is conversational (like talking to a friend)
- ✅ Admits limitations (damaging admission)
- ✅ Doesn't over-promise
- ✅ Uses real stories (not fictional examples)
- ✅ Feels honest and trustworthy

**Bad Example:**
"This AMAZING product will CHANGE YOUR LIFE! Everyone who buys becomes INSTANTLY RICH!"
- Salesy; over-promises; not credible

**Good Example:**
"This system works best if you're willing to implement it. It's not a magic bullet, but if you follow the steps, 96% of customers see results. John took 2 weeks to get set up but then his business transformed."
- Honest; admits requirements; realistic

**How to Test:**
Show copy to non-customer. Do they think "This sounds like marketing BS" or "This person gets it"? If former, add authenticity and damaging admissions.

**Status:** ☐ PASS ☐ PARTIAL ☐ FAIL

## Priority Fixes

Look at the items marked ❌ FAIL or ⚠️ PARTIAL. Prioritize fixes in this order:

1. **Critical (Blocks sales):**
   - Point 1: Headline clarity
   - Point 4: Target customer
   - Point 14: CTA clarity

2. **High Impact (Significantly improves conversion):**
   - Point 5: Problem clarity
   - Point 8: Proof presence
   - Point 11: Objection preemption

3. **Medium Impact (Improves conversion noticeably):**
   - Point 6: Solution clarity
   - Point 12: Guarantee clarity
   - Point 18: Specificity

4. **Lower Priority (Nice to have):**
   - Point 19: Scannability
   - Point 20: Authenticity
