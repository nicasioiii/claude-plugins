# Test Priority Matrix

**Sources:** All 8 source courses, synthesized.

---

## The "Big Before Small" Testing Philosophy

Before looking at the priority matrix, internalize this principle from multiple sources:

**Eddie Shleyner (Eugene Schwartz philosophy):** When trying to beat a control landing page, create something that looks and reads NOTHING like the original. Big differences are much more likely to create big shifts (2x, 3x conversions). Small tests (headline tweaks, button color) come AFTER you have had a breakthrough. The goal is NOT 1% or 10% improvement -- the goal is to DOUBLE or TRIPLE.

**Copy Hackers (Joanna Wiebe):** Learning IS winning. Never guarantee a win -- guarantee a learning. Allow early tests to figure out what works vs. what does not work. Exposing 100% of traffic to something untested is problematic for the business.

**OnePeak:** Their $10M product page was based on more than $6M in A/B testing. Copy-heavy vs. design-heavy was a BIG test that produced a 25% lift worth $2M+ annually. A single headline change was worth $3M. These are transformational tests, not micro-optimizations.

**Todd Brown:** The offer is the single most valuable use of marketing time. An irresistible offer makes the copywriter's job dramatically easier. Copy cannot save a bad offer. Test the offer BEFORE testing the copy.

**Practical rule:** If you have not tested at least one P1 or P2 element, do not waste time on P5-P7 tests. That is like rearranging deck chairs.

## Detailed Priority Level Guidance

### P1: Offer and Pricing (Expected: 50-500%+)

This is where the biggest wins live. Todd Brown's Offer Supremacy Principle: "The better the offer, the weaker the copy can be."

**What to test:**
- Different offer entirely (free trial vs. freemium vs. paid, opt-in vs. demo request)
- Pricing tiers and packaging (single vs. tiered, monthly vs. annual)
- Guarantee structure (30-day vs. 60-day, conditional vs. unconditional)
- Value stack composition (different bonuses, different bundling)
- Payment terms (pay-in-full vs. payment plan, different split amounts)

**Real examples:**
- Iyengar jam study: 6 options = 30% purchase rate vs. 24 options = 3% (Todd Brown)
- Patrick McKenzie annual plan strategy: Offering annual upgrade at month 2-3 with 35% savings removes 12 monthly re-decisions (Copy Hackers)
- OnePeak 7 Elements scoring: Scoring your offer across Outcome, Price, Time, Effort, Perceived Likelihood, Risk, and Urgency reveals which dimension needs improvement

**Hypothesis templates for P1:**
- "If we change the offer from [current offer] to [new offer], then conversion rate will increase because [reason based on audience research]."
- "If we add a [30/60/90]-day guarantee, then purchase rate will increase because our audience's primary objection is [financial risk / uncertain results]."
- "If we restructure pricing from [current] to [new], then revenue per visitor will increase because [anchoring / perceived value / reduced friction]."

### P2: Headline/Hook and Page Type (Expected: 20-100%+)

The headline is the gateway. David Ogilvy found 5x more people read the headline than the body. A great headline on a mediocre page outperforms a mediocre headline on a great page.

**What to test:**
- Benefit-driven vs. curiosity-driven vs. problem-driven headline
- Direct headline vs. story-based headline
- Long-form sales page vs. short-form landing page
- Video sales letter vs. text-based page
- Listicle format vs. standard product page (OnePeak: listicles can double conversions)
- Copy-heavy vs. design-heavy page (OnePeak: copy-heavy outperformed by 25%)

**Real examples:**
- OnePeak $3M headline change: Single headline test worth $3M over the testing period
- Copy Hackers review-mined headlines: 20%+ more leads, 125% more clicks to trial
- Arman Assadi 20-25 iteration process: The magic happens around headline 20-25 where you exhaust the obvious
- Sandy Franks story-driven subject lines: 47-49% open rates consistently

**Hypothesis templates for P2:**
- "If we change the headline from feature-focused to benefit-focused, then conversion rate will increase because our audience is problem-aware and needs to see the outcome, not the tool."
- "If we test a long-form sales page against our short-form page, then revenue per visitor will increase because our audience needs more education before purchasing (solution-aware stage)."
- "If we switch from a design-heavy page to a copy-heavy page, then conversion rate will increase because OnePeak data shows copy-heavy pages consistently outperform design-heavy by 25%+."

### P3: Awareness Targeting and Proof Strategy (Expected: 10-50%)

Matching copy to awareness stage is the single most impactful strategic decision according to Copy Hackers. Getting this wrong means writing brilliant copy for the wrong audience.

**What to test:**
- Problem-aware messaging vs. solution-aware messaging
- Testimonial-heavy sections vs. case-study-heavy sections
- Different proof placement (near claims vs. near CTA vs. separate section)
- Authority proof vs. peer proof vs. results proof

**Real examples:**
- Slack homepage evolution: Required fundamentally different messaging at each awareness stage
- Ryan Deiss contrarian: Do NOT model your favorite brand's homepage -- those brands speak to most-aware audiences. Your audience is probably problem-aware.
- Todd Brown Proof of Three: One piece of evidence = fluke. Two = coincidence. Three = pattern.

**Hypothesis templates for P3:**
- "If we rewrite the page for [problem-aware] visitors instead of [product-aware], then bounce rate will decrease because our primary traffic source sends [cold / problem-aware] visitors."
- "If we add a testimonial next to the CTA, then click-through will increase because our audience is skeptical and needs proof at the point of decision."
- "If we add three proof points per major claim instead of one, then time-on-page and conversion will increase because our audience requires pattern-level evidence before believing."

### P4: CTA Copy and Social Proof Placement (Expected: 5-25%)

**What to test:**
- First-person CTA ("Show me my results") vs. second-person ("Get your results")
- Value-focused CTA ("Generate more leads") vs. action-focused ("Download now")
- Click trigger text below buttons (reassurance copy)
- Number and placement of CTA buttons on the page
- Social proof notifications (real-time purchase alerts)

**Real examples:**
- Copy Hackers: "Get" as the first word on a button is magic
- Crazy Egg: Single focused CTA = 45% boost in account starts
- Gumroad: First-person button copy + green glowing box + testimonial click triggers = 20% lift
- Arman Assadi first-person buttons: "Enroll me", "Sign me up", "I want this now"

### P5: Body Copy and Guarantee Presentation (Expected: 5-15%)

**What to test:**
- Complete body copy rewrite (different argument structure, different CPB order)
- Guarantee duration and framing
- Story-driven body vs. logic-driven body
- Amount of agitation in the problem section

### P6: Visual Design and Form Fields (Expected: 3-10%)

**What to test:**
- Layout changes (single column vs. two column)
- Form field reduction (every field is a decision point that can cause abandonment)
- Image direction (person looking toward CTA vs. away)
- Background colors, whitespace, section breaks

**Real examples:**
- Ryan Deiss: If a person is in the hero shot, their eyes should look TOWARD the CTA
- TG Store: Simplifying to 2 choices = 96.6% and 104.5% more visitors to respective categories
- Copy Hackers: Coupon code fields INCREASE cart abandonment

### P7: Micro-Optimizations (Expected: 1-5%)

**What to test:**
- Button color and size
- Image swaps (lifestyle vs. product shot)
- Micro-copy tweaks (subhead rewording, bullet point reordering)
- Font size and paragraph spacing

**Rule:** Only test P7 elements after you have already found your winning offer, headline, page type, and proof strategy. P7 tests are polish on a proven foundation.

## Decision Framework: What to Test Next

### Step 1: Run the 20-Point Audit
Score every element. The lowest-scoring elements with the highest priority ranking go first.

### Step 2: Identify the Bottleneck
Where are people dropping off?

| Symptom | Bottleneck | Test Priority |
|---------|-----------|---------------|
| High bounce rate (>70%) | Hook/headline or message mismatch | P2: Headline, P3: Awareness match |
| Low scroll depth (<30%) | Body copy or page type wrong | P2: Page type, P5: Body copy |
| Good scroll but no clicks | CTA weakness or missing objection handling | P4: CTA, P3: Proof |
| Clicks to checkout but no purchase | Offer, price, or checkout friction | P1: Offer/pricing, form fields |
| Good conversion but low revenue | AOV too low | P1: Pricing/packaging, upsells |
| High cart abandonment (>80%) | Price shock, trust gap, or friction | P1: Pricing, P3: Proof near checkout, P6: Form fields |
| Low time on page (<15 sec) | Message mismatch with traffic source | P3: Awareness targeting, P2: Headline |
| Traffic but no scroll past hero | Hero section failing to hook | P2: Headline, visual design, hero CTA presence |

### Step 3: Form a Hypothesis
Every test needs a hypothesis before running.

**Template:**
"If we [change], then [metric] will [improve/increase/decrease] because [reason based on research or audit finding]."

**Additional hypothesis examples:**
- "If we replace the generic stock photo with a customer in their ideal after-state, then trust and conversion will increase because visitors need to visualize the transformation."
- "If we remove the navigation bar from the landing page, then conversion rate will increase because nav links are distracting visitors away from the CTA."
- "If we restructure the page to lead with the problem (PAS framework) instead of the product, then scroll depth will increase because our audience is problem-aware, not product-aware."
- "If we add a calculator tool that lets visitors estimate their own savings, then conversion will increase because our 'save X hours' claim needs self-generated proof to be believed." (Source: Copy Hackers UserTesting.com discovery)

## Sample Size Requirements

Use a statistical significance calculator before launching. Minimum requirements:

| Baseline Conv Rate | Minimum Detectable Lift | Visitors Per Variant (95% confidence) |
|-------------------|------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| 1% | 50% relative (to 1.5%) | ~14,000 |
| 2% | 25% relative (to 2.5%) | ~10,000 |
| 5% | 20% relative (to 6%) | ~3,500 |
| 10% | 15% relative (to 11.5%) | ~3,000 |
| 20% | 10% relative (to 22%) | ~2,500 |

### How to Read This Table
- "Baseline Conv Rate" = your current conversion rate
- "Minimum Detectable Lift" = the smallest improvement you want to be able to detect
- "Visitors Per Variant" = how many visitors EACH version (control AND variant) needs

### Sample Size Decision Rules
- **If you can reach required sample in 2-4 weeks:** Run a standard A/B test
- **If you need 4-8 weeks:** Consider testing a BIGGER change (bigger changes need smaller samples to detect)
- **If you need 8+ weeks:** Use before/after measurement or qualitative methods instead
- **Never run a test shorter than 1 full business cycle** (minimum 1 week, ideally 2)
- **Never declare a winner during a weekend or holiday** -- wait for a full normal-traffic period

### Recommended Tools
- **A/B Testing:** Convert.com (used by OnePeak for $10M page), Google Optimize, VWO, Optimizely
- **Social Proof Notifications:** WiserNotify, Proof (used by OnePeak)
- **Heatmaps/Recordings:** Hotjar, Crazy Egg (referenced by Copy Hackers)
- **Quick Validation:** FiveSecondTest.com, UserTesting.com

**Rule:** If you cannot reach the required sample size in 4 weeks, either:
1. Test a BIGGER change (bigger change = needs smaller sample to detect)
2. Use before/after measurement instead of A/B
3. Use qualitative methods (5-second test, user testing)

## When A/B Testing Is Not Possible

For low-traffic pages (under 1,000 visitors/month):

| Method | Use When | Confidence Level | Source |
|--------|---------|-----------------|--------|
| Before/After | Major changes, low traffic | Low-Medium (directional) | Copy Hackers |
| 5-Second Test | Headline/hero validation | Medium (qualitative) | Copy Hackers (FiveSecondTest.com) |
| 7-Second Test | Full page assessment | Medium (qualitative) | Ryan Deiss |
| User Testing | UX issues, comprehension | Medium-High (behavioral) | Copy Hackers (UserTesting.com) |
| Expert Audit | Getting started, no data | Low (opinion-based) | Synthesized |

### Before/After Measurement Guidelines (Copy Hackers)
- Compare conversion rate before the change vs. after
- Example: 3% ebook downloads before, 6% after = signal it is working
- Not publishable as a case study but directionally useful
- Be honest when conversion rate drops -- learn from non-winners too
- Allow at least 2 weeks of data on each side for reliability

### 7-Second Test Process (Ryan Deiss)
1. Explain the role (who the reviewer should pretend to be -- their avatar)
2. Do NOT explain your solution or business
3. Have reviewers open the page for exactly 7 seconds, then stop
4. Ask three questions: What is it? Why should I care? What now?
5. Do not argue with feedback -- document insights
6. The 7 seconds you get in a test environment is MORE focused attention than real visitors will give

## Common Testing Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Testing too small:** Button color tests on pages with broken offers waste time. Test big first. (Source: Shleyner/Schwartz philosophy)
2. **Declaring winners too early:** Wait for statistical significance. Minimum 2 weeks, minimum sample size.
3. **Testing too many things at once:** Only change ONE element per test. Otherwise you do not know what caused the result.
4. **Ignoring losing tests:** A "loss" is a learning. Document WHY it lost and apply the insight.
5. **Never testing at all:** Even imperfect testing beats no testing. Start somewhere.
6. **Testing without a hypothesis:** Random tests teach nothing. Always know what you expect and why.
7. **Testing on 100% of traffic:** Split 50/50 (or even 80/20 for low-risk tests). Never expose everyone to untested changes.
8. **Seasonal blindness:** Account for day-of-week, time-of-month, and seasonal patterns.
9. **Stopping successful tests:** When you find a winner, that becomes the new control. Immediately plan the next test.
10. **Not documenting results:** Build a test log. Patterns emerge across tests that individual tests cannot reveal.
11. **Copying someone else's winning test:** A winning headline for one audience almost always backfires on another. Test for YOUR audience. (Source: Copy Hackers)
12. **Guaranteeing test wins to clients/stakeholders:** Guarantee a learning, not an outcome. (Source: Copy Hackers)
13. **Letting gut feelings override data:** Five-second test results and A/B data should inform decisions, not the boss's opinion. (Source: Copy Hackers -- but also note Wiebe's contrarian: do not lose your art to pseudo-science)
