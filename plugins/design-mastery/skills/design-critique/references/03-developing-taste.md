# Developing Design Taste: Eye Training, Creativity, and Portfolio

Methods for improving design judgment, the creativity process, visual harmony, anti-trend philosophy, and portfolio review framework. Synthesized from Flux Academy (Ran Segall), Oliver Gareis, and Shift Nudge (MDS).

---

## FIVE METHODS TO IMPROVE YOUR DESIGN EYE [Flux]

### 1. Improve Your Visual Diet

Quality in = quality out. Replace social media scrolling with:
- Design books (Massimo Vignelli's "Canon," Josef Muller-Brockmann's grid systems)
- Design magazines and annuals
- Museum visits and gallery exhibitions
- Architecture and interior design observation
- Quality blogs and curated design collections

### 2. Gather Stylistic References

- Collect visuals that resonate (even if you don't know why)
- Save to organized folders by mood/style, not project
- Patterns will emerge revealing your aesthetic preferences
- This collection becomes your design vocabulary

### 3. Learn the Language

Vocabulary lets you precisely identify what's happening:
- Hierarchy, focal point, visual weight
- Tracking, leading, kerning, x-height
- Tones, saturation, brightness
- Serif, sans-serif, slab, geometric
- Composition, balance, rhythm, pacing

**Without language, you can only say "this looks good."** With language, you can say "the tracking is too tight on the headline, which compresses the luxury feel they're going for."

### 4. Play Art Director

**Process:**
1. Find a design (good or bad)
2. Describe it objectively (what is happening?)
3. Analyze it critically (what works? what doesn't?)
4. Prescribe improvements (how could it be better?)

Description reveals truth. Most people skip straight to opinion. Force yourself to describe first.

### 5. Find Your Influences' Influences

[CONTRARIAN] Trace the lineage of designers you admire:
- Michael Beirut -> Massimo Vignelli
- Peter Saville -> Jan Tschichold
- David Carson -> Wolfgang Weingart

Old masters contain depth not found in trending Dribbble shots. Go deeper, not wider.

## VISUAL HARMONY FRAMEWORK [Flux]

### The World Test

Think like a TV production designer. Everything must look like it belongs in the same world. A modern sans-serif font next to a Victorian illustration = visual anachronism.

### Three Steps to Visual Harmony

**Step 1: Define the world**
- What era does this design live in?
- What mood/feeling should it evoke?
- What aesthetic territory does it occupy?

**Step 2: Design an overall scheme**
- Establish consistent elements: shapes, typography, color palette, photography style
- Reuse them systematically
- If using triangles, don't randomly introduce circles
- If using solid color, don't randomly add gradients

**Step 3: Analyze cross-element compatibility**
- Do your fonts work with your images?
- Does your color palette match your photography style?
- Does everything point toward the same message/feeling?

### Diagnostic Questions

When something feels "off" but you can't identify why:
- What is the feeling I'm trying to evoke?
- What is the message?
- What is this brand about?
- What do we want people to do?
- Does every element answer these questions consistently?

## CREATIVITY PROCESS FOR DESIGNERS [Flux]

### [CONTRARIAN] Ideas Come from Process, Not Scrolling

Big ideas are not inside the computer. Don't scroll feeds hoping for inspiration. Ideas emerge from the brief, from research, from your creative process, and from natural curiosity.

### The Process

1. **Research the brief thoroughly.** Ask the right question = get the right answer
2. **Write your idea in words first** -- before touching any software
3. **Sketch rough, small, fast** -- brainstorming on paper generates new ideas beyond what was in your head
4. **Create lots of small scamps/quick versions** in your medium

### Idea-Generation Shortcuts

**Verbs lead to graphic approaches:**
- "Persuade" -> infographics, data visualization
- "Listen/mirror" -> reflection motifs, mirroring layouts
- "Touch" -> tactile elements, texture, grain
- "Connect" -> bridges, links, interlocking shapes
- "Grow" -> organic forms, botanical patterns, upward motion

**Adjectives lead to decorative styles:**
- "Luxurious" -> ornate or minimalist-sophisticated, wide tracking, serifs
- "Vintage" -> era-specific design language, muted colors, textures
- "Raw/unfiltered" -> hand-drawn, imperfect, visible process marks
- "Technical" -> grids, mono fonts, structured layouts
- "Playful" -> bright colors, rounded shapes, hand-drawn elements

## ANTI-TREND PHILOSOPHY [Gareis] [CONTRARIAN]

**Core position:** "Design trends are shit." Trends are inherently temporary. Building your work on trends guarantees it will look dated.

**Instead focus on:**
- Timeless principles (hierarchy, typography, contrast, spacing)
- First principles thinking (why does this work?)
- Historical design movements (understand what endures vs what was fad)
- Your own aesthetic voice (developed through the 5 methods above)

**Reconciliation with reality:** You can be aware of trends without being enslaved by them. Know what's current so you can make conscious choices about whether to use or reject trend elements.

## PORTFOLIO REVIEW FRAMEWORK [Flux]

### Portfolio Structure (One-Pager Framework)

**Recommended section order:**
1. Navigation (name/logo + links)
2. Hero section (photo + headline + CTA)
3. Work showcase (2-4 projects)
4. Process/services/values highlights
5. Testimonials (social proof)
6. About section
7. Contact CTA
8. Footer

### Hero Section Audit

**Three questions visitors must answer in 3 seconds:**
1. What do you do?
2. Why should I care? (Is this for me?)
3. What's my next step? (Clear CTA)

**50 milliseconds** -- time it takes visitors to form an opinion about your website.

### Three Headline Templates

1. **Client-goal formula:** "Helping [client type] achieve [their goal]"
   Example: "Helping startups win more business through web design"
2. **Belief-based formula:** "[What you do] for [who] who believe [shared value]"
   Example: "Websites for coaches who know that prioritizing people leads to profits"
3. **Differentiation formula:** "[What you do] + [how it's different]"
   Example: "Creating websites that are visually stunning AND technically sound"

**Specs:** 9-14 words ideal. Subheading expands with one sentence.

### Work Showcase Audit

[CONTRARIAN] Do NOT create in-depth case studies with process images. Most clients judge work quality in 1-2 seconds from the visual.

**Focus on making one hero image per project look exceptional.**

**Two mockup approaches:**
1. **Scene generators** -- custom PSD scenes with device mockups, color-matched backgrounds
2. **Simple elevated screenshots** -- complementary background (sampled from project), subtle shadow, slight rotation. Never post raw screenshots

**Project description formula:**
- Heading: "Website for Italian restaurant" (what + client category) -- better for skimming
- Paragraph: One sentence expanding on brief/challenge. Keep short

### Process/Services/Values Section

Pick ONE angle:
- My process (step-by-step)
- My services (what you deliver)
- My values (what you believe)

Replace numbers with icons. Each item: short headline + one-sentence expansion.

### Portfolio Photography [Flux]

Your photo matters because people hire people.

**Four quality factors:**
1. Resolution -- high-quality camera (smartphone portrait mode acceptable)
2. Lighting -- natural side light from window; avoid direct sunlight
3. Wardrobe -- dress how you want clients to perceive you
4. Storytelling -- location, expression, props communicate who you are

**No-photo alternatives:** Abstract illustration of yourself, or high-quality workspace image.

### Testimonials When Starting Out

- Ask past bosses, colleagues, friends
- Quote should highlight what it's LIKE working with you
- Best: includes measurable results
- [CONTRARIAN] Don't wait for "real" clients to build portfolio -- conceptual projects are legitimate

### Conceptual Projects [Flux] [CONTRARIAN]

High-quality concept work > uninspiring real client projects.

**Even for concept projects:**
- Create a case study page
- Describe purpose, goals, problem being solved
- Explain deliverables and solution
- Treat with same professionalism as paid work

**Brief generators:** Good Brief website, design challenges, ChatGPT prompts for industry-specific briefs.

### Portfolio Attracts More of Same

Your portfolio is your most powerful filter. Showing brochure work = attracting brochure clients. Only when you start showing hospitality branding work do hospitality clients appear.

**Action:** Stop showing work you don't want more of. Start showing work you do want.

## THE ITERATION IMPERATIVE [MDS]

Great design starts as bad design. The secret is spending more time refining than anyone else would reasonably expect.

**The loop:**
1. Design (get something on the page)
2. Review (step back, squint, check on device)
3. Adjust (small refinements: padding, type size, color)
4. Repeat (iterate until it clicks)

**Never ship first draft.** Make copies before major changes. Zoom out for composition, zoom to 100% for readability.

**Copy work [MDS]:** Recreating designs pixel-by-pixel is valuable for learning. It exposes you to decisions you wouldn't have made on your own. But follow the One-Thing Rule: only lift ONE specific thing (typography, color, OR layout) from an inspiration source.
