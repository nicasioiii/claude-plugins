# Client Workflow & Acquisition

## CLIENT ONBOARDING FLOW [Flux]

```
Proposal approved
    ↓
Contract signed (separate from proposal)
    ↓
Deposit invoice paid (Stripe or PayPal)
    ↓
Welcome email sent
    ↓
Kick-off meeting scheduled
```

### Contract Approach

[CONTRARIAN] Send contract separately from proposal because:
1. Legal language can scare starting business owners
2. If proposal needs changes, contract may need changes too -- creates complex back-and-forth

Use digital signature tools (Adobe Sign, Pandadoc, DocuSign). Never ask clients to print, sign, scan, and return.

### Welcome Email Template

When contract signed and deposit paid:
1. **Thank them** for their trust. Express excitement and gratitude
2. **Outline next steps** -- what they need to do (fill a form, book strategy call) and by when
3. **Set boundaries** -- preferred communication method, office hours, expected response times
4. **Kind sign-off** -- reassure them they made the right decision

## SCOPE CREEP PREVENTION FRAMEWORK

| Prevention Method | When to Apply |
|-------------------|--------------|
| Precise deliverables in proposal | During proposal writing |
| Process phases with defined gates | During proposal and kickoff |
| Proposal as anchor document | When client requests extras |
| Change request process | During project execution |
| Validity clause on proposal | At proposal delivery |

**When scope changes happen:**
1. Acknowledge the request positively
2. Explain how it affects timeline and budget
3. Present options: add to current scope (with adjustment) or save for Phase 2
4. Get written approval before proceeding

## CLIENT COMMUNICATION CADENCE

| Touchpoint | Timing | Purpose |
|-----------|--------|---------|
| Pre-call reminder | 24h + 1h before | Reduce no-shows |
| Post-call recap | Same day | Confirm understanding in writing |
| Proposal delivery | Within 24h of call | Speed shows professionalism |
| Progress updates | Weekly or per milestone | Keep client informed |
| Review requests | Per phase gate | Get formal approval |
| Launch coordination | 1 week before | Align on go-live |

## CLIENT RELATIONSHIP NURTURING [Flux]

[CONTRARIAN] Biggest freelancer mistake: closing the account when the project launches. Past clients are your best salesforce.

### Post-Launch Nurturing Sequence

| Timing | Action |
|--------|--------|
| Launch day | Celebratory email |
| 1 month post-launch | Check on tangible results, ensure everything runs smoothly. Ask for referral here |
| 3 months post-launch | Casual check-in, no ask, just relationship maintenance |
| 6-12 months post-launch | Another check-in, offer additional help |
| 1-year anniversary ("brandversary") | Small gift or electronic gift card |

## CLIENT ACQUISITION: 4-WEEK SYSTEM [Flux]

### Week 1: Personal Network Mining

**Day 1:** Public announcement -- social media post, email to contacts.

**Day 2:** Network mapping (4 circles):
1. Private circle -- friends, family, their extended networks
2. Professional circle -- current/former coworkers, bosses, networking contacts
3. Educational circle -- school peers, professors, mentors
4. Hobbies/communities -- clubs, groups, organizations

[CONTRARIAN] Projects from personal connections ARE real. They provide foundation experience AND generate referral chains.

**Day 3:** Direct personal outreach using a lead tracker.

### Week 2: Outreach Beyond Your Circle

**Day 1:** Master the follow-up.
- 80% of sales happen between 5th and 12th follow-up
- Short-term: 3 follow-ups at 5-7 day intervals, each adding value
- Long-term: "Second chance" follow-up 1-3 months later

**Day 2:** Agency partnerships.
- Search Google Maps for local design agencies
- Check Careers/Join Us pages for freelance openings
- Search LinkedIn Jobs for "freelance designer" + remote

**Day 3:** Virtual door knock (cold outreach with value).
- Research: Google "[industry] [location]", skip pages 1-2, go to page 7+
- Record short personalized video showing 3-4 specific improvements for their website
- Include link to book a call

### Week 3: Direct Opportunities

**Day 1:** Online communities (3 strategies):
1. Become the resident expert (long-term, high-trust)
2. Reply to direct inquiries with portfolio image + Calendly link
3. Search design communities for outsource/white-label opportunities

**Day 2:** Complementary service provider partnerships.
- Who serves your same audience with different services? (SEO experts, copywriters, developers, photographers)
- Build relationship first, then explore collaboration

**Day 3:** Freelance platforms (Upwork, Dribbble) -- taught LAST intentionally.

### Week 4: Long-Term Strategies

**Day 1:** Client relationship nurturing sequence (see above).
**Day 2:** Revive dead leads -- months-later follow-up either gets closure or reopens conversations.
**Day 3:** Grow exposure through expert positioning -- content, podcast appearances, speaking.

## NICHE DEFINITION PROCESS [Flux]

### Four Questions

1. What businesses would I LOVE to work with?
2. What problems do these businesses face? (Research via communities, competitor analysis)
3. What results would they want? (Map problems to deliverables)
4. Where do they hang out? (Platforms, communities, events)

### Niche Evolution

[CONTRARIAN] Your niche will and should change over time. Starting point is not permanent. Portfolio attracts more of the same -- only when you start showing hospitality branding work do hospitality clients appear.

### Specialist vs. Generalist

For an ear infection, you would choose an ENT specialist over a GP. Same with design:
- "Freelance designer" = massive competition
- "Webflow designer for hospitality businesses" = tiny competitive pool

**Three benefits:** Narrows competitive pool. Enables better value proposition. Makes marketing easier.
