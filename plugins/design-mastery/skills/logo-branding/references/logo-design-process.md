# Logo Design Process: Research → Sketching → Vectorization → Refinement

This reference provides detailed step-by-step workflow for each phase of professional logo design.

---

## PHASE 1: RESEARCH & DISCOVERY (1-2 weeks)

### Discovery Questionnaire

Send client this questionnaire before kickoff meeting:

**Business Fundamentals**
- What problem does your business solve?
- Who is your ideal customer? (Age, income, profession, lifestyle)
- How is your business different from competitors?
- What's your core value proposition in one sentence?

**Brand Personality**
- If your brand were a person, describe them in 5 words
- What emotions should customers feel about your brand?
- Are you more: [Bold / Subtle], [Playful / Serious], [Modern / Classic], [Aggressive / Nurturing]?
- What brands do you admire (in any industry)?

**Visual Preferences**
- Colors you love / colors to avoid?
- Any visual symbols or shapes meaningful to your business?
- Do you want icon/wordmark/combination?
- Any existing logos or design you like/dislike?

**Application Scope**
- Where will logo appear? (Business cards, website, packaging, app, signage, vehicle, social media)
- Will logo animate or move? (Web animations, transitions)
- Will it need to work in specific color limitations? (One-color printing?)
- Any industry-specific requirements?

### Competitive Analysis Framework

Audit 5-10 competitors and direct/indirect alternatives:

**For each competitor, document:**
1. **Logo type** — Wordmark, icon, combination, monogram, abstract
2. **Visual approach** — Geometric, organic, stylized, literal
3. **Color palette** — Primary color(s), secondary, how many colors?
4. **Typography** — Serif/sans-serif, weight, custom/standard typeface?
5. **Personality** — Bold/subtle, premium/budget, modern/classic
6. **Strengths** — What works about this logo?
7. **Weaknesses** — Design problems or outdated elements
8. **Opportunity** — How could your logo be different?

**Competitive Analysis Table:**

| Competitor | Type | Colors | Personality | Strength | Weakness | Opportunity |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Competitor A | Icon | 1 color (blue) | Premium/Modern | Bold mark | Generic sans font | Add personality |
| Competitor B | Wordmark | 3 colors | Playful/Casual | Custom lettering | Too trendy | Timeless approach |

**Key insight from analysis:** Identify visual patterns (everyone uses blue? Everyone uses sans-serif?). Your logo should break those patterns.

### Brand Positioning Statement

Write a single sentence that defines the brand's core positioning:

**Template:**
"[Brand name] is the only [category] that [unique value] for [target audience]."

**Example:**
"Slack is the only workplace communication tool that makes teams more productive through organized conversations for [busy professionals]."

This statement guides all design decisions. Every logo sketch should somehow express or support this positioning.

### Market Research Findings Document

Compile research into a brief document (2-3 pages):

**Cover:**
- Project name, client, date, designer

**Discovery Findings:**
- Business summary (2-3 sentences)
- Target audience profile (demographics + psychographics)
- Brand positioning statement
- Key competitors and market insights

**Design Direction:**
- Recommended logo approach (wordmark, icon, combination, why?)
- Visual personality direction (3-5 descriptive words)
- Color direction (warm/cool, bold/subtle, premium/budget)
- Typographic direction (serif/sans-serif, modern/classic, custom/standard)

**Deliverables:**
- Quantity of concepts (typically 3-5 final directions)
- Revision rounds included
- Timeline
- Next steps

**Client sign-off:** Have client review and approve research direction before moving to sketching phase

## PHASE 2: SKETCHING & EXPLORATION (2-3 weeks)

### Sketching Setup

**Tools needed:**
- Pencil (HB or mechanical pencil for precision)
- Fine-tip pen (0.5mm or 0.3mm) for refined sketches
- Sketch paper (blank 8.5x11 or larger)
- Eraser (no erasing while sketching, but you'll need one later)
- Ruler (optional, useful for geometric shapes)

**Sketching environment:**
- Quiet space with good natural light
- Large surface (not cramped)
- Minimal distractions
- Time blocks of 2-3 hours per session

### The Sketching Philosophy

**Goal:** Generate 50-150 sketch variations across 2-3 weeks

**Key principle:** Sketching is about volume and exploration, not perfection

**Rules:**
1. Sketch with pen or pencil (no erasing while sketching)
2. Don't judge while sketching (evaluate later)
3. Fill a full page before moving to next page
4. Quick rough sketches (3-5 seconds per sketch) are perfect
5. Refine promising directions on separate page
6. Never show raw sketch pages to client

### Sketch Organization by Category

**Week 1: Wordmark Variations (50+ sketches)**

If client wants wordmark-based logo:

1. **Letterform modifications** (20 sketches)
   - Stretch first letter taller
   - Compress entire wordmark
   - Overlap letters
   - Negative space within letters
   - Connect letters differently
   - Vertical vs. horizontal orientation

2. **Hand-lettering styles** (20 sketches)
   - Serif letterforms
   - Sans-serif geometric shapes
   - Organic/flowing letters
   - Stacked arrangement
   - Overlapping arrangements
   - Display typography

3. **Decorative elements** (10 sketches)
   - Underline or accent marks
   - Small icons paired with word
   - Flourishes (subtle ornaments)
   - Border or frame around text

**Week 1-2: Icon/Symbol Variations (50+ sketches)**

If client wants icon-based or combination logo:

1. **Literal representations** (20 sketches)
   - Direct visual of what business does
   - Obvious/clear symbol
   - Recognizable at glance
   - Vary line weight and simplification

2. **Abstract simplifications** (15 sketches)
   - Essence/core concept in geometric shapes
   - Distilled to basic forms
   - Play with positive/negative space
   - Overlapping geometric elements

3. **Geometric constructions** (10 sketches)
   - Circle-based compositions
   - Grid-based structures
   - Golden ratio or mathematical proportions
   - Modular/repetitive elements

4. **Negative space explorations** (5 sketches)
   - Clever use of white space
   - Figure/ground reversals
   - Optical illusions or hidden symbols
   - Space as important as form

**Week 2-3: Combination & Refinement (50+ sketches)**

1. **Icon + Wordmark combinations** (20 sketches)
   - Side by side (icon left, text right)
   - Stacked (icon top, text bottom)
   - Integrated (text wrapping around icon)
   - Icon becomes part of letter

2. **Direction refinement** (30 sketches)
   - Take top 3-5 promising concepts
   - Create 10 refinements per concept
   - Vary size, weight, proportions
   - Test different orientations

### Sketching Tempo: Sample Weekly Schedule

**Day 1-2: Wordmark Exploration**
- Morning session: 20-30 rough wordmark variations
- Afternoon: Identify 2-3 promising directions
- Evening: Refine selected directions (5-10 sketches each)

**Day 3-4: Icon Exploration**
- Morning: 30-40 icon variations (rough, fast)
- Afternoon: Review, identify best 3-5 concepts
- Evening: Refine those directions

**Day 5: Combination & Integration**
- Morning: Sketch icon + wordmark combinations
- Afternoon: Review all work, select final 3-5 concepts
- Evening: Clean up selected concepts for presentation

**Repeat cycle** for weeks 2-3, diving deeper into refined directions

### Scan and Archive

Once sketching complete:

1. **Scan all sketch pages** at 600 DPI (high quality)
2. **Organize by category** (wordmark sketches, icon sketches, combinations)
3. **Create PDF with all sketches** (reference for revision process)
4. **Archive originals** (keep for record)
5. **Select 3-5 strongest concepts** for vectorization

## PHASE 3: VECTORIZATION (1-2 weeks)

### Preparation Steps

**In Illustrator:**

1. **Create new document** (8.5" x 11" at 300 DPI, RGB color mode)
2. **Create layer called "Template"**
3. **Place scanned sketch** (File > Place)
4. **Lock template layer** (click lock icon)
5. **Reduce opacity** to 50% (easier to trace)
6. **Create new layer above template** called "Logo Outline"

### Pen Tool Basics (Critical Skill)

The pen tool is how you trace hand-drawn sketches into vectors.

**Essential technique: Creating anchor points**

Every vector shape is made of anchor points connected by paths. Three types of points:

1. **Straight point** — Path is straight through the point
   - Create by: Click once with pen tool
   - Used for: Straight edges, sharp corners

2. **Curve point** — Path curves through the point
   - Create by: Click and drag (creates handles)
   - Used for: Smooth curves, elegant lines

3. **Corner point** — Handles are asymmetrical (different lengths/angles)
   - Create by: Hold Alt while dragging one handle
   - Used for: Sharp transitions between straight and curve

**Practical workflow:**

1. Start at a corner of shape
2. Click to place first anchor point
3. Click next location to create straight segment
4. For curves, click and drag to create handles
5. Continue around entire shape
6. Click first point to close shape

**Speed tip:** Most of tracing is clicking, not dragging. Drag only for curves.

### Step-by-Step Vectorization Example

**Scenario: Tracing a hand-drawn "M" letterform**

**Step 1: Starting point**
- Click at top-left corner of left stroke
- This creates first anchor point

**Step 2: Straight edges**
- Move to top-right corner of left stroke
- Click (don't drag) to create straight edge
- Continue clicking at each corner

**Step 3: Curves**
- When you encounter a curve (e.g., bottom-left of M)
- Click and drag outward to create curve handles
- Adjust the drag angle to match curve direction
- The handle length controls curve depth

**Step 4: Complex curves**
- Inside curves vs. outside curves have different handle angles
- Adjust using Bezier curve handles (the little arrows)
- A is best indicator of correct curve = smooth, no bumps

**Step 5: Close the shape**
- After tracing all edges, click on first anchor point again
- This closes the shape

**Step 6: Refine**
- Select Direct Selection tool (white arrow, keyboard: A)
- Click on anchor point and drag to adjust
- Drag handles (the little arrows) to adjust curves
- Press Enter when satisfied

### Simplifying Paths

After tracing, clean up unnecessary anchor points:

**Object > Path > Simplify** (or Cmd+Alt+Shift+S)

This removes redundant points while preserving shape. Fewer points = cleaner file = easier to edit later.

**Manual point deletion:**
- Direct selection tool
- Click on anchor point
- Press Delete

### Color & Stroke Settings

**While vectorizing:**

1. **Remove stroke** (click stroke box, hit /)
2. **Set fill to black** (click fill box, color picker to black)
3. **Keep strokes off** unless shape specifically designed with outline

**Why:** Cleaner vectorization, easier to adjust later

### Proportion Adjustments

As you vectorize, you may notice proportions that don't work:

- Some curves look too shallow/deep
- Letters might have weight inconsistencies
- Overall width/height ratios might feel off

**It's OK to adjust while vectorizing.** The sketch is reference, not blueprint.

Common adjustments:
- Make letters slightly taller/wider
- Increase/decrease curve depth
- Adjust spacing between elements
- Straighten wobbly lines
- Uniform stroke weights (if outline-based)

### Creating Multiple Versions Simultaneously

Once main logo is vectorized:

**Create separate artboards for each version:**

1. **Full color version** (on artboard 1)
2. **Icon-only** (remove wordmark, copy to artboard 2)
3. **Wordmark-only** (remove icon, copy to artboard 3)
4. **Monochrome black** (copy main, remove colors, copy to artboard 4)
5. **Monochrome reversed** (white fill on black background, artboard 5)
6. **Grayscale** (if using multiple colors, create grayscale version)

**Each artboard = one variation**

### Testing at Small Sizes

Critical step often skipped:

1. **Export as PNG at 100%** (full size)
2. **Export as PNG at 50%** (medium size)
3. **Export as PNG at 25%** (small size)
4. **Export as PNG at 12.5%** (favicon size, 16px)
5. **View each at actual size on screen**

At small sizes, you'll notice:
- Thin lines disappear
- Complex curves become blobs
- Details become unreadable
- Spacing might collapse

**If logo fails at small size:** Simplify, increase stroke weights, remove details, increase spacing.

## PHASE 4: TESTING & REFINEMENT (1 week)

### Scalability Testing Checklist

**Create test file with multiple sizes:**

Use Illustrator or Figma to display logo at:
- 16px (favicon/app icon)
- 32px (small app icon)
- 64px (medium icon)
- 128px (large icon)
- 256px (mockup size)
- 512px (large print)
- 1000px (billboard/large print)
- Actual size on business card mockup

**Evaluation questions:**
- Is logo readable at 16px?
- Do thin strokes hold up at small sizes?
- Does complexity become a blob?
- Is negative space clear at all sizes?
- Do curved elements hold shape?

### Color Testing

Test logo in these scenarios:

**Monochrome tests:**
1. Black on white background
2. White on black background
3. Black on light gray
4. White on dark gray
5. Black on bright colored background
6. White on bright colored background

**Color tests:**
1. Primary color on white
2. Primary color on black
3. Colored logo on light gray
4. Colored logo on dark gray
5. Logo on colored background (if applicable)

**Printing tests:**
1. One-color printing (will any colors be hard to print?)
2. Full-color printing (do colors separate well?)
3. Reversed printing (will white areas print clearly?)

**Failure signals:**
- Logo becomes unreadable in monochrome
- Color doesn't separate well when printing
- Reversed version loses legibility

### Refinement Loop

Based on testing results:

**If problems identified:**

1. **Simplification issues** — Remove unnecessary details, increase main stroke weights, increase spacing
2. **Readability issues** — Increase letter width, remove thin strokes, separate overlapping elements
3. **Color problems** — Test different color combinations, increase contrast between colors
4. **Proportions feel off** — Adjust height/width ratios, shift elements, reorganize composition

**After adjustments:**
- Re-test at small sizes
- Check monochrome viability
- Compare to original sketch (maintain intent while solving problems)
- Get fresh eyes (ask colleague for feedback)

## PHASE 5: PREPARATION FOR PRESENTATION

### Clean Mockup Creation

Before showing to client, create professional mockups:

**Essential mockups:**
1. Business card (full-color, monochrome, reversed)
2. Website header placement
3. App icon/favicon
4. Social media profile picture
5. Envelope or letterhead
6. Packaging (if applicable)

**Mockup tools:**
- Use Figma, Adobe XD, or Photoshop to create mockups
- Download mockup templates (Behance, GraphicBurger)
- Place logo in context
- Show how it works in real application

### Creating a Presentation Deck

**Structure for 3-concept presentation:**

**Slide 1: Title Slide**
- Project name, client, date, designer

**Slide 2: Research Summary**
- Key findings from discovery
- Brand positioning
- Design direction explained

**Slide 3-5: First Concept**
- Concept name and explanation (2-3 sentences)
- Why this direction works for brand
- Full-color version
- Monochrome version
- Icon-only and/or wordmark-only versions

**Slide 6-8: Second Concept**
- Same structure as concept 1
- Different visual direction
- Different positioning/approach

**Slide 9-11: Third Concept**
- Same structure
- Another distinct direction

**Slide 12: Mockup Applications**
- Show concepts on business cards, website, app icon
- Show in real-world contexts
- Multiple sizes if applicable

**Slide 13: Next Steps**
- Revision rounds included
- Timeline
- File delivery details
- Usage guidelines process

### Presentation Talking Points

For each concept, prepare 30-second explanation covering:

**1. The concept name** (headline)
"This direction we're calling '[Name]'"

**2. What it represents** (visual explanation)
"The [element] represents [brand value]. We used [geometric form] to express [personality trait]."

**3. Why it works for this brand** (strategic rationale)
"This connects to [positioning statement] because [specific reason]. The [design element] reinforces [brand promise]."

**4. Versatility** (practical benefits)
"This concept works in single color, scales beautifully from favicon to billboard, and feels timeless."

**Keep talking points concise.** Let the visual do most of the communication.

### Revision Process Documentation

Before meeting, prepare revision documentation:

**Revision terms:**
- **Revision Round 1:** Major direction feedback (client selects preferred concept)
- **Revision Round 2:** Refinements within selected direction (adjust proportions, colors, details)
- **Revision Round 3:** Final polish and tweaks (minor adjustments, finalization)
- **Beyond Round 3:** Scope expansion (additional concepts or major restructuring = additional cost)

Typical project includes 2-3 revision rounds.

## PHASE 5B: REVISION MANAGEMENT

### Collecting Client Feedback

After presenting concepts, DON'T ask vague "Which do you like?"

**Instead, ask specific questions:**

"Which concept best represents your brand personality?"
"Which direction feels most distinctive from competitors?"
"Which version are you most excited about?"
"What would need to change in [concept] to make it perfect?"

**Document feedback in revision brief:**
- Which concept is preferred
- Specific changes requested (not vague "make it better")
- Priority of changes (must-have vs. nice-to-have)
- Any new direction or clarification needed

### Revision Rounds

**Round 1 Revisions** (typically 1 week):
- Client selects favorite concept
- Provide specific feedback/changes
- You refine that direction (major changes OK)
- Present updated concept

**Round 2 Revisions** (typically 1 week):
- Client provides refinement feedback
- Adjust proportions, colors, details
- Finalize variations
- Present final options

**Round 3 Revisions** (if included):
- Minor polish and tweaks
- Final color confirmation
- File export verification
- Brand system planning begins

### Handling Difficult Feedback

| Client feedback | What they mean | Your response |
|---|---|---|
| "Make it more creative" | Unclear what they want | "What specific element feels too simple? Should we try [alternative direction]?" |
| "I don't like the color" | Color doesn't match brand | Test 3-5 color options, present with feedback questions |
| "Make it more modern" | Feels dated or not contemporary | Show reference brands from their market; adjust if dated |
| "Make it bigger" | Logo feels too small/delicate | Usually not a scaling issue; typically need to simplify or increase boldness |
| "It's too simple" | Lacks personality or distinctiveness | This is often a compliment (simplicity is good); show how it works in context and at small sizes |
| "I want to see more options" | Not satisfied with direction | Clarify: Do they want variations of this concept, or completely new concepts? (May be scope expansion) |

**Pro tip:** When uncertain about feedback, ask clarifying questions. "When you say simple, do you mean the mark itself feels basic, or that the overall system lacks personality?"

### Scope Creep Prevention

Define clear boundaries upfront:

"This project includes:
- 3 initial concepts
- 2 revision rounds on selected concept
- 3-5 variations (icon-only, wordmark-only, monochrome)
- Basic usage guidelines

This project does NOT include:
- More than 3 initial concepts
- More than 2 revision rounds
- Complete brand system development (separate scope)
- Additional concepts after Round 2"

If client requests beyond scope: "This would be a scope expansion. Here's the additional cost/timeline..."

## Quality Checklist Before Delivery

Before handing off final logo files, verify:

**Vector Quality**
- [ ] Logo is 100% vector (not placed image)
- [ ] All shapes use simple fills (no gradients or effects)
- [ ] Paths are simplified (no unnecessary anchor points)
- [ ] No strokes (shapes use fills instead)
- [ ] All anchor points are smooth where needed (no accidental sharp points)

**Versatility**
- [ ] Tested at 16px, 32px, 64px, 256px, and large print
- [ ] Works in monochrome (black on white)
- [ ] Works reversed (white on black)
- [ ] Works in color on various backgrounds
- [ ] Icon-only version is standalone readable (if combination logo)
- [ ] Wordmark-only version is readable (if combination logo)

**File Organization**
- [ ] Main Illustrator file (with all artboards)
- [ ] Exported versions: AI, EPS, PDF, SVG, PNG (transparent)
- [ ] Named clearly with version labels
- [ ] Color files separate from monochrome
- [ ] Size variations labeled
- [ ] Clean layer naming in Illustrator

**Documentation**
- [ ] Design rationale document (1 page explaining concept)
- [ ] Usage guidelines (do's and don'ts)
- [ ] Color specifications (Hex, RGB, CMYK, Pantone if print)
- [ ] Minimum size guidelines
- [ ] Clear space specifications
- [ ] Contact information for designer

## Timeline Summary

**2-Week Sprint (Compressed):**
- Week 1: Research (3 days) + Sketching (4 days)
- Week 2: Vectorization (3 days) + Testing & Refinement (2 days) + Presentation (1 day)
- Revisions: Next 2-3 weeks

**6-Week Professional Engagement (Recommended):**
- Week 1-2: Research & sketching
- Week 3: Vectorization & refinement
- Week 4: Presentation & Round 1 revisions
- Week 5: Round 2 revisions & brand system start
- Week 6: Final polish & brand guidelines completion

**10-Week Premium Engagement:**
- Week 1-3: Deep research, competitive analysis, mood boards
- Week 4-5: Extended sketching (100+ variations)
- Week 6: Vectorization & refinement
- Week 7: Presentation & revisions
- Week 8-10: Complete brand identity system, style guide, applications

Choose timeline based on budget and project complexity.
