## THE INSPIRATION PROCESS [Gareis]

### Before Opening Design Software

1. **Spend 1+ hours of focused search** -- saves days of directionless execution
2. **Create dedicated artboards** per project for different aspects:
   - Typography inspiration
   - Color palette references
   - Layout/grid references
   - Photography/imagery direction
   - Overall mood/feeling

3. **Screenshot specific sections** -- do not save entire websites
4. **Write notes on WHY** you are saving each reference:
   - "Relationship between headline and subhead sizes"
   - "70% neutral, 30% accent color approach"
   - "Baseline grid creating vertical rhythm"
   - "Asymmetrical balance with heavy left image"

5. **Analyze references systematically** across:
   - Grid structure
   - Typography choices
   - White space usage
   - Color palette
   - Pacing and density

### After Gathering References

1. Close ALL reference materials before creating first concepts
2. Start with rapid sketching -- let absorbed inspiration guide subconsciously
3. Only return to references after establishing your own direction
4. Goal is not to be completely original -- goal is to be authentic in synthesized influences

## REFERENCE SELECTION CRITERIA [Gareis]

### Four Evaluation Factors

| Factor | Question | Weight |
|--------|----------|--------|
| Composition structure | Does the grid/layout approach solve a similar problem? | High |
| Mood alignment | Does this match the emotional direction of the project? | High |
| Practical usability | Can principles from this translate to my project context? | Medium |
| Originality | Does this push beyond expected/obvious solutions? | Medium |

### Curation Stats

- Save liberally during initial search
- Reject ~80% of initial selections after evaluation
- For client projects: present 12-50 reference images
- Each image chosen for specific attributes, not general "it looks nice"
- Being selective upfront saves enormous time later

## THE ONE-THING RULE [MDS]

**Only lift ONE specific thing from an inspiration source:**
- Typography approach, OR
- Color palette, OR
- Layout structure

**Never copy multiple elements from the same source.**

### Process
1. Make a list of things you like about a design
2. Close it -- do not look at it again
3. Apply only one extracted principle to your project
4. Move to a different source for the next element

### Boundary: Do Not Lift Brand-Synonymous Elements
- Stripe's angle gradient
- Apple's product photography style on white
- Spotify's duotone treatment

If it immediately makes someone think of another brand, do not use it.

## COPYING VS. BEING INSPIRED [Gareis]

### Definitions

**Copying:** Replicating a solution without understanding the thinking. Borrowing someone else's decisions.

**Being inspired:** Extracting principles and applying them to your unique problem in your own way.

### The Five-Step Framework

1. **Study MULTIPLE sources** (not one) -- diversifying forces new connections
2. **Extract principles** not visual elements -- "tight tracking on headlines creates urgency" not "use -4% tracking"
3. **Understand the WHY** behind decisions -- why did they use asymmetry here?
4. **Translate concepts** across different contexts -- editorial grid applied to SaaS dashboard
5. **Preserve your unique perspective** -- your combination of influences is inherently original

### Copy Work for Learning [MDS]

Recreating designs pixel-by-pixel is valuable for LEARNING:
- Exposes you to decisions you would not have made on your own
- Forces you to understand specific values (spacing, sizing, weight choices)
- Builds muscle memory for quality

**Key distinction:** Learning how someone made something vs. trying to pass it off as your own.

## INDUSTRY-SPECIFIC INSPIRATION DIRECTION [Gareis]

### Fashion
- Bold, creative, unseen compositions
- Draw from fashion photography and street styles
- High editorial quality
- Unexpected typography treatments

### Tech Startups
- Modern, clean aesthetic
- Could be bold or light depending on positioning
- Focus on clean interfaces and digital experiences
- Geometric shapes and structured layouts

### Financial
- Traditional designs showing trust
- Clean typography: sans-serif for numbers, thin serif for text
- Reliable, not trendy
- Conservative color palette (blues, darks, whites)

### Food & Beverage
- Warm, inviting photography
- Organic shapes and textures
- Script or display fonts for headlines
- Earth tones and warm palettes

### Creative/Portfolio
- Maximum creative freedom
- Unusual font combinations work especially well
- Less information means more room for experimental layouts
- [CONTRARIAN] Even "incompatible-looking" fonts can produce fantastic results when you know your use case [Gareis]

## ANTI-TREND PHILOSOPHY [Gareis]

[CONTRARIAN] "Design trends are shit" -- trends stop you from being creative, limiting you to what is currently popular instead of developing your own voice.

- Neumorphism, glassmorphism, 3D illustrations, gradients everywhere -- all look dated 6 months later
- Following trends = using someone else's solution
- Best work comes from ignoring trends completely
- Focus on fundamentals: typography, color theory, composition, hierarchy -- these never go out of style
- Typography and layout fundamentals have not changed in 12+ years
