# Font Classification & Pairing Guide

## FONT CLASSIFICATIONS [Gareis + Arash]

### Serif Fonts
- Small decorative strokes extending from main strokes
- Associated with: traditional, readability, formality
- Best for: editorial/publishing, newspapers, legal, financial, historical
- **Subcategories:** Old Style (organic, calligraphic -- Garamond, Bembo), Transitional (refined, balanced -- Times New Roman, Georgia), Didone (high contrast thick/thin -- Bodoni, Didot), Slab Serif (bold square serifs -- Heptus Slab)
- **Favorites [Gareis]:** OC Bartok (traditional, historic, excellent in caps), Soul Display (best in italic), Adobe Caslon Pro (the all-rounder -- works for headlines and long paragraphs at any size)

### Sans Serif Fonts
- No serifs -- clean, simple, minimalistic
- **Best default for UI/UX design** -- easy to read on screens [Arash]
- Best for: digital interfaces, technology, cosmetics, architecture, marketing, healthcare
- **Favorites [Gareis]:** Swiss International (fits almost every style; fine-tune tracking for headlines), Bezier Square (narrower; better paragraph readability), Ionik (lots of character; balanced reading)

### Script Fonts
- Mimic handwriting or calligraphy; flowing cursive letterforms
- Use mostly for headlines only -- hard to read at paragraph size
- Best for: weddings, beauty, art, restaurants, vintage, nature
- **Favorites [Gareis]:** English 157 BT (18th century elegance; tracking -4%), Nu Script (playful, casual; usable at paragraph size if minimum size maintained)

### Display Fonts
- Designed for large sizes, bold visual statements
- Most need line height reduction so text sticks together
- Best for: advertising, entertainment, events, food, sports
- **Favorites [Gareis]:** Druk Condensed (no tracking needed; perfect for all-caps big statements), Foska (mix of serif and display), Integral CF (only capitals; works even for body copy)

### Monospace Fonts
- Each character occupies same horizontal width
- Highly readable for precise alignment; not ideal for long body text
- Best for: retro, data visualization, science, technical, price tables
- **Favorites [Gareis]:** IBM Plex Mono (open source; multiple weights), GT Pressure Mono (compressed; easier body reading)

### Decorative Fonts
- Highly styled, unique, attention-grabbing
- Do NOT change tracking -- perfectly crafted by designer
- Best for: art, fashion, weddings, entertainment, portfolios, vintage
- **Favorites [Gareis]:** Byzantium (serif base with extended curves; headlines only), Pieter Tron (futuristic; works for headlines and body), Voyage (voluminous capitals; headlines only)

## SYSTEM FONTS [MDS]

| Platform | Font | Advantage |
|----------|------|-----------|
| iOS | San Francisco | Optimized for Apple screens; zero load time |
| Android | Roboto | Material Design native; zero cost |
| Windows | Segoe UI | System-optimized rendering |

**When to use system fonts:** Apps prioritizing performance, platform-native feel, or when brand differentiation is not the primary goal.

**When to use custom fonts:** Marketing sites, brand-heavy products, when the typeface needs to carry the design aesthetic.

## FONT PAIRING EXAMPLES [Gareis]

### Demonstrated Pairings

| Pairing | Style | Best For |
|---------|-------|---------|
| ABC Normal (sans) + Golden Warrior (serif) | Elegant black-and-white editorial | Fashion, portfolio |
| Champions HTF (display heavyweight) + English 157 BT (handwritten) | Bold editorial with orange signal color | Sports, editorial |
| Untitled Sans (sans) + Byzantium (decorative) | Earth tones, numbers prominent | Nature, lifestyle |
| F37 Zagma Mono (mono) + Editor's Note (serif) | Elegant fashion, typewriter charm | Fashion, editorial |
| Pieter Tron (decorative) + Nara Pro (serif) | Energetic portfolio with green signal | Creative, portfolio |

### Pairing Strategies

| Strategy | How | Risk Level |
|----------|-----|-----------|
| Same typeface, different weights | Go up 2+ weights (regular to bold, not regular to medium) | Lowest |
| Same typeface, different widths | Condensed + regular from same family | Low |
| Serif + sans-serif | Clear structural separation; most reliable mixed pair | Low |
| Display + serif | Headline energy + body elegance | Medium |
| Two distinct typefaces | Must be CLEARLY distinct, not similar-but-different | Medium |
| Decorative + standard | Decorative for headlines ONLY; standard for body | Medium-High |
| "Rule-breaking" pairs [CONTRARIAN: Gareis] | Incompatible-looking fonts CAN work if you know your use case | High |

### Pairing Test Questions

1. Do the fonts belong in the same era/world?
2. Is the contrast between them intentional and clear?
3. Would someone who does not know design think they "go together"?
4. Are they different ENOUGH to justify having two fonts?

| Industry | Primary | Secondary | Avoid |
|----------|---------|-----------|-------|
| Finance/Legal | Thin serif | Clean sans | Script, decorative |
| Technology | Modern sans | Mono for code/data | Ornate serif |
| Fashion/Luxury | Display or didone serif | Minimal sans | Generic sans (Arial) |
| Health/Wellness | Friendly sans or rounded | Soft serif | Heavy display |
| Food/Restaurant | Script or display | Readable serif | Sterile sans |
| Sports/Fitness | Bold display or condensed | Strong sans | Delicate serif |
| Education | Readable serif or sans | Mono for technical | Over-styled display |

## LEGIBILITY VS. READABILITY [Gareis]

### Legibility (Determined by Font Choice)
- X-height: taller = more legible
- Character width: average width most legible; very condensed/extended = less legible
- Weight: extremes (very light or very heavy) harder to read; stick to book/regular for body
- Stroke contrast: extreme contrast (Bodoni) harder at small sizes
- Counter spaces: smaller counters = more challenging to read

### Readability (Controlled by the Designer)
- Type size: smaller = harder (obvious but critical)
- Type case: ALL CAPS for lengthy text is harder; use sentence case
- Line height: tight = hard to read; generous spacing improves readability dramatically
- Line length: optimal 45-70 characters
- Contrast: always better to have slightly more contrast than less
- **Key insight:** Even very legible fonts become hard to read when values are set wrong. These factors are more important than the font itself.
- Example: 20pt font with 20pt line height = bad; 20pt font with 27pt line height = good; 16pt font with 22pt line height = excellent (smaller font + more line spacing can outperform larger font + tight spacing)

## FONT SELECTION PROCESS [Gareis]

### Step-by-Step Method
1. Never start by randomly browsing font websites -- this wastes time and rarely leads to good results
2. Write down: What mood? What function? What format? How much text?
3. If there is a mismatch between font personality and content, people will feel something is wrong even if they cannot explain it
4. Technical quality checks: Does the font have good built-in kerning pairs? Is character design consistent across all glyphs? Does it include all necessary special characters? How does it perform across different sizes?

### Tracking and Spacing Guidelines [Gareis]

| Context | Tracking Value | Notes |
|---------|---------------|-------|
| Body copy | -3% to +3% | Fine-tuning paragraphs; stay conservative |
| Headlines | up to -5% | Compact, authoritative feel |
| Subline/editorial | up to +80% | Single words only; destroys paragraph readability |
| Condensed fonts (e.g., Druk) | 0% (default) | Already tight; negative tracking causes overlap |
| Decorative fonts | 0% (default) | Do NOT change -- perfectly crafted by designer |
| All-caps labels | +5% to +80% | Wider tracking suits uppercase |

### Kerning vs Tracking [Gareis]
- Kerning: adjustment of space between specific character pairs (e.g., A and V, T and O) -- built into the font by the type designer
- Tracking: uniform adjustment of spacing between ALL characters in a text block
- High-quality fonts have hundreds or thousands of kerning pairs precisely adjusted
- For body copy, use Metrics kerning (respects designer intent)
- For headlines, use Optical kerning or manual adjustment

## TYPOGRAPHY AS TEXTURE [Gareis]

Typography creates "texture" (sometimes called "color" by typographers -- not actual color). Texture comes from combination of: type size, weight, line spacing, alignment.

| Texture Type | Properties | Best For |
|-------------|------------|---------|
| Lighter texture | Lighter weight + increased line spacing + more paragraph spacing | Luxury brands, contemporary designs, approachable content |
| Denser texture | Heavier weights + tighter line spacing + compact paragraphs | Academic content, authority-establishing designs |

## HIERARCHY CREATION TOOLS [Gareis]

Six tools for establishing hierarchy (in order of impact):
1. **Size** -- most obvious; use dramatic contrast (2x+ multiples, not small increments)
2. **Font weight and style** -- bold attracts attention; light for less emphasis; italic for editorial quotes
3. **Contrast** -- color contrast (change headline to orange = more attention); reduce tone to de-emphasize
4. **White space** -- elements need space to breathe; headline and body diagonal placement for balance
5. **Alignment** -- varying alignments create focal points without losing coherence
6. **Indent** -- subtle shifts in position create sub-hierarchy

[CONTRARIAN: Gareis] There are NO rules dictating font selection for specific audiences or subjects -- there are only recommendations. Be open to intentionally break away and explore new paths.
