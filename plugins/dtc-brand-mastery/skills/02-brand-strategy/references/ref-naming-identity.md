# Naming Frameworks & Brand Identity

## GRETTA'S FOUR CATEGORIES OF BRAND NAMES

### 1. Generic / Exact Match
- Examples: Hotels.com, Cars.com
- Avoid unless you already own the domain AND trademark
- Extremely expensive to acquire and nearly impossible to trademark

### 2. Descriptive
- Examples: SkinnyMeTea, All Bran, National Australia Bank
- Makes the product immediately clear
- Risk: can box you in if you expand beyond original product category

### 3. Associative / Suggestive (Gretta's Favorite)
- Examples: Dropbottle, The Fifth, Apple, Uber, Nike
- Creates connection without being literal
- Flexible enough for brand evolution and product line expansion

### 4. Coined / Abstract
- Examples: Kodak, Xerox, Google
- Blank slate -- you define the meaning entirely
- Harder to build recognition initially, but no baggage

---

## GOOD NAME CRITERIA (Love & Money Agency)

**A name SHOULD be:**
- Easy to remember
- Distinct from competitors
- Peaks interest or curiosity
- Makes sense in context
- Open to change and development

**A name should NOT:**
- Try to tell the whole story
- Be too literal
- Box you in to one product
- Steal the show from the product
- Slow things down (hard to spell, pronounce, or type)

## NAME STORMING PROCESS

1. **Word dump:** Set 5-minute timer, write every word relating to brand/product/benefits/feelings
2. Aim for minimum 15 words
3. Pick 5-10 favorites, go to thesaurus.com and "follow them down the rabbit hole"
4. Generate minimum 3-5 name options (first choices are often taken)
5. Check against good brand name criteria above
6. Do NOT get attached until you confirm you can actually use it

### Verification Steps
- Domain availability check (Namecheap, GoDaddy)
- Trademark search (USPTO, WIPO, local trademark offices)
- Social media handle availability
- Google search for existing brands with same name

## BRAND NAMING CAUTIONARY TALES

### SkinnyMeTea
- Chose "skinny" because it was the main marketable effect (weight loss)
- "Me" for inclusivity, "Tea" as descriptive element
- Problem: the word "skinny" became controversial in media -- people thought the brand message was "everybody needs to be skinny"
- Lesson: think about wider implications and "what if the media picks this up?"
- Contrarian: the controversial name may have actually contributed to success -- "it was contrarian and very obvious exactly what the product did"
- If she could redo it: would name the PRODUCT "Skinny Me Tea" but give the BRAND a different name entirely
- Key distinction: product name vs. brand name CAN and sometimes SHOULD be different

### The Fifth
- Gretta was in New York, had just been to Fifth Avenue (iconic fashion location)
- Numerical name worked for time-based brand (watches)
- Extended the name into all brand elements: blog "Five O'Clock," Instagram theme "The Fifth View" (bird's eye perspective)
- Later reworked entire brand experience around the name: selling on the 5th for 5 days

### Frank Body
- Gretta's sister's coffee scrub company
- "Frank" works as noun (guy's name, used for seductress first-person brand persona) AND adjective (honest/humble)
- Tagline/hashtag: "Let's be Frank"
- Multi-dimensional name creates richer brand storytelling

## KIAN GOLZARI'S PRODUCT NAMING STRATEGY

- Name should create emotional or functional association
- Example: "Veltra" = letters of "travel" rearranged -- tech-forward, precise, travel-associated
- Example: "Reebok" = gazelle in US dictionary (running association); Reebok founder won dictionary as race prize
- Military products: use phonetic alphabet (Alpha, Bravo, Echo) for tactical association
- Competitive advantage: Chinese sellers create meaningless brand names. A clever name is your edge.

## BRAND LOOK & FEEL

### Strategic Color Selection (Dropbottle Example)
- Forest green = nod to sustainability
- RGB ocean blue = trending color + ocean sustainability stat
- Yellow = lemon water (core product is fruit-infused bottle)
- Principle: tie every visual choice back to brand strategy and values

### Pantone Color Consistency (Kian Golzari)
- Select ONE Pantone color for your brand
- Give that Pantone number to ALL suppliers (fabric, coating, packaging, printing)
- Without Pantone: different shades from different suppliers
- With Pantone: consistent brand appearance across all touchpoints
- Think Tiffany blue, Burberry tartan, Apple packaging

### Logo Design Simplification
- Contrarian: "Your logo doesn't need to be an icon-based logo. It can honestly just be the name of your brand in a stylized font."
- Many top DTC brands use typography-only logos: Ritual, Recess, Dame, Cove, By Humankind
- Quick validation: post 4 logo options in a community, ask people to vote 1/2/3/4

### Mood Board Process
- Create 2 entirely different creative directions for the designer
- Direction 1: more mature, raw, authentic (larger DTC brand vibe)
- Direction 2: bright, color pop, closer to existing brand (easier iterative transition)
- Save inspiring Instagram posts to collection for ongoing reference
- Sources: The Dieline (packaging design), Pinterest (indie artists), cross-industry inspiration

### Merch as Brand Building + Perceived Value
- Dropbottle examples: fishnet/mesh grocery bag, stickers, compostable mailer bags
- Merch as purchase incentive: "Buy a Dropbottle and receive free tote bag" -- list tote at $40 on website so customer sees value
- Increases perceived value of the total purchase

## DESIGNER BRIEF DOCUMENT STRUCTURE (9 Sections)

1. **Rebrand outline** -- goals, data-driven insights
2. **Brand pyramid/strategy recap** -- from MVB Pyramid work
3. **Product direction** -- what products look like, feel like
4. **Brand kit** -- colorways, typography, logo specifications
5. **Merch ideas** -- branded items beyond core product
6. **Competition analysis** -- separate from brand SPO
7. **Brand SPO** -- aspirational brands you want to be like (NOT competitors)
8. **Logo inspiration section** -- visual references
9. **2 mood board options** -- two different creative directions

Ask designer to apply visual identity to real-life objects: packaging, thank you cards, emails, social posts, billboards. This tests whether the identity works across all touchpoints.

## AI BRAND BLUEPRINT (Kian Golzari)

Load 9 brand pillars into a GPT or Claude project:
1. Mission statement
2. Core values
3. Brand voice and tone
4. Target audience personas
5. Visual identity guidelines
6. Product positioning
7. Competitive landscape
8. Brand story
9. Key messaging framework

Use this loaded context for consistent brand-aligned content generation across all channels.
