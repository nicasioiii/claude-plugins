# Product Development & Prototyping

Develop products from concept through prototype to production-ready. Covers prototyping workflows, specification sheets, sampling processes, regulatory compliance, packaging design, and feature innovation.

## BEFORE STARTING: DETERMINE CONTEXT

Ask these questions if not already provided:

1. **Product type:** What category? (Physical good, ingestible, cosmetic, electronic?)
2. **Complexity:** Simple (existing product modification) or complex (new mold/tooling)?
3. **Stage:** Concept only, or have samples/prototypes already?
4. **Budget:** Available for prototyping? ($250-$500 for simple, $2,000+ for molds)
5. **Supplier:** Have you found a manufacturer yet?

## JASON WONG'S PROTOTYPING WORKFLOW

Complete process from idea to manufacturer-ready:

1. **Sketch on paper** -- hand-drawn concept showing key features and dimensions
2. **2D digital render by designer** -- clean visual representation
3. **Determine dimensions and materials** -- exact measurements, material specifications
4. **3D CAD file** -- the file format manufacturers need to create physical prototypes
5. **Send CAD to manufacturer for prototype production** -- they build the physical item

### Component-Level Prototyping
You do NOT need to prototype the entire product. If making a keyboard, prototype just the switches and caps rather than the whole keyboard. This dramatically reduces cost.

### Business Canvas Model for Prototyping
Use this tool during prototyping to identify strengths and weaknesses. Write down weaknesses, improve them through prototypes, cross them off as you go.

### Test-to-Failure Principle
The goal of prototype testing is to find WHERE the product breaks and understand its limitations -- not to confirm it works. Example: Jason found sample lashes could be worn 20+ times (expected only a handful) -- this limitation became the core marketing message.

### Jason's LED Sign Example
Drew concept on paper > sent to designer for 2D render > got dimensions/materials spec'd > sent to 3D designer for CAD file > manufacturer made several prototypes with different lights, colors, wires > tested for durability and overheating.

## GRETTA'S PROTOTYPE APPROACH

### Prototype vs. 3D Render Distinction
- **Prototype:** physical early sample/model to test a concept
- **3D Render:** digital visual representation (can inspire a prototype)
- If you can create a physical prototype, go for it; if not, 3D render is second best
- 3D renders can double as marketing assets (use on landing pages, pre-orders)

### Budget Benchmarks
- $250-$500 for quality prototype/3D render on freelance basis
- Luxury products (e.g., watch prototypes): significantly more
- "More time and more complexity equals more money"
- Each watch mould for The Fifth cost about $2,000; new watch face = $2,000-$3,000 per mould

### Where to Find Designers
- **Upwork, 99designs** -- search for "industrial designer" or "product designer" (ensure physical products, not software)
- Check portfolio and reviews before committing
- Agencies cost "a couple thousand dollars to tens of thousands" for packaging design
- Budget alternatives: The Dieline community, 99designs contests

### Designer Job Ad Template (Gretta)
Three key elements to include:
1. Brief product description and your vision
2. Reference images or mood board (what the product should feel like)
3. Specific deliverables expected (2D render, 3D CAD, prototype dimensions)

## KIAN GOLZARI'S PRODUCT DEVELOPMENT RESEARCH

### Competitor Analysis Online
- Reviews: both NEGATIVE (pain points to solve) and POSITIVE (features to include)
- Q&A section: reveals unexpected use cases (e.g., sleeping bags used for sleepovers, not just camping -- led to adding phone pouch feature)
- Competitor selling points: analyze images, sales copy, feature descriptions, product naming
- Contrarian: "Never be influenced by competition to develop a product. Create what YOU want first, then check competition to validate."

### Competitor Analysis Offline (In-Store)
- Visit specialist retail stores; touch, feel, try products
- Ask store staff: "What are biggest issues with this product? Many returns?"
- Staff sell products daily, deal with returns -- invaluable knowledge
- Study packaging, hang tags, QR codes, messaging

### Reverse Engineering Competitor Samples
- Purchase competitors' products online and offline
- Use the product extensively in real conditions
- Cut products open to see raw materials: foam type, fabric weight, coating, stitching method
- Test to point of failure -- that is where quality improvement is needed

### Create Action List
After all research, create bullet-point list of: features to add, problems to solve, materials to use. No point doing research without actioning it.

### Vision Board Method
Print all images: product features, packaging, colors, patterns, customer avatar, influencers. Put on wall as "visual meditation." Connections and new product ideas emerge from seeing everything together.

## SPECIFICATION SHEET (Kian Golzari)

The most critical document in product development. A one-pager with clear, concise information for the manufacturer.

### Kian's Product Spec Sheet Checklist (21 Fields)
1. Brand name
2. Product name
3. Product code
4. Launch season
5. Fabrics/materials (breakdown per component)
6. Pantone colors
7. Material specifics (e.g., Polyester 300D/900D, Nylon, waterproof rating)
8. Product dimensions (length, width, height, weight)
9. Feature list with annotations
10. Packaging type (poly bag, color box, hang tag)
11. Packaging dimensions
12. Certification requirements
13. Size guide (if applicable)
14. Competitor images for reference
15. Design modification annotations with images/mockups
16. New feature requests with sketches
17. Testing requirements
18. Compliance needs (region-specific)
19. Barcode/UPC requirements
20. Master carton specifications
21. Labeling requirements

Purpose: enables apples-to-apples price comparison across multiple factories. "Where mistakes happen is when you drip information to manufacturers over time. All info must be upfront at once."

## 3-STAGE SAMPLING PROCESS (Kian Golzari)

### Stage 1: Development Sample
- First attempt from manufacturer based on spec sheet
- Typically 90-95% correct
- Review carefully; provide detailed feedback document
- Expect 1-3 rounds of revision

### Stage 2: Pre-Production Sample
- 100% perfect sample you approve
- Label it, tag it, date it, photograph it, sign off
- Send images to supplier BEFORE paying deposit
- RULE: Never place deposit until you have 100% perfect pre-production sample

### Stage 3: Pre-Shipment Sample
- Actual production unit sent to you during or after production run
- Must be 100% match to pre-production sample
- Any discrepancy must be resolved before shipping
- Final check: third-party pre-shipment inspection (PDF report with images, pass/fail)

### Product Testing Rigor
- Test your own samples extensively in real conditions
- Send to third-party testing companies for legal/industry standards
- Beta test with community members (Facebook group): "Who wants to be first to test our new product?"
- Send to influencers for feedback DURING development (not after) -- they promote products they helped shape more passionately

## FEATURE INNOVATION (Kian Golzari)

Principle: Add features that cost pennies but deliver massive perceived value.

### Kian's Veltra Travel Backpack Examples
- V-shaped zipper pullers (brand identity): minimal cost
- RFID pocket for credit cards/passport
- Dedicated charger pocket with labeled tabs
- Free TSA padlock included
- Okobahn QR code for lost-bag tracking
- Free packable daypack inside main bag
- Scratch-proof sunglasses pocket
- Zipper puller that converts to phone/tablet kickstand: 15-20 cents to manufacture, massive perceived value
- iPhone SIM-eject pin built into zipper puller
- Emergency whistle on chest strap
- Removable shoulder straps (converts to messenger bag)
- "First Edition" label + unique messaging
- Hidden valuables pocket under compression straps

## PACKAGING DESIGN

### Jason Wong's Four Elements
1. What to communicate on packaging
2. Choosing the packaging type
3. Packaging material
4. Designing the actual packaging

### Unboxing Experience Hierarchy
1. Protect the product
2. Represent the brand
3. Elevate the customer experience

### Gretta's Packaging Considerations
1. Weight (affects shipping cost)
2. Size (affects storage and fulfillment)
3. Presentation (brand experience)
4. Protection (product integrity)
5. Sustainability (matches brand values?)

### Gretta's Decision Rule
- 100-300 units: use stock/generic packaging or simple branded stickers
- 500+ units: invest in custom packaging (economies of scale kick in)

### Kian's 1-Color Box Hack
- Brown box = too cheap looking
- Full color box = expensive ($2-3+)
- 1C (1-color) box: brown box with all text/instructions in single brand Pantone color
- Affordable, recyclable, branded appearance

### Social Media Optimization for Packaging
- Include social handles on all packaging
- Create "share-worthy" unboxing experience
- Include surprise gifts, message cards, unique patterns

### DTC vs. Retail Packaging
- DTC: does not need barcodes or as much shelf info
- Retail: must stand out on shelf, be stackable, communicate brand
- Jason's tip: prepare packaging as if selling in retail already (even if DTC-only at launch)

## PRODUCT REGULATORY COMPLIANCE

### Jason Wong's Condom Story (Cautionary Tale)
Created Ramen Noodle Condom based on a meme. Found factory with all certifications. Changed only wrapper design. At shipping time, freight forwarder asked for FDA documents. Condoms are Type 2 medical devices. Certification would have cost "tens of thousands of dollars and taken years." Lost "a few thousand dollars" on the shipment.

### Five Types of Regulatory Requirements
1. **Product-specific safety standards:** Different categories have specific standards (children's toys, apparel, furniture). CPSC has "Regulatory Robot" tool to help identify applicable regulations
2. **Substance regulations:** Lead content is the most important violation in US. Key regulations: California Prop 65 (US), REACH (Europe)
3. **Documentation requirements:** Declaration of Conformity (EU), General Certificate of Conformity (US). Your freight company will know what documents you need
4. **Labeling requirements:** Different requirements for toys, apparel, games. Include in QA checklist every time
5. **Testing requirements:** Products with medical claims, cosmetics, skincare need lab testing. SPF claims require lab-certified testing

### FTC Crossover
If you circumvent FDA regulations and use claims in marketing, you can also violate FTC rules for misleading advertising. Double exposure.

### Tool
CPSC Regulatory Robot -- helps identify which regulations apply to your product.

## PRODUCT TIMING: THE 365 MODEL (Kian Golzari)

- Use Google Trends to see when products are searched most
- Plan production timeline backwards from peak selling season
- Example: Valentine's Day product needs stock by late January > production starts November > samples finalized October > spec sheet sent September
- Evergreen products = year-round demand (preferred for beginners)
- Seasonal products can be very profitable if timed correctly

### Speed of Product Development
- Simple product (no customization): 2-4 weeks for samples, 30-45 days production
- Complex product (custom molds, electronics): 3-6 months total
- Key bottleneck: often packaging, not product itself
- Factor in shipping time: sea freight 30-45 days, air freight 5-10 days
