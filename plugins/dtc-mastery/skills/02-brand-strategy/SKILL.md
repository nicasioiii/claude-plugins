# Customer Personas & Brand Strategy

Build customer personas, brand pyramid, positioning frameworks, and brand identity. Covers the complete brand-building journey from audience definition through visual identity.

## BEFORE STARTING: DETERMINE CONTEXT

Ask these questions if not already provided:

1. **Product defined?** Do you have a validated product, or still ideating?
2. **Target audience:** Who is your ideal customer? (Even a rough description helps)
3. **Stage:** Starting brand from scratch, or rebranding/evolving?
4. **Platform:** DTC-first, Amazon-first, or omnichannel?
5. **Brand goal:** Building for long-term exit, or quick revenue?

## BRAND vs. STORE DISTINCTION

Gretta's foundational philosophy every entrepreneur must understand:

- "A brand isn't your branding. It isn't just a name, a logo, or even a product. A brand only exists in someone's mind."
- A brand is a perception, created via experience AND association -- factual/rational (yellow box) or emotional/irrational (makes me happy)
- Marty Neumeier (The Brand Gap): "Your customers will develop a gut feeling about your business. That feeling IS your brand."
- Love & Money Agency definition: "A brand is something you can sell, even if you no longer have products or services to sell."

**Renting vs. Buying Analogy:** Dropshipping/store = renting a house (someone else owns it). Building a brand = owning a house (an asset you can sell).

Acquisition data points proving brand value:
- Native (natural deodorant): acquired by P&G for $100M after only 2.5 years
- Movement Watches: acquired by Movado for $200M (over 2x revenue)
- Dollar Shave Club: acquired by Unilever for $1B
- Key insight: heritage brands want the consumer relationships DTC startups built

## CUSTOMER PERSONA FRAMEWORK (Gretta Van Riel)

### Components to Define
- **Demographics:** age, gender, job, marital status, income
- **Psychographics:** interests, habits, attitudes, emotions, preferences
- **Behavioral patterns:** what they do, where they spend time online and offline
- **Motivations & goals:** what drives purchasing decisions
- **Pain points:** specific problems they experience
- Create 2-3 personas per brand, give them names and photos

### Detailed Persona Examples

**SkinnyMeTea -- Three Personas:**
1. **Maddie (Detox-Retox Repeater)** -- Age 18-28; wants to have cake and eat it too; exercises casually; loves reality TV; does not mind being "basic"
2. **Grace (Day-to-Dayer)** -- Age 29-39; learned fad diets do not work; cleanses seasonally; prefers "cleanse" over "detox"; preventative health mindset
3. **Sophie (Pre-Eventer)** -- Age 18-39; time-sensitive; needs product for specific event (wedding, birthday); wants fast results

**Reusable Straw -- Two Contrarian Personas:**
1. **Todd (True Environmentalist)** -- Genuinely cares about impact; already has reusable keep cup; appeals to via big picture messaging, statistics, charity initiatives
2. **Susie (Virtue Signaler)** -- Cares about APPEARING eco-conscious; loves how the straw LOOKS; appeals to via design/aesthetics, ego/status

Key insight: Personas can be oppositional (contrasting values for same product). Marketing to both requires different messaging angles.

### Kian Golzari's Customer Avatar for Product Development
- Age, gender, height, disposable income
- Where do they live? Where do they hang out?
- What type of people do they associate with?
- How many times/year do they travel/vacation?
- Is this a must-have or a want? How to turn want into must-have?
- Good/Better/Best product range based on avatar's spending capacity
- Avatar is organic document that changes as you develop products

## MINIMUM VIABLE BRAND PYRAMID (Gretta Van Riel)

Source: Love & Money Agency. 6 elements, built bottom to top:

### Element 1: TARGET AUDIENCE
Demographics + psychographics. How they expect to be spoken to. This is your foundation.

### Element 2: BENEFITS (Rational/Tangible)
Why you created the product. The gap you are filling.
- Dropbottle: (1) Functional (innovative hydration, high-quality materials), (2) Fashionable (form + function), (3) Sustainable ($1/bottle to Drop for Drop charity)

### Element 3: VALUES (Emotional/Intangible)
"People won't remember what you said or did, but they'll remember how you made them feel."
- Dropbottle: (1) Pragmatic, (2) Altruistic, (3) Transparent, (4) Conscious

### Element 4: APHORISM / BRAND VALUE PROPOSITION
Pithy 1-2 sentence observation that defines the brand. Must be broad enough for the whole business, specific enough to differentiate.
- Dropbottle: "Every Drop Counts" (references hydration + social impact). Evolved from "Bottles with Purpose" (too broad).

### Element 5: BRAND VISION
What the business aspires to achieve beyond the bottom line.
- Dropbottle: "Reinvent the way people drink water and stay hydrated while leading the way for sustainable and socially conscious businesses"

### Element 6: BRAND PERSONALITY (Archetypes)
Pick 1 core + 2 sub-archetypes.
- Key archetypes: Joker, Mother, King, Friend, Caregiver, Lover, Creator, Sage, Innocent, Explorer, Wise, Dreamer, Magician, Ruler, Shadow
- Dropbottle: Core = The Wise; Sub 1 = The Dreamer; Sub 2 = The Friend

## SAVE FRAMEWORK (Jason Wong)

Position your brand using Solution, Access, Value, Education:

**1. SOLUTION:** Position products as answers to pain points, NOT as features. Glossier does not sell concealer -- it sells "simple skincare essentials for busy women."

**2. ACCESS:** Remove all barriers. Financial: payment plans. Functional: simplify UX. Accessibility: broader distribution. Glossier: $14-$38 price points for Gen Z/Millennial budgets.

**3. VALUE:** Create perceived value exceeding price through brand, community, experience. "Buying Glossier gets you into the cool kids club."

**4. EDUCATION:** Build trust through how-to content, ingredient education, industry insights.

Result: Glossier went from blog to 13K Instagram to 1.6M followers to $1.2B valuation by consistently applying SAVE.

## KIAN GOLZARI'S CORE VALUES EXERCISE

Write 5 core values BEFORE developing your first product. These feed into product development, materials, packaging, hiring, and messaging.

Nike example (1970s): "Our business is change" + "We're on offense all the time" + "Your job isn't done until the job is done"

### Brand Inspiration Exercise
- List 5 brands you know and love as "brand SPOs" (brand inspiration)
- Study their visual identity, tone, values, customer experience
- Note what specifically resonates and WHY
- These brands guide your positioning but should NOT be copied

## BRAND BUILDING TACTICS (Matt Clark)

- Brand name: memorable, available as domain, hints at positioning ("Aviator" for watches)
- Brainstorm 50+ brand name options, then conduct trademark search
- Logo: use Fiverr ($50-$500) or 99designs; should work in B&W, scalable
- Packaging is MARKETING, not logistics: Apple's minimalist packaging, Bark Box's wooden box
- Custom packaging adds $0.50-$2 per unit
- Product inserts: printed card with QR code to email signup, thank you + discount code, review CTA -- cost-effective customer acquisition
- Document your journey from day one: founder story is one of your biggest marketing assets

## INSTRUCTOR DISAGREEMENT: Pre-Launch Strategy

| Instructor | Approach | Best When |
|---|---|---|
| Gretta | Build audience months before product exists ("Market-Product Fit"); 800K followers before Dropbottle launched | You have social media skills and time |
| Matt Clark | 12-week sprint; 6-month total timeline; "action trumps everything" | You have capital for ads |
| Jason Wong | Opportunity cost of delayed launch outweighs imperfection | General principle |

Resolution: If you have social skills and time, audience-first yields lowest CAC. If you have capital, speed approach works. Delayed launches cost more than imperfect ones. Route by available resources and skills.
