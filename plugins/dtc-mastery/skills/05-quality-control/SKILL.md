# Quality Control & Inspections

Ensure product quality from sample through production runs. This skill covers the complete QC process including when to inspect, how to inspect, what standards to use, and what to do when things go wrong. Quality control is non-negotiable -- skipping it has cost real entrepreneurs hundreds of thousands of dollars.

## BEFORE STARTING: DETERMINE CONTEXT

Ask these questions if not already provided:

1. **Order stage:** First order, repeat order, or evaluating samples?
2. **Product type:** What are you manufacturing? (Category affects defect standards)
3. **Supplier relationship:** New supplier or established relationship?
4. **Payment terms:** Have you paid in full, or is 70% balance still pending?
5. **Past QC issues:** Any previous quality problems with this supplier?

## WHY QC IS NON-NEGOTIABLE: REAL HORROR STORIES

### Gretta's SkinnyMeTea Disaster ($1M+ Loss)
Ordered a massive bulk shipment of tea without inspection. Product arrived with quality issues that could not be sold. The financial loss exceeded $1M. This single experience shaped her entire approach to manufacturing.

### Gretta's Dropbottle QC Failure
Defective glass bottles kept arriving. Bled money on replacements and customer refunds. Taught her that quality can drop significantly from prototype to mass production.

### Jason Wong's Inspection Mistake
Forgot to get products inspected. Received thousands of dollars in unsellable inventory. "A few hundred dollars" for inspection "could save you thousands of dollars in potential loss."

### Jason's Candle Brand Packaging Failure
Glass shattered during delivery in the first month. Had to reship every order because packaging did not protect the glass. Cost him money on every single order until packaging was redesigned.

Core lesson from all instructors: **Quality can and WILL drop from prototype to production run. Never assume mass production matches your approved sample.**

## THIRD-PARTY INSPECTION PROCESS

### How It Works
1. **Approve a single unit sample** -- your "golden sample"
2. **Send golden sample details to inspector** -- photos, spec sheet, acceptable tolerances
3. **Inspector visits factory on inspection date** -- compares production units against golden sample
4. **Inspector tests random sample** from production run (typically 20% of units)
5. **Inspector provides detailed report** -- photos, measurements, pass/fail for each criteria
6. **You make ship/reject/rework decision** based on report

### Inspection Options
1. **Alibaba's Inspection Service** (inspection.alibaba.com): Alibaba-certified inspection firms. Convenient if already using Alibaba for sourcing.
2. **Qima** (formerly AsiaInspection): "One of the largest firms that have experience in a wide variety of industries" -- Jason Wong's recommendation. Global coverage.
3. **Your freight forwarder:** Some freight forwarders offer inspection services. Convenient but may be less thorough.

### Inspection Cost
- $100-$400 depending on company, location, and inspection level
- First inspection is YOUR cost
- Rework inspections (after failed inspection) should be at SUPPLIER'S cost
- ROI: a $300 inspection can prevent $10,000+ in losses

### When to Inspect
- EVERY new supplier's first production run (mandatory)
- Every new product from an established supplier
- Every order for the first 3-6 months of a supplier relationship
- Minimum every 3 months for established relationships
- After any previous quality issue (verify corrective action worked)

## AQL 2.5 STANDARD

### What AQL 2.5 Means
AQL stands for Acceptance Quality Limit. AQL 2.5 means a maximum 2.5% defect rate is acceptable. This is the industry standard for consumer products.

### How Sampling Works
- Inspector does NOT check every unit
- Random sample from the production run
- Sample size determined by total order size and AQL level
- Typical: 20% of units randomly selected for inspection

### AQL Sampling Table (Simplified)

| Order Size | Sample Size | Max Defects Allowed (AQL 2.5) |
|---|---|---|
| 2-8 units | All units | 0 |
| 9-15 units | All units | 0 |
| 16-25 units | All units | 1 |
| 26-50 units | 8 | 0 |
| 51-90 units | 13 | 1 |
| 91-150 units | 20 | 1 |
| 151-280 units | 32 | 2 |
| 281-500 units | 50 | 3 |
| 501-1200 units | 80 | 5 |
| 1201-3200 units | 125 | 7 |
| 3201-10000 units | 200 | 10 |

If defects found exceed the maximum allowed, the lot FAILS inspection.

## DEFECT CLASSIFICATION

### Critical Defects (ZERO Tolerance)
- Dangerous to user (sharp edges, electrical hazard, toxic materials)
- Regulatory violation (missing safety labels, non-compliant materials)
- Product completely non-functional
- ANY critical defect = automatic fail regardless of quantity

### Major Defects
- Missing parts or components
- Functionality significantly impaired
- Wrong color, size, or material vs. specification
- Packaging damage that affects product quality
- Typically: no more than 2.5% of sample (AQL 2.5)

### Minor Defects (Cosmetic)
- Small scratches or marks not visible during normal use
- Slight color variation within acceptable range
- Minor packaging imperfections
- Typically: higher tolerance (AQL 4.0 often used for minor)

### Writing Inspection Criteria
Use simple English. Your inspector may not speak fluent English.
- GOOD: "Button must click. Light must turn on. No scratches on front panel."
- BAD: "Ensure the haptic feedback mechanism provides satisfactory tactile response upon actuation."
- Include PHOTOS of acceptable vs. unacceptable for each criteria
- Number each checkpoint for easy reference

## INSPECTION BENCHMARKS

| Metric | Standard |
|---|---|
| Normal defect rate | 2-5% of production |
| Maximum acceptable (AQL 2.5) | 2.5% for major defects |
| Random sample percentage | ~20% of total units |
| Inspection frequency (new supplier) | Every order |
| Inspection frequency (established) | Every 3 months minimum |
| Critical defect tolerance | ZERO -- any critical defect = fail |

## 30/70 PAYMENT LEVERAGE

This is your most powerful QC tool. If you are using standard 30% deposit / 70% on completion payment terms:

- You have NOT paid the final 70% when inspection happens
- If inspection reveals problems, supplier has strong financial incentive to fix them
- "You have the upper hand in these situations" -- Jason Wong
- Options when problems found:
  - Demand supplier rework defective units at their cost
  - Negotiate discount on the 70% balance
  - Refuse shipment entirely (extreme, but available)
  - Accept partial shipment of good units only

### Why 100% Upfront Is Dangerous
If you have already paid 100%, you have zero leverage. Supplier has your money AND your defective products. This is why payment terms matter for quality.

## MANUFACTURER WARRANTY CONTRACTS

### What to Include
- Defect liability period (minimum 30 days after receipt, ideally 90 days)
- Rework/replacement obligations for defective units
- Who bears inspection and shipping costs for rework
- Maximum acceptable defect rate written into agreement
- Corrective action requirements after quality failures

### Trade Assurance (Alibaba)
- Alibaba protection service that helps buyers when orders go wrong
- Covers: defective products, delayed shipments, lost products
- Important: specifics MUST be in the agreement. If you want max 2% defect rate, that MUST be written in. Without it, it is not covered.

## FAILED INSPECTION ACTION PLAN (Matt Clark's 7+ Steps)

When inspection results come back with problems:

### Step 1: Understand the Nature of the Problem
Is it a material issue, assembly issue, cosmetic issue, or design issue? Different problems have different solutions.

### Step 2: Contact Both Inspector AND Supplier
Get both perspectives. The inspector reports what they found; the supplier explains what went wrong.

### Step 3: INSIST on Pictures
Demand photos of every defect type found. Do not accept verbal descriptions only.

### Step 4: Review Results with Both Parties
Three-way discussion: you, inspector, supplier. Align on the facts.

### Step 5: Request Complete Inspection
If initial spot-check found problems, request full inspection to determine if the problem is throughout the entire order or isolated.

### Step 6: Prepare Action Plan
Options (from least to most severe):
- **Rework the defective units:** Supplier fixes and re-inspection occurs (most common)
- **Sort and ship only good units:** Accept partial shipment
- **Negotiate discount and ship anyway:** If defects are minor/cosmetic
- **Refuse the goods entirely:** Nuclear option; use when defect is critical or pervasive
- **Ship as-is:** NOT recommended unless defects are truly negligible

### Step 7: Create Corrective Action Report
Document: problems found, root causes identified, solutions implemented, timeline for corrections. This becomes the standard for all future orders.

### After Resolution
- Re-inspect before shipment (at supplier's cost)
- Implement corrective measures for future orders
- Update spec sheet with clearer specifications if ambiguity caused the issue
- Do not expect suppliers to completely alter their manufacturing process for you
- If supplier does not value your business, they will not implement corrections -- consider switching

## FIRST ORDER RISK MANAGEMENT

### Gretta's Principle
"Version one is better than version none." Your first product will not be perfect. The goal is to get it good enough to sell and generate real feedback.

### Jason Wong's Approach
"I always like to place a minimum amount for my first order because samples won't always tell the bigger picture. Suppliers could always send you a good sample unit, but the mass production would be made with something of less quality."

### First Order Checklist (Jason Wong)
- Inform supplier you will perform an inspection (your cost first time)
- Confirm: price, spec sheet, shipment date, payment terms
- Confirm: inspection date, logistics provider, label instructions
- Confirm: carton information, destination instructions, export carton markings, rating labels

### Managing Expectations
- First production run WILL have issues -- plan for it
- Budget for rework or partial rejects (5-10% contingency)
- Keep first order small to limit exposure
- Use first order feedback to improve spec sheet for second order
