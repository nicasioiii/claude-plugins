# Newsletter Writing Craft Reference

## Sam Bennett Writing Rules -- Complete Set [E05]

### Email Fundamentals
- Average person receives 190 emails/day
- 99% of consumers check email daily; 50%+ check 10+ times/day
- Email ROI across all industries: 3,800% ($1 spent = $38 returned)
- Email is the only marketing 100% controlled by the consumer
- 3 types of emails: Inquiry (one-to-one asks), Transactional/Informational, Marketing/Sales

### Rules to BREAK (from school)
- One-sentence paragraphs are your email's best friend (people scan, not read; white space feels easier)
- Numbered lists and bullet points = visual cue to pay attention

### Rules to FOLLOW
1. Only one exclamation point per email -- if sentence needs one, make stronger word choices
2. Avoid all caps, bold, colored fonts (sparingly if at all)
3. Never start sentence with "it was/it is/they were" -- repeat the specific noun
4. Eliminate filler words: really, just, very, so
5. Eliminate adverbs completely when possible
6. **3:1 "you" to "I" ratio** -- talk to them about THEM
7. Don't use "we" -- sounds weak. "You have this problem, I have an answer"
8. One topic per email; if two topics, send two emails
9. Each email needs a clear desired result + clear next step for the reader

### How to Write a Terrible Email (What NOT to Do)
1. **Lack of clarity:** Too much going on. Each email = one thing
2. **Trying to be too clever:** Sarcasm, humor, wordplay fail in electronic communication. Be cheerful, pleasant, clear
3. **Jargon:** If a bright nine-year-old cannot understand it, rewrite
4. **Indefinite articles:** Don't start with "it seems," "this was." Use the proper noun
5. **Starting with apology:** Say "thank you" instead. "Thank you for being patient" not "Sorry it took so long"
6. **Assuming reader has opinion about you:** "You're probably thinking I send too many emails" -- they were not thinking about you at all

## The Number One Writing Question [E05/Sam Bennett]

**"How are they misunderstanding their problem?"**

- If they understood their problem, they would have fixed it (people are not stupid)
- Enter the conversation already happening inside their heads
- Example: Nobody thinks "I need a nootropic." They think "It's 4pm, I need coffee" or "I can't remember stuff." Use THAT as your subject line
- Then: "It's not your fault" -> Benefits -> Call to action

## The Graceful Interruption Template [E05/Sam Bennett]

Brain is motivated two ways: toward what it wants, away from what it doesn't want.

**Template:**
"Hi [specific person who wants X], want [ABC desired outcome]? Let me show you how to get it without [XYZ thing they don't want]. [Do this action step now]."

- Interrupt with value (homemade cookies metaphor), not more work
- Must feel like a gift, not an intrusion

## The "You Get Because" Formula [E05/Sam Bennett]

Three most important words in email copy:

1. **You:** Identify exactly who you are speaking to so they raise their hand
2. **Get:** What specifically do they receive?
3. **Because:** Why? (The Xerox study: even a weak "because" dramatically increases compliance)

Example: "You [alumni of Hogwarts] get [25% off school sweatshirts] because [it's Dumbledore's birthday]"

## Making Blast Emails Feel Personal [E05/Sam Bennett]

**The secret:** Talk to them about them, specifically. Use specific, empathic, real-life examples.

Progression of specificity:
- Bad: "I'm a health and wellness coach"
- Better: "I'm a health and wellness coach who works with perimenopausal women"
- Best: "You know how your favorite pants won't button anymore? You know how you burst into tears in the frozen food aisle for no reason? I can help."

When people feel seen and special, they think of you as a friend and your emails become welcome.

## Elements of a Must-Read Email List [E06/Throssell]

A list becomes must-read when it has these elements (you do not need all, but more is better):

1. Radically different, thought-elevating insights on a topic
2. The impression of being at the cutting edge, sharing things people would miss
3. A strong and trustworthy opinion on developing news -- especially if controversial
4. A good sense of humor
5. A unique individual writing style with a distinctive voice
6. Excellent storytelling
7. Personal (and real) insights into daily life or behind-the-scenes, with recurring characters
8. A voyeuristic look into the dream lifestyle of the reader
9. Precise insight into the market's pain points and struggles
10. The occasional good old-fashioned rant

## Storytelling in Email -- Complete Guide [E06/Throssell]

### Exaggeration Technique
Most boring stories could use a good whack of exaggeration. Amplify emotion in words. Turn statements into screams. People instantly teleport, burst into rooms instead of entering them. You are not surprised -- your eyes are wide with shock. Be exciting. People love it.

### Write "Based on a True Story"
Take creative license. Compress timelines. Eliminate unnecessary characters. Rewrite dialogue for clarity or humor. Do not lie to make yourself look better. Philosophy: "based on a true story."

### Always Be on the Lookout for Story Ideas
Most people have several story-worthy moments each day. Look for SMALL things, not big things. Everyday events make more relatable stories. Constantly note ideas down on phone. Your "story sense" gets better with practice.

### The Triforce of Email Leads: Relevant, Entertaining, Brief
Three factors for the lead of your email:
- a) Relevance to your tip/lesson/pitch
- b) Entertainment value
- c) Brevity

If your lead falls short in one, compensate in the other two. Must hit at least 2 of 3.

### The Sitcom Secret
Stories exist NOT to relate an interesting series of events but to flesh out your CHARACTER by showing how you RESPOND to those events. Think of how a sitcom works -- you bond with characters by watching how they navigate trials. Focus on your REACTIONS, not just the events.

### How to Use Stories to Sell
The story must plant an IDEA in the reader's mind that makes them desire or believe they need the product. Like the movie Inception -- plant the idea. Do not use any random story. Pick a story that plants the right idea for the product you are selling.

### Horror Stories for Persuasion
The most effective sales emails tell HORROR stories. Humans are psychologically hardwired to be more persuaded by horror stories than anything else. Show someone a clear, horrific vision of the awful fate awaiting them without your solution -- they persuade THEMSELVES. Find your market's horror stories, show them, then show your product as the way out.

### Don't "Write Like You're Writing to a Friend" [CONTRARIAN]
None of the best emails truly sound like emails to a friend. Think of it more as writing an entertainment column for a newspaper or magazine. Still write to one reader ("you" not "you guys"), but the style is more crafted and contrived.

## Personality, Character, and Voice [E06/Throssell]

### Building Characters
- Make characters of the people in your life -- like a sitcom cast
- Readers should know you better after every email (not necessarily like you more)
- Caricature everyone including yourself -- amplify the interesting parts
- Brands need a voice too -- best with a single representative behind the brand

### The Authority-Figure Trick
Paint yourself as a character in stories who is some form of authority figure: Teacher/student, general/soldier, kidnapper/prisoner. Reinforces status while entertaining.

### Multiple Personas in Launch Sequences
Different personas with different tones: excitement, story-based, unique insider perspective. Each persona brings a fresh angle to the same promotion.

## Balancing Stories and Selling [E06/Throssell]

### The Diet Coke Principle
If the news media is high-sodium junk food (bad news), be the refreshing entertainment. If your marketing is the good news, the laugh, the smile in their day, you will be where they WANT to go.

### Emotional Balance Rules
- Be careful about getting heavy or serious -- people prefer fun and lighthearted
- DO use pain points with empathy -- nailing exact pain points has inherent HOPE (like an ER doctor diagnosing symptoms)
- Express pain points as STORIES = still entertaining while targeting pains
- Do not show weakness -- stay in character; never let emotion undermine your projected persona
- Fights get more attention than anything else -- but must be for a good cause
- Play the ball, not the man -- passionate first draft, then remove nastiness

## Call to Action Best Practices [E05/Sam Bennett]

- Be crystal clear about what, how, and by when
- Don't just say "click here" -- say "click here to buy now" / "click here to read the article"
- Use embedded links and/or buttons, max 2-3 per email
- Don't use link shorteners (tacky and spammy)
- Habituate clicking through low-stakes CTAs so they click when you need them to buy

## Getting Responses (Inquiry Emails) [E05/Sam Bennett]

**3-point checklist for non-responses:**
1. Did they know a response was needed? (State it explicitly)
2. Did they know by when? (Specific date + "because")
3. Did you give them an easy obvious way to respond?

**Follow-up formula:** Resend original with "Hi [name], just wanted to bubble this up in case it slipped through the cybercracks. Yours, [name]"
- Cheerful, non-blaming, gives them a plausible out

## Finding Sure-Fire Copy [E05/Sam Bennett]

- Surveys are mostly useless: only survey-takers respond, ask wrong questions, humans are terrible at affective prediction [CONTRARIAN]
- **Amazon reviews = free focus group:** Exact customer language about desires, complaints, suggestions
- Use their exact words (anonymously) in your email copy
- Mine any review site for adjectives and language patterns

## Current Events Email Strategy [E06/Throssell]

### The Bridge Technique
1. Use current event in subject line (gets the open)
2. Pay off the subject line quickly (satisfy curiosity)
3. Smoothly pivot from event to your topic through a clever bridge
4. Deliver your core message/pitch

### Warning
When you use current events to get opens, you focus emotional state on that news, not your offer. The bridge must be smooth enough that the emotional transition happens naturally.

### Email Reuse Rules
- Can reuse emails, but no more than once per year
- Annual frequency: readers vaguely recognize but have forgotten content
- Every few months = readers get tired and resentful
