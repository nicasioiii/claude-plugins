# Write Subject Lines & Preview Text

Write high-open-rate subject lines using curiosity gaps, self-interest, specificity, and proven formulas. Includes preview text strategy and A/B testing methodology.

## THE SUBJECT LINE MATH THAT MATTERS

Doubling your open rate has a greater total impact than doubling your click-through rate. If 1,000 people receive an email:
- 20% open rate with 5% CTR = 10 clicks
- 40% open rate with 5% CTR = 20 clicks (doubled)
- 20% open rate with 10% CTR = 20 clicks (doubled)

Both double clicks, but improving open rate also means more people read your message, see your brand, and build familiarity. Subject lines are the highest-leverage element in email marketing.

---

## THE QUESTION-FORMING PRINCIPLE (Core Method)

A subject line must set up a QUESTION in the reader's mind and imply the answer is in the email. The more interesting the question, the more likely it gets opened.

### How to Evaluate Any Subject Line
Look at it and ask: "What question does this form in my reader's mind, and how badly would they want to know the answer?"

### Examples
| Subject Line | Question Formed | Quality |
|-------------|-----------------|---------|
| "How to stop panic attacks with a tennis ball" | "Wait, you can stop a panic attack with a tennis ball?!" | Excellent -- surprise + curiosity |
| "This is not the email I wanted to send you today" | "What happened that made him not want to send it?" | Strong -- personal intrigue |
| "The $20 copywriting seminar that saved my business" | "What seminar? How did $20 save a business?" | Strong -- specificity + self-interest |
| "Hey" (Barack Obama) | "Why is the President emailing me?" | Context-dependent -- sender name creates the question |
| "Summer breeze, ice cream and sandy shoes" | No clear question formed | Weak -- pretty but no curiosity gap |

---

## THE DOUBLE WHAMMY OF CURIOSITY

If the question your subject line forms starts with "Wait..." or "Huh?" or "Hang on..." -- you have achieved the double whammy: SURPRISE plus curiosity. This is more powerful than standard curiosity alone.

"How to stop panic attacks with a tennis ball" = surprise ("Wait, a tennis ball?!") + curiosity ("How?!")

---

## SELF-INTEREST VS. CURIOSITY

Self-interest subject lines (promising a benefit) generally pull better than pure curiosity subject lines. Include the core interest word (e.g., "copywriting," "fitness," "revenue") in the majority of your subject lines.

**Pure curiosity:** "You will not believe what happened yesterday" -- could be anything
**Self-interest + curiosity:** "The weird copywriting trick that doubled my revenue" -- clear topic + curiosity gap

---

## SPECIFICITY AND CREDIBILITY PUMPS

Two ways to fix a flat subject line:

### Specificity Pump
Before: "How to stand out as a freelancer"
After: "How to get 36% more leads in December"

### Credibility Pump
Before: "How to make your business stand out"
After: "The Steve Jobs secret to making your business stand out"

Stack both for maximum impact: "How the world's biggest fitness brand increased leads 36% with one email change"

---

## YOUR SUBJECT LINE PROBABLY IS NOT "OUT THERE" ENOUGH

Find the craziest angle you can legitimately exploit:
- Instead of "How to have customers begging you to send emails" try "Dating advice for business owners"
- Instead of "Why we don't see the world as it is" try "You're too biased"

Look for the most interesting, weird, or unusual angle that is legitimately in the body copy.

---

## LONG SUBJECT LINES CAN WORK

Long subject lines give more of a "writing to a friend" vibe. When truncated on mobile, the ellipsis (...) adds inherent curiosity. Front-load with interesting words for best of both worlds. Do not be afraid to write more than 60 characters occasionally.

### Short vs. Long Decision
- Under 50 characters: Best for ecom campaigns, mobile-first audiences, direct offers
- Over 60 characters: Best for personality-driven lists, story hooks, creator broadcasts
- Test both approaches -- no universal rule

---

## PRE-TEST SUBJECT LINES BY "GRAFTING"

Take your subject line and imagine it got sent to you by someone on your "rarely read" or "never read" list. Would it make you open their email? If not, try another. This removes the bias of familiarity with the sender.

---

## PREVIEW TEXT STRATEGY

Preview text appears alongside the subject line in the inbox. It is the second-most visible element.

### Best Practices
- Use preview text to COMPLETE the curiosity gap started by the subject line
- If subject line is vague, preview text can add specificity (or vice versa)
- Test: discount in subject line vs. discount in preview text
- Test: swap subject line and preview text content
- When first lines of email are an opt-out, set strategic preview text to override

### Ecom Example
- Subject: "First Day of Fall"
- Preview: "20% off your first purchase of the month"

### Creator Example
- Subject: "Come to my room?"
- Preview: "It is not what you think. Or maybe it is."

## SENDER NAME TESTING

### Sender Name Matters More Than You Think
For regular readers who know and trust you, the sender name becomes more important than the subject line. New subscribers care most about subject lines; loyal readers care most about who sent it.

### What to Test
- Brand name vs. founder name
- "Stacy at [Brand]" (honeypotting -- personal name from the team)
- Support email address vs. marketing email
- Female sender names can get higher CTR for intimidating/scary product categories

---

## A/B TESTING METHODOLOGY

### Campaign Testing (Klaviyo)
- Create A/B split test for subject line OR preview text OR sender name
- Winning metric: open rate (for subject/preview/sender)
- Test size: 10-20% of list
- Test duration: 2-4 hours
- Account for test period when scheduling (test = 2 hours, desired send = 10 AM, schedule at 8 AM)

### Throttle vs. 50/50
- **Throttle:** Send to 20%, wait 4 hours, platform picks winner, sends to remaining 80%
- **50/50:** Half to A, half to B. Clean data, no auto-selection
- Use throttle for campaigns. Use 50/50 for learning

### What to Test First
1. Subject line (highest impact)
2. Preview text
3. Content/body (lower impact but worth testing for multi-SKU brands)
4. From name (use sparingly)

### Key Testing Insight
One early test to run with a new brand: discount visibility.
- Version A: "Save big for Labor Day Weekend" (curiosity)
- Version B: "30% off for Labor Day Weekend" (specificity)
- This reveals whether the audience prefers mystery or transparency -- informing all future subject line strategy.

## NAMES IN SUBJECT LINES

- First name boosts opens
- Full name boosts opens more
- Do not overdo it -- you do not want to sound like a bank
- Use personalization when you have the data; skip it if the merge field might be blank

---

## UNSATISFIED CURIOSITY WARNING

If you tease something and do not pay it off quickly (or at all), readers will be resentful. It is risky to title an email "12 words that close any sale" and then say someone has to buy to find out. Always clearly and quickly pay off anything you tease.

## SUBJECT LINE PATTERN CATEGORIES

See `ref-subject-line-formulas.md` for extensive examples in each category:

1. **Vague + emotional:** "Too soon?" / "oh, that's a story"
2. **Direct + brackets:** "[BONUS ends tomorrow am!]" / "[WATCH THIS]"
3. **Personal/intimate:** "Come to my room?" / "Let's not fake it"
4. **Numbered + curiosity:** "You're one of these 5 kinds of people"
5. **Taboo/tension:** "Brag" works because bragging is taboo
6. **Combination:** "Brag - and 45 mins left to get the answers to life"
7. **Misleading-but-fun:** "have a few left - want this?" (minutes, not items)
