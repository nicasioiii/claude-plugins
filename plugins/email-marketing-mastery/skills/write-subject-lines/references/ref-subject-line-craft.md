# Subject Line Craft -- Principles, Techniques & Psychology

## THE QUESTION-FORMING PRINCIPLE (DEEP DIVE)

### The Mechanism
Every subject line works by creating an information gap. The reader's brain detects incomplete information and generates a question. The intensity of the desire to answer that question determines whether they open.

### Evaluating Question Strength
Rate any subject line on two dimensions:
1. **Clarity of question:** Does it form a clear, specific question? (Vague = weak)
2. **Desire to know the answer:** Would the reader actually want to know? (Irrelevant = weak)

### Strengthening Weak Subject Lines
| Weak | Problem | Stronger Version |
|------|---------|-----------------|
| "Monthly newsletter" | No question formed | "The one thing we changed this month (results inside)" |
| "New product announcement" | Predictable, no curiosity | "We almost did not release this one" |
| "Happy holidays from us" | No self-interest | "Your holiday gift from [Brand] (expires midnight)" |

## THE DOUBLE WHAMMY -- ADVANCED APPLICATION

### Trigger Words That Create Surprise + Curiosity
- Unexpected combinations: profession + unrelated object ("panic attacks + tennis ball")
- Contradictions: "The lazy way to [ambitious result]"
- Scale violations: small cause + big effect ("The $20 seminar that saved my business")
- Category violations: applying one domain to another ("Dating advice for business owners")

### Crafting Double-Whammy Subject Lines
1. Start with your topic/benefit
2. Find an unexpected connection, object, or framing
3. Combine them so the reader thinks "Wait, what?" AND "How?"
4. Test: would this make someone on your "rarely read" list open?

## SELF-INTEREST MECHANICS

### The Core Interest Word
Include the word that describes what your reader cares about most in the majority of subject lines. For a copywriting list: "copywriting." For a fitness list: "fat" or "muscle." For a business list: "revenue" or "clients."

### Self-Interest Subject Line Templates
- "How to [achieve specific result] in [timeframe]"
- "The [number] [things] that [transformed/fixed/changed] my [relevant area]"
- "[Number]% more [desired outcome] using [unexpected method]"
- "Why [common approach] is killing your [desired result]"

### When Pure Curiosity Beats Self-Interest
- Subject lines for daily personality-driven lists (readers already trust the sender)
- Cart-close emails where urgency does the heavy lifting
- Mid-launch emails where context from previous emails fills the gap

## SPECIFICITY + CREDIBILITY STACKING

### Specificity Techniques
- Replace vague numbers with exact: "a lot more" -> "36% more"
- Replace vague timeframes with specific: "soon" -> "in December" / "by Friday"
- Replace vague results with measurable: "better" -> "$4,200 in the first month"
- Replace categories with instances: "exercise" -> "three push-ups"

### Credibility Techniques
- Name-drop a recognizable authority: "The Steve Jobs secret to..."
- Reference a credible source: "According to Harvard research..."
- Cite a specific result: "How [Name] went from [Before] to [After]"
- Reference a known entity: "What [Brand/Publication] taught me about..."

### The Stack
Specificity + Credibility together: "How Mayo Clinic doctors reverse 73% of chronic fatigue cases with one supplement"

## THE GRAFTING PRE-TEST

### Method
1. Write your subject line
2. Imagine you are the reader
3. Imagine the sender is someone on your "rarely read" or "never read" list
4. Ask: would this make me open THEIR email?
5. If no, the subject line is not strong enough on its own merit
6. Rewrite until the answer is yes

### Why This Works
Removes the bias of sender reputation. Forces the subject line to work purely on curiosity and self-interest merit.

## PREVIEW TEXT OPTIMIZATION (DEEP DIVE)

### The Inbox Display Equation
What the reader sees: **Sender Name | Subject Line | Preview Text** (all at once, in a fraction of a second)

### Five Preview Text Strategies

**1. Complete the Thought**
- Subject: "I almost did not send this"
- Preview: "But you need to see it before midnight"

**2. Add Specificity to Vague Subject**
- Subject: "Quick update"
- Preview: "Your 30% discount code expires in 6 hours"

**3. Add Curiosity to Direct Subject**
- Subject: "New arrivals just dropped"
- Preview: "Including something we have never made before"

**4. Contrast/Surprise**
- Subject: "We are sorry"
- Preview: "...that we did not tell you about this sooner"

**5. Social Proof**
- Subject: "Still thinking about it?"
- Preview: "2,847 customers already made their decision"

### Technical Notes
- Keep preview text under 90 characters (truncation varies by client)
- Set explicitly in ESP -- otherwise inbox shows first line of email body
- When first lines are an opt-out notice, preview text is critical to override

## OPEN RATE CONTEXT (USE CORRECTLY)

### Open Rates Are Only Useful for Relative Comparison
- Compare A vs. B on the same list at the same time -- valid
- Compare this week vs. last week for absolute benchmarks -- unreliable
- iOS 15+ inflates open rates with pre-fetching -- actual human opens are lower than reported

### Diagnostic Framework
- Low opens = problem with sender name, subject line, or preview text
- Opens normal but clicks low = problem with email body, CTA, or messaging
- High clicks but low revenue = unqualified clicks, insufficient pre-selling
- Low clicks but high revenue per email = well-qualified clicks (email did good pre-selling)

## SENDER NAME TESTING (DEEP DIVE)

### The Honeypotting Technique
"Stacy at [Brand]" -- use a real team member's name. Creates personal connection while maintaining brand context. Best for larger brands where the founder is not the face.

### Testing Protocol
1. Test brand name vs. founder first name
2. Winner vs. "[Name] at [Brand]" format
3. Winner vs. different team member names
4. Female names often outperform for intimidating product categories

### When Sender Name Matters Most
- Long-term subscribers (50+ emails received) -- sender name dominates
- New subscribers (under 10 emails) -- subject line dominates
- Reactivation campaigns -- sender name and subject line both critical

## EMOTIONAL SPIKE TECHNIQUE

Subject lines that mimic real-life anxiety trigger physiological responses:
- "[Name], I want to see you in my office tomorrow" -- triggers heart rate increase
- "We need to talk" -- mimics relationship anxiety
- "I saw what you did" -- creates accountability spike

Use sparingly. Powerful but can damage trust if overused or the payoff is weak.

## CURIOSITY MECHANICS IN SUBJECT LINES

### Creating Curiosity = Withholding Information
Tell them WHAT they will get (benefits) but not HOW. The job of every subject line is to generate enough curiosity to earn the open.

### The Cliffhanger Effect
Each email's subject line should create anticipation for the content AND (in sequences) for the next email. Same principle as TikTok "Part 2" -- the need to see the rest.

### Minimal Curiosity Emails
Subject: "Message for you." Body: Product shot + "It's here." + CTA. Everyone asks "What's here?" Sometimes less is more.
