# Advertorial Templates -- Native, Meta & Section Guidance

## NATIVE ADVERTORIAL TEMPLATE (Editorial/News Format)

### Section 1: Editorial Headline + Hook
The headline must look like a news article or feature story, not an ad.

**Headline patterns for native:**
- "[Specific Person/Group] Discovers [Surprising Method] for [Outcome]"
- "Why [Common Solution] Doesn't Work -- And What [Expert/Researchers] Found Instead"
- "The [Adjective] [Noun] That's Changing How [People] [Solve Problem]"
- "[Number] [People] Have Already Tried This [New Approach] -- Here's What They Found"
- "Scientists Explain Why [Common Belief] Is Wrong About [Topic]"

**Hook (first 2-3 sentences):**
- Start with a surprising fact, statistic, or contrarian claim
- Create an information gap -- the reader needs to keep reading to close it
- Do NOT mention the product in the first paragraph

**Example:**
"For decades, doctors told patients with swollen legs to elevate and rest. But a 2024 study from [University] found that this advice may actually be making the problem worse. The real culprit? A little-known muscle group that most people have never heard of."

### Section 2: Emotional Story
Introduce a relatable character experiencing the problem:
- Use a specific person (real or composite) -- name, age, situation
- Describe their daily frustration in sensory detail
- Show what they've tried and how it failed
- Create empathy: the reader should see themselves in this person

**Template:**
"[Name], a [age]-year-old [occupation] from [location], had been dealing with [problem] for [time period]. She'd tried [solution 1], [solution 2], even [solution 3]. Nothing worked. 'I was about to give up,' she says. 'I thought this was just something I had to live with.'"

### Section 3: Problem Education (UMP Introduction)
This is where the advertorial earns its keep. Educate the reader on WHY the problem persists:
- Introduce the mechanism of the problem (UMP) in accessible language
- Explain why common solutions only address symptoms, not the root cause
- Use analogies to make complex concepts simple
- Reference studies or expert opinions for credibility

**Template:**
"According to [expert/study], the reason most [solutions] fail is that they only address [surface symptom]. The real issue goes deeper. [Explain mechanism in 2-3 sentences using an analogy]. When [this process breaks down], [the visible problem] is just the tip of the iceberg."

### Section 4: Solution Tease
Don't fully reveal the product yet. Tease the discovery:
- "Then [character] came across something unexpected..."
- "A friend mentioned a [new approach/discovery] that was getting attention..."
- "While researching, she stumbled onto [a different method]..."

**Rule:** The solution tease should create anticipation, not satisfy curiosity. The reader should want to learn MORE -- which is what the click-through delivers.

### Section 5: Product Reveal + Key Benefits
Now introduce the product, but through the lens of the story character:
- "[Character] decided to try [product name]"
- Describe 3-5 key benefits through the character's experience
- Keep it factual and specific (numbers, timeframes, measurable results)
- Include a brief "how it works" tied to the mechanism

### Section 6: Social Proof
Add credibility through additional voices:
- 1-2 short testimonials from other users
- Star ratings or review counts
- "Over [number] people have tried [product]"
- Expert endorsements or media mentions if available

### Section 7: Click-Through CTA
The CTA must sell the click, not the product:
- "Learn more about how [product] works"
- "See the full research behind [mechanism]"
- "Find out if [product] is right for you"
- Include a prominent button or linked text
- Optional: add a scarcity element ("limited availability" / "special offer ending soon")

## META ADVERTORIAL TEMPLATE (Story-Driven Social Format)

### Section 1: Personal Hook
More casual and personal than native. First-person voice works best.

**Hook patterns for Meta:**
- "I never thought I'd be writing this, but [surprising outcome]..."
- "My [family member/friend] told me about this and I was skeptical..."
- "After [years/months] of dealing with [problem], I finally found something that works."
- "This is going to sound crazy, but [contrarian claim]..."
- "I almost didn't try [product/method] because [common objection]. I'm so glad I did."

### Section 2: Personal Story (Struggle)
- First-person narrative of dealing with the problem
- Specific details that create relatability (what daily life looks like with this problem)
- Failed solutions mentioned briefly
- Emotional low point: "I was ready to give up..."

### Section 3: Discovery Moment
- How you found out about the solution (friend, article, ad, doctor)
- Initial skepticism: "I was hesitant because..."
- What made you try it anyway

### Section 4: Experience + Results
- What happened after trying it
- Specific results (timeframes, numbers, measurable changes)
- How life changed
- Brief mention of how/why it works (mechanism in 1-2 sentences)

### Section 5: Recommendation + Social Proof
- "I've since recommended this to [friends/family]"
- 1-2 sentences about others' experiences
- Star rating or review count mention

### Section 6: CTA
- "If you're dealing with [problem], you should check this out"
- Link to product page or VSL
- Scarcity element if applicable

## CURIOSITY HOOK EXAMPLES (Both Platforms)

### Health/Supplement Hooks
- "This [number]-second [time of day] ritual is changing how people deal with [problem]"
- "Doctors are surprised by what [new research] says about [common remedy]"
- "Why [common advice] might be making your [problem] worse"

### Business/Info Product Hooks
- "[Person] went from [before state] to [after state] using a method most experts call 'backward'"
- "The [industry] hack that [number] of entrepreneurs are using to [outcome] -- without [common requirement]"
- "Why your [business metric] isn't improving (hint: it's not what your [coach/consultant] told you)"

### Personal Finance/BizOpp Hooks
- "How [regular person] turned [small amount] into [large amount] using [unusual method]"
- "The [surprising thing] that's making ordinary people [financial outcome]"
- "Why [traditional advice] is keeping you from [financial goal]"

## EMOTIONAL OPENING STORY TEMPLATES

### Template A: The Everyday Struggle
"[Name] woke up at [time] like every other day. But instead of [normal activity], she found herself [struggling with problem symptom]. It had been [time period] since the last time she felt [desired state]. 'I just want to be able to [simple activity] without [consequence],' she told her [family member]."

### Template B: The Breaking Point
"It was [specific moment] when [Name] realized something had to change. She'd just [embarrassing/painful specific incident]. Her [family member/friend] looked at her and said, '[concerned statement].' That was the moment she started searching for a real answer."

### Template C: The Skeptic's Journey
"When [Name]'s [friend/colleague] told her about [method/product], she rolled her eyes. 'I've heard it all before,' she thought. She'd already tried [solution 1], [solution 2], and [solution 3]. Why would this be any different? But [specific detail] caught her attention..."

## PROBLEM EDUCATION SECTION PATTERNS

### Pattern 1: The Hidden Cause
"What most people don't realize is that [visible problem] is actually a symptom of [deeper issue]. When [mechanism explained simply], the body/business/system can't [function] properly. This is why [common solution] only provides temporary relief."

### Pattern 2: The Myth Buster
"For years, the conventional wisdom has been [common belief]. But recent [research/data/experience] suggests this approach is incomplete at best -- and counterproductive at worst. The missing piece? [Mechanism in one sentence]."

### Pattern 3: The Expert Revelation
"According to [expert/institution], the reason [percentage] of people fail to solve [problem] is that they're focused on [wrong thing]. [Expert name] explains: '[brief quote or paraphrase about the real cause].'"

## CTA PATTERNS FOR CLICK-THROUGH

### Curiosity CTA (Best for awareness-building)
- "Learn how [mechanism] works and whether [product] could help you"
- "See the full science behind this [number]-second [ritual/method]"
- "Watch the free presentation that explains everything"

### Social Proof CTA (Best for credibility-driven)
- "Join [number] people who have already tried [product]"
- "See why [product] has [number] 5-star reviews"
- "Find out what [number] customers are saying"

### Urgency CTA (Best for action-driving)
- "Check availability -- [product] frequently sells out"
- "See if you qualify for the special introductory offer"
- "Claim your [discount/offer] before [deadline]"

### Information Gap CTA (Best for mechanism-heavy)
- "Discover the [mechanism nickname] that's changing everything"
- "Get the full story behind [surprising claim from earlier]"
- "See exactly how [product] addresses [the mechanism you just explained]"

## PLATFORM CONGRUENCY CHECKLIST

Before publishing, verify your advertorial maintains congruence:

- [ ] **Visual style** matches the platform (editorial for native, social for Meta)
- [ ] **Tone** matches the ad that preceded it (if ad was casual, advertorial is casual)
- [ ] **First impression** doesn't feel like a sales pitch (reader should feel they're reading content)
- [ ] **Product isn't mentioned** in the first paragraph (education/story comes first)
- [ ] **Images** feel appropriate to the platform (editorial photos for native, lifestyle for Meta)
- [ ] **CTA** sells the click, not the product (purpose is to drive them to the sales page)
- [ ] **Reading experience** is smooth (no jarring transitions between story and mechanism)
- [ ] **Mobile formatting** is clean (short paragraphs, subheadings, scannable structure)
- [ ] **Length** matches audience awareness level (longer for unaware, shorter for aware)
- [ ] **Mechanism education** is present but accessible (no jargon without explanation)
