# Backend Email Patterns

## PATTERN 1: "HOW DID YOU MAKE ME BUY?" (IVA SEQUENCE)

Best for: First backend offer, under $100 product, customers who just entered your world.

### Email 1 -- The Acknowledgment
- Acknowledge HOW they found you (e.g., from an ad, from a friend, from a podcast)
- State the question everyone asks when they first encounter your brand
- Present the product as the answer to that question
- Urgency: "Price will go up to $97 soon!"
- Include a direct link to buy

### Email 2 -- The Reframe
- Lead with the customer's stated need ("I need more customers" / "I need to lose weight")
- State the low price clearly in the first few lines
- Reframe the problem (e.g., "Ads fail because the creative doesn't make people want to buy, not because ads don't work")
- Connect this product to what they already bought -- it is the natural next step
- Second link to buy

### Email 3 -- The Actionable Advice
- Give actionable advice FIRST (make 9 posts using these frameworks / try this workout split)
- THEN position the product as the amplifier of that free advice
- Use checkmarks for benefits (scannable, visual)
- Frame as "how you scale without [common painful method]"
- Third link to buy

### Email 4 -- The Urgency Close
- Social proof opening (reference someone's success, a retargeting stat, or a customer win)
- Direct problem-solution pitch (shortest email in the sequence)
- Heavy urgency: "Price will go up" or "Flash sale ends TONIGHT"
- Final link to buy

## PATTERN 2: "BINGE-BUY METHOD" (DCC SEQUENCE)

Best for: Second backend offer, $100-$300 product, customers who have already bought once from email.

### Email 1 -- Name the Method
- Name the buying psychology: "The Binge-Buy Method"
- Help them see they are ALREADY experiencing it: "You bought X, you binged it, now you're thinking: what else does she have?"
- Make them feel understood and validated
- Offer with 48-hour coupon (e.g., 80% off with code)
- State the original price AND the discounted price

### Email 2 -- The Concept Hook
- Introduce a concept with a catchy name ("Candy bar millions" / "The snowball effect" / "Micro-course stacking")
- Define the concept: "a candy bar = small offer that gives a quick win, then they want everything else"
- Reinforce with revenue proof ("to the tune of millions in revenue" / "this is how I built a 7-figure business")
- 24-hour deadline
- Link to buy

### Email 3 -- The Benefit Stack
- Stack the benefits of the product (additional revenue stream, runs on autopilot, qualifies customers for high-ticket)
- State the discount contrast: "Normally $997, grab today for $147"
- Include one testimonial with specific results
- "Sale ends TONIGHT at 11:59PM EST!"

### Email 4 -- The Final Shot
- Shortest email in the sequence
- "The 80% off flash sale is almost over!"
- "This is your final shot."
- Clock is ticking. Link to buy.
- Nothing else. Pure urgency.

## PATTERN 3: "SCALE GAME" (ATSDP SEQUENCE)

Best for: Higher-ticket backend ($200-$500+), customers who have bought multiple times and trust you.

### Email 1 -- The Bold Question
- Open with a bold, aspirational question: "Do you want 1,000 new customers next month?"
- Hard truth: "The only way to do this without chaining yourself to your computer is [method]"
- Address resistance head-on: "I know what you're thinking -- it's too expensive / too risky / too complicated"
- Specific price + time-limited offer: "$247 for the next 72 hours"

### Email 2 -- The Customer Story
- Lead with a detailed customer success story with specific numbers
- Structure: "She spent $50,000 on other courses and agencies. Then she found this $247 course. Made her first sale the same day."
- Include the emotional arc (frustration -> discovery -> transformation)
- Mission statement: why you created this and what you believe

### Email 3 -- The Contrarian Flip
- Flip conventional wisdom: "Everyone says start with organic... ads are so risky. It's actually the opposite."
- Reframe the risk: "Time is the most expensive currency. Organic takes months. Paid gets results in days."
- Control vs. chaos framing: "Paid = you control the inputs. Organic = you hope the algorithm likes you today."
- Link to buy

### Email 4 -- Attack Bad Advice
- "If I hear this advice one more time, I'm going to lose it"
- Debunk common myths in the space: "You don't need thousands in ad testing" / "You don't need a huge audience first"
- Specific proof: "Student Nikki: spent $40, made a sale the same day."
- "Flash sale ends TONIGHT."
- Final link

## HIGH-TICKET EMAIL SELLING (KATIE)

### The $42,000 Day Framework
Three emails sent to existing customer list promoting a high-ticket accelerator ($5,000+).

**Email 1 -- The Announcement**
- Announce that a small number of spots are open
- Describe the transformation (coaching, mentorship, direct access)
- Include a discount code for early action
- Mention limited spots: "Only 5 spots available"

**Email 2 -- The Deep Dive**
- Explain exactly what they get (weekly calls, Voxer access, custom strategy)
- Position as coaching/transformation, NOT more information
- Include one powerful testimonial from a past participant
- Remind of discount code and remaining spots

**Email 3 -- The Close**
- "Last chance" messaging
- Restate the transformation, not the features
- Remind of exact number of spots remaining
- Discount code expires at midnight

### What Moves High-Ticket via Email
1. **Discount codes** -- proven effective
2. **Limited spots** -- must be genuine
3. **Bonuses** -- tested, did NOT significantly improve results for Katie

### What Does NOT Work for High-Ticket Email
- Selling it like another course (they already buy courses from you)
- Offering it as the first backend product (must warm up with lower-ticket first)
- Generic "learn more" CTAs (be specific about what they get and the transformation)

## ABANDON CART SEQUENCE FRAMEWORK (GEORGI)

### Sequence Structure
Send 3-5 emails over 48-72 hours to people who started checkout but did not complete.

### Email 1 -- The Reminder (Send 1 hour after abandonment)
- Simple, friendly reminder: "You left something in your cart"
- Show what they were about to buy (product image, name, price)
- Direct link back to checkout
- No hard sell -- just a nudge

### Email 2 -- Address Doubt (Send 24 hours after abandonment)
- Address the most common objection for your product
- Include a testimonial that specifically addresses that objection
- "Still thinking about it? Here's what [Customer Name] said after trying it..."
- Link back to checkout

### Email 3 -- Value Reframe (Send 36-48 hours after abandonment)
- Reframe the value: cost of inaction vs. cost of product
- "For less than [relatable daily expense], you could [transformation]"
- Stack the benefits one more time
- Optional: offer a small incentive (free shipping, small discount, bonus)

### Email 4 -- Urgency (Send 60-72 hours after abandonment)
- Cart will expire / price will increase / offer ending
- Final social proof
- "This is your last reminder"

### Economics Impact
Converting just 15% of cart abandoners changes everything:
- Before: 100 visitors, 20 buy at CPA $250 each
- After: 100 visitors, 35 buy at CPA $143 each
- That 15% recovery rate can turn a losing offer into a profitable one

## UNIVERSAL BACKEND EMAIL RULES

### Rule 1: Each Email Stands Alone
Every email must be able to sell independently. Never assume they read previous emails. Restate the product, price, and deadline in each email.

### Rule 2: Social Proof in Every Sequence
At minimum, include one customer success story with specific numbers per 4-email sequence. Numbers create credibility: "$50,000 in revenue" beats "great results."

### Rule 3: Ascending Prices Across Sequences
First backend: under $100. Second: $147-$247. Third: higher. Each sequence acclimates them to spending more. The trajectory is always upward.

### Rule 4: Discount Strategy for Backend
80% off with coupon codes works because:
- Customer is already acquired (zero marginal acquisition cost)
- They know the retail value (price-anchored from seeing the full-price offer)
- The discounted price still generates pure profit margin
- Hormozi's "free or full price" rule applies to acquisition, NOT backend monetization

### Rule 5: Timing Between Sequences
Allow breathing room between backend promotions. Do not send customers from one 4-email sequence immediately into the next. Give 3-7 days of value-based content or delivery emails between promotional sequences.

### Rule 6: Segment by Purchase History
Customers who bought upsell 2 at $197 are different from customers who only bought the front-end at $44. Segment your backend sequences:
- Buyers who took zero upsells: Start with the most affordable backend offer
- Buyers who took 1-2 upsells: They are already comfortable spending -- offer mid-ticket sooner
- Buyers who took all upsells: These are your best customers -- offer high-ticket earlier

### Rule 7: Re-Use Declined Upsell Products
If a customer declined your $197 upsell during checkout, that product becomes your first backend email offer -- but at a discount. The context has changed: they have now consumed some of your content and have more trust. A product they said "no" to immediately post-purchase may get a "yes" after 7 days of value delivery.

## BACKEND SEQUENCE ARCHITECTURE BY BUSINESS MODEL

### Digital Info Products (Katie's Model)
- **Sequence 1 (Days 7-10):** Sub-$100 product. Re-use a declined upsell at discount. 4 emails.
- **Sequence 2 (Days 17-20):** $100-$300 product. New offer or premium bundle. 4 emails.
- **Sequence 3 (Days 27-30):** High-ticket offer ($2,000+). Coaching or accelerator. 3 emails + application.
- **Between sequences:** 3-5 days of content delivery, tips, or community engagement emails.

### E-Commerce / DTC (Georgi's Model)
- **Sequence 1 (Days 3-7):** Reorder reminder or complementary product at bundle discount. 3 emails.
- **Sequence 2 (Days 14-17):** Cross-sell to adjacent product line. 4 emails.
- **Sequence 3 (Days 28-35):** Subscription conversion offer. 3 emails.
- **Between sequences:** Usage tips, educational content about ingredients/benefits, customer spotlights.

### Service Businesses (Hormozi's Model)
- **Sequence 1 (Days 2-5):** Add-on service or supplement sale. Menu Upsell approach via email.
- **Sequence 2 (Days 14-21):** Continuity conversion -- downsell the upfront service into a monthly membership.
- **Sequence 3 (Days 21-28):** Prepayment offer on the membership for annual commitment.
- **Between sequences:** Service delivery updates, milestone celebrations, results tracking.

### SaaS / Software
- **Sequence 1 (Days 7-10):** Annual plan upgrade from monthly. Show savings math.
- **Sequence 2 (Days 21-28):** Premium tier upgrade. Show features they are missing.
- **Sequence 3 (Days 45-60):** Enterprise or team plan. Show collaboration benefits.
- **Between sequences:** Usage tips, feature announcements, case studies of successful users.

## MEASURING BACKEND SEQUENCE PERFORMANCE

### Key Metrics to Track Per Sequence
- **Open rate:** Target 25-40% (lower is normal for promotional sequences vs. welcome emails)
- **Click rate:** Target 3-8% per email
- **Conversion rate:** Compare to benchmarks by price tier (6%+ under $100, 3.5%+ under $300)
- **Revenue per customer entered:** Total backend sequence revenue / total customers who entered the sequence
- **Unsubscribe rate:** If above 1% per email, the offer or frequency is too aggressive

### When to Fix a Backend Sequence
- If conversion rate is below benchmark: Apply Position/Price/Switch from funnel-cro
- If open rate drops below 20%: Subject lines need work (defer to email-marketing-mastery)
- If click rate is healthy but conversion is low: The landing page or offer page is the problem
- If unsubscribe rate spikes: Reduce frequency or increase value content between promotions
