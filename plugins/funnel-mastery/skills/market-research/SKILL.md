## Eugene Schwartz 5 Awareness Levels (Georgi)

The foundation for all funnel copy decisions. Where your market sits determines copy length, funnel complexity, and messaging approach.

| Level | Definition | Typical % of TAM | Copy Implication |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Unaware** | Don't recognize they have a problem | 30-40% | Most education needed; longest content; typically NOT targeted in DR |
| **Problem Aware** | Recognize the problem, don't know solutions exist | 30-40% | Sweet spot for cold traffic; acknowledge pain, introduce solution category |
| **Solution Aware** | Know solutions exist, haven't chosen approach | 15-20% | Differentiation content; why YOUR solution category is superior |
| **Product Aware** | Know your product category, not your brand | 5-10% | Minimal education; features, pricing, comparisons |
| **Most Aware** | Know your brand, researched you, ready to decide | 1-5% | Least education; direct offers, pricing, retargeting |

**Critical DTC modification (Georgi):** "Product Aware" means awareness of the product CATEGORY (e.g., peptide eye creams), not necessarily your specific brand. Most DTC brands, even at $20-30M revenue, have less than 1-2% most-aware penetration.

## The Scaling Challenge (Georgi)

Businesses plateau at $1-3M because they exhaust product-aware and most-aware segments (the top 10-15% of the market).

To scale to $10M, $30M, $50M+, you must effectively market to the much larger problem-aware and unaware segments (70-80% of most markets). This requires:
- Different messaging (education-heavy, not feature-heavy)
- More educational content (advertorials, longer VSLs)
- Often higher customer acquisition costs
- Different funnel architectures (more steps, more warming)

## Awareness-to-Copy-Length Relationship

| Audience Awareness | Funnel Complexity | Copy Length | Example Path |
|---|---|---|---|
| Most Aware | Minimal | Short | Ad -> Product Page -> Checkout |
| Product Aware | Low | Medium | Ad -> Landing Page -> Checkout |
| Solution Aware | Medium | Medium-Long | Ad -> Advertorial/Quiz -> Landing Page -> Checkout |
| Problem Aware | High | Long | Ad -> Advertorial -> VSL (20-45 min) -> Checkout |
| Unaware | Very High | Very Long | Multi-touch content -> Education -> VSL -> Checkout |

## When to Do Deep Research vs. Quick Research

**Deep research required when:**
- Entering a new market you don't know well
- Market is problem-aware or unaware (need education angles)
- Competition is saturated (need differentiation)
- Writing a long-form VSL or advertorial
- Building a unique mechanism (UMP/UMS)

**Quick research sufficient when:**
- You already operate in the market
- Market is product-aware or most-aware
- Writing short-form ads or retargeting
- Running split tests on existing copy

## Instructor Alignment on Research

**Where they agree:** All sources insist on deep market understanding before writing. Georgi structures it as the formal "R" in RMBC. Gusten has 9 pre-funnel questions. Both say research before copy, always.

**Where they diverge:** Georgi's research is extremely structured and AI-prompt-driven, designed for DTC/supplement markets. Gusten's research is more intuitive and focused on personal brand/info product markets. Both approaches are valid -- route by business model.

**Resolution:** Use Georgi's structured approach (awareness assessment + competitor + psychographic + unified doc) for any market where you need deep understanding. Use Gusten's 9 pre-funnel questions as a quick-start framework when building a funnel for a market you already know.
