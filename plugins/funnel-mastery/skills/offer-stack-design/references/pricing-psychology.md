# Pricing Psychology

## The Penny Gap (Dan Ariely's Hershey Kiss Test)

Free vs. 1 cent: **9x more people** took the free version.

The gap between free and any price -- even one cent -- is the biggest psychological barrier in commerce. This is why "free" is so powerful as an acquisition tool. It's not about the money. It's about the decision threshold.

---

## The Chick-fil-A Pricing Model (Hormozi)

Either it's **free** or it's **full price**. Never discounted.

- Mess up? Whole meal on us (free)
- Otherwise? Premium, full price
- **No in-between**

This protects brand value while still using "free" strategically for recovery and acquisition.

---

## Discount Rules (Hormozi)

**Moderate discounts (10-20% off) are stupid.** They don't change behavior. The same number of people respond at 10% off as at 0% off. You just lose margin.

**Minimum effective discount:** 50%+ off something people already know the value of. The only purpose of free/discount is to change behavior -- get people who wouldn't have acted to act.

**Critical rule:** Market must already understand the retail value. A $29 chiropractic adjustment works because people know it costs more. For intangible or unique products, discount framing is less effective -- use value stacking instead.

---

## Four Ways to Display Discounts (Rotate Monthly for Ad Freshness)

1. **Percentage off:** "87% off first visit"
2. **Absolute amount off:** "$87 off" or "$181 off first week"
3. **Relative equivalent:** "Less than going out to lunch"
4. **Discounted price next to retail:** "$800" with "$1,200" crossed out
5. **Portion off (subscription):** "4 months free" on a 12-month plan

**Pro tip:** Rotate these monthly. Internally nothing changes, but the market perceives fresh offers every month. This fights ad fatigue without changing the underlying economics.

## Anchor Pricing Technique (Hormozi)

**Brute force pricing psychology.**

Present premium item FIRST at 5-10x the price. Customer gasps. Ask if they care about what makes it premium (secondary features). Present main offer -- customer feels RELIEVED and sees it as a great deal.

**Steps:**
1. Present the anchor (10x price)
2. Get the gasp (if no gasp, you didn't anchor high enough)
3. Come to the rescue -- ask about secondary features
4. Present main offer -- customer feels relieved
5. Ask how they want to pay

**Suit story:** Budgeted $500. First suit shown: $16,000. Blood drains from face. Second suit: $2,200. Felt like a steal. Bought suit + $500 in accessories. Spent 5-6x original budget.

**Rolls Royce insight:** Stopped showing at car shows ($400K next to $50K looks egregious). Started showing at plane and yacht shows. After considering a $50M jet, $400K = candy.

**Math:** If 1 in 10 buys the 10x option, you DOUBLE your business. Plus increased average spend from everyone else due to anchoring effect.

## "Smart People Buy More" Push (Georgi)

In VSL closes, a brief push toward the bigger package. Not paragraphs -- just a few sentences.

"The 6-month supply is the best bet for anyone who's serious... $49/bottle instead of $69... click now to get started."

Keep it quick. AOV-obsessive copy (long paragraphs pushing 6-bottle) can actually hurt conversion rate. Get it converting first, then optimize AOV.

## Monthly Price vs. Total Commitment Psychology (Hormozi)

People think about MONTHLY cash flow more than total term value.

- A 3-year contract at lower monthly feels better than higher monthly with no commitment, even if total cost is higher
- $200/month for 12 months ($2,400 total) feels cheaper than $2,000 one-time
- Payment plans help emotional buyers: analytical buyers take one-time (see it as a deal), emotional buyers take monthly (lower perceived risk)

**The power of commitments and contracts:** Significantly increases LTV if you deliver value. The commitment creates switching costs.

## Master Naming Technique -- "Minimum" (Hormozi)

Calling something the "minimum" package instead of "economy" or "basic":
- "Minimum" implies you MUST get it
- ~90% take rate just from naming

**Other naming options for startup/onboarding fees:** Initiation, onboarding, enrollment, startup, setup, processing, activation, build, commencement -- just add "fee" to any word.

---

## The Implied Sale (Hormozi)

Language patterns that assume the purchase:

- "Do you want to UPGRADE?" -- implies they already have the base
- "Just the minimum then?" -- implies they must get at least the minimum
- "You don't want anything ELSE, do you?" -- implies prior items are already theirs
- "Vanilla or chocolate?" -- bypasses the "should I buy?" question entirely

---

## Package Display Strategy (Gusten)

When showing packages side-by-side:
- Subscription vs. one-time with visual differentiation
- Subscription gets MORE checkmarks/features than one-time at lower monthly price
- Example (Primal Queen): Subscription has 6 checkmarks at $44 vs. one-time with 2 checkmarks at $64

This drives subscription purchases by making the value gap visually obvious.

---

## Price Testing Guidance (Gusten)

Sometimes higher prices outperform lower prices (perceived value effect). Test $27 vs. $37 vs. $47 for the same offer. The middle or higher price may win.

This is counterintuitive but consistent: cheap can signal low value. The price itself is a quality signal.

## Instructor Disagreement: Discount Philosophy

**Hormozi:** Moderate discounts (10-20%) are stupid -- go free or full price. Never discount.

**Katie:** Uses 80% off coupons in backend email sequences. $997 product sold for $147 with coupon code.

**Resolution:** Both are right in context. Hormozi's rule applies to acquisition/front-end -- use free or full price to change behavior. Katie's deep discounts work for BACKEND sequences where the customer is already acquired and the marginal cost of the product is near zero. The customer was already paid for; any backend revenue is pure profit, so heavy discounting makes economic sense.

---

## Instructor Disagreement: When to Sell High-Ticket

**Katie:** Don't sell high-ticket immediately. Warm them first with low-ticket. Put a sub-$100 product as the first backend offer before pitching multi-thousand dollar offers.

**Hormozi:** Sell at the point of greatest deprivation, which can be immediately. When someone just identified a new problem, that's peak receptivity.

**Resolution:** Katie's approach fits digital info product funnels where trust must be built over time. Hormozi's applies when the buyer just identified a new problem through the sales process itself (e.g., signed up for gym, now realizes they need nutrition -- sell supplements immediately). Route by whether the NEXT PROBLEM is already felt.
