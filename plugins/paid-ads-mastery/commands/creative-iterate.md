# /creative-iterate -- Creative Iteration Generator

## OVERVIEW

This command takes a winning ad (or ads showing early signs of winning) and produces iteration variations at each stage of the 5-stage framework. Iterations have a higher success rate than new concepts and help scale ~50% higher than without them.

**Skills activated:** `test-creative`, `create-ad-copy` OR `create-video-ads`

---

## INTAKE QUESTIONS

### Question 1: The Winner
- Describe or share the winning ad (video, static, or copy)
- What format is it? (video UGC, mini VSL, static image, carousel)
- What platform? (Meta, Google, TikTok)
- How long has it been performing?
- What are the current metrics? (ROAS, CPA, hook rate, CTR)

### Question 2: Performance Status
- **Still performing well?** (Goal: more scale from the winner)
- **Starting to fatigue?** (Goal: extend life before it dies)
- **Already dead?** (Goal: revive with headline/hook changes)

### Question 3: Iteration History
- Have you already iterated this ad? If so, which stages?
- What variations have you already tested?
- Any specific elements you believe are the "magic" of this ad?

### Question 4: Resources
- Do you have access to the original footage/assets?
- Can you produce new hooks/footage? (or need copy-only iterations)
- Budget for iteration testing?

## THE 5-STAGE ITERATION FRAMEWORK

**Rule: Go gradually. Do not skip to Stage 5. Each stage must be explored before moving to the next.**

### Stage 1: Ad Copy & Headline Testing
**What to change:** The text below the video/image (Meta primary text + headline)
**What stays the same:** The creative itself (video or image)

**Produce these variations:**
1. **Summarize claims differently:** Same benefit, new wording
2. **Add new claims:** Introduce a benefit NOT in the original headline
3. **Address concerns:** Turn an objection into the headline
4. **Add urgency:** Time-limited language
5. **Add scarcity:** Stock/quantity language

**Output:** 5 headline variations + 3 body copy variations

### Stage 2: Hook Changes
**What to change:** The first 3-5 seconds of video (or visual hook for static)
**What stays the same:** The body and CTA of the ad

**Two approaches:**
1. **Change hook TYPE:** Switch from claim to question, or from question to visual pattern interrupt
   - Example: "Here's why pets mark in spots" becomes "Do you know why pets mark in spots?"
2. **Change hook FORMAT:** Different visual execution of the same message
   - Example: Split screen showing problem vs. talking-head asking question

**Output:** 3 hook variations (mix of type changes and format changes)

### Stage 3: On-Ramp Changes (First 10-15 Seconds)
**What to change:** The opening 10-15 seconds after the hook
**What stays the same:** The off-ramp (sales sequence) and CTA

**Approaches:**
- Add more psychological triggers or pain points in the opening
- Switch from b-roll-only on-ramp to talking-ad on-ramp (or vice versa)
- Test different aspect ratios: 9:16 vs 4:5 vs 1:1
- Change the awareness level approach (make it more/less direct)

**Output:** 2-3 on-ramp variations with visual direction notes

### Stage 4: Sales Sequence Modifications
**What to change:** The order, length, and emphasis of the selling section
**What stays the same:** The hook and core message

**Approaches:**
- Trim the video -- cut the fat, make it lean
- Move explanatory material to end of video (only new viewers encounter it)
- Rearrange the order of selling blocks
- Remove scenes that can be cut without losing the core message

**Output:** 1-2 restructured versions of the ad with cut list

### Stage 5: Adapting, Paraphrasing & Rhetorical Reframing
**What to change:** The entire creative approach while preserving what works
**What stays the same:** The core insight/angle that made it win

**Three sub-approaches:**
1. **Adapting:** Take strategic elements from OTHER winning videos and insert them
   - Example: Replace one creator with another, swap locations, add new b-roll
2. **Paraphrasing:** Same claims in different language, different voiceover, lean more on visuals
3. **Rhetorical Reframing:** Address skeptics directly. Use negative comments from the winning ad as fodder.
   - "I was skeptical whether this would work, but somehow it did"
   - Add: social proof, guarantees, product variety, ease-of-use messaging

**Output:** 1-2 reimagined versions with full creative briefs

## STATIC AD ITERATION (IF APPLICABLE)

For static ads, use this iteration priority (highest to lowest impact):
1. **Headline** -- change first, highest impact
2. **Body text / testimonial** -- second priority
3. **Color scheme** -- surprisingly impactful
4. **CTA** -- lowest impact

**Output:** 4-5 static variations, one per element change

---

## CROSS-TESTING ITERATION METHOD

After producing iterations, also produce cross-tests:
- Take winning hook from Ad A, apply to Ad B's body
- Take winning body from Ad A, apply to Ad B's hook
- Mix male + female UGC in same video (broader audience)

This isolates whether the hook or the body was the key variable.

## OUTPUT FORMAT

The iteration plan should include:

### 1. Iteration Map
Visual showing which stages are being addressed and priority order.

### 2. Stage-by-Stage Deliverables
For each applicable stage:
- What specifically is being changed
- The actual creative (copy, script, visual direction)
- Testing setup (one variable at a time, same conditions)
- Kill criteria (1x CPA spend with no sale)

### 3. Testing Timeline
- Stage 1 iterations: Test immediately (copy changes only, no production)
- Stage 2 iterations: Test within 1 week (new hook production)
- Stage 3-5: Test over 2-4 weeks as creative is produced

### 4. When to Stop Iterating
- When new iteration batches consistently underperform below average account ROAS
- This signals diminishing returns
- Reallocate budget to new concepts or iterate a different winner

### 5. Naming Convention
Each iteration should follow: [Original-ID]_[Stage#]_[Variation-Letter]
Example: VID001_S2_A = Video 001, Stage 2 (Hook Change), Variation A
