# Brand Strategy Foundations

## Seth Godin's Strategic Framework

### Two Core Questions (Ask Before Every Campaign)
1. **Who is it for?** Who exactly are we seeking to serve? Who would miss us if we were gone?
2. **What is it for?** Why are we showing up? To serve them or to profit from them?

### Strategy as a Philosophy of Becoming
- Help people get to where they ALREADY want to go
- It is very difficult to persuade someone to go somewhere they do not want to go
- Most brand successes in the last five years: when people encounter them, they do not need to be persuaded -- they simply need to know about them

## The Smallest Viable Audience (SVA)

### The Concept
Ask: how FEW people would you need to delight for your project to work?

### Why SVA Works
- Forces specificity (no hiding behind "everyone is our customer")
- Puts you on the hook for actually delighting a specific group
- Creates a ratchet: more connection leads to more action leads to more status and affiliation

### The iPhone Example
The iPhone only sold 100,000 units its first month. That was the SVA -- enough early adopters to create momentum, word of mouth, and tribal belonging.

### The Anti-Pattern: Newton vs. iPhone
Apple Newton sold 100,000 units at $1,000 each in its first period -- $100M revenue. Still failed. Why? Buyers were not a group, they were a BUNCH. Not connected, not responding to the same story. The SVA must be a connected tribe, not random individuals.

### The Tom's Shoes Case Study
- Found women who wanted to be first, feel like philanthropists, engage online
- Logo on back of shoes created visibility: "nice shoes, where'd you get them?"
- Status transfer: "I knew about these and you didn't" + "I'm a philanthropist and you're not"
- Tension in friends who feel behind triggers purchase cycle
- Sold company for $500M
- **Anti-pattern:** Tom's coffee failed because nobody says "interesting coffee" -- the conversation went away, the tribal relationship disappeared

## Brand Definition

### A Brand Is a Promise (Not a Logo)
Test: If Nike opened a hotel, you could guess what it would be like. If Hyatt made sneakers, you could not. Nike has a brand; Hyatt just has a logo.

### Brand Loyalty Components
1. If your thing costs more than competitors, does anyone still buy yours?
2. If you announce something, are people interested? Do they give you the benefit of the doubt?

If the answer to both is no, you do not have a brand. You have a commodity.

## Three Fundamental Human Desires

Every purchase decision connects to one or more of these:

1. **Status** -- Who is up, who is down, who eats lunch first. Luxury goods, exclusive memberships, professional tools.
2. **Affiliation** -- Who is to our left, who is to our right, who is like me. Community products, team gear, identity brands.
3. **Freedom from fear** -- Safety, security, certainty. Insurance, health products, home security, supplements.

### Application to Creative
- Identify which desire your product serves
- Lead with that desire in your advertising
- Status brands: exclusivity, limited editions, "for those who..."
- Affiliation brands: community, belonging, "join the movement"
- Fear brands: safety, protection, peace of mind, guarantees

## Positioning Framework

### The XY Grid Method
1. Draw an XY grid with two relevant axes for your market
2. Plot existing competitors on the grid
3. Find an under-inhabited quadrant
4. Fill that quadrant with your brand

### Rules
- There is no "best" position, only a USEFUL one
- Positioning is a generous act -- eagerly send people who should NOT be your customers to competitors
- Supreme example: position is not "you need a t-shirt, we have one" -- it is "for people who view clothing as status and affiliation"

## Strategy Cards

### Card 1: Do Not Play Games You Cannot Win
You cannot out-Amazon Amazon. But you can do things Amazon cannot: personal relationships, niche expertise, brand community, remarkable experiences.

### Card 2: Avoid False Proxies
Easy to measure does not mean important. Tracking clicks makes clicks go up; it does not make your brand better. The things that matter most (trust, loyalty, word-of-mouth) are hardest to measure.

### Card 3: Genre Is Not Generic
Genre means "what do I expect?" A yoga studio and a CrossFit gym are both fitness, but different genres. Match platform conventions (genre) when they build trust -- then surprise within the genre to stand out.

### Card 4: Tension
FOMO, scarcity, anticipation. Tension is the rubber band pulled back before release. Without tension, there is no reason to act now. Tension creates the gap between "I want it" and "I don't have it yet."

### Card 5: The Story Test
"What will I tell my friends/boss?" If you do not give people a story to tell about your product, the only story left is "I did what we did last time" or "I got the same thing cheaper."

## Consistency Over Authenticity

**Contrarian position:** Authenticity is a trap. Authenticity is for friends and family. For professionals, what matters is CONSISTENCY.

- A brand is a promise. Keep it.
- Consistency = make a promise and keep it
- Social media platforms reward going off-script because that makes THEM money, not because it makes YOU succeed
- Ad agencies rewarded for awards or clicks break the consistency promise with one-offs and gimmicks

## Decision-Making Framework (Annie Duke)

Good decisions do not equal good outcomes. They are unrelated.

- Buying a lottery ticket and winning = bad decision, got lucky
- Driving carefully and getting hit = good decision, got unlucky

### Good Creative Decisions Have Two Criteria
1. **Do not run out of time or money** (survive to iterate)
2. **Be strategically coherent** -- have you thought deeply about who it is for, what it is for, and the change you seek to make?

### Application to Advertising
- A campaign that loses money is not necessarily a bad decision if the strategy was sound
- A campaign that makes money is not necessarily a good decision if it was luck
- Judge the PROCESS, not the outcome. Then adjust the process based on accumulated data.

## Additional Strategy Cards

### Card 6: The Ratchet Effect
Create systems where each success makes the next success easier:
- More customers = more reviews = more social proof = lower CPA = more customers
- More data = better targeting = better creative = more data
- More brand awareness = higher CTR = lower CPC = more brand awareness
- Design your marketing to create positive feedback loops, not one-off campaigns

### Card 7: People Like Us Do Things Like This
The most powerful marketing message is tribal identity:
- "People like us drink this coffee"
- "People like us use this tool"
- "People like us would never settle for that"
- Your job is to define the tribe and make belonging easy

### Card 8: Sunk Costs Are Not Strategy
- Just because you spent $50K building a campaign does not mean you should keep spending
- The money is gone. Evaluate forward-looking ROI only.
- Emotional attachment to past spend is the most expensive bias in advertising

## Positioning Examples by Category

### E-Commerce Positioning
| Axis 1 | Axis 2 | Example Position |
|---|---|---|
| Price (low to high) | Quality (mass to artisan) | High-price, artisan quality (craft goods) |
| Speed (slow to instant) | Customization (standard to bespoke) | Instant delivery, standard products (Amazon) |
| Niche (broad to specialized) | Experience (transactional to community) | Specialized niche with strong community (Peloton) |

### Service Business Positioning
| Axis 1 | Axis 2 | Example Position |
|---|---|---|
| Price (budget to premium) | Involvement (DIY to done-for-you) | Premium, done-for-you (agency) |
| Speed (weeks to same-day) | Scope (single service to full solution) | Same-day, single service (emergency plumber) |
| Expertise (generalist to specialist) | Relationship (transactional to long-term) | Specialist, long-term relationship (fractional CMO) |

### How to Use the Grid in Practice
1. List your top 5 competitors
2. Choose two axes that matter most to YOUR audience (not to you)
3. Plot each competitor honestly
4. Identify the empty or under-served quadrant
5. Validate: does the empty quadrant represent actual customer desire or a dead zone nobody wants?
6. If dead zone: pick a different axis pair and repeat
7. If real opportunity: build your brand story, creative, and positioning around owning that quadrant

## Brand vs. Direct Response: The Integration Model

### The False Dichotomy
Most advertisers treat brand and DR as separate activities. The best advertisers integrate them:
- Every DR ad should reinforce brand (consistent visual identity, tone, promise)
- Every brand touchpoint should have a clear next step (even if soft)
- Brand campaigns feed DR campaigns (familiarity increases CTR and CVR on DR ads)
- DR campaigns feed brand (every customer interaction builds brand equity)

### Integration Rules
1. Visual identity must be consistent across ALL touchpoints (same colors, fonts, imagery style)
2. Tone of voice must be consistent even when message varies (DR copy and brand copy sound like the same company)
3. Measure brand health alongside DR metrics: search volume for brand terms, direct traffic, repeat purchase rate
4. Allocate 10-20% of total ad budget to brand-building activities once DR campaigns are profitable
