# Deal Recovery Reference

## Five Serhant Negotiation Techniques (Full Detail)

### Technique 1: The Pull
Pull the deal away entirely.

**Script:** "Thank you so much for working with me and getting us this far. I truly appreciate your hard work. Unfortunately, it's just not going to work out this time. Good luck!"

**When to use:** When you have nothing to lose. When the prospect is stuck and no other technique has moved them. When you need to break the pattern.

**Risk level:** HIGH. Client must understand the risks if you are advising them to use this.

**Serhant's assessment:** "This has worked more for me more often than it hasn't."

**Psychology:** Loss aversion kicks in. The prospect suddenly realizes what they are about to lose. Many come back with renewed interest and better terms.

### Technique 2: The Facts
Purely analytical approach. Remove all emotion.

"These are the facts. Here are the comps. This is our justification."

**When to use:** With analytical prospects. When emotion is clouding judgment. When you need objective ground to stand on.

**Key:** Hard to argue with facts. If you do not have the facts, it is hard to have a good deal. Preparation is everything.

### Technique 3: "What Do You Want to Do?"
Put the decision entirely in their hands.

**When to use:** When you believe in the deal. When the prospect needs to feel control. When pushing harder would be counterproductive.

**Critical rule:** Only fight for a deal you BELIEVE in. Never advise a buyer to overpay. Never advise a seller to accept too low. If the deal is right, let them make the call.

### Technique 4: The Emotions
Lean into the emotional stakes when SO close to closing or losing.

**Frame:** "[Extra amount] over [time horizon] is only [small amount per period]. Is that worth [their dream outcome]?"

**When to use:** When the gap is small but feelings are big. When ego or pride is the real blocker, not money.

**Serhant's insight:** "It's almost never about the money. It's about ego, pride, fear, or emotion."

### Technique 5: The Reality
Deliver hard truth with respect.

"This is what it is. This is where we are. This is what the market is saying. The end."

**When to use:** When the prospect has unrealistic expectations. When all other approaches have failed.

**Hardest technique to execute.** Sometimes you must tell someone the truth they do not want to hear. The best closers tell the truth.

## The HALL Method (Serhant -- Pre-Recovery Framework)

Before any recovery attempt: deep breath, straighten posture, visualize success.

### H -- Have Empathy
This is not just a transaction. It is emotional for the prospect. Be patient. Remain calm at ALL times.

### A -- Always Counter
No matter how unreasonable the situation:
- If you do not counter, the deal dies
- The worst offer or worst situation is just a beginning
- "Conversations got to start somehow"

### L -- Look for the Sweet Spot
What is being asked for that can be used as incentive? Not about giving things away -- about working for the deal. Find where both parties can feel satisfied.

### L -- Listen
Listen to respond, not just to reply. Let it sink in. Take a few seconds before responding. There is no rush. Blurting out a response before thinking will not help.

## Red-Button Negotiation (Serhant -- When Deals Fall Apart)

When buyers are getting anxious, second-guessing, or threatening to walk -- hit the red button.

### Strategy 1: Setting
Get them back in front of what excited them. Out of sight = out of mind.

If they were excited about the views, get them in front of the views while discussing the deal-killing issue. They will realize the issue feels smaller than it seemed on paper.

### Strategy 2: Bring Parties Together
Usually you would not put buyer and seller (or their representatives) in the same room. But when a deal is dying, let them see real people with real concerns. People on paper are easy to reject; people in person are hard to walk away from.

### Strategy 3: Research in Action
Use comps, math, and marketplace knowledge in front of everyone. Let the data tell the story and bridge the gap. Facts presented live carry more weight than facts sent by email.

### Concession Creativity
When the gap seems unbridgeable, look for creative non-monetary solutions:
- Include items that offset the perceived gap
- Find things valuable to one side but low-cost to the other
- Turn "deal-breakers" into "nice-to-haves" with creative solutions

## Five-Phase "No" Progression (Voss)

For setting boundaries when the other side makes unreasonable demands during a deal:

### Phase 1: "How am I supposed to do that?"
Use this calibrated question 2-3 times before moving on. Sounds like a counterbid. Counterpart often softens position or bids against themselves.

### Phase 2: "Your offer is very generous. I'm sorry, that just doesn't work for me."
"Generous" encourages them to live up to it. The apology softens the impact.

### Phase 3: "I'm sorry, but I'm afraid I just can't do that."
Triggers empathy. Removes leverage of external criteria.

### Phase 4: "I'm sorry, no."
Deliver gently. Short and clear.

### Phase 5: "No."
Downward inflection. Tone of respect. Last resort.

### Key Rule
Say no at least twice. Many negotiators are hardwired to not let up until they have heard no at least twice. If you only say no once, they think more concessions exist.

## When to Walk Away

### Walk-Away Indicators
- Prospect is genuinely unclosable (no resources, no need, no urgency)
- Terms being demanded would make the deal unprofitable or unethical
- The prospect has shown multiple signs of bad faith
- Your attempts to recover have been exhausted (3+ different approaches)

### How to Walk Away with Grace
Serhant: "A dead deal today could be an amazing deal three years from now because of how you handled a bad situation."

- Express genuine appreciation for their time
- Leave the door open for future conversations
- Do not burn the bridge with frustration or bitterness
- Maintain professional presence throughout

### The "No Deal" Principle (Voss)
"No deal is better than a bad deal. A bad deal will bleed you for years."

If you are not willing to walk away, YOU have been taken hostage.

## Recovery Tactics When Losing Control (Serhant)

### Tactic 1: Get Water
Physically get up. Get a glass of water. Use the movement to reset.
Remind yourself: this is a speed bump, not a brick wall.

### Tactic 2: Set Boundaries
If the setting is not in your favor (impasse while at daycare pickup), call a pause.
Be transparent. Schedule a callback. "Throw the red flag."

### Tactic 3: Shift the Focus
Arguing price for days? Start talking concessions, furniture, closing date, closing costs.
When something besides money goes on or off the table, people see the bigger picture.

### Tactic 4: Change It Up
Hang up the phone. End the lunch. Move to another place or time.
Like "icing the kicker" -- the surprise of changing setting puts you back in control.

## Emotional Control Rules

**Serhant:** "The second you lose control of your emotions, the deal is dead."

**Voss:** "If words are about to come out and you can taste how good it'll feel to say them -- that's an indicator they're probably the wrong words."

**Gordon:** When the prospect goes alpha, do not go alpha back. Use the parent frame. Grounded, unwaivable, not condescending. "Two teenagers trying to fight" = both lose.

### Mantras for Deal Recovery (Serhant)
- "Everything is always OK."
- "We will always figure it out."
- "We are in this together."
- "I am on your side."
- "If not this deal, we will find another."
- "I will never quit for you."
