# Urgency & Commitment Building Reference

## Push/Pull/Persist System (Serhant)

### PUSH -- Create Timeline Structure
- Make sure clients have a timeline with clear milestones
- Have attorney, bank, inspector already lined up (shows preparedness)
- Use market data to motivate: comps, number of interested parties, competitive landscape
- Remind them WHY they wanted this in the first place
- "Be their doctor -- tell them they need this medicine."

**Push is NOT:** "Buy now or lose it." Push is creating structure that makes action feel natural and timely.

### PULL -- Take the Deal Away
- "Well, maybe now isn't the best time..."
- "Perhaps [current painful situation] isn't that bad after all."
- "That [solution feature] isn't really that special anyway..."

This reminds them of WHY they were searching. The fear of loss (not having the solution) often exceeds the fear of committing.

**Serhant's pull technique:** "Thank you for working with me. Unfortunately, it's just not going to work out this time. Good luck!" Either they come back wanting it more, or they don't.

### PERSIST -- Repeat the Story
- Facts tell, stories sell
- Politicians use the same words in the same order for a reason
- There is power in repetition and persistence
- Keep repeating the core narrative: their problem, the solution, the outcome

### Critical Warning (Serhant)
Creating urgency is NOT creating anxiety. You are building structure and timelines. Timelines and urgency under a timeline is what people yearn for when making big decisions.

**Rate urgency 1-10** for each party before the close. Write it down.

### The Time Paradox
"Time kills deals, but sometimes time creates deals." Know when to push for speed and when patience builds more value.

## The Commitment Close (Alexander)

### Pre-Price Verbal Commitment
Before the investment drop, secure verbal commitment to follow through:

"I just want to make sure -- if you decide to do something, you follow through. What's your level of commitment for something like this?"

### Why This Works
- Creates consistency pressure (they said they commit, now they must act consistently)
- Makes backing out feel like breaking their word to themselves
- Sets the frame that this is a decision person, not a "think about it" person

### Identity Frame Integration
Reference the identity they built during discovery:
"Remember that version of yourself we talked about -- the one who's disciplined, pushes through fear, makes decisions. Is that the person sitting here right now?"

## Rule of Three (Voss -- Testing Commitment)

Get the prospect to agree to the same thing 3 times in the same conversation.

### Execution
1. **Label:** "It sounds like you're good with this"
2. **Paraphrase:** Restate the agreement in different words. Watch for alignment in tone and body language.
3. **Summarize to get "that's right":** Full summary of what they are committing to + how it will work.

### Why Three Times
- Nearly impossible to repeat counterfeit yeses 3x without a flinch
- Tests 7-38-55 alignment (words 7%, tone 38%, body 55%)
- If they flinch on repetition 2 or 3, there is a hidden concern

### Close with a "No" (Voss)
When you really want to be sure:
"Do you disagree?" / "Are you against making a firm commitment now?"
A "no" to these = a real yes.

## Guarantee Execution (Voss)

### Core Principle
"Yes is nothing without how." Great closers are implementation architects.

### Forced Empathy
Calibrated "how" questions force the prospect to take your position into consideration:
- "How will we know we're on track?"
- "How will we address things if we're off track?"
- After they respond: mirror them, then summarize until you get "that's right"

### Why Implementation Matters
- Agreement is square one; value comes from implementation
- 50% of signed deals at a major telecom carrier are killed internally (Voss statistic)
- Without implementation architecture, you close deals that churn

## Internal vs. External Pressure (Gordon)

### Internal Pressure (Preferred)
Prospect motivated for THEIR OWN reasons. Cost of doing nothing exceeds cost of investment. This is what you build through discovery.

### External Pressure (Supplement)
Fast-action incentives, limited spots, deadlines. Not inherently bad -- just should not be the ONLY lever.

### The Balance
Healthy urgency = primarily internal pressure, supplemented by external. If you need heavy external pressure to close, your discovery was weak.

## Consequences of Inaction

### Urgency Through Consequences (Alexander)
- "What happens if you decide not to change anything for another 6 months?"
- "Would you be satisfied with that?"
- "The same pain you feel today -- do you think it's the same 3 years from now, or worse?"

### The Compounding Frame (Alexander)
"If you're thirsty today and don't drink water, you're even more thirsty tomorrow. The pain compounds."

### Responsibility Question
"Whose responsibility is it to decide whether you get to [goal] or stay where you are?"

## Belief Building Sequence (Before Close)

Before asking for commitment, establish three beliefs in order:

1. **It's Possible** -- Share testimonials, case studies, community wins
2. **It's Possible For Them** -- Get them to admit they have similar capabilities
3. **It's Worth It** -- Payoff justifies investment and effort

**Why order matters:** Logical agreement on their own criteria BEFORE asking for money.

## Sunk Cost and Commitment Psychology

### The Deposit Effect
- Small deposit creates sunk cost bias
- People are less likely to abandon something they have invested in
- Even a small financial commitment changes the psychological equation

### The Public Commitment Effect
- Verbal commitments in front of others (spouse, team) carry more weight
- Getting them to state their commitment out loud makes it harder to reverse

### Homework as Commitment Device
Assigning homework immediately after close:
- Creates engagement before buyer's remorse can set in
- Gives them a sense of progress and ownership
- Shifts identity from "prospect" to "participant"

## The Deposit as Commitment Device (Gordon)

### When to Use Deposits
- Spouse objection with genuine need to consult (not a smokescreen)
- Prospect is 9-10/10 on process but needs 24-48 hours for logistics
- Logistical money movement (getting funds from another account, paycheck timing)

### Deposit Rules
1. **Never offer deposits before completing the full objection handling process.** Deposits are for logistics AFTER certainty is established.
2. The deposit creates sunk cost bias -- people are less likely to abandon something they have invested in.
3. Use the CC Close as an alternative: take the credit card number but do not run it. Acts as a psychological commitment device.
4. Always pair with a specific follow-up time: "I'll call you tomorrow at 2pm to finalize."

### The Public Commitment Amplifier
When possible, get them to state their commitment in front of others:
- On a spouse/partner call: "So both of you are aligned that this is the right move?"
- On a team call: "Can everyone confirm this is the direction we want to go?"
- Even on a 1-on-1 call: "I want you to say out loud: I am committed to [goal]."

Public verbal commitments carry more weight than private ones due to consistency bias.

## Common Urgency Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Fails | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| Fake scarcity ("only 3 spots left") | Prospects see through it | Use real constraints or structural urgency |
| Heavy discounting for speed | Trains price sensitivity | Hold payment plans as re-offer instead |
| Pushing timeline without reason | Feels manipulative | Tie urgency to THEIR goals and timeline |
| Creating anxiety not urgency | Prospect shuts down | Create structure and milestones instead |
| Using only external pressure | Feels like manipulation | Build internal pressure through discovery |
| Urgency without empathy | Comes across as pushy | Always pair urgency with understanding (HALL method) |
