# Bargaining Models Reference

## Ackerman Bargaining Model -- Complete Walkthrough

### Origin
Developed by ex-CIA agent Mike Ackerman. Designed for the toughest bare-knuckle bargainers: kidnappers. If it works against kidnappers, it works in business.

### Step-by-Step Process

**Step 1: Set Your Target Price**
- What you actually need/want from the deal
- Calculate this BEFORE the negotiation begins
- This number guides everything -- all moves are percentages of this target

**Step 2: Open at 65% of Target**
- Example: Target $100K --> Open at $65K
- This is YOUR number regardless of what other side says
- Does NOT mean you go first -- deploy when appropriate
- Pre-seed with emotional anchor: "You're going to be disappointed by this..." then PAUSE, go SILENT, count to 10
- They will imagine something worse, making your actual number seem reasonable

**Step 3: Three Raises at Decreasing Increments**
| Move | Calculation | Running Total | Signal |
|------|-----------|---------------|--------|
| Open | 65% of target | 65% | Anchors low but rationally |
| Raise 1 | +20% of target | 85% | Shows willingness to move |
| Raise 2 | +10% of target | 95% | Smaller move = running out of room |
| Raise 3 | +5% of target | 100% | Barely moving = at ceiling |

**Step 4: Between Each Move (Black Swan Group Addition)**
- Deploy relentless tactical empathy
- Use calibrated "no" techniques: "How am I supposed to do that?"
- Get them to counter BEFORE you raise
- Work to get them bidding against themselves
- Summarize their position to trigger "that's right"
- Be prepared: they may cave at any moment. If so, hit with empathy and summaries to seal it.

**Step 5: Final Number = Precise, Non-Rounded**
- $37,893 not $37,500
- $987 not $1,000
- Precise numbers signal careful calculation
- Perceived as more permanent and immovable

**Step 6: On Final Number, Add Non-Monetary Item**
- Something they probably do not want
- Shows you have hit absolute ceiling
- Examples: "a year supply of smoothies," "6 months of implementation support," "bonus training access"
- Signals finality -- you are reaching beyond money for value

### Full Example

**Target: $100,000 contract**

1. Open: $65,000 (pre-seeded with "You're going to be disappointed...")
2. Their pushback expected
3. Deploy tactical empathy: "I understand. Let me show you how we got here..."
4. First raise: $85,000 (+$20K)
5. Their counter likely
6. Calibrated questions between: "How am I supposed to do that?"
7. Second raise: $95,000 (+$10K)
8. Another pause. Summarize to get "that's right."
9. Third raise: $100,000 (+$5K)
10. Final offer: "$100,000 plus 6 months of implementation support at no charge."

## Emotional Anchoring Tactics (Voss)

### 1. Anchor Their Emotions
Start with a single anchoring statement:
- "I've got bad news..."
- "You're not going to like this..."
- "This is expensive, more than you've got..."
- Invokes automatic negative triggers, bending perception of what comes next

### 2. Let the Other Side Go First (on price)
- Going first is usually not the best idea in negotiation
- Their naming a price creates commitment that a deal COULD be made
- Prepare mentally for the "punch in the nose" (extreme anchor)
- Anchor and adjustment effect: people adjust from the first reference point

**Warning:** High anchors make deals go away that should have been made. One of the greatest invisible killers of opportunity.

### 3. Pivot to Non-Monetary Terms
- Brainstorm things valuable to you and easy for them to give
- Invests them in the deal-making process
- Keep a list of non-monetary items ready BEFORE negotiation

### 4. Use Odd/Precise Numbers
- Numbers ending in zero seem like placeholders
- Odd, precise numbers give authority ($105,357 vs. $105,000)
- Perceived as calculated and immovable

## Eight Bargaining Deflection Methods (Voss)

When hit with an extreme anchor:

1. **"How am I supposed to do that?"** -- Calibrated question. Sounds like a counterbid. They often soften.
2. **"What are we trying to accomplish here?"** -- Redirects to shared goals.
3. **"Let's put price aside and talk about what makes this a good deal."** -- Pivots to non-monetary.
4. **"What else could you offer to make that price work?"** -- Asks for added value.
5. **"Other firms usually charge [higher amount]."** + SILENCE -- External reference point.
6. **"It sounds like you're not prepared to make a deal."** -- Label as fishing technique.
7. **"It seems like you have a range in mind."** + SILENCE -- Probes their flexibility.
8. **Offer a range** where YOUR desired number is the end they jump to. They feel victorious choosing the anchor you wanted.

## Strategic Umbrage (Voss -- Tactical Anger)

**Shown to be effective** at getting SHORT-TERM concessions.

**But:** Concessions gained through anger ALWAYS have implementation problems. ALWAYS.
- Anger degrades relationships
- Triggers poor decision-making
- "Give a speech when you're angry and you'll give the best speech you'll ever regret."

**Rule:** Don't fight fire with fire. "Ever hear of a fireman showing up carrying a flamethrower?"

## Setting Boundaries Without Escalation (Voss)

"I'm sorry, that just doesn't work for me." -- Late Night FM DJ voice, slow, measured.

Critical: avoid sounding condescending. Sound UNFAZED, unrattled. Calm is contagious -- trigger their mirror neurons.

## The Confrontation Framework (Voss)

Confrontation = a focused comparison, not a fight.

1. Label the counterproductive action
2. State the goal it contradicts
3. Ask how they line up

Example: "You stated you want to come to agreement, and you're against our suggestion. How do those things line up?"

Use genuinely curious Late Night FM DJ voice.

## First Offer Benchmarks (Serhant)

### Representing Buyer
- Always find a comp that supports the offer, even if low
- Never put in an offer that gets no response
- "Bad" market: Start 10-20% below asking
- "OK" market: Start 5-10% below

### Representing Seller
- Set listing price using three numbers: first offer, best target, walkaway
- Never share best target with other side

## Going First on Price -- Context Guide

| Situation | Who Goes First | Why |
|-----------|---------------|-----|
| Negotiation with alternatives | Let them (Voss/Serhant) | Their anchor creates commitment |
| Sales call with set pricing | You (Gordon) | Confidence and leadership |
| Unknown market conditions | Let them (Voss) | Gather information first |
| You have strong data | You (Serhant) | Facts support your position |
| They are begging for your number | You (Voss exception) | Resistance wastes momentum |

## Compromise: When and How

**Voss:** "Compromise is a formula for failure."
**Serhant:** "Second-choice compromise."

### When Compromise Might Be Acceptable (Rare)
- Both parties have exhausted creative alternatives
- The gap is genuinely small and immaterial
- Time pressure is real (not manufactured)
- Relationship value exceeds the specific deal value

### When to NEVER Compromise
- When doing so creates a bad deal for either party
- When creative alternatives have not been explored
- When the other side is using compromise as a manipulation tactic
- When "meeting in the middle" rewards their extreme anchor
