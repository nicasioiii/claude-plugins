# Counterpart Analysis Reference

## Three Negotiator Types (Voss) -- Complete Profiles

### The Black Swan Rule
"Don't treat others the way you want to be treated. Treat them the way THEY need to be treated."

The Golden Rule is wrong for negotiation. "I am normal" is one of the most damaging assumptions -- you unconsciously project your style onto others. There is no "normal."

2 out of 3 people you deal with will NOT be your type. Impasses are most often type mismatches.

### Type 1: ANALYST

**Core Need:** Obtaining all information possible

**Characteristics:**
- Methodical, diligent, pragmatic, skeptical
- Not reaching agreement is acceptable if they got all the info
- Time is of little consequence -- will take as long as needed
- Self-image linked to minimizing mistakes
- Prefers working alone; hides emotions
- Naturally uses FM DJ voice (but comes across cold)
- Hates getting caught off guard -- researches for weeks to avoid it

**Communication Style:**
- Hypersensitive to reciprocity -- sees it as a trap
- Questions raise their guard (even calibrated ones)
- Silence means they are thinking, not angry
- Apologies have little value (negotiation and relationship are separate things)

**Best Approach:**
- **Primary tool: LABELS** -- not questions
- "It seems like you've put a lot of time into this"
- "It looks like accuracy is really important to you"
- If they disagree with your approach: "Let's make a dispassionate comparison of data"
- Build in a day or two for each significant response
- Provide detailed written information they can analyze privately
- Be extremely prepared with data

**What NOT to Do:**
- Do not rush them
- Do not push for relationship before substance
- Do not use emotional appeals as primary strategy
- Do not interpret silence as rejection

### Type 2: ACCOMMODATOR

**Core Need:** The RELATIONSHIP

**Characteristics:**
- Sociable, peace-seeking, optimistic, distractable, poor time managers
- As long as relationship is being worked on, time is well spent
- Goal: be on great terms; caught up with win-win
- Tend to put your needs before their own
- Most likely to build great rapport WITHOUT accomplishing anything
- Even without agreement, want to remain friends
- Will yield concessions hoping for reciprocation (one-sided deals)

**Communication Style:**
- Objections extremely difficult to uncover (afraid objections hurt relationship)
- Much broader view of "white lies" -- may agree to things they cannot deliver
- Horrified of silence -- that is what THEY use to signal fury
- (The person whose name on your phone = 45-minute conversation)

**Best Approach:**
- **Primary tool: Implementation-focused "how" questions**
- "How will we make this work for your team?"
- "How do you see this playing out day to day?"
- If going silent, tell them WHY (or they'll fill it with anxious talk)
- Apology is ESSENTIAL if they've been offended -- lack of apology becomes permanent distraction
- Use their natural warmth to build genuine connection
- Be patient with their tendency to tell stories

**What NOT to Do:**
- Do not take their agreement at face value (test with Rule of Three)
- Do not use silence as a negotiation tactic without explanation
- Do not push too hard on uncomfortable topics without building rapport first
- Do not assume their friendliness means the deal is done

**Strength:** Naturally likable and friendly. 6x more likely to make a deal with someone you like.

### Type 3: ASSERTIVE

**Core Need:** Being HEARD

**Characteristics:**
- Time = money. Hate wasting it.
- Self-image: things accomplished per period
- Fiery, loves winning, feels in control when speaking
- Very direct and candid -- no confusion about where they stand
- Aggressive but shortsighted communication
- Silence = opportunity to speak more
- Oblivious to reciprocity expectations (they figure they earned whatever you gave)
- "Give an inch, take a mile"
- Business relationships based on respect, nothing more

**Communication Style:**
- Cannot listen to you until they know you've heard them
- Mirrors work very well on them
- Summaries trigger easy "that's right" (may say "that's it exactly" or "you hit the nail on the head")

**Best Approach:**
- **Primary tools: MIRRORS and SUMMARIES**
- Let them talk first. Show you've heard them.
- Mirror their key points to get them to elaborate
- Build summaries aimed at "that's right"
- Once they feel heard, they become much more receptive
- Be direct and respectful -- they value candor

**What NOT to Do:**
- Do not try to out-talk them
- Do not interpret their directness as rudeness
- Do not give unsolicited concessions (they'll just ask for more)
- If you are assertive: soften tone, use labels, "never be so sure of what you want that you wouldn't take something better"

**Strength:** CANDOR. You always know where you stand.

## Opposition Research Framework (Serhant)

### Three Research Areas

**1. The Subject / Property / Deal**
- Full history (sales, pricing, ownership)
- Market conditions when acquired
- Current market data and comps
- Adjust for inflation, market changes
- Will they profit or lose? How much?

**2. The Other Agent / Representative**
- Experience level (years, deal volume)
- Is this price point familiar or new to them?
- LinkedIn, social media, public presence
- Mutual connections
- Communication style tendencies

**3. Supporting Data**
- Comps (THROUGH THEIR EYES, not just yours)
- Regulatory constraints (taxes, zoning, regulations)
- Local market shifts and trends
- Recent comparable transactions

### Deep Research Tactics
- Check LinkedIn for mutual connections
- Look at social media for life changes (divorce, relocation = urgency)
- Check for any hidden motivations in the cast of characters
- Identify the deal killers (not just decision makers)

## Stealth Negotiation Tactics (Serhant / Jennifer Elise)

### Reading the Other Agent

**Experienced agent:**
- Soften your approach
- Let them think they have something to teach you
- Play down what you have and know
- Ask: "What would you do if you were in my position?" -- reveals their thinking

**New/inexperienced agent:**
- Take on mentor role
- "If it were my client, I would do A, B, and C."
- Write emails they are "welcome to forward" to their client -- ensures your message gets through accurately
- Key phrases: "We're in this together." "Let's work together to figure this out."

### Transparent Communication + Selective Silence
- Almost never answer quickly when emotions are high
- Silence speaks for itself -- puts you in position where they tell their story

**Silence case study (Serhant):** Buyer's agent sent under-ask offer. Seller's agent knew buyer was hooked (eyes lit up during showing). Told agent she received the offer, it was below what seller would accept, she would present it. Then went SILENT for several days. Buyer's agent came back with revised offer AT asking price.

### Reading Communication Patterns
- Pushing hard on price = either firm on value OR ego is in the way (determine which)
- Frequent follow-up after showing = more motivated than they want to appear = more negotiation room
- Following up too much in negotiation = REVEALING you want the deal more than the other side

## Black Swans and Leverage (Voss)

### What Are Black Swans?
Hidden pieces of information that, if uncovered, change everything about the negotiation. The unknown unknowns.

### Three Types of Leverage

**Positive Leverage:**
- What they want that you control
- What they need that you provide
- What would benefit them if the deal happens
- Create by tying your solution to their success

**Negative Leverage:**
- What they want to avoid
- Pain of status quo
- Cost of delay or missed opportunity
- Create by helping them see consequences of inaction

**Normative Leverage:**
- What they believe is right/fair
- Industry standards and norms
- Community expectations
- Create by framing your position as aligned with fairness

**Combining leverage:** Best deals use all three. Multi-lever deals close faster and implement better.

### "Know Their Religion" (Voss)
Understand your counterpart's worldview, identity, and deepest values. This is the most powerful form of leverage.

How to discover it:
- Listen for what they care about beyond the deal
- Notice what they reference repeatedly
- Ask about their values and priorities
- Observe what triggers emotional responses
- Research their background, interests, affiliations

When you speak to their "religion" -- their deepest held beliefs about how the world should work -- resistance dissolves.

### Discovering Black Swans
- Ask open-ended questions and LISTEN
- Pay attention to off-hand comments and asides
- Notice inconsistencies in their story
- Research background information before negotiation
- Follow up on emotional reactions with labels
- Use the Rule of Three to test if what they said is real

## Pronoun Usage as Power Indicator (Voss)

- **More I/me/my (singular) = LESS important** in decision-making
- **Avoidance of personal pronouns; use of we/us = MORE important** person
- Decision makers defer to people away from the table to avoid being cornered
- Excessive first-person = less important; second/third-person = larger influence

### Implication for Negotiation
If your counterpart keeps saying "I," they may not be the real decision maker. Ask: "How does this affect the rest of your team?" to surface the actual influencers.

## The Pinocchio Effect (Voss)

- The harder someone works to convince you (especially if lying), the MORE WORDS they use
- Added complexity to sentences
- More words + higher complexity = increased probability of deception
- Simple, direct answers are more likely truthful
- Watch for sudden increases in verbosity when answering specific questions

## The Team Behind the Table (Voss)

There is ALWAYS a team on the other side. Spouse, attorney, implementers, advisors.

**The deal killer is not the decision-maker -- it is the person AFFECTED by the decision.**

50% of signed deals at a major telecom carrier were killed internally (Voss). Never consummated.

**Use calibrated questions:**
- "How does this affect the rest of the team?"
- "What do your colleagues see as their main challenges?"
- Ask these MORE THAN ONCE over the course of the negotiation

### Identifying Hidden Players (Serhant -- Funnel Technique)
Ask about family, partners, advisors early in the relationship. The funnel technique reveals hidden players that surface only months later if not discovered proactively.
