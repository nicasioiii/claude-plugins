# Deal Structuring Reference

## Interests vs. Positions Framework (Serhant)

### Core Principle
Positions are WHAT people want. Interests are WHY they want it.

Splitting the difference addresses positions. Creative deal structuring addresses interests.

### How to Uncover Interests
Ask WHY behind every demand:
- "Why is that number important to you?"
- "What would that allow you to do?"
- "What happens if we can't meet that exact number?"

### Common Interests Behind Positions

**Buyer interests behind "can't come up":**
- Loan limits
- Monthly payment constraints
- Renovation budget already allocated
- Has not sold current property yet

**Seller interests behind "can't come down":**
- Needs money for new home purchase
- Medical bills or family care
- Emotional attachment / ego
- Not in a rush (low urgency)

### Creative Bridge Examples
If you know the interests, you can bridge the gap in ways neither side considered:
- Include furnishings to offset renovation costs
- Adjust closing timeline to match cash flow
- Cover certain closing costs instead of lowering price
- Add services or warranty instead of discount
- Split the cost of repairs discovered during inspection

## Game Theory / Nash Equilibrium Applied (Serhant)

No single person gets exactly what they thought they wanted, but everyone ends up in the best possible position.

### The Process
1. List what each party REALLY wants (interests, not positions)
2. Find where giving one side something costs the other side little
3. Create packages where both parties get their priority items
4. Neither gets everything, but both get what matters most

### Questions for Finding Creative Solutions
- Other than a deeper discount, what might satisfy the buyer's need?
- How might you help the seller save money so they ultimately net more?
- Where can both clients save on out-of-pocket costs?
- Think about the FULL duration: closing costs, moving costs, implementation, maintenance

### Example
Buyer wants discount. Seller wants top dollar. Neither will happen.
- Deeper research reveals: Seller moving abroad (needs to sell furniture). Buyer moving from studio (needs to furnish dream home).
- Creative solution: Include furniture with purchase. Seller saves moving/selling costs. Buyer gets furnished home. Price stays higher but total value increases for both.

## Five Steps of CODO -- Deal Structuring Application (Serhant)

### Step 1: Know the Star Players
Map everyone involved in the deal:
- Decision makers on both sides
- Influencers (family, advisors, partners)
- Gatekeepers (admin, legal, finance)
- Deal killers (implementers, affected parties)

Draw a line with parties at each end. List all players and desired results in between. Look for overlaps.

### Step 2: Opposition Research
Research the other side thoroughly:
- Sales/purchase history
- Agent/rep experience level
- Public information (LinkedIn, social, press)
- Mutual connections

**Key insight:** Most reps comp only for their own client. Comp through the OTHER side's eyes. What data will THEY use?

### Step 3: Create Urgency
Rate urgency 1-10 for each party. The party with lower urgency has more leverage. Create structural urgency (timelines, milestones) not anxiety.

### Step 4: Study the Market
Be an information machine. Facts overcome feelings. Bring market knowledge to day one -- if you only demonstrate expertise at the close, trust is already lost.

### Step 5: Work the Setting
- Meeting at YOUR office = power position
- Meeting at THEIR office = they feel safe (playing for other side)
- Neutral coffee shop = collaborative feel
- Never send bad news by text or email -- deliver live

## Non-Monetary Pivots (Voss)

### Why Non-Monetary Matters
Often more valuable than price concessions. Things that cost you little can be worth a lot to the other side.

### Categories of Non-Monetary Value
| Category | Examples |
|----------|---------|
| **Time** | Earlier/later delivery, extended warranty, flexible timeline |
| **Service** | Implementation support, training, consulting hours |
| **Risk reduction** | Guarantees, insurance, trial periods |
| **Convenience** | Handling logistics, paperwork, coordination |
| **Relationship** | Future business commitments, referral agreements |
| **Knowledge** | Strategy sessions, exclusive access, data sharing |

### How to Use
1. Brainstorm list of non-monetary items BEFORE negotiation
2. Identify which items are low-cost to you but high-value to them
3. Deploy strategically -- do not put all on table at once
4. Save best non-monetary items for final Ackerman move (Step 6)

## The Sweet Spot (Serhant)

The place where deals are closed, money is made, and everyone feels positive.

**Finding the sweet spot:**
- Start with each party's ideal outcome
- Map the overlap zone
- Within overlap, find the configuration that gives each party their PRIORITY items
- Trade off less-important items for more-important ones

### Sweet Spot Visualization
```
Buyer's ideal range: [----lowest-----buyer target-----highest willing]
Seller's ideal range:       [lowest willing-----seller target-----highest----]
Sweet spot:                      [====OVERLAP ZONE====]
```

The art is finding where within the overlap zone both parties feel they got a good deal.

## Setting Failsafe Terms (Serhant)

### Start High or Start Low (Contextual)
- **Seller's market:** Advise buyer to come in at or above asking, but include concessions to negotiate down. Ask for things buyer does not feel strongly about just to pad negotiation room.
- **Buyer's market:** Start with lower offer to keep buyer happy. Build from there.

### Professional Presentation
A well-structured, professionally presented offer legitimizes the proposal and leaves the impression you mean business.

### Always Counter
No matter how unreasonable the other side's position, ALWAYS counter. Not countering kills the deal. The worst offer is just a beginning, not an ending.

## Concession Management

### Serhant's Rule
"You could have a million concessions up your sleeve. But if you put everything on the table from the get-go, you lose control and have no cards left."

### Concession Timing Principles
1. Never give a concession without getting one in return
2. Make concessions SLOWLY (signals each one costs you)
3. Never concede on your core interests -- only peripheral items
4. Each concession should be smaller than the last (Ackerman principle)
5. Frame each concession as something you are giving up (even if it costs you little)

### Concession Creativity
When the gap seems unbridgeable:
- Find something besides price to bridge it
- Trade timeline for price, service for discount, terms for number
- Turn deal-breakers into nice-to-haves with creative solutions
- Let the decision-maker make the choice: present options, not ultimatums

## Managing Expectations (Serhant)

### Buyer Expectations
- Let them name their offer first (gives control, validates them)
- If higher than you would suggest: "You could start lower and negotiate up"
- If lower than asking: "That's a great starting point, but be prepared for [counter]"

### Seller Expectations
- Never price in a vacuum. Every seller thinks their asset is worth more than market.
- Use data and comps to ground expectations EARLY
- "Your first offer is always your best offer" -- use as urgency leverage

### Multi-Party Expectations
- Manage expectations of ALL parties: your client, other agent, their client, yourself
- No surprises. Forecast likely scenarios before they happen.
- "I want you to be prepared if they come back with X. Here's our plan for that."
