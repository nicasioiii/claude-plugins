# Advanced Objection Handling Techniques

This file contains sophisticated psychological techniques and reframes from negotiation experts, elite closers, and behavioral scientists. Use these after mastering basic frameworks.

---

## Labeling (From Negotiation - Chris Voss)

Labeling involves identifying and naming the emotion you observe, which validates it and often gets the prospect to open up.

### Why It Works
- Validates emotions without agreeing with them
- Often releases the emotions so they can move forward
- Shows you understand their concern
- Creates psychological safety

### How to Label

**Observe an emotion and name it:**
- "It seems like this matters a lot to you"
- "I sense some hesitation"
- "It looks like you're concerned about implementation"
- "You seem worried about whether this will work"

**Key principle:** Label the emotion, not the objection.

Not: "You're making excuses"
Yes: "It seems like you're feeling uncertain about this"

### When to Use Labeling

When emotions are running high or they're resistant. Labeling often releases the emotional block.

**Example:**
**Prospect (emotional):** "I don't know, man, this just feels risky"

**You:** "I hear you. It sounds like risk is what's coming up for you here. Like you're concerned about making the wrong move."

**Prospect (often opens up):** "Yeah, exactly. I'm just worried I'll invest and it won't work"

**You:** Now you've got the real objection: fear it won't work.

### Anti-Pattern
**DON'T say:** "You're being emotional" or "You're being irrational"
This triggers defensiveness.

**DO say:** "I sense [emotion] is coming up here." This validates.

## Accusation Audit (From Negotiation - Chris Voss)

Proactively list every accusation, criticism, or negative thing they might think about you or your offer BEFORE they say it.

### Why It Works
- Disarms defensiveness before it happens
- Shows you understand objections from their perspective
- They can't hit you with accusations you've already acknowledged
- Creates safety and transparency

### How to Do an Accusation Audit

**List 3-5 potential accusations they might have:**
- About you: "You're just trying to make money off me"
- About the offer: "This is probably a scam / not as good as advertised"
- About results: "These testimonials are probably fake"
- About the cost: "This is way overpriced"
- About the process: "This probably takes way more work than you're saying"

**Then proactively address them:**

**Script:**
"Before we go further, I want to address some things you might be thinking. You might be thinking 'This guy is just trying to get my money.' You might wonder 'Are these testimonials real, or are they actors?' You might think 'This is overpriced for what I'm getting.' You might worry 'The timeline they're saying is way shorter than reality.' Here's what I'll tell you about each of those..."

### When to Use It

- At the beginning of a call with a skeptical prospect
- When you're the new/unknown person in the market
- When price is a factor
- When you have strong social proof

### The Teaching

When you address accusations proactively, you demonstrate:
1. You understand their skepticism
2. You're confident enough to name it
3. You've thought about their concerns
4. You're willing to be transparent

This builds trust faster than defending.

## The Power of "That's Right" (From Negotiation - Chris Voss)

When a prospect says "That's right" (as opposed to "You're right"), they're confirming genuine agreement.

### The Distinction
- **"You're right"** = They're getting you to stop talking. Weak agreement. Means they want to move on/dismiss you.
- **"That's right"** = They've confirmed you understand them. Strong agreement. Means they're genuinely bought in.

### How to Trigger "That's Right"

Summarize their situation back to them in your words (not theirs):

**Script:**
"So let me make sure I understand. You're at [their situation], you want to get to [their goal], but right now [their constraint] is in the way. And you've tried [their past attempts] but that didn't work because [their insight]. Is that right?"

When they say "That's right," they've confirmed your understanding. Now you can move forward confidently.

### Why This Matters in Objection Handling

When handling an objection, don't just accept their surface answer. Get them to confirm you understand the REAL objection:

**Example:**
**Prospect:** "I need to think about it"

**You (Summarize):** "So what I'm hearing is - you're not 100% certain this will work for your specific situation, and you want time to process that. Is that right?"

**Prospect:** "That's right"

**You:** Now you can address the REAL objection (certainty/fit), not "thinking about it"

### Anti-Pattern
Don't ask leading questions disguised as summarization:
Not: "So really what you're saying is you're afraid this won't work?"
Yes: "So you want to make sure this is the right fit for you?"

## The Power of "No" (From Negotiation - Chris Voss)

Counterintuitively, getting them to say "no" early in the conversation can be useful.

### Why "No" Is Valuable

- "No" is safe for them (they're not committing)
- "No" reveals their true concerns
- Getting small "no"s early often leads to bigger "yes"s later
- "No" is where truth often lies

### How to Use It

Ask permission questions that get "no" answers:

**Script:**
"Is it possible you're concerned this won't work for your situation?" (Likely: "No, it's not that")

Now they've identified what IT'S NOT, which narrows down what it IS.

**Script:**
"Do you think you couldn't commit time to this?" (Likely: "No, I could find time")

Now you know timing isn't the issue.

**Script:**
"Would you be interested if this cost $10K?" (Likely: "No, I'd want it cheaper")

Now you know the range.

### The Teaching

"No" is more honest than "yes." People say "yes" to end conversation. "No" reveals truth.

## Identity-Based Closing (Cole Gordon)

Instead of asking if they want to do the program, ask about identity - who they want to be.

### Why Identity Matters

People will move toward aligning with their identity. Identity is one of the highest drivers of behavior (along with purpose).

### The Framework

Present two identity choices:

**Script:**
"So here's really what this comes down to. There's two ways this could go:

1. You're the person who keeps telling yourself you'll do it, but your actions don't align with your words. You stay in the same place for the next 15 years.

2. You're the person who realizes the pattern IS the problem. You metaphorically draw a line in the sand and say 'Enough is enough. I'm putting myself first.' You become someone who makes decisions out of vision, not circumstance.

Which person do you want to be moving forward?"

### Why This Works

- Moves from "Do you want this?" (features/benefits) to "Who do you want to be?" (identity)
- People are more motivated by identity than by outcomes
- Creates internal pressure to align actions with chosen identity

### When to Use It

When standard closing questions aren't working. When they're stuck on objections. Identity-based closing often breaks through.

## The Circumstances vs. Vision Reframe (Cole Gordon)

A powerful reframe for when they cite obstacles (money, timing, approval).

### The Script (Full Version)

"Can I be honest with you for a second? I get that financially/timing-wise it's [tight/unclear] right now. And I totally understand that. But here's what I want to challenge you on - if we make decisions based on our current circumstances, we're always gonna generate more of the same circumstances, right?

But when we make decisions out of a place of vision - out of what we actually want to create - we open ourselves up to new possibilities.

So the real question isn't 'Is now the right time with the money situation?' or 'Is now the right time scheduling-wise?'

The real question is: 'Who do you want to be and how do you want to make decisions about your [business/health/life] moving forward?'

Because there's two ways this could go:

1. You could be the type of person who keeps saying you're gonna do it, but your actions don't align with your words, and you stay in the same place for the next 15 years.

2. Or you could be the type of person who realizes that the pattern IS the problem, and you metaphorically draw a line in the sand and say 'Enough is enough. I'm putting myself first because I know that's the only way I can serve others at the highest level.'

Which type of person do you want to be moving forward?"

### Why This Works

- Reframes obstacle from "real problem" to "excuse"
- Moves from logical (can't afford) to emotional (who do I want to be)
- Creates identity alignment
- Leverages consistency bias

### Teaching

Once they choose identity #2, they'll find a way to afford it or make time for it. Circumstances shift when you shift identity.

## The Call Out Technique (Cole Gordon)

When a prospect contradicts themselves, call it out directly but with care.

### Why It Works

Leverages **consistency bias** - people experience internal pressure to align their words with their actions.

### The Setup

**Earlier in call:**
"This is a 10 out of 10. This will change my life. I'm ready to commit."

**At close:**
"I need to think about it / I don't have the money"

### The Script

"Help me understand - earlier you told me this was a 10 out of 10 and you said you were ready to commit. But now it seems like you're not even sure if you want to do this. So what changed? Because if the exact reason that brought you on this call is the exact reason keeping you from moving forward, then you're confusing the problem with the solution. And you're stuck, right?

So what's really going on?"

### Why This Works

The contradiction creates cognitive dissonance. They have to:
- Either justify the contradiction
- Or admit the real issue

Usually the real issue comes out: "Yeah, I guess I'm just scared it won't work"

Now you've got the REAL objection.

### Anti-Pattern

**DON'T be aggressive.** Use a curious, caring tone.

**DO use their exact words.** "You said... but now..."

## Reframing via Comparison (Luke Alexander)

When they raise an objection, reframe it by comparing to alternatives.

### The Structure

**The Objection:** "The price is too high"

**Reframe via Comparison:**
"So you're investing $X to stop losing $Y per month. Over a year, that's $12Y of gains for a $X investment. Compare that to [competitor] who charges $Z and doesn't deliver [key benefit]. Or do-it-yourself which costs you 6 months of wasted time. The ROI math actually makes this cheap."

### Why It Works

Prospect psychology: We perceive value through comparison.

No price exists in a vacuum. By comparing to alternatives (especially cost of inaction), your price suddenly feels like a bargain.

### Examples

**Fitness offer:**
"You're investing $2K for a transformation that keeps you healthy for life. Compare that to:
- $5K/year on medical costs if you stay unhealthy
- $500/month gym memberships you don't use
- Your quality of life

The $2K isn't expensive. It's cheap compared to the alternative."

**B2B offer:**
"You're investing $5K/month for process that generates $20K/month in new revenue. The cost of staying with your current broken system is $60K in lost revenue annually. We're not expensive; we're profitable."

## The Guarantee as Objection Killer (Luke Alexander)

A strong guarantee removes fear objections immediately.

### Why It Works

You're essentially saying: "I'm so confident this works, if it doesn't deliver, I'll fix it myself."

This reverses risk. They have nothing to lose.

### Guarantee Formulas

**90-Day ROI Guarantee:**
"If you go through the entire process and implement everything we teach, and don't see [specific result] in 90 days, we'll set up additional 1-on-1 calls to figure out what's off. With our students, we KNOW the system works. If it's not working, there's a specific reason. We'll find it and fix it."

**Satisfaction Guarantee:**
"If within [timeframe] you don't feel this was the best investment you made, full refund. No questions."

**Result Guarantee:**
"We guarantee you'll see [specific result] or your money back. That's how confident we are."

### Anti-Pattern

Vague guarantees don't work: "Money-back guarantee if not satisfied"

Strong guarantees are specific: "If you don't hit $10K/month revenue by day 90, we work 1-on-1 with you at no cost until you do."

## Finding the Fear (Oren Klaff - CODO)

When price is the objection, the real issue is often fear, not cost.

### The Framework

**Ask directly:**
"Let me ask you this - when you hear the price, what's the feeling that comes up?"

Usually they'll name the emotion:
- "Scared"
- "Uncertain"
- "Worried it won't work"
- "Concerned I'll fail"

### Why This Works

Price objection is usually FEAR disguised as money. Get them to name the fear, you can address it.

### The Follow-Up

Once they name the fear:
"So it's not really about the $X. It's about [fear]. Got it. Let me address that..."

Then provide social proof, testimonial, or personal credibility to overcome the specific fear.

**Example:**
**Prospect:** "The price seems too high"
**You:** "What feeling comes up when you hear it?"
**Prospect:** "Honestly? I'm scared I'll take the course and still not be able to sell"
**You:** "So it's not the price - it's the fear you won't succeed. Let me show you [testimonial from similar person who struggled, then succeeded]..."

Now you're addressing real objection.

## The Personal Vulnerability Move (Luke Alexander)

Share that you were scared/uncertain too and moved forward anyway.

### Why It Works

- Removes the "I'm above you / this is easy for me" energy
- Creates identification (you were like them)
- Demonstrates you succeeded despite fear
- Builds trust through vulnerability

### The Script

"So I actually went through this program myself a few years ago. Same position as you. When I heard the price I was like, 'Oh goodness, this is a lot.' And I was genuinely scared it wouldn't work. But I did it anyway. And here's what I realized..."

[Share the transformation]

"So I totally get the hesitation. But I also know what's on the other side. And I think you could be there too."

### Key Elements

- **Specific:** Share actual details, not generic story
- **Honest:** Admit you were scared
- **Resolved:** Show how fear wasn't accurate
- **Connected:** Draw line from your experience to theirs

## The "That's Not The Real Issue" Technique

When they keep repeating same objection, call it out.

### The Script

"I notice you keep coming back to [stated objection]. And I wonder if that's actually what's going on, or if there's something deeper? Because usually when someone keeps returning to the same thing, there's something else underneath."

### Why It Works

You're giving them permission to be honest. Often they'll admit the real issue:

"Yeah, I guess I'm just scared I can't do it"
"To be honest, I'm worried my partner will judge me"
"I'm concerned you're like every other [offer] I've tried"

Now you've got what's really blocking them.

## The Community/Support Reframe (Luke Alexander)

For people with unsupportive environments, position community as essential, not bonus.

### Why This Matters

For people escaping difficult situations, community replaces unsupportive family system. It's not a nice-to-have; it's survival.

### The Script

"Look, there might be people in your world who don't understand what you're doing. That's normal. But here's what you'll get with [community/program] - you'll have people on the exact same journey. When I had down days, what got me through wasn't just the material. It was having someone who could tell me how to change AND people supporting me on the journey.

That's the real power here. It's not just the course. It's that you're not doing this alone."

### Teaching

For the right person, community becomes the primary reason they buy, not the secondary benefit.

## The Pain-Based vs. Gain-Based Question (Luke Alexander)

When fear is present, ask pain-avoiding questions, not gain-focused questions.

### The Difference

**Gain-based:** "Which path gets you there fastest?" (Assumes motivation)
**Pain-based:** "Which path keeps you stuck?" (Addresses fear)

When someone is afraid, they're not motivated by gain. They're motivated by pain avoidance.

### Example

**Weak (gain-based):**
"Wouldn't it be great to have freedom and make $200K/year?"

**Strong (pain-based):**
"If nothing changes, you'll still be here in 5 years making what you're making now. Are you willing to accept that?"

### Application

When handling fear objections, ask:
- "What happens if you don't do this?"
- "How much longer can you stay stuck?"
- "What's the cost of staying where you are?"

Pain avoidance is more motivating than gain for fearful people.

## The Anti-Pattern Summary

**DON'T:**
- Get emotional or defensive
- Argue with their concerns
- Dismiss their fears as irrational
- Skip to price discussion
- Rely only on logical arguments
- Try too hard (sounds desperate)

**DO:**
- Acknowledge and validate
- Understand the underlying fear
- Use identity and emotional appeals
- Build certainty before price
- Combine logic with emotion
- Let them come to conclusion

The best objection handlers rarely "win" the argument. Instead, they guide the prospect to discovering the answer themselves.
