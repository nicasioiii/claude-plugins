# Diagnostic Frameworks - Finding the Real Objection

This file contains frameworks for diagnosing the REAL objection hiding behind surface-level concerns. Most stated objections are smoke screens. Use these diagnostic tools to find what's actually blocking the deal.

---

## The Belief Ladder Framework (Cole Gordon)

The Belief Ladder identifies exactly where a prospect's certainty breaks down. When someone's uncertain, run through this:

1. **Do they believe the PROBLEM is real?**
   - Can they articulate the pain they're experiencing?
   - Do they see it as costly?
   - "Do you agree this [problem] is real?"

2. **Do they believe a SOLUTION exists?**
   - Do they believe people can solve this type of problem?
   - "Do you think problems like this CAN be solved?"

3. **Do they believe THIS SOLUTION works?**
   - Have you shown proof through testimonials, case studies?
   - "Based on what you've heard about our approach, do you think it works?"

4. **Do they believe it works for PEOPLE LIKE THEM?**
   - Can they see themselves in success stories?
   - "Do you think someone in your situation could succeed with this?"

5. **Do they believe THEY can execute it?**
   - Do they have the discipline, capability, or resources?
   - "Do you think you could stick with this process?"

6. **Do they believe the TIMING is right?**
   - Is now the right time in their business/life?
   - "Is now a good time for you to make this move?"

7. **Do they believe it's WORTH the investment?**
   - Does the payoff justify the time and money?
   - "When you think about the return, does it feel worth it?"

### How to Use This Ladder

**During Discovery/Pre-Pitch:**
Find which rung they drop off on. That's where your focus needs to be.

If they drop on Rung 3 (doesn't believe solution works): You need more social proof, testimonials, case studies.

If they drop on Rung 4 (works but not for them): You need to get them identifying with similar success stories.

If they drop on Rung 5 (can't execute): You need to address support, accountability, and remove friction.

**Example Conversation:**
"So let me walk through this. You agree [problem] is real, right? And you know solutions exist for it. You've heard about our approach. The question is - do you see yourself succeeding with it? Because I'm wondering if maybe you're not 100% convinced you could stick with it. Is that fair?"

Often that's the real issue, not the stated objection.

### The Teaching

Most salespeople try to close before the prospect has climbed all 7 rungs. You present an offer and get pushback. You think it's price. Really, they're still uncertain on Rung 4.

**Your job in discovery/pre-pitch is to move them from Rung 1 → Rung 7 before you ever ask for money.**

## FEAR / MONEY / LOGISTICS Classification Framework

All objections reduce to three types. Classify correctly and you know how to respond.

### FEAR Objections
The prospect doubts whether this will work, whether it will work for THEM, or whether they can execute.

**Typical Language:**
- "I'm not sure if this will work"
- "What if I fail?"
- "I'm scared I'll waste money"
- "I don't know if I can stick with it"
- "I'm not convinced"
- "Seems too good to be true"

**Root Causes:**
- No personal reference point (never tried similar thing)
- Testimonials don't feel real
- Can't see themselves in success stories
- Past failures creating limiting beliefs

**How to Diagnose:**
Ask: "Do you think it's possible? Is it possible for YOU specifically? Would it be worth it if you could do it?"

If hesitation on any of these → It's FEAR, not money/logistics.

**How to Address FEAR:**
- Share testimonial videos (real people, not actors)
- Get them identifying with similar success stories
- Share community wins and proof
- Provide guarantee or risk reversal
- Personal credibility: "I was scared too, and here's what happened"
- Community/support as safety net

### MONEY Objections
The prospect says they can't afford it, price is too high, or ROI isn't clear.

**Typical Language:**
- "It's too expensive"
- "I don't have the budget"
- "Can I get a discount?"
- "The price seems high"
- "I need to check my budget"

**Root Causes:**
1. Actual budget constraint (legitimately can't pay right now)
2. Perceived value < cost (don't see how it's worth the price)
3. Fear disguised as money (they're really uncertain, using price as excuse)

**How to Diagnose (Critical):**
Ask: "Money aside, all that away - if I handed you the capital, would you do this?"

- If YES → Not really about money. It's FEAR or LOGISTICS
- If NO → Might be real, but likely combined with doubt

Ask: "What would feel like the right investment for [outcome]?"

Their answer reveals if it's truly budget or perceived value issue.

**How to Address MONEY:**
- Calculate cost of inaction vs. your investment
- Offer payment plans/installments
- Propose deposit (shows commitment, removes full upfront pain)
- Use contrast principle: "You're investing $X to stop losing $Y per month"
- Offer risk reversal (guarantee)
- Don't lower price; reframe value instead

### LOGISTICS Objections
The prospect has practical/process questions. They need clarity on HOW it works.

**Typical Language:**
- "How does this work exactly?"
- "When can we start?"
- "What's the timeline?"
- "What's included?"
- "How much time do I need to invest?"
- "Will I need to [specific concern]?"

**Root Causes:**
- Lack of clarity on process
- Uncertainty about implementation
- Practical concerns about execution
- Need to understand steps before committing

**How to Diagnose:**
Ask the specific question directly. Usually they'll tell you exactly what they need to know.

Pay attention to their tone - if it's genuinely curious (not skeptical), it's a logistics question, not fear.

**How to Address LOGISTICS:**
- Walk through process step-by-step
- Show timeline visually
- Answer the specific question clearly
- Reduce complexity: "You only need to do [X, Y, Z]"
- Provide SOP or process document
- Share examples of implementation

### Priority When Multiple Objections Present

If they give you 4 different concerns (common):

**Handle in this order:**
1. **FEAR first** - Removes emotion, builds confidence
2. **LOGISTICS second** - Builds clarity and removes uncertainty
3. **MONEY last** - Only after certainty/clarity is high

**Why this order?**
- Fear blocks everything. Until they believe it's possible and possible for them, nothing else matters
- Once fear is addressed, logistics questions are just logistical (easy to answer)
- Only address money after they're 100% certain on process. Money objections become irrelevant once certainty is high

**Example:**
Prospect lists: "I need to talk to my spouse, I'm not sure it will work, and the price is high"

- Handle spouse concern (LOGISTICS) + get them as ally
- Address "not sure it will work" with testimonials (FEAR)
- Get them past money concern through payment plan (MONEY)

Not: Jump to price negotiation first (common mistake).

## The Three Core Certainty Questions (Luke Alexander)

When a prospect is uncertain, narrow it down to three criteria:

### Question 1: "Do you think this has worked for other people?"
**Purpose:** Establish that the solution works in general

Usually they say YES because you've shown testimonials, case studies, community wins.

If they say NO: "What would prove to you that it works?"

### Question 2: "Is it possible for YOU specifically?"
**Purpose:** Get them to see themselves as capable

This is where most people hesitate. They see others' success but don't see themselves as "one of those people."

**How to overcome:**
- Share testimonial of someone similar to them
- Ask: "What's different about [success story] that means you COULDN'T do it?"
- Usually they admit: "Nothing, I guess I could"
- That admission moves them past this rung

### Question 3: "Is it worth it though?"
**Purpose:** Confirm ROI justifies time/money/effort

Even if they think it's possible, they might not think the payoff is worth the effort.

**How to overcome:**
- Help them articulate what "worth it" means to them
- Show: "You said your goal is [X]. This delivers [X]. Payoff = [stated goal]. Worth it?"
- Usually they agree

### The Teaching

"If they answer YES to all three, they're going to buy. If they say NO to any one of them, that's your real objection."

**Example:**
- They say: "I need to think about it"
- You ask these three questions
- They hesitate on #2: "Is it possible for me?"
- That's your real objection, not "thinking about it"

Now you address #2 (share similar success story, get them to admit they're capable).

## The Isolation Sequence (Luke Alexander)

When a prospect gives you an objection, use this sequence to isolate the REAL one:

### Step 1: Listen & Acknowledge
"Okay, I hear you. You're concerned about [stated objection]."

### Step 2: The First Isolation Question
"Besides [stated objection], is there anything else that's keeping you from being 100% certain about this?"

This often reveals a second, third, or fourth objection hiding behind the first.

### Step 3: Prioritize
If multiple objections:
- List them
- Classify each as FEAR, MONEY, or LOGISTICS
- Handle FEAR first
- Then LOGISTICS
- Then MONEY

### Step 4: Ask Deeper
"Help me understand - when you say [objection], what specifically do you mean?"

Sometimes "price is too high" really means "I don't think it will work for me."

### Example Isolation Sequence
**Prospect:** "I need to think about it"

**You (Step 2):** "Besides needing time to think, is there anything else that's keeping you from saying yes right now?"

**Prospect:** "Well, I'd have to talk to my spouse too"

**You:** "Got it. Besides talking to your spouse, anything else?"

**Prospect:** "I guess I'm also not 100% sure this will work for my situation"

**You:** "Okay so we've got three things - needing time to think, spouse approval, and doubt about whether it will work for your situation. Which one is the biggest blocker? Like if we could solve one, which matters most?"

**Prospect:** "Honestly the doubt about whether it will work. That's the big one."

**You:** "Perfect. Let me focus on that then." [Go back to social proof, testimonials, similar success stories]

## Smoke Screen Detection Framework

A smoke screen is a fake objection used to avoid the real objection. Common smoke screens:

### Common Smoke Screens

| Stated Objection | Often Covers | How to Flush |
|------------------|-------------|-------------|
| "I need to think about it" | Uncertainty or fear | Ask three questions + "What specifically?" |
| "Need to talk to spouse" | Their own doubt / Fear of decision | "What would THEY want to know?" |
| "Need to check budget" | Not convinced enough | "Money aside, would you do it?" |
| "Want to compare options" | Not certain you're best choice | "If we proved we were best, would you move forward?" |
| "Can I try it first?" | Fear of commitment | "What would the trial need to prove?" |
| "Not the right time" | Fear of timing or doubt | "If it was perfect timing, would you?" |

### How to Detect a Smoke Screen

A smoke screen usually has these characteristics:

1. **Vague** - "I need to think" is vague. Real objections are specific.
2. **Changeable** - You address it, then they mention a different one
3. **Repeating** - They keep coming back to the same objection even after you've addressed it
4. **Convenient** - It's always something preventing action right now

### How to Flush a Smoke Screen

**The Direct Question:**
"Help me be straight with you - is [stated objection] the real thing, or is there something else making you hesitate? Because I want to address what's actually blocking this."

Usually they'll admit the real objection.

**Example:**
They keep saying "I need to think about it" even after you address concerns.

You: "I notice every time we get close, you want more time. Is thinking about it the real thing, or are you just not 100% convinced this is for you? Because I want to know what's actually going on."

Often: "Yeah, I guess I'm just not sure it will work for me."

Now you've got the real objection.

## Temperature Check Framework (Before Price)

**Critical:** Get the temperature check BEFORE you introduce price.

### The Script
"Before we even talk about investment, I want to make sure we're aligned. So on a scale of 1-10, where 1 is you hate this and 10 is this is exactly what you need, where do you fall?"

### Why This Matters

**1. Creates an Anchor**
If they say 9-10 BEFORE hearing price, that becomes their anchor. When price comes and they drop to 7, you reference: "You just said this was exactly what you need."

**2. Temperature Reveals Readiness**
- 9-10: They're ready. Close now.
- 7-8: They're interested but have concerns. Address them.
- Below 7: Might not be fit. More discovery needed.

**3. Prevents "Wrong Objection" Handling**
If you don't get temperature check, you think they're uncertain on price. Really they're uncertain on process. You try to overcome price, they stay stuck.

### What to Do After Temperature Check

**If 9-10:**
"Great, so we're aligned. Let's talk about investment." [Proceed to close]

**If 7-8:**
"What would it take to move you from a 7 to a 9? What would need to be different?"

**If Below 7:**
"What would need to change for this to feel like the right fit? Because I want to make sure this is actually right for you before we move forward."

### Anti-Pattern

**DON'T ask temperature check AFTER price.** You lose the anchor and price becomes your lowest point of reference.

Wrong: Pitch → "How do you feel?" → They say 8 → "Price is $5K" → They drop to 4 → Now they're anchored to price

Right: Pitch → "How do you feel?" → They say 9 → "Price is $5K" → They might drop to 7 but reference: "You said 9 before" → They move back up to 8-9

## The Hypothetical Reframe (Luke Alexander)

Use a hypothetical to test if the stated objection is real:

### Structure
"Let me ask you a hypothetical - if I [removed the stated objection], would you move forward?"

**Examples:**

If they say "price is too high":
"If I handed you the capital tomorrow, would you do this?"
- YES → Not really about money
- NO → Might be about money, but likely combined with doubt

If they say "need to think about it":
"If you could think about it right now and come to a decision, would you move forward?"
- YES → They're ready, you can close today with permission
- NO → There's something else blocking it

If they say "need to talk to my spouse":
"If your spouse said 'Yes, let's do this' right now, would you?"
- YES → Spouse is the blocker (logistics)
- NO → It's them (fear)

### The Teaching

Hypotheticals force them to engage with the real decision, not hide behind the stated objection. Often reveals their true hesitation.

---

## Summary

**To find the real objection:**
1. Use the Belief Ladder to find where certainty breaks
2. Classify as FEAR, MONEY, or LOGISTICS
3. Use the three certainty questions
4. Run the isolation sequence to uncover hidden concerns
5. Flush smoke screens with direct questions
6. Use hypotheticals to test if stated objection is real
7. Get temperature check BEFORE price introduction

Most stated objections are symptoms. Your job is to find the root cause and address that.
