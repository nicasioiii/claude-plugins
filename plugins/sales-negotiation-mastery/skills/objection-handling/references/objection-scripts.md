# Objection Scripts Reference

## "I Need to Think About It" (INTAI)

**This is NEVER the real objection. It is always a smokescreen.** -- Alexander, Gordon

### Approach 1: Light (Alexander)
- "No worries, I totally understand. But just to make sure -- this is something you want to do, right?"
- "Okay, so if this is what you want to do, what's keeping you from moving forward?"
- Real objection emerges.

### Approach 2: Resistant Prospect -- Multiple Loops (Alexander)
1. Confirm desire: "You want to do this, right?" --> Yes
2. Confirm belief: "Do you think our program can help you reach your goal?" --> Yes
3. Confirm specifics: "What specifically about it do you think will help?" --> They name features
4. Reveal: "If you believe all of that, what's coming up that's making you hesitant?"
5. Real objection emerges: "It's a lot of money" or "I've never invested like this"

### Approach 3: Direct Leveling -- Last Resort (Alexander)
- "Listen, I totally understand. Between you and I, I know it's either one of a few things: you're fearful, you've never done this before, maybe you got burned, or it's about money. If you can be honest with me, I can help you figure this out. Which is it?"
- Only use with strong rapport. Can feel confrontational without it.

### Gordon's Pacing Approach (Preferred Default)
Do NOT ask "What do you want to think about?" (dead rebuttal).
Instead, pace the first objection:
1. "Let's pretend money is out of the equation. How do you feel about the process?"
2. Get tie-down on certainty
3. Use "How vs. Should" isolation
4. Real objection surfaces through pacing, not confrontation

## Fear/Uncertainty Objections

### The Bike Analogy (Alexander)
"Have you ever ridden a bike? Remember the first time -- you were terrified, right? Thought you'd crash. But you did it anyway, and now you ride just fine. If you never got on that bike, could you ride today? No. This is exactly the same."

### The Dating Analogy (Alexander -- for "burned in the past")
"Do you have a girlfriend/partner? Is she the first person you ever dated? So previous relationships didn't work out, but that didn't keep you from dating again. Why? Because you knew you'd never find the right person if you let one failure keep you out of the game. Business is the same way."

### The Driving Analogy (Alexander)
"First time you drove a car -- scared, right? Maybe even had an accident. But that didn't keep you from driving. Because you know you NEED to drive."

### Identity Frame Callback (Alexander)
Reference the identity they built during discovery:
"Remember the person we were talking about -- the one who's disciplined, pushes through fear, doesn't make excuses. Would that person let this happen again? Or would they make the decision?"

### Version 1 / Version 2 Frame (Alexander)
"The future version of yourself making [amount], living the life you described -- is that the kind of person who lets fear keep them from acting? Or the kind who makes decisions despite fear?"

### Circumstances vs. Vision Reframe (Gordon)
"Can I be honest? If we make decisions based on current circumstances, we generate more of the same. But when we make decisions out of vision -- out of what we actually want to create -- we open new possibilities. So the real question isn't whether it's the right time. It's: who do you want to be moving forward?"

### Labeling Fear (Voss)
Before any analogy or reframe:
- "It seems like this decision feels bigger than just the program."
- "It sounds like there's some real concern about whether this will work."
- Let the label work. Pause. Then apply the appropriate analogy.

## Money Objections

### Step 1: Diagnose Fear vs. Logistics (Alexander)
"If I came to you with a duffel bag of [price] in free cash right now, would you use it for this program?"
- Yes --> "Why would you spend it on this instead of an Xbox?" (recommits them)
- Then: "Be honest -- is this a situation where you've never spent this much on something like this, or is it that you truly don't have the funds?"

### If Fear-Based (Has Money, Scared to Spend)
Treat as fear objection. Use bike/dating/driving analogies above. The money is not the issue -- the fear of losing it is.

### If Logistical (Truly Doesn't Have It)

**Gordon's Financial Objection Process (after double tie-down):**

1. Open with: "Most clients take care of investment upfront. But for certain clients, I don't feel that's best based on their situation. We allow them to break it up."
2. "Since it's your GOAL to do this -- do you want to explore ways we could make this work?"
3. "Are you comfortable having an open and honest conversation about where you're at financially? Based on that, we'll figure out the best next step."

**The Open Wallet Trick (Gordon):**
- Get exact cash on hand (checking + savings)
- Credit score / available credit
- Money coming in within next 30 days
- If they share willingly AND still cannot afford = genuine condition
- If they WON'T share = trust issue (smokescreen for uncertainty)

**Payment plan options:** See `references/risk-mitigators.md` for full payment structuring.

### Ethical Line (Alexander)
If someone is a single parent with $500 -- do NOT try to sell them a high-risk program. Have morality.

## Spouse/Partner Objections

### Step 1: Isolate and Tie Down
"Aside from letting your spouse know, there's nothing else keeping you from being less than 100% certain?"
"Hypothetically -- if your spouse was on this call and said 'I believe in you, do whatever is best,' you'd be 100% in?"

### Step 2: Respect vs. Permission (Gordon -- Critical Distinction)
"Just for clarity: Is this a RESPECT thing where you're going to do this regardless, you just want to let them know beforehand? Or do you need their PERMISSION to move forward, meaning it's not ultimately your sole decision?"

99% say respect. This reframes the entire dynamic.

### Step 3: Information Gathering
- What do you think they'll think about this?
- What will they say about the investment?
- Are they supportive of you trying to fix [problem]?
- Are you on the same page that this is a problem?
- Do they know you're on this call?

### Step 4: The Two Guys Reframe (Gordon)
"There's two ways you can bring this up:

Guy #1: 'Hey honey, you know that business I've spent money on? Well I hopped on a call with this guy from the internet who might help, it's [price] -- what do you think?'

Guy #2: Sits down confidently and says, 'Babe, I know I've let you down with this and despite that you've supported me. I've DECIDED and COMMITTED to growing this. I've found someone to help. Before I move forward, I want your emotional support so we're in this together.'

Which guy is going to convince his spouse? Because if YOU'RE not certain, she won't be either."

### Step 5: Close Options (in order of aggression)
1. **$1K Deposit Close** (if "respect"): Refundable deposit to hold spot. Follow-up call where spouse can join.
2. **CC Close** (if deposit won't work): Take card number but don't run it. Acts as commitment device.
3. **Follow-Up Close** (if nothing will work now): Set follow-up call, use follow-up language.

### B2B Partner Objections
"Totally understand. What time is your partner available? I'd hate for you to relay this and something gets lost. Let's all hop on together." Book the three-way call.

## Burned in Past Objections

### Core Principle
This is a trust/solution belief issue. They are not objecting to YOUR offer -- they are protecting themselves from past pain.

### Resolution Process
1. Label it (Voss): "It sounds like you've been through this before and it didn't go well."
2. Dig into specifics: "What did you try? What went wrong? What was missing?"
3. Position your solution as the explanation of WHY past attempts failed: "The reason [past approach] didn't work is [gap]. That's exactly what we do differently."
4. Use the dating analogy if needed.
5. If still resistant, offer risk mitigator (7-day refund works well here).

### The Accusation Audit (Voss -- Particularly Effective Here)
"I know what you might be thinking: 'Here's another coach/program making promises. I've heard it all before. Why should this be any different?' Let me address that directly..."

## Time Objections

### Devil's Advocate Technique (Alexander)
- "Why is this important NOW rather than in 6 months?"
- If they give weak answer, push: "Why not just wait?"
- They'll give you their own urgency: "Because I'm sick of [pain]"
- Lock that in and loop back if needed

### Urgency Amplification
- "What specifically makes transitioning into this important NOW?"
- "What happens if you decide not to change anything for another 6 months? Would you be satisfied?"
- "The same pain you feel today -- do you think it's going to be the same 3 years from now, or worse?"

### Compounding Frame (Alexander)
"If you're thirsty today and don't drink water, you're even more thirsty tomorrow. The pain compounds."

## The Call-Out Technique (For Contradictions)

When prospect contradicts themselves between discovery and close:

"Help me understand -- earlier you said this was exactly what you needed. You told me you were ready. But now it sounds different. What changed? Because if the pattern of hesitating is the problem, that pattern is what's keeping you stuck."

Leverages consistency bias. Use caring tone, not accusatory.

## Putting Responsibility on the Prospect

Final technique when all else has been applied:

- "Whose responsibility is it to decide whether you get to [goal] or stay exactly where you are?"
- "I can't make the choice for you. I can only help show you the way once you've decided."

This is leadership, not pressure. It invokes the identity frame.
