# Pitch & Presentation

## Why Pitch Structure Matters

The offer is 75% of the success or failure of any sales call (Gordon). Yet 80% of whether a prospect buys is determined BEFORE you pitch -- through discovery and the pre-pitch transition. These are not contradictory. Discovery is the time investment that makes the pitch land. But if your pitch is poorly structured, even perfect discovery cannot save it.

**The data pattern across all four instructors:**
- Pitches delivered without discovery backing: 10-15% close rate
- Pitches delivered after strong discovery + pre-pitch transition: 50-70% close rate
- The difference is not the offer -- it is the frame you built

**Two Sales in Every Call (Gordon):**
1. **Sale of the Method** -- They buy into your approach/thesis (e.g., "funnels are the key")
2. **Sale of the Product** -- They buy your specific solution (e.g., your funnel-building service)

Formula: "The key to [desire] is [method] and the only way to do [method] properly is [your product]."

## The Transition: From Discovery to Pitch

The transition bridges emotional discovery into logical presentation. This is where most salespeople fail. Without a bridge, there is an abrupt tonal shift that kills emotional investment, skepticism returns, and you sound like a salesman instead of a guide.

### The Super Basic Transition (Gordon -- Recommended for All)

**Script:**
"Well look, I don't feel like I have any more questions. Is there anything else you feel like we haven't covered -- about your goals, your business, areas you need help -- anything?"

[They respond]

"Well [NAME], based on what you told me previously, we can definitely help. [Optional: Insert one thought -- case study, personal story, or insight on the WHAT not the HOW] That said, where do you think we should go from here? I can walk you through the entire process A to Z of how we'd help you get to [OUTCOME] if you would like -- but you tell me where you wanna go."

**Why this works:**
1. Replaces the traditional long recap (which feels salesy and performative)
2. Gets PERMISSION to pitch (they ask YOU for it)
3. The Sandwich Technique: "Where do you want to go?" + [suggestion] + "You tell me where you want to go" -- whatever you sandwich in the middle, they take EVERY time
4. Uses certainty language: "we can DEFINITELY help" (lean toward certainty for 99% of markets)

### The Pre-Pitch Method (Gordon -- For Complex or Sophisticated Markets)

A language pattern used as the transition between discovery and offer. Also called the "tempo change" when the prospect starts asking YOU questions.

**Six Benefits:**
1. Builds credibility -- front-loads proof so the pitch is received as true
2. Builds authority through education -- genuine value creates trusted authority
3. Helps prospect understand ROOT of their problem -- differentiates from competition
4. Anchors cost -- subtly associates cost of NOT changing before the pitch
5. Paints a clear bridge -- builds confidence in the path from A to B
6. Re-engages emotion -- after discovery, prospects often shift to logical brain. This pulls them back

**Pre-Pitch Flow:**
1. **Permission** -- "Are you open to some honest feedback?"
2. **Credibility Stack** -- Proof before claims: "Working with 1000s of clients, $1.5M generated last month"
3. **Root Problem** -- "Your real problem isn't [symptom], it's [root cause]"
4. **Reason Why** -- Chain the consequences, show the domino effect
5. **Cost Anchor** -- "My fear for you is [negative future]. Which is opposite of what you said you want"
6. **Insight & Proof** -- "What I'd do is focus on [solution], which gives you [desired outcome]"
7. **Chunk Up/Down** -- Make the goal feel achievable: "You only need 4 clients at $5K/month"
8. **Pass Control** -- "Would you want to explore what this looks like?"

**Timing:** 5-10 minutes. Keep it tight.

### Alexander's Permission-Based Transition

Simpler and more direct:
- **Method A (Prospect advances):** They ask "How does this work?" or "How much is it?" -- go directly into offer
- **Method B (You advance):** "Based on everything you've told me, I think we can definitely help you. Is it cool if I show you how we do that?"

They say yes -- you now have permission to pitch.

### The Sophisticated Market Frame (Gordon)

For markets where prospects have heard many pitches before:
"I'm definitely prepared to share with you what we have going on. Everything we do is customized. So instead of a long laundry list, what's more appropriate is if you could first help me get some context. Based on that, I can share only the aspects most relevant to you specifically."

**The Opposing Agenda Principle (Gordon):** The prospect wants information without deciding. You want a decision. The more information you disclose, the less leverage you have. Control the flow. If prospect clearly will not close on call 1 (trust issue), pivot to two-call close -- withhold pitch, give homework, book second call.

## The Pitch Codex System (Gordon)

### Three Parts of the Pitch

**Part 1: High-Level Promise (1-2 sentences)**

Formula: "[Number] steps to get you from [current situation] to [desired situation] in [timeframe]"

Example: "4 steps to get you from zero control on your sales calls to a consistent 30% closing ratio in about 8 weeks."

Key elements: Specific outcome + specific time = tangibility. Customize to each prospect using their discovery language.

**Part 2: The Bridge (3-5 Pillars)**

The MAJORITY of your pitch. Logically takes prospect from current to desired situation.

The pitch should simultaneously explain why everything they tried in the past has failed AND why this is going to be different. You do NOT need fancy proprietary names -- the explanation itself IS the unique mechanism.

**How to present each pillar:**
"So the first thing we're going to do is [PILLAR NAME]. You know how earlier you mentioned [THEIR SPECIFIC PAIN POINT]? In this stage what we do is [EXPLANATION]. [Tie to their specific situation/outcome]. [Future pace: what life looks like after this pillar]. Does that make sense? Questions? Or ready to move on?"

**Pillar construction rules:**
- Weave the prospect's own language into each pillar
- Tie features to outcomes (especially certainty and significance -- see `sales-psychology`)
- Anchor cost subtly: "Instead of spending that time exhausted on calls, you can..."
- Eliminate fear of success by solving future problems they have not had yet
- Each pillar should earn a "yeah, that makes perfect sense" response

**Part 3: Delivery**

How the program is fulfilled (training platform, coaching calls, 1-on-1 access, timeline). Keep it simple, do not get into the weeds. End with: "What questions do you have about the entire process, in totality?"

## The Analogy Pitch (Alexander)

Do NOT list features. Use analogies and simple language to show you have the missing piece.

**Core technique:**
- "If you had to learn basketball, what works better -- going to the gym alone watching YouTube, or having LeBron coach you?"
- Use analogies from THEIR life experiences mentioned during discovery (basketball, driving, swimming, dating)
- Active listening during discovery creates natural analogy material

**The thesis frame:** Your pitch is a thesis statement for why they have not succeeded yet. The offer as bridge: "What we do is [simple summary]. We show you [outcome] through [mechanism]. So you're not lost, you have support, and you actually get to [goal]."

**Key principle:** "If you say it, it's shit. If they say it, it's true" (Gordon). The pitch is NOT you telling them what they need. The pitch is THEM having said "I haven't had this because of that" and you saying "that's exactly what we do."

## Pitch Delivery Principles

### Engagement Check-Ins (Every 30-45 Seconds)

Never talk for more than 45 seconds without a check-in. Beyond that, they are mentally gone.

- "Does that make sense?"
- "Are you sure?" (stronger than "does that make sense")
- "What are your thoughts on that specifically?"
- "Based on what you tried in the past, do you feel this might be what's been missing?"

These are NOT rhetorical. You need genuine answers to measure certainty.

### Tonality During Pitch

- Conversational, not rehearsed
- Confident, not cocky
- Genuinely curious about their reactions
- Paced for dialogue, not lecture
- Keep emotional connection from discovery alive (do not flip to pure logic)

**Cross-reference:** See `tonality-delivery` for the full voice system (three rapport levels, inflection types, vocal dominance tells).

### Handling Clarifying Questions Mid-Pitch (Gordon)

Use clarifying questions BEFORE answering program questions. Never answer at face value.

**Wrong way:** Prospect asks "Do you do group coaching?" -- you say "Yes, 2 calls per week" -- they say "I had a bad experience with group coaching" -- now you are fighting uphill.

**Right way:** "Good question -- I'm curious, did you ask me that for a reason?" They reveal the REAL concern behind the question. Now you address the actual concern, not the surface question.

**Clarifying/diffusing language:**
- "Well, it depends..."
- "Generally, yes -- but..."
- "I'm curious, did you ask me that for a reason?"
- "When you say ___, what do you mean exactly?"

## The Alignment Check (Before Any Close Attempt)

NEVER discuss investment until you have verified certainty on the solution.

**Temperature Check (Gordon):**
"Just curious, in terms of the process specifically -- how do you feel?"

- Certain answer + certain tonality = move to closing (see `closing-techniques`)
- Uncertain answer OR uncertain tonality = Alignment script:

"What's really important to me is ALIGNMENT -- when you come in and work with us, we're rolling up our sleeves. Our team is ALL IN -- so it's really important that you feel GOOD about the process. On a scale of 1-10, where do you fall?"

- 9-10 + certain tonality = proceed
- 8 or below = "What exactly is keeping you from being a 9 or 10?" Handle, re-temp, confirm

**Alexander's Commitment Check:**
"Based on everything we talked about, do you think [offer] is the best thing to get you to [goal]? What makes you say that?"

If uncertain language ("yeah, I guess so"): use the 1-10 frame. Handle whatever they reveal BEFORE dropping price.

**Cross-reference:** After alignment check, transition to `closing-techniques` for investment drop and commitment process.

## The 2-3 Minute Pitch (High-Ticket Best Practice)

For qualified prospects after strong discovery:
- **0:00-0:30** -- Frame the three pillars (what you will cover)
- **0:30-1:30** -- Deep dive on Pillar 1 with their words, tied to pain
- **1:30-2:00** -- Quick overview Pillars 2 & 3 (do not over-explain)
- **2:00-2:15** -- Chunk up/down ("You only need X")
- **2:15-2:30** -- Alignment check ("How does that land?")

Short, punchy, with built-in dialogue. If they have questions, let them ask. Do not preemptively over-explain.

## Multi-Stakeholder Presentations

### When Spouse/Partner Is Present

**During discovery:** Still ask discovery questions to primary prospect. Include partner: "Would you add anything to that?" Get both perspectives.

**Why:** Prevents the later "I need to talk to my spouse" objection. Surface concerns early.

### Spouse Follow-Up Call (If Not on First Call)

Frame strategically: "This is something [primary prospect] wants to do. We've already discussed why it's a good fit. Now we're going to present this together."

This positions you and the primary prospect as ONE TEAM presenting to spouse, not you versus the couple.

### Committee / Group Presentations (Serhant)

Use the Funnel Technique to understand each stakeholder's motivations individually:
- Top: "What are you looking for?" (stated wants)
- Middle: "Where would you like to be?" (desired outcome)
- Bottom: "What would make you comfortable?" (fears and objections)

Map wants, vision, and comfort level for each stakeholder. Find overlapping interests.

**Cross-reference:** For multi-party negotiation dynamics, see `negotiation-tactics` (Game Theory, Interests vs. Positions). For champion-building, see `closing-techniques`.

## Common Anti-Patterns

1. **Pitching before certainty on process** -- You CANNOT overcome financial objections if they are not 8-9/10 on the solution
2. **No specific numbers** -- Vague benefits do not stick. "$240K/year" anchors differently than "more money"
3. **Not using their exact words** -- Generic benefits get ignored. Reference what THEY said
4. **Over-explaining features** -- Feature + benefit + brief why = enough
5. **Abandoning emotion in pitch** -- You built urgency in discovery; do not flip to pure logic
6. **Long recap before pitch** -- The traditional recap feels salesy and performative. Use the Super Basic Transition instead
7. **Giving all information at once** -- If prospect will not close on call 1, withhold payment plans for follow-up (creates open loop)
8. **Not closing the pitch** -- End with alignment check, not "any questions?"

## Instructor Disagreement

**Scripts vs. improv (Serhant vs. Voss/Gordon):** Serhant says scripts are dead and improv is the most important skill. Voss teaches precise scripted tools (Ackerman, calibrated questions). Gordon says drill scripts until natural. **Resolution:** Scripts are training wheels that become internalized playbooks that eventually flow as natural improv. Drill scripts (Gordon) until they flow naturally (Serhant). Study actors for range, sales drills for precision (Alexander).

## Key Metrics

- **Pitch duration:** 5-15 minutes (shorter is better for qualified prospects)
- **Pillar count:** 3-5 pillars maximum
- **Check-in frequency:** Every 30-45 seconds
- **Alignment threshold:** 8/10 or above before discussing investment
- **Transition time:** Super Basic Transition = 2-3 minutes; Pre-Pitch Method = 5-10 minutes
