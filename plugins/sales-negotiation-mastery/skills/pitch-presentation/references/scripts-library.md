# Scripts & Talk Tracks Library

## Pre-Pitch Transition Scripts (Use These Verbatim)

### Opening Permission
**Script:**
"Are you open to some honest, genuine feedback on where you're at right now in your business?"

**Response Expected:**
"Absolutely, that's why I'm on the call."

**Why it works:**
Converts potential criticism into consensual feedback. They've agreed to listen.

---

### Credibility Stack (Build Before Claims)
**Script:**
"And I want to let you know this isn't coming from esoteric theory, but from working with thousands of clients, having over a thousand case studies, and clients who reported $1.5M in revenue generated just last month. I'm telling you that not to impress you, but to impress upon you that this is coming from actually working in the trenches right now, not just my random opinion on what I think you should do. Does that make sense?"

**Key elements:**
- Numbers first (proof before claims)
- "Not to impress you, but to impress upon you" (removes arrogance)
- Ends with easy yes question

---

### Root Problem Identification
**Script:**
"I totally get that you feel like your problem is [their stated symptom]. But what I want to challenge you on is that your real problem is really [root problem]. See, the biggest challenge with [their situation] is [reason]. So what that means for you is [chain of consequences]. Does that make sense? Do you agree?"

**Framework:**
1. Validate their symptom (don't dismiss it)
2. Introduce root problem gently ("what I want to challenge you on")
3. Educate why it's the real issue
4. Show consequences
5. Get double agreement (make sense + agree?)

**Example (Agency):**
"I totally get you think your problem is 'Facebook ads aren't working.' But the real issue is your offer isn't differentiated. Here's why: in a high-opportunity, low-barrier market, undifferentiated offers get commoditized. Commoditized offers compete on price, which kills margins, which means you can't scale. That's the chain. Does that make sense? Do you agree with that?"

---

### Cost Anchoring (Logically)
**Script:**
"So my biggest fear for you is that [negative future outcome tied to what they said they value]. Which is the opposite of [what they said they want], isn't it? Does that make sense?"

**Framework:**
1. "My biggest fear for you..."
2. Specific dark future (not vague)
3. Tie to their stated values (they said they want X)
4. Easy yes question

**Example (Coach wanting more freedom):**
"My biggest fear for you is that you build a business to $15K a month, but in doing that you create a glorified job—not the freedom-based business that's the opposite of what you said you wanted. In 5-10 years you're still trading time for money. Which is the opposite of what you said you wanted, isn't it? Does that make sense?"

---

### Insight and Proof (The Answer)
**Script:**
"What I would do if I was you, based on working with hundreds of successful clients, is [root solution]. [Benefit chain]. And you know what that means? That gives you [their desired outcome]. Make sense?"

**Framework:**
1. "What I would do if I was you..."
2. Reference credibility (hundreds of successful clients)
3. State the root solution
4. Show benefit chain (leads to → leads to →)
5. Tie to their desired outcome

**Example:**
"What I would do, based on working with hundreds of successful agency owners, is focus on positioning your offer first—making it differentiated and premium. Which allows you to attract ideal clients instead of tire-kickers. Which allows you to charge premium prices. Which gives you the margins to hire a team and scale without being a bottleneck. Which is the freedom and business ownership you said you wanted. Make sense?"

---

### Future Pace With Your Solution
**Script:**
"And as you start to think about [their desired future with specifics], what you're gonna realize, especially coming in and working with us, is the more we focus on [root problem/pillar 1], the more [desired outcome] naturally falls into place as a byproduct."

**Framework:**
1. "As you start to think about..."
2. Be specific about their desired future (don't be vague)
3. "What you'll realize working with us..."
4. "The more we focus on [root problem]..."
5. "The more [other benefits] naturally falls into place"

**Example:**
"And as you start to think about building a business that gives you control, freedom, and lets you send your kids to private school and actually retire in 10 years, what you'll realize, especially coming in and working with us, is the more we focus on dialing in your offer and positioning, the more leverage, team multiplication, and that freedom naturally falls into place as a byproduct. You know what I mean?"

---

### Chunk Up and Chunk Down
**Script:**
"The real beauty is, to get to $240K a year with a well-put-together offer, you really only need 4 clients paying $5K a month. That's it. And if you're closing 20% of conversations, you only need 5 conversations a month to get there."

**Framework:**
1. Chunk up (show the big number)
2. Chunk down (break it into small pieces)
3. Keep chunking down until it feels achievable
4. Simplifies the goal

**More examples:**
- "To hit 50 leads per week, you really just need a $500/month ad spend in 3 targeted places"
- "To lose 30 pounds, you really just need 3 workouts per week and 80% nutrition compliance"
- "To add $100K to your business, you really just need 10 new clients at $10K each"

---

### Pass Control (They Ask About the Offer)
**Script:**
"Would you be open to me walking you through what that process looks like?"

or

"Would you want to explore what this could look like for your business?"

or

"Should we go deeper into how we'd structure this for you?"

**Key:** Use "would you" not "would you like to" (stronger)

## The Pitch Scripts (3-Part Framework)

### Pillar 1: Problem + Solution
**Script:**
"So the first thing is [specific solution to their stated pain]. Here's why that matters—[reason why it's the real solution]. Because [connection to what they said they wanted], you get [immediate benefit]."

**Framework:**
1. State feature (what you do)
2. Frame with their words (not generic)
3. Reason why it matters (not why it's cool)
4. Show immediate benefit
5. End with engagement question

**Example (Fitness):**
"The first thing is you learn how to eat the foods you love without sabotaging your results. Here's why that matters—because you said the last diet failed because it was too restrictive. And compliance is what determines weight loss success. So you get the sustainability you need plus the food freedom you actually want."

**Example (B2B):**
"The first thing is we position your offer using our proprietary framework. Here's why that matters—you said your ads aren't getting clients, but the real issue is your offer looks like everyone else's. Once your offer stands out, your marketing becomes 10x more effective. You're not competing on price anymore."

---

### Pillar 2: Support System
**Script:**
"The second thing is [support element—what they get]. That's critical because [why it's needed]. Which means [benefit of having support]."

**Framework:**
1. Describe what support looks like
2. Explain why it's needed (what was missing from past attempts)
3. Show the benefit (what it prevents or enables)

**Example (Coaching):**
"The second thing is you get direct access to me for weekly strategic calls plus mid-week and end-of-week touchpoints. That's critical because what we found is support and accountability are completely missing from online programs—people get the information but have nobody to keep them on track. Which means you don't fall off the wagon like you have before."

**Example (SaaS):**
"The second thing is you get onboarding support and dedicated account management for the first 90 days. That's critical because most software doesn't stick if people don't know how to actually use it. Which means you see results fast instead of abandoning the tool."

---

### Pillar 3: Knowledge Transfer / Independence
**Script:**
"And the third pillar is teaching you [what they learn—the principles], not just giving you [the solution]. That's important because [why independence matters]. Which means [benefit of knowing how to do it themselves]."

**Framework:**
1. What they'll learn (principles, frameworks, knowledge)
2. Why it matters (independence, sustainability, future-proofing)
3. Benefit (they're not dependent on you)

**Example (Coaching):**
"And the third pillar is teaching you the principles behind positioning and messaging, not just giving you a done-for-you offer. That's important because you need to be able to apply this to future offers and evolve as your market changes. Which means in 90 days you don't need me anymore—you have the knowledge to position any offer yourself."

**Example (Fitness):**
"And the third pillar is teaching you the principles behind nutrition and training, not just giving you a meal plan and workout. That's important because after working with me, you need to know how to do this independently. Which means you won't gain the weight back because you know how to manage it yourself."

## Alignment Check Scripts

### Standard Alignment Check
**Script:**
"So before we even talk about investment, I just want to make sure we're on the same page. How do you feel about the process?"

**Listen for:** Enthusiastic response. If anything less:

### 1-10 Scale Deep Dive
**Script:**
"On a scale of 1-10, where 1 is you hate this and 10 is this is exactly what you need, where would you fall?"

**If they say 7 or below:**
"What would it take to move you to a 9 or 10?"

**Listen:** For the real objection. They'll now reveal it.

**Example responses:**
- "I'm at a 7 because I'm not sure if I can stick with it"
- "I'm at a 6 because I want to know more about the payment options"
- "I'm at a 5 because I've tried similar things before"

**Your next move:** Handle the specific objection they revealed, then come back to the 1-10 scale.

---

### Double Tie-Down Pattern (Use During Transition)
**Script:**
"Does that make sense?"
[They say yes]
"Do you agree with that?"
[They have to actually think]

**Why both:** First is politeness ("yeah, sure"). Second creates real mental agreement.

## Objection Handling Scripts

### The "I Need to Think About It" Response
**Script:**
"I appreciate that, and I think that's smart to think things through. But help me understand—what specifically do you want to think about?"

**They'll reveal the REAL objection:**
- "I want to make sure I can afford it"
- "I'm not sure if this will actually work"
- "I need to talk to my spouse"

**Then address that specific objection.**

---

### "I've Been Burned in the Past"
**Script:**
"Yeah, I've heard that a lot. Here's the thing—most of those solutions were doing [what failed for them]. We do the opposite—we focus on [different approach]. Here's a case study of someone who had the exact same experience you did..."

**Framework:**
1. Normalize ("a lot of people have this story")
2. Differentiate ("but here's how we're different")
3. Provide proof (case study of similar situation)
4. Lower risk ("we also offer [guarantee/refund policy]")

---

### "This is Expensive"
**Script:**
"I get that. So let's just look at it real quick. You're investing $5,000 to generate $240K in annual revenue. Or think about it this way—you only need 4 clients at $5K a month. Does that investment feel different when you frame it that way?"

**Framework:**
1. Don't defend the price immediately
2. Reframe against outcome (ROI)
3. Chunk down the commitment
4. Check if reframing worked

---

### The Circumstances vs. Vision Reframe (Advanced)
**Script:**
"Can I be honest with you for a second? I get that financially it's tight right now. And I totally understand that. But here's what I want to challenge you on—if we make decisions based on our current circumstances, we're always gonna generate more of the same circumstances, right?

But when we make decisions out of a place of vision—out of what we actually want to create—we open ourselves up to new possibilities.

So the real question isn't 'Is now the right time with the money situation.' The real question is 'Who do you want to be and how do you want to make decisions about your [business/health/life] moving forward?'

Because there's two ways this could go:
1. You could be the type of person who keeps saying you're gonna do it, but your actions don't align with your words, and you stay in the same place for the next 15 years.
2. Or you could be the type of person who realizes that the pattern IS the problem, and you metaphorically draw a line in the sand and say 'Enough is enough. I'm putting myself first because that's the only way I can serve others at the highest level.'

Which type of person do you want to be moving forward?"

---

### The Call-Out (Consistency Challenge)
**Script:**
"Earlier you told me this was a 10 out of 10. You said this is exactly what you needed to change. You said you're ready to commit. But now it seems like you're not even sure if you want to do this. So help me understand—what changed? Because if the exact reason that brought you on this call is the exact reason that's keeping you from moving forward, then you're confusing the problem with the solution. And you're stuck, right? So what's really going on?"

**When to use:** When they contradict themselves (said it was perfect, now have objections)

**Why it works:** Consistency bias—internal pressure to align actions with stated words.

## Multi-Stakeholder Scripts

### When Spouse/Partner Is on the Call
**Script:**
"And [partner name], would you add anything to that?"

or

"[Partner name], what about in your case, what are your thoughts on that?"

or

"[Partner name], if you think about this—what does financially feel right for you, or what are your goals here?"

**Why:** Gets both perspectives, surfaces concerns early, prevents later "I need to talk to my spouse" objection.

---

### Spouse Follow-Up Call Framing
**Script:**
"Hey, this is something that [primary prospect name] wants to do. We've already had a conversation talking about why it's a good fit for them. And now we're going to present this to them together."

**Why this works:** Frames you and primary prospect as ONE TEAM presenting to spouse, not a salesman vs. a couple.

---

### Spouse-Focused Questions (Second Call)
**Script:**
"So [spouse], what do you feel like are the important things that would be important to you? And why has it been important for [primary] to change? And how have you seen [primary] go through this type of process before?"

**Three-part question structure:**
1. "What's important to you?"
2. "Why is change important to you?"
3. "What's your experience seeing [primary] change in the past?"

---

### The Spouse Deflection (When They Say "I Need to Talk to My Spouse" at Close)
**Script:**
"Yeah, I get it. I mean, you don't want to end up on the couch tonight, right? [Laugh] That makes sense. But here's the thing—if you go through that process and talk to [spouse], and they agree this is what you need to do, then the question is just payment options. Sound fair?"

**Framework:**
1. Validate (humor helps)
2. Show understanding
3. Repitch/close again
4. If they still insist: accept gracefully and set second call with both

## Payment & Investment Scripts

### The Payment Plan (Only Use After Certainty)
**Script:**
"Look, I don't expect everybody to pay the whole thing up front. I totally get it. So what I do is require a minimum deposit—in this case, $500—which holds your spot and shows me you're serious. The balance can be figured out over time in installments. Does that work for you?"

**Important:** Only offer payment plans if they said "I'm all in, I just don't have the cash."

---

### When They're Undecided on Payment Amount
**Script:**
"So most of my clients don't pay all upfront. They do a deposit plus installments. You're thinking deposit of $300, then $400 per month? Or would you prefer $500 deposit and two larger payments? Let me know what actually feels doable for you."

**Key:** YOU propose options. Don't ask "what works for you?"

---

### The Budget Qualifier (Early, If Needed)
**Script:**
"Just so we're on the same page—the investment for what we're talking about is around $5,000. Is that something you'd be open to investing, or should we talk about scaled-down options?"

**Use:** Early if unclear they can afford it. Better to know upfront.

---

## Closing Scripts

### Assumptive Close (After Full Alignment)
**Script:**
"Great, let's get you set up. I'll send you over a form to fill out, and we'll get you access to [product/service] by [timeline]. Sound good?"

**Key:** Assume yes. Use "let's" and "we'll" not "would you like to?"

---

### Soft Close (Less Certainty)
**Script:**
"So what do you think—should we move forward with this?"

or

"Ready to make this happen?"

---

### The Question Close (Open-Ended)
**Script:**
"So what are you thinking? How do you want to proceed?"

**Listen:** For their answer. They'll tell you if they're ready.

## Question Library (Use Throughout)

### Discovery Questions
- "Tell me about your current situation with [area]"
- "What's been the biggest challenge?"
- "What have you tried so far?"
- "Why didn't that work the way you wanted?"
- "What would the ideal situation look like?"
- "If we could solve this, what would change for you?"

### Engagement Questions (Mid-Pitch)
- "Does that make sense?"
- "What are your thoughts so far?"
- "How's that landing?"
- "Can you see how this would work for you?"
- "Based on what you've tried before, does this feel different?"

### Probing Questions
- "What do you mean specifically?"
- "Can you give me an example?"
- "What else?"
- "How does that show up for you?"
- "What's the impact of that?"

### Objection-Surfacing Questions
- "Besides that, is there anything else keeping you from moving forward?"
- "What would need to be true for this to work?"
- "What's your biggest concern?"
- "Help me understand what's holding you back"

### Close Questions
- "How do you feel about the process?"
- "Are you ready to get started?"
- "Should we move forward?"
- "What do you think?"
