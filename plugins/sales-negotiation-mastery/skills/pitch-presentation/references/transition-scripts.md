# Transition Scripts & Opposing Agenda Tactics

## The Super Basic Transition (Gordon)

The recommended transition for all markets and experience levels.

### Full Script

**Step 1 -- Close Discovery:**
"Well look, I don't feel like I have any more questions. Is there anything else you feel like we haven't covered -- about your goals, your business, areas you need help -- anything we haven't covered?"

[Let them respond. If they add something, address it briefly.]

**Step 2 -- Bridge:**
"Well [NAME], based on what you told me previously, we can definitely help."

**Step 3 -- Optional Thought Insert:**
Choose ONE:
- Quick case study (2-3 sentences max): "We actually just worked with someone in a similar situation who..."
- Personal story of overcoming similar challenge
- Insight on the WHAT (not the HOW) -- open a loop without closing it

**Step 4 -- The Sandwich:**
"That said, where do you think we should go from here? I can walk you through the entire process A to Z of how we'd help you get to [OUTCOME] if you would like -- but you tell me where you wanna go."

### Why Each Element Works

1. **"I don't feel like I have any more questions"** -- signals you have done thorough diagnosis
2. **"Is there anything else?"** -- ensures nothing is left unsaid (prevents later objections)
3. **"We can DEFINITELY help"** -- certainty language (lean toward certainty for 99% of markets)
4. **The sandwich:** "Where do you want to go?" + [your suggestion] + "You tell me" -- whatever is sandwiched in the middle, they take EVERY time
5. **Permission:** They ask YOU for the pitch, not the other way around

### What NOT to Do

- Do NOT do a long 5-minute recap of everything they said (feels salesy, performative)
- Do NOT say "I think we might be able to help" (weak, uncertain)
- Do NOT jump straight from discovery into "So here's what we offer" (no bridge = jarring)

## The Sophisticated Market Frame Script (Gordon)

For prospects who have heard many pitches and are skeptical of sales processes.

### Full Script

"I'm definitely prepared to share with you a little bit of what we have going on over here. Now, everything we do is all customized. So opposed to going through a long laundry list of every little different thing we could do, what's probably going to be more appropriate is if you could first help me get some context on your business and what you're trying to do. Based on that, then I can share with you only the aspects of what we do that would be most relevant and useful to you specifically. Does that make sense?"

### When to Use

- Experienced buyers who have seen many presentations
- High-ticket B2B where the prospect is a sophisticated operator
- Markets with heavy competition and pitch fatigue
- When you sense the prospect is bracing for a sales pitch

### Why It Works

- Positions you as customized, not cookie-cutter
- Gives THEM control of what information they receive
- Sets the frame that your pitch will be tailored, not generic
- Prevents the "I've heard this all before" objection

## Permission-Based Transitions (Alexander)

### Method A: Prospect Advances (Best Case)

When the prospect asks "How does this work?" or "How much is it?" -- go directly into the offer. No transition needed. They have given themselves permission.

### Method B: You Advance

"Based on everything you've told me, I think we can definitely help you. Is it cool if I show you a little bit about how we do that?"

They say yes. You now have explicit permission to pitch.

### The Key Principle

The prospect should feel like they are CHOOSING to hear the pitch, not being subjected to it. When they opt in, resistance drops dramatically.

## The Opposing Agenda Principle (Gordon)

### The Core Dynamic

- **Prospect wants:** Information (without committing to a decision)
- **You want:** A transformation (a decision)
- **Rule:** The more information you disclose, the less leverage and leadership you have

### Information Leverage Tactics

**Tactic 1: Control the flow of information.** Use the frame script to establish that YOU decide when and what information is shared. The prospect gets information after you have diagnosed, not before.

**Tactic 2: Two-call close (when trust is low).** If prospect clearly will not close on call 1 (high trust deficit, very skeptical), pivot to a two-call approach:
- Call 1: Discovery + pre-pitch (no offer)
- Give homework between calls (creates investment and open loop)
- Call 2: Shortened discovery recap + full pitch + close

**Tactic 3: Withhold payment plans.** Never give ALL financial information in one sitting. Present pay-in-full first. If they object, payment plans are held in reserve for follow-up. This creates an open loop and a reason to reconnect.

**Tactic 4: Withhold the HOW.** During the pre-pitch, share the WHAT (what they need to do) but not the HOW (how to do it). The HOW is what they pay for. This creates an information gap that the offer fills.

### When the Prospect Pushes for Information Early

If prospect pushes for price or program details before you have finished discovery:
- "I totally want to share all of that with you. What I've found works best is if I get some context first so I can share only what's relevant. Otherwise I'm just throwing things at the wall."
- If they insist: the real issue is usually weak discovery. If your diagnostic is compelling, they will not push for details early.

## Clarifying Question Patterns (Gordon)

### Why Clarifying Questions Matter

Answering program questions at face value is dangerous. The surface question often hides a real concern that, if missed, becomes an objection.

### The Pattern

Prospect asks a question about your program. BEFORE answering:

**Option 1 -- Reason probe:**
"Good question -- I'm curious, did you ask me that for a reason?"
[They reveal the real concern]
[Now address the concern, not just the surface question]

**Option 2 -- Clarify intent:**
"I'm not sure I'm understanding your question -- are you asking ___ or do you mean ___?"

**Option 3 -- Hedged response:**
"Well, it depends..." / "Generally, yes -- but..." / "That's not something we do a lot of, but..."

### Example

**Wrong:**
- Prospect: "Does your program include group coaching calls?"
- You: "Yes, 2 calls per week where we cover..."
- Prospect: "I had a bad experience with group coaching."
- Now you are fighting uphill.

**Right:**
- Prospect: "Does your program include group coaching calls?"
- You: "Good question -- did you ask me that for a reason?"
- Prospect: "Yeah, last time I did group coaching nobody got personal attention"
- You: "That makes total sense. Our groups are capped at 15 and every person gets a hot seat. Plus you get 1-on-1 access for anything the group doesn't cover."
- Now you have addressed the REAL concern.

## The Commitment Close Before Price (Alexander)

Before dropping price, secure verbal commitment to the solution:

"I just want to make sure -- if you decide to do something, you follow through. What would you say your level of commitment is for something like this?"

They commit verbally. THEN the price drop hits differently because they have already said they are committed.

### Why This Works

Consistency bias (Cialdini): the highest driver of human behavior is the drive to appear consistent with what we have already said. When they verbally commit to following through, walking away from the deal becomes incongruent with their self-image.

## Turning Uncertain Language into Certain Language (Alexander)

During and after the pitch, watch for uncertain language:

| Uncertain | How to Challenge | Certain |
|-----------|-----------------|---------|
| "It should work" | "Is that a yes or no? I want to make sure." | "Yes, it will work" |
| "I feel like it could help" | "You sound a little uncertain. What specifically makes you think it CAN help?" | "I think this can get me there" |
| "I guess so" | "I need you to be honest -- is this what you need or not?" | "Yes, this is what I need" |
| "Maybe" | "Help me understand what 'maybe' means for you" | Clear answer |

**Rule:** If you let uncertainty slide, the whole call becomes uncertain and ends in no sale. Challenge uncertain language the moment it appears.

## The Improv Mindset (Serhant)

### Core Principles for Pitch Delivery

1. **"Yes, and..."** -- Accept what is thrown at you and build on it. Client suddenly demands a feature? "That's great. What kind of [feature]? Let's talk about what that would look like."
2. **Specificity** -- You cannot talk vaguely. Get specific so clients think specifically about what they actually want.
3. **Listen to respond, not reply** -- Truly hear what is being said before responding. Take a beat.

### Why Scripts Fail in Pitch Delivery

"What happens when the other side goes off script? You freeze." (Serhant). The pitch framework (Codex, pillars) provides structure. But within that structure, delivery must be conversational and adaptive.

**Resolution across instructors:** Scripts are training wheels (Gordon drill approach) that become internalized playbooks (Serhant improv approach). Drill the Pitch Codex until the structure is automatic, then deliver it conversationally. Study actors for range and vocal variety (Alexander), practice through sales-specific drills (Gordon), and develop improv skills for adaptability (Serhant).
