# Presence & Power

Everything about the physical, nonverbal, and environmental dimensions of selling and negotiating. This skill covers in-person meetings, face-to-face negotiations, setting manipulation, and the body language that communicates power, trust, and authority before you say a single word.

---

## Core Principle: The 7-38-55 Rule

**Source:** Voss (citing Albert Mehrabian's research).

| Channel | Impact on Communication |
|---------|----------------------|
| Words | 7% |
| Tone of voice | 38% |
| Body language | 55% |

Your words account for only 7% of how your message is received. The other 93% is HOW you deliver it -- tone and body. This is why presence matters enormously in any face-to-face interaction.

> **Cross-reference:** For tone of voice (the 38%), see `tonality-delivery`. This skill covers the body language 55% plus environmental control.

## Section 1: The 7-Second Rule

**Source:** Serhant (Myka -- etiquette expert).

You have 7 seconds to make a powerful first impression. In that window, people decide whether you are likable, confident, authoritative, and trustworthy.

### The 7-Second Checklist
1. **Face forward when entering.** Do not turn your back to the room.
2. **Smile.** Start every interaction with a genuine smile. Science shows smiling tricks your brain into feeling better.
3. **Eye contact.** Meet their eyes within the first second.
4. **Posture.** Stand tall, shoulders back, chin level.
5. **Handshake.** Firm, appropriate (see Power Plays below for variants).
6. **Voice.** First words should be clear, warm, and confident.
7. **Dress.** You are already being judged by what you wear.

### First Impression Goals
Be perceived as: **Likable, Confident, Authoritative, Trustworthy.**

If you miss even one of these in the first 7 seconds, you spend the rest of the meeting recovering.

## Section 2: The Six Power Plays

**Source:** Serhant. Subtle authority signals that establish dominance without aggression.

### Power Play 1: "Please, After You"
Lead with your hand, show which door or chair. Subtle authority signal that says "I am guiding this interaction."

### Power Play 2: Hand on Top in Handshake
Placing your hand on top during a handshake communicates "I am in charge." Use selectively -- do NOT use when you are the guest (that is a dominance play against someone with VIP status).

### Power Play 3: Hand on Shoulder
Caring and respectful but also a massive authority signal. Common in politics. Deploy to get things back on track after frustration or to signal reassurance with authority.

### Power Play 4: Intentional Leaning In
Shows assertiveness, authority, command. Lean in when making key points. Lean back when listening or creating space.

### Power Play 5: Controlled Hand Gestures
Not frantic. Slow, authoritative movements. Controlled gestures communicate confidence. Erratic gestures communicate anxiety.

### Power Play 6: Voice Control
The most powerful power play. Controlled and powerful, not loud. Common mistakes: trailing off at end of sentences, going up (uptalk), monotone.

**Practice:** Record yourself. Show two points of inflection per sentence.

> **Cross-reference:** For deep voice control training, see `tonality-delivery`.

## Section 3: Setting Manipulation

**Source:** Serhant. The WHERE of your meeting is as important as the WHAT.

### The Grade-School Framework
Before every meeting, answer: **Who** am I talking to? **Why** am I talking to them? **What** do I need to say? **When** is the best time? **Where** is the best place?

### Setting Strategy by Objective

| Goal | Setting Choice |
|------|---------------|
| Want to feel powerful / control the frame | Schedule at YOUR office, morning time |
| Want prospect to feel comfortable | Find a great quiet coffee shop near THEM |
| Want opposing party to feel they have the upper hand | Meet at THEIR office (playing for the other side to win) |
| Neutral ground needed | Restaurant, conference center, hotel lobby |
| Need to re-ignite excitement | Get them back to the product/property/environment |

### Communication Rules for Setting
- **Never send bad news via text or email.** Deliver it live. Your voice is comfort when something goes wrong.
- A dead deal today could be an amazing deal three years from now because of how you handled the bad news.
- Vocabulary, tone, and formality adjust depending on audience and setting.

## Section 4: The Red-Button Negotiation

**Source:** Serhant. When a deal starts falling apart -- bring everyone together in person.

### When to Press the Red Button
- Buyers are getting anxious or second-guessing
- Negotiation has stalled over text/email/phone
- Emotions are high but unresolved
- Multiple parties are misunderstanding each other
- Deal is about to die over a relatively small issue

### Three Strategies for the Red-Button Meeting

**Strategy 1: Return to the Setting**
Get them back in front of what excited them originally. Out of sight = out of mind. If they loved the views, get them in front of the views while discussing the issue. They will realize the floor scratches they are fighting about do not matter.

**Strategy 2: Bring the Other Side In**
Let each party see real people with real concerns. Names on paper become human beings with families, dreams, and fears. Empathy flows naturally in person.

**Strategy 3: Data in the Room**
Use comps, math, and research live in front of everyone. Let the data tell the story and bridge the gap. It is harder to argue with facts when they are displayed in real-time.

### Concession Creativity in Red-Button Meetings
When stuck on one issue, introduce something else:
- Fighting over price? Offer to include furniture, extend timeline, cover a repair.
- Fighting over terms? Shift to non-monetary concessions.
- Fighting over principle? Acknowledge their position, then pivot to practical outcomes.

> **Cross-reference:** For structured bargaining and concession strategy, see `negotiation-tactics` (Ackerman model, interests vs. positions framework).

## Section 5: Improv as the #1 Negotiation Skill

**Source:** Serhant. A contrarian position: improv, not scripts, is the most important skill for closing and negotiation.

### Core Improv Principles for Sales

**"Yes, and..."**
Accept what is thrown at you and build on it. Client suddenly demands a pool? "Pools are awesome. What kind of pool? Olympic? Small? Let's talk."

**Specificity**
You cannot talk vaguely. Get specific so clients think specifically about what they actually want. Vague conversations produce vague decisions (which is usually "no").

**Listening to Respond, Not Reply**
Truly hear what is being said before responding. There is a difference between waiting for your turn to talk and actually processing what they said.

### Practice Method
Take improv classes. Join an improv group. If you can keep a weird uncomfortable conversation going, you can handle any objection from any buyer or seller.

### The Ad Game (Practice Exercise)
Invent a product. Keep adding features. Improvise a commercial for it. Do this with a partner who throws curveballs. Builds the muscle of thinking on your feet.

### Why Scripts Fail in Person
> **Serhant:** "What happens when the other side goes off script? You freeze."

Scripts work as training wheels. Eventually, they must become internalized playbooks that flow as natural improv.

> **Instructor Disagreement (Resolved):** Serhant says scripts are dead. Voss uses precise scripted tools (Ackerman, calibrated questions). Gordon says drill scripts until natural. **Resolution:** Scripts are training wheels that become internalized playbooks that become natural improv. Drill scripts (Voss/Gordon) until they flow naturally as improv (Serhant). All three agree the end state is fluid, not robotic.

## Section 6: Reading Nonverbal Cues

### The Pinocchio Effect (Voss)
More words = more likely they are lying or uncomfortable. When someone's explanation gets longer and more elaborate than necessary, they are compensating for something. Short, direct answers signal confidence and truth.

### Pronoun Usage as Power Indicator (Voss)
- Heavy use of "I" = person is less important than they claim (compensating)
- Use of "we" / "they" / "them" = person has more authority (not needing to self-promote)
- Listen for pronoun shifts mid-conversation -- they signal changes in comfort or honesty

### Status Drops (Gordon)
Use case studies and name drops to create a "status delta" (difference in perceived status). ONLY do status drops when PROVING a point -- never just to brag. Must be tactful, not off-putting.

### Reading the Other Agent/Negotiator (Serhant)
- **Experienced agent:** Soften approach. Let them think they have something to teach you. Play down what you have and know.
- **Newer agent (first 10 deals):** Take on mentor role. "If it were my client, I would do A, B, and C."
- **Big ego agent:** Agree with them, "defer" to their expertise. Ask: "What would you do in my position?" They reveal their thinking.
- **Agent keeps following up after showing:** They are more motivated to sell = more negotiation room.

## Section 7: Four Recovery Tactics (When You Lose Control)

**Source:** Serhant. When the meeting goes sideways:

### Tactic 1: Get Up, Get Water
Physical pattern interrupt. Remind yourself this is a speed bump, not a brick wall. The movement resets your emotional state.

### Tactic 2: Set Boundaries
If the setting is not in your favor (impasse while at daycare pickup, phone ringing), call a pause. Be transparent. Schedule a callback.
```
"I want to give this the attention it deserves. Let me
call you back in 30 minutes when I can focus."
```
Serhant calls this "throwing the red flag."

### Tactic 3: Shift the Focus
Arguing price for days? Start talking concessions, furniture, closing date, closing costs. When something besides money goes on or off the table, people start seeing the bigger picture.

### Tactic 4: Change It Up
Hang up the phone. End the lunch. Move to another place or time. Like "icing the kicker" -- the surprise of changing setting puts you back in control.

## Section 8: Name Memory Trick

**Source:** Serhant. The brain remembers two related pieces of information better than one.

### The Technique
Associate the person's name with a visual feature:
- "Ryan with the gray hair"
- "Myka with the red lip"
- "Dave with the blue tie"

This simple association dramatically improves name recall in networking situations, group meetings, and multi-party negotiations.

## Section 9: Dress & VIP Awareness

### Dress How You Want to Be Addressed (Serhant)
Everything you wear, carry (cell phone to pen) is being watched and judged. Your appearance is the first data point people use to assess your status, competence, and trustworthiness.

### VIP Status Protocol
- Determine who has VIP status in the room and what role location plays
- If you are the guest: show respect, wait to be shown where to sit, do not put hand over theirs in handshake
- If neutral space and you want dominance: sit first, choose your seat
- Power positions: middle and ends of table

## Decision Tree: In-Person Meeting Preparation

```
Upcoming in-person meeting or negotiation
    |
    +--> Where should we meet?
    |       |
    |       +--> I need control: My office, morning
    |       +--> They need comfort: Near them, quiet spot
    |       +--> Neutral needed: Restaurant, hotel lobby
    |       +--> Deal dying: Red-Button -- back to the product/property
    |
    +--> How do I prepare physically?
    |       |
    |       +--> Dress appropriately for the audience
    |       +--> Review 7-second checklist
    |       +--> Plan which power plays are appropriate
    |       +--> Research attendees (names, roles, potential motivations)
    |
    +--> What if I lose control?
            |
            +--> Get water (pattern interrupt)
            +--> Set boundaries (pause and reschedule)
            +--> Shift focus (move away from the stuck point)
            +--> Change it up (change venue, time, or medium)
```
