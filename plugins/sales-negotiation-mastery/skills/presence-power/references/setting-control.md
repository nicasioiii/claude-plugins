# Setting Control: Manipulation, Recovery & Nonverbal Reading

## Setting Manipulation -- Full Tactical Guide

**Source:** Serhant.

### The Core Principle
The WHERE of your meeting is a negotiation tool. Controlling the setting gives you a baseline advantage before the conversation even starts.

### Setting Selection Matrix

| Your Objective | Setting Choice | Why |
|---------------|---------------|-----|
| Establish authority | Your office, morning time | Home turf advantage, you control everything |
| Make them comfortable | Near them, quiet coffee shop | They feel at ease, lower defenses |
| Appear non-threatening | Their office | "Playing for the other side to win" -- they feel powerful |
| Neutral negotiation | Restaurant, hotel lobby | Neither party has home advantage |
| Revive a dying deal | Back to the product/property | Re-ignite the emotional connection (Red-Button) |
| Surprise/reset dynamic | Change the venue mid-process | "Icing the kicker" -- the disruption resets power |

### The Scouting Process
Before choosing a setting:
1. Yelp/Google the location (quiet? well-lit? good reviews?)
2. Visit in advance if possible
3. Know where to sit (back to wall, facing door = power position)
4. Know the parking/access situation (do not be flustered on arrival)
5. Arrive first (whoever arrives second is on the other's turf)

### Time-of-Day Strategy
- **Morning meetings:** Higher energy, clearer thinking, projects authority
- **Lunch meetings:** Relaxed, builds rapport, but less business-focused
- **Afternoon meetings:** Can be sluggish; bring energy or keep short
- **Evening meetings:** Informal, relationship-building, can loosen negotiation

## The Four Recovery Tactics -- Deep Dive

**Source:** Serhant. What to do when you lose control of a meeting or negotiation.

### Tactic 1: Get Up, Get Water

**What it does:** Physical pattern interrupt. Movement resets your emotional state and gives you 30-60 seconds to recompose.

**When to use:**
- You feel your emotions rising
- You just received unexpected bad news
- The other side made an aggressive move you were not prepared for
- You need a moment to think

**How to do it:**
Stand up naturally. "Excuse me, going to grab some water. Can I get you anything?" Walk slowly. Breathe. Return composed.

### Tactic 2: Set Boundaries (Throw the Red Flag)

**What it does:** Pauses the negotiation when the environment is working against you.

**When to use:**
- You are negotiating during an unexpected phone call while driving
- The other side surprised you with an ambush (extra people, new demands)
- You are emotionally compromised and need to reset
- The meeting has gone off the rails

**Script:**
```
"I want to give this the attention it deserves. Can we pause
and pick this back up [tomorrow morning / in an hour / when
I'm back at my desk]? I want to be fully focused."
```

**Key:** Be transparent about WHY you are pausing. Honesty builds trust even in tense moments.

### Tactic 3: Shift the Focus

**What it does:** Moves the conversation away from the stuck point to something that can create movement.

**When to use:**
- Arguing about price for days with no progress
- Both sides are entrenched in their positions
- Emotions are high and logic is not working
- You need a "win" to build momentum toward the bigger issue

**Technique:**
```
"We've been going back and forth on price. Let me ask about
something else -- what about the closing timeline? If we
could [accelerate/extend] that, would it change how you
see the overall deal?"
```

When something besides money goes on or off the table, people start seeing the bigger picture more clearly.

### Tactic 4: Change It Up (Ice the Kicker)

**What it does:** Disrupts the pattern by changing the physical setting, time, or medium.

**When to use:**
- Negotiation has been going in circles
- The current setting is working against you
- A fresh start would help both sides
- You need to surprise the other side into reconsidering

**Methods:**
- Hang up the phone and call back later
- End the lunch and suggest continuing over email
- Move from conference room to a walk outside
- Switch from phone to in-person (or vice versa)

The surprise of changing setting puts you back in control, like a football coach calling timeout to "ice the kicker" before a crucial play.

## Reading Nonverbal Cues -- Advanced

### The 7-38-55 Rule Applied (Voss)

When observing your counterpart, allocate your attention:
- 7% to their words (what they are saying)
- 38% to their tone (how they are saying it)
- 55% to their body (what they are showing)

### The Pinocchio Effect (Voss)

**Principle:** The more words someone uses, the more likely they are lying or uncomfortable.

**What to watch for:**
- Answers that are much longer than the question warrants
- Excessive detail about irrelevant points
- Qualifying statements ("honestly," "to tell you the truth," "I swear")
- Circular explanations that keep coming back to the same point

**What short answers signal:**
- Confidence in their position
- Truthfulness
- Comfort with the topic

### Pronoun Usage as Power Indicator (Voss)

| Pattern | What It Signals |
|---------|----------------|
| Heavy "I" usage | Person is less important/powerful than they claim (compensating) |
| "We" / "they" / "them" | Person has more authority (does not need to self-promote) |
| Shift from "I" to "we" mid-conversation | Moving from personal defense to collaborative mode |
| Shift from "we" to "I" mid-conversation | Losing confidence or becoming personally defensive |

### Reading Engagement Level

| Signal | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| Leaning in | Interested, engaged |
| Leaning back, arms crossed | Defensive, disengaged |
| Looking at phone/watch | Wants to leave, losing interest |
| Nodding while you speak | Processing and agreeing (or performing agreement) |
| Breaking eye contact upward | Thinking / processing |
| Breaking eye contact downward | Uncomfortable / hiding something |
| Touching face/neck | Stress, discomfort, or deception |
| Open palms | Openness, honesty |
| Hands hidden (pockets, under table) | Guarded, withholding |

### Status Drops as Power Tool (Gordon)

Use case studies and name drops to create a "status delta" -- the difference in perceived status between you and the prospect.

**Rules:**
- ONLY do status drops when PROVING a point (never to brag)
- Must be tactful and relevant to their situation
- Deploy when prospect is questioning your authority or expertise
- Too many status drops = insecure; too few = missed opportunity

**Example:**
"We worked with [well-known name] on exactly this issue. What we found was [relevant insight]."

## Reading the Other Agent/Negotiator (Serhant)

### Experienced Agent
- **Signal:** Calm, measured, not easily impressed
- **Strategy:** Soften approach. Let them think they have something to teach you. Play down what you have and know.
- **Why:** Experienced agents respond to respect, not ego battles.

### Newer Agent (First 10 Deals)
- **Signal:** Eager, asking lots of questions, possibly nervous
- **Strategy:** Take on mentor role. "If it were my client, I would do A, B, and C."
- **Why:** They need guidance and will follow your lead if you provide it respectfully.

### Big Ego Agent
- **Signal:** Name-dropping, interrupting, aggressive posturing
- **Strategy:** Agree with them, "defer" to their expertise. Ask: "What would you do if you were in my position?"
- **Why:** They reveal their thinking AND feel respected. Ego fights waste everyone's time.

### Anxious-to-Close Agent
- **Signal:** Keeps following up after every showing, rapid-fire emails, pushing for quick decision
- **Strategy:** Recognize they are more motivated to sell = more negotiation room for you.
- **Why:** Urgency on their side is leverage for your side.

### The Transparent + Selective Silence Combination (Serhant)
- Almost never answer anything too quickly, especially with lots of emotion at play
- Silence puts you in a place where the other person tells you their story
- Be transparent about your position, then go silent and let them fill the space

**Case Study:** Buyer's agent sent under-ask offer. Serhant knew the buyer was hooked (watched their eyes light up at kitchen/views). Told the agent she received the offer, it was lower than the seller would accept, she would present it. Then went silent for several days. Buyer's agent came back with revised offer at asking price. Silence did the negotiating.

## The Red-Button Negotiation -- Full Protocol

**Source:** Serhant.

### When to Press It
- Deal is dying over text/email/phone
- Buyers are second-guessing
- Emotions are high, understanding is low
- Small issue is being blown out of proportion
- Both sides need to see each other as human beings

### Execution Steps

**Step 1: Call the Meeting**
```
"I think the best thing we can do is get everyone in the same
room. There's too much being lost over email. Can we meet
[tomorrow/this week] at [location]?"
```

**Step 2: Choose the Setting Strategically**
- If you need to re-ignite excitement: meet AT the product/property/location
- If you need a neutral reset: meet at a restaurant or neutral office
- If you need authority: meet at your office

**Step 3: Run the Meeting**
1. Acknowledge both sides' concerns openly
2. Present data and facts (not opinions)
3. Introduce creative concessions
4. Let each person speak without interruption
5. Find the sweet spot -- what Serhant calls "the place where deals close, money is made, and everyone feels positive"

**Step 4: Close in the Room**
Do not leave without agreement or a clear next step with a hard deadline.
