# Breakthrough Techniques: "That's Right," Forced Empathy, and Commitment Testing

## Getting to "That's Right"

### The Formula

**Paraphrase + Extensive Labeling = Summary**

This summary, when done correctly, triggers "that's right" -- the breakthrough moment where the trust-based influence bridge is complete.

### Step-by-Step Construction

1. **Summarize their arguments.** Not your interpretation -- THEIR view of the situation.
2. **Summarize ALL their feelings about those arguments.** Both positive and negative.
3. **Give voice to negative feelings counter to your position.** Be fearless. If they think your product is overpriced, acknowledge that. If they doubt it will work, name it.
4. **No agreement or disagreement.** No denials. No corrections. Just pure articulation of their world.
5. **Deep dive on positives AND negatives.** Do not cherry-pick only what supports your position.
6. **Repeat what the world looks like ACCORDING TO THEM.** Not according to you.

### What "That's Right" Signals

- A subtle epiphany in their mind
- An enlightening moment they did not quite see before
- Confirmation they feel empathy FROM you
- A powerful feeling of connection to you specifically
- What people say when they believe they have heard something indisputable

### What "That's Right" Does for You

- Words get traction sooner in the conversation
- You renegotiate less and fix problems faster
- Saves time throughout the rest of the interaction
- In many cases, triggers the counterpart to simply GIVE you your deal

### "You're Right" = DANGER ZONE

**"You're right" is the WORST form of false agreement.**
- Even worse than counterfeit yes
- The "black hole of negotiations"
- What we all say to people we want to STOP TALKING
- Used with people we feel obligated to maintain a relationship with

**If you hear "you're right":** You have sought to be understood before showing understanding. BACK UP. Go back to mirroring, labeling, and summarizing THEIR view.

### "I Understand" = Ineffective

This phrase is the ineffective negotiator's favorite. Deals fall apart or people stop talking when this is used. It does NOT convey true understanding. Replace with labels and summaries.

## Forced Empathy Through Calibrated Questions

### Definition (Black Swan Group Term)

Calibrated "how" questions force the counterpart to take your position into consideration. They MUST think about your perspective to answer the question.

### Implementation Questions

After reaching an agreement or getting a "yes":
- "How will we know we're on track?"
- "How will we address things if we find we're off track?"
- After they respond: mirror them, then summarize until you get "that's right"

### "How Am I Supposed to Do That?"

The greatest calibrated question of all time. This says "no" without saying no.

**How to use:**
- Sounds like a counterbid, so counterpart often softens their position
- You can express "no" 3 times using calibrated questions before actually saying the word
- Use FM DJ voice. Genuine curiosity, not sarcasm.

### The Five-Phase "No" Progression

When you need to decline something:

1. **"How am I supposed to do that?"** (use 2-3 times before moving on)
2. **"Your offer is very generous. I'm sorry, that just doesn't work for me."** ("Generous" encourages them to live up to it)
3. **"I'm sorry, but I'm afraid I just can't do that."** (Triggers empathy + removes leverage)
4. **"I'm sorry, no."** (Deliver gently)
5. **"No."** (Downward inflection, respectful tone. Last resort.)

**Rule:** Say no at least twice. Many negotiators will not stop until they hear no at least twice.

### Bundling Questions in Groups of Three

Ask the same thing THREE DIFFERENT WAYS (not three different things):
- "What's the biggest challenge you face?" / "What's the key issue?" / "What are we up against?"
- "How does that help?" / "What problem does that solve?" / "What's the objective?"

This pulls more complete information than a single question.

## The Rule of Three (Testing Commitment)

### Purpose

Get counterpart to agree to the same thing 3 times in the same conversation. Nearly impossible to repeat counterfeit yeses 3x without a flinch or break in alignment.

### Execution

1. **Label:** "It sounds like you're good with this" (watch for 7-38-55 alignment)
2. **Paraphrase agreement:** Restate what they agreed to in your own words (watch for flinch)
3. **Summarize + calibrated question:** Summary to get "that's right" + "How would you like to proceed?" or "What steps would we take next?"

### Signs of Fake Agreement

1. **"You're right"** = huge red flag. They want you to stop talking.
2. **"I'll try"** = they plan to fail. Not invested in the agreement.

**Counter:** Label: "It still seems like there's something here I'm not getting" + pause + more calibrated questions.

## The Behavioral Change Stairway

### FBI Crisis Model
1. Active Listening --> 2. Empathy --> 3. Rapport --> 4. Influence --> 5. Behavioral Change

### Business Adaptation (Black Swan Group)
1. Tactical Empathy --> 2. Rapport --> 3. Reciprocation of Empathy --> 4. Trust-Based Influence --> 5. Deal-Making

### Key Insight

You CANNOT skip steps. The entire stairway is built on Carl Rogers's principle: "Real change can only occur when a therapist accepts their patient for who they really are."

Most people experience conditional positive regard their entire lives. They develop habits of suppressing their true personality and keeping their guard up. This is why deals that should be made are not made.

**Application:** Unconditional positive regard in a sales context = tactical empathy. You accept their perspective without agreeing or disagreeing. This acceptance creates the safety for them to lower their guard and make a real decision.

### The Team Behind the Table (Voss)

There is ALWAYS a team on the other side (spouse, attorney, implementers, boss).

**Critical insight:** It is not the decision-maker you need to worry about -- it is the DEAL KILLER. The person affected by or critical to implementation.

**Questions to uncover them:**
- "How does this affect the rest of the team?"
- "What do your colleagues see as their main challenges?"
- Ask these MORE THAN ONCE over the course of the conversation.

**Stat:** 50% of signed deals at a major telecom carrier are killed internally -- never consummated. Implementation questions prevent this.
