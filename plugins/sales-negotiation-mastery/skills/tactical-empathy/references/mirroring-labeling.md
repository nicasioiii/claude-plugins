# Mirroring and Labeling: Detailed Mechanics

## Mirror Mechanics -- Complete Protocol

### The Four Steps (Every Time)

1. **FM DJ voice.** Slow, calm, controlled. This is non-negotiable -- mirroring in a rushed or uncertain tone fails.
2. **"I'm sorry..."** This opener softens the mirror and prevents it from sounding aggressive or confused.
3. **Mirror the KEY 1-3 words with upward inflection.** Choose the most information-rich or emotionally loaded words, not just the last words.
4. **Silence for at least 4 seconds.** Shut up. Count in your head if needed.

### Choosing What to Mirror

- Default: last 1-3 words of their statement
- Better: the CRITICAL 1-3 words (the most emotionally loaded or strategically important)
- Mirror words that will get them to explain, expand, or reveal more

**Example:**
- Prospect: "We've been trying to solve this for months but nothing seems to work and we're running out of options."
- Mirror: "Running out of options?"
- NOT: "Out of options and we've been trying?" (too long, not focused)

### What Mirrors Trigger

- Re-explanation in different words (gives you new information)
- Emotional expansion (reveals deeper feelings)
- Strategic revelation (they explain their thinking/position)
- Extended talking time (more data for you)

### Common Mistakes

- **Stepping on the mirror:** Continuing to talk after the mirror. The silence IS the tool.
- **Mirroring too many words:** Keep it to 1-3. More than that sounds like parroting.
- **Wrong tone:** A mirror in assertive tone sounds confrontational. Always FM DJ or positive/playful.
- **Rushing the silence:** 4 seconds minimum. Most people break at 2 seconds. Hold longer.

### Mirror Neurons vs. Mirroring (Different Concepts)

Mirror neurons = involuntary limbic system reactions. If you are calm, they become calmer. If you are tense, they become tense. This is emotional contagion.

Mirroring (the technique) = deliberate verbal repetition. This is a strategic tool.

Both matter. Your emotional state (mirror neurons) affects how your mirroring technique lands.

## Label Construction -- Complete Protocol

### Starter Phrases (Choose One)

- "It seems like..."
- "It sounds like..."
- "It looks like..."
- "It feels like..."
- "You seem..."
- "You sound..."
- "You look..."

### The "I" Rule

**Severely limit "I" in labels.**

| Wrong | Right |
|---|---|
| "What I'm hearing is..." | "It sounds like..." |
| "I think you're feeling..." | "You seem..." |
| "I understand that..." | "It seems like..." |

**Exception:** "It sounds like I've upset you" (appropriate because YOU are the subject of the action)

Using "I" assigns personal responsibility, centers yourself, and distracts them from the thought processes you want them to engage in.

### The Two-Millimeter Shift

Instead of DENYING a negative:
- WRONG: "I don't want you to think I'm being pushy"
- RIGHT (label): "It seems like I'm being pushy" OR flip to positive: "It seems like you value making careful decisions"

Labels do NOT plant negatives. DENIALS plant negatives.

### Two Levels of Emotion to Label

1. **Presenting behavior** (visible): tone of voice, facial expressions, body language, word choice
2. **Underlying feeling** (beneath surface): the real motivator that may not show directly

**Example:**
- Presenting: "I just need to think about it" (calm, deflecting)
- Underlying: Fear of making the wrong decision, fear of commitment, past burn

**The skill:** Label the underlying, not just the presenting. "It seems like there's something deeper here keeping you from feeling confident."

### After Throwing Out a Label

1. SHUT UP. Do not expand.
2. Do not provide rationale for your label.
3. Do not ask "have I got that right?" or "does that resonate?"
4. Let the label work.
5. If silence continues and they look thoughtful: you are on track. Wait.
6. If the label seems to miss: "I was just guessing -- what IS going on?"

### Over-Labeling

Over-labeling is NOT a problem. Sometimes the other side responds with "hey, don't be so hard on yourself." That means they just stepped to YOUR side.

### Labels as Truth Serum

Described as "unlocking the floodgates of truth-telling." In law enforcement: "the closest thing to a true serum."

Labels help uncover the PRIMARY emotion driving behavior. Once you find that primary emotion, their fears diffuse.

## Objections as Counteroffers in Disguise (Voss)

Instead of seeing objections as things to overcome, see them as ways to establish rapport. Each objection is a clue to narrow in on the true source of reluctance.

**Response pattern:**
1. Hear the objection
2. Label the emotion behind it: "It seems like you're worried about..."
3. Let them confirm or correct
4. Label deeper: "It sounds like this is really about..."
5. When they say "that's right" -- you have found it

## Advanced Mirroring Patterns

### Compound Mirrors

When a prospect gives a long answer, you can chain mirrors to go deeper:

1. First mirror on the emotionally loaded phrase
2. After they expand, mirror the NEW key phrase
3. Repeat until you hit the core issue

**Example chain:**
- Prospect: "We've been trying different vendors for months and nothing sticks."
- Mirror 1: "Nothing sticks?"
- Prospect: "Yeah, every time we think we found the right partner they drop the ball on delivery."
- Mirror 2: "Drop the ball on delivery?"
- Prospect: "Our last vendor promised 30-day turnaround and it took 90 days. We lost two major clients."
- NOW you have the real issue: delivery reliability, not price or features.

### Mirrors on Assertive Types

Assertive negotiator types (Voss) respond especially well to mirrors because:
- They want to be HEARD above all else
- Mirrors signal you are listening carefully
- They will keep talking and revealing position
- Combine mirrors with summaries to get "that's right" faster

### When Mirrors Fail

If a mirror gets a flat response ("Yeah") or confusion ("What do you mean?"):
- Switch to a label: "It seems like there's more to that story"
- Or ask a calibrated question: "What's the biggest challenge with that?"
- Some people do not respond to mirrors -- adapt to their communication style

---

## Label Stacking for Objection Handling

When facing resistance, stack 2-3 labels before any other technique:

1. **Label the surface emotion:** "It sounds like this feels like a big decision."
2. **Label the underlying fear:** "It seems like there's a worry about whether this will actually work."
3. **Label the positive desire:** "It sounds like you really want this to be the thing that finally gets you to [goal]."

The stack moves from acknowledging resistance to reinforcing desire. After the third label, they often self-resolve their hesitation.
