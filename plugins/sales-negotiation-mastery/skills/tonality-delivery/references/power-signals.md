# Power Signals: Body Language, Status, and Nonverbal Communication

## The Six Power Plays (Serhant via Myka)

### 1. "Please, After You"
Leading with hand, showing which door or chair = subtle authority signal. Use when entering rooms or guiding transitions.

### 2. Hand on Top in Handshake
Placing your hand on top during a handshake = "I'm in charge." Use selectively -- not every handshake. If you are the guest, do NOT use this.

### 3. Hand on Shoulder
Caring and respectful but also a massive authority signal. Common in politics. Deploy to get things back on track after frustration or to reinforce a breakthrough moment.

### 4. Intentional Leaning In
Leaning forward shows assertiveness, authority, command. On video calls, this means leaning slightly toward the camera during key moments, not slouching back.

### 5. Controlled Hand Gestures
Not frantic. Slow, authoritative movements. Emphasize key words with measured gestures. Frantic gesturing = nervous energy.

### 6. Voice Control (The Most Powerful)
Controlled and powerful, not loud. Common mistakes:
- Trailing off at end of sentences
- Going up (uptalk) on statements
- Monotone delivery

**Practice:** Show two points of inflection per sentence. Record and review.

## The 7-Second Rule (Serhant)

You have 7 seconds to make a powerful first impression. Tactics:
- Do not turn your back to the room
- Always face forward when entering and exiting
- Determine VIP status and location role before the meeting
- Dress how you want to be addressed
- Everything you wear and carry (cell phone to pen) is being watched

## Status Drops (Gordon)

Use case studies and name drops to create a "status delta" (difference in perceived status).

**Rules:**
- ONLY do status drops when PROVING a point -- never to brag
- Must be tactful: "We worked with someone similar who was doing $2M in revenue..."
- Used during pitch to build credibility for your method
- Used during objection handling to demonstrate social proof

**Anti-pattern:** Name-dropping without purpose = perceived as insecure or douchey.

## Reading Nonverbal Cues

### On Video Calls

- **Leaning in** = engaged, interested
- **Leaning back / crossing arms** = skeptical, defensive
- **Looking away / checking phone** = checked out (you have lost them)
- **Nodding** = agreeing (but verify verbally -- nodding can be automatic)
- **Facial tension / tight jaw** = discomfort with what you just said
- **Smile breaking through** = positive reaction they may be trying to hide

### Pronoun Usage as Power Indicator (Voss)

- **More I/me/my (singular)** = LESS important in decision-making
- **Avoidance of personal pronouns; use of we/us** = MORE important person
- Decision makers defer to people away from the table to avoid being cornered
- Excessive first-person = less influence; second/third-person = larger influence

### The Pinocchio Effect (Voss)

The harder someone works to convince you (because they are lying), the MORE WORDS they use.
- Added complexity to sentences
- More words + higher complexity = increased probability of lying
- "Don't let your counterpart baffle you with BS"

### Detecting Misalignment (Voss)

When tone and body do not match words = problem. Common misalignments:
- Words say "I'm fine with that" + body is tense = not fine
- Words say "Let me think about it" + tone is decisive = they have already decided no
- Words say "This sounds great" + no energy in voice = counterfeit yes

**Response to misalignment:** Use a label. "It seems like something's not sitting right." Then silence.

## Mirror Neurons and Emotional Contagion

Everyone has mirror neurons -- involuntary reactions in the limbic system (Voss):
- If you show up grumpy, people feel uncomfortable
- If you radiate happiness and warmth, people flock to you
- Calm is contagious. Positivity is contagious.

**Practical application:**
- Smile before dialing. Your tone changes immediately.
- If the prospect is agitated, stay in FM DJ voice. Your calm will transfer to them.
- Start every sentence by smiling (Serhant): "Science shows smiling tricks your brain into feeling better."

## The Smile Technique (Serhant)

Start every sentence by smiling. Between natural pauses, bring in a smile.

**Why it works:**
- Smiling releases endorphins
- Counterpart hears the smile in your voice (even on phone)
- Creates positive emotional contagion
- "Don't fake it until you make it. Practice it until you become it."

## Video Call Presence Checklist

1. Camera at eye level (not looking down or up)
2. Good lighting on your face (not backlit)
3. Clean, professional background
4. Sit upright -- not slouching
5. Look at the camera when speaking (not the screen)
6. Lean in during key moments
7. Use controlled hand gestures
8. Smile at the start of the call and during rapport
9. Nod slowly when they are speaking (shows active listening)
10. Keep phone out of sight

## Advanced Body Language on Video Calls

### The Lean-In/Lean-Back Rhythm

Use physical positioning as a punctuation tool on video:
- **Lean in** when making a key point, asking a critical question, or during the investment drop
- **Lean back** when listening, during silence after a mirror/label, or to give space after a heavy question
- **The shift itself** is what communicates -- a sudden lean-in signals "this is important"

### Gesture Calibration by Call Phase

| Call Phase | Gesture Style | Why |
|------------|--------------|-----|
| Rapport/Opening | Open palms, relaxed hands | Signals warmth, approachability |
| Discovery | Minimal gestures, stillness | Doctor frame -- focused listening |
| Pitch | Measured, deliberate gestures | Confidence, authority on key points |
| Objection handling | Still, grounded, hands visible | Oak tree energy, no nervous movement |
| Close | Slight lean forward, controlled nod | Assured, certain, not pushy |

### Reading Micro-Expressions on Video

Beyond the basics, watch for:
- **Lip compression** (pressing lips together) = suppressing disagreement or discomfort
- **Eye narrowing** = skepticism about what you just said
- **Head tilt** = genuine curiosity or processing (positive signal)
- **Sudden stillness** after animated behavior = something you said hit a nerve
- **Looking up and to the right** = constructing an answer (may be honest consideration or fabrication)

**Response to any negative micro-expression:** Do not call it out directly. Use a label: "It seems like something about that doesn't quite sit right." Then silence.
