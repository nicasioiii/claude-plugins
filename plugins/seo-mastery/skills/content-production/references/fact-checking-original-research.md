# Fact-Checking & Original Research

Master verification workflows to ensure accuracy and build credibility through original data.

## Fact-Checking Process

### Step 1: Identify Claimable Statements

**What Needs Fact-Checking**:
- Statistics and percentages ("73% of..." or "Only 15% of...")
- Health/medical claims ("Reduces wrinkles by 20%")
- Causation statements ("Leads to better sleep")
- Specific numbers ("Contains 500mg of...")
- Comparisons ("Better than X")
- Quotes (verify exact wording)
- Product ingredients or specs

**What Doesn't Always Need Checking**:
- Common knowledge ("Dogs are animals")
- Observable facts ("The sky is blue")
- Your own opinion ("I think this is better")
- General statements ("This product is expensive")

### Step 2: Find Authoritative Sources

**Source Hierarchy**:

**Tier 1 - Primary Research**:
- Peer-reviewed studies (PubMed, Google Scholar)
- Government databases (.gov sites)
- Official product specs from manufacturer
- Original research conducted by your company

**Tier 2 - Secondary Authority**:
- Government health agencies (FDA, CDC, NIH)
- Professional associations (American Medical Association)
- University research centers
- Major established publications

**Tier 3 - Tertiary (Use Cautiously)**:
- News articles citing studies
- Expert blogs with citations
- Industry publications

### Step 3: Verify Information

**For Statistics**:
1. Find original source (not secondary report)
2. Check date (is it current?)
3. Verify full context (statistic might be partial truth)
4. Check sample size and methodology (is study valid?)
5. Look for conflicting studies (are there alternative findings?)

**Example**:
```
Claim: "90% of people prefer natural sunscreen"

❌ WRONG: Find article saying "90% prefer"
✓ CORRECT: Find original study/survey showing this statistic
- Who conducted survey?
- How many people surveyed?
- When was survey done?
- What was exact question?
- Are results still valid?
```

**For Health Claims**:
1. Find medical sources (MD, RN, peer-reviewed studies)
2. Check if claim is proven vs theoretical
3. Look for disclaimers or conditions
4. Verify with multiple sources
5. Note if it's new research (might be preliminary)

**For Product Specifications**:
1. Check manufacturer's official website
2. Verify against product label
3. Cross-reference with retailer site
4. Check if specs have changed (updated version)

### Step 4: Cite Your Sources

**In Article**:
```
Claim: Natural sunscreen is preferred by 85% of dermatologists

Citation: According to a 2023 survey by the American Academy of Dermatology,
85% of dermatologists recommend mineral sunscreen for sensitive skin.
[Link to source]
```

**Using Multiple Sources**:
```
Claim: Mineral sunscreen is more effective than chemical sunscreen

Citation 1: A 2022 study in the Journal of Dermatology found...
Citation 2: The FDA recommends mineral sunscreen for...
Citation 3: According to dermatologist Dr. Smith...
```

## Handling Conflicting Information

### When Multiple Studies Disagree

**Approach**:
1. Find all relevant studies
2. Note publication dates (newer is usually better)
3. Check methodology (larger, peer-reviewed studies more credible)
4. Look for bias (who funded the research?)
5. Present accurately

**Example**:
```
"While some studies suggest caffeine improves alertness (Smith, 2021),
a more recent study found minimal effect (Johnson, 2023) when testing with
a larger sample size. Current evidence suggests moderate caffeine consumption
may help some people, but individual response varies."
```

### When You Don't Have Perfect Information

**Be Honest**:
```
✓ "Limited research exists on this topic, but preliminary evidence suggests..."
✓ "While studies are ongoing, current understanding indicates..."
✗ "Everyone knows that..." (if not verified)
```

## Original Research & Data

### Why Original Research Matters

**Google's 2024 Update**: Content with original research/data ranks better than regurgitated content

**Examples of Original Research**:
- Survey you conduct
- Testing you perform
- Analysis you create
- Case study from your business
- Data from your company

### Types of Original Research

**1. Surveys**

**How to Conduct**:
1. Create survey (SurveyMonkey, Typeform, Google Forms)
2. Ask clear questions (yes/no, rating scale, multiple choice)
3. Recruit respondents (email list, social media, paid survey sites)
4. Minimum sample: 100-500 respondents (more is better)
5. Analyze results
6. Publish findings

**Example**:
```
"We surveyed 500 dermatology patients about sunscreen preferences.
Here's what we found:
- 78% prefer natural sunscreen
- 65% experienced irritation with chemical sunscreen
- 82% would pay premium for reef-safe options"
```

**2. Product Testing**

**How to Conduct**:
1. Test multiple products yourself (or with team)
2. Use consistent testing criteria
3. Document results (photos, measurements, impressions)
4. Test over sufficient time period (at least 2-4 weeks)
5. Note any bias or limitations

**Example**:
```
"I tested 15 natural sunscreens on my sensitive skin for 90 days.
Testing criteria:
- Applied daily for 2 months
- Used on face and body
- Noted any irritation or reactions
- Tested with other skincare products
- Evaluated water resistance during swimming

Results:
- 3 products caused reaction
- 7 products worked well
- 5 products were excellent (recommended)"
```

**3. Competitive Analysis**

**How to Conduct**:
1. Analyze competitor offerings
2. Compare features, pricing, positioning
3. Document findings
4. Create comparison table or analysis

**Example**:
```
"We analyzed 20 email marketing tools and compared:
- Price (free vs paid)
- Features (automation, segmentation, templates)
- Customer support quality
- Integration options

Findings:
- Tool A best for beginners ($29/month)
- Tool B best for advanced users ($199/month)
- Tool C has best customer support"
```

**4. Case Studies**

**How to Conduct**:
1. Work with client/customer willing to be featured
2. Document their situation (before/after)
3. Show specific results/metrics
4. Include their story and results
5. Get permission for publication

**Example**:
```
"Case Study: How Brand X Increased Email Open Rates 45%

Client: E-commerce brand selling natural skincare
Challenge: Low email engagement (2% open rate)
Solution: Implemented segmentation + personalization
Results:
- Open rate: 2% → 9.2% (+360%)
- Click-through rate: 0.5% → 2.1%
- Revenue from email: $5K → $18K/month
Timeline: 3 months"
```

## Plagiarism Checking

### Why Plagiarism Matters

- Google penalizes duplicate content (especially AI-generated)
- Ethical issue (credibility destruction)
- Legal issue (copyright)

### Tools for Plagiarism Checking

**Copyscape Premium** (copyscape.com)
- Paste article or upload file
- Checks internet for identical text
- Best for: AI-generated or heavily edited content
- Cost: ~$0.05 per check

**Grammarly** (grammarly.com)
- Built-in plagiarism detection
- Checks against 16 billion web pages
- Best for: Writing as you go
- Cost: Included with Premium ($12/month)

**Turnitin** (turnitin.com)
- Academic plagiarism detection
- Comprehensive database
- Cost: Per-use or institutional

**QuillBot** (quillbot.com)
- Includes plagiarism checker
- Can also paraphrase content
- Cost: Free or Pro ($11.67/month)

### Plagiarism Check Process

1. Generate full article (via Claude or manual writing)
2. Copy entire article
3. Paste into Copyscape
4. Run check
5. Review any matching content
6. Rewrite matching sections if necessary
7. Run check again to verify

### Common Plagiarism Sources

**Unintentional Plagiarism** (from AI):
- Claude/ChatGPT generates content similar to training data
- Solution: Rewrite in your own words, add unique elements

**Accidental Plagiarism** (from research):
- Paraphrase too closely to source
- Solution: Cite source properly, rewrite more completely

**Citation Without Attribution**:
- Use fact/statistic without crediting source
- Solution: Always cite sources, not just in bibliography

## Common Fact-Checking Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Citing Secondary Sources**
```
❌ Article A says "Study shows..." → You cite Article A
Result: If Article A misquoted study, you repeat the error

✓ Find original study, cite directly
```

**Mistake 2: Trusting Social Media Statistics**
```
❌ "According to a viral post, 90% of people prefer..."
✓ Find actual study backing the statistic
```

**Mistake 3: Using Outdated Information**
```
❌ Cite 2015 study when 2024 study exists
✓ Use most current research available
```

**Mistake 4: Ignoring Context**
```
❌ "This treatment reduces wrinkles by 50%" (but in lab conditions)
✓ "In controlled studies, treatment reduced visible wrinkles by 50%"
```

**Mistake 5: Over-Citing**
```
❌ Every sentence has citation (disrupts readability)
✓ Cite major claims, general knowledge doesn't need citation
```
