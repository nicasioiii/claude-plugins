# HARO Mastery: Daily Workflow for Media Links

## What Is HARO?
Help A Reporter Out = journalists request expert quotes daily. You respond → your response gets published → backlink to your site.

**Why It's Powerful**:
- High authority links (DR 60-85 typical)
- Brand-building (quote attributed to you)
- Free (no cost for links)
- Sustainable (not going away)

---

## Part 1: Setup (One-Time, 30 minutes)

### Step 1: Sign Up for HARO
1. Visit `helpareporter.com`
2. Click "Sign Up"
3. Choose "Source" (you're an expert, not a journalist)
4. Enter email + expert category
5. Confirm email

### Step 2: Select Relevant Queries Categories
HARO sends 3 daily emails (morning, afternoon, evening).

**Categories** (pick 3-5 most relevant):
- Business/Finance
- Health/Medicine
- Technology
- Marketing/PR
- Legal
- Real Estate
- Lifestyle

**Example**: SEO agency → pick "Technology" + "Marketing/PR" + "Business"

### Step 3: Set Up Email Filter
Create Gmail label "HARO Queries":
1. Open email filter rules
2. Filter by: From = "reply@helpareporter.com"
3. Label as "HARO Queries"
4. Enable star/notification so you don't miss queries

## Part 2: Daily Process (20-30 minutes/day)

### Workflow for Each HARO Email

**Step 1: Scan Query Headlines** (5 min)
Read 50+ query headlines. Note 3-5 you can answer well.

**Red Flags to Avoid**:
- Queries about products you're selling (looks promotional)
- Queries from bloggers (low authority)
- Queries from websites with no domain authority (waste of time)

**Green Lights**:
- Queries from major publications (Forbes, TechCrunch, etc.)
- Queries related to your expertise
- Queries asking for unique perspective/data

### Step 2: Identify Your Best Answers (5 min)
For each query you'll answer:
1. Note the journalist's publication
2. Note what they're asking
3. Plan your response (unique angle)

### Step 3: Write Responses (10-15 min total)
**Key Rule**: Keep responses SHORT (2-3 paragraphs max)

**Response Structure**:
```
[Opening sentence answering the question directly]

[1-2 sentences providing unique insight/data]

[Optional: 1 sentence about your business if relevant]

[Sign-off with name, title, company, phone/email]
```

**Example Response** (for "What's the biggest 2024 SEO trend?"):
```
"The biggest 2024 trend is AI-powered SEO. Sites using AI to identify content gaps and optimize internal linking are seeing 30-50% faster ranking improvements."

"We've tracked 200 websites this year, and the top 10% all implemented AI for keyword research and content gap analysis. It's no longer optional."

"John Smith, SEO Director, Example.com, john@example.com"
```

### Step 4: Respond to Query (2 min)
1. Find query details (journalist email, deadline, publication)
2. Send response via email or form
3. Include: Name, title, company, contact info, credentials

**Pro Tip**: Mention specific credentials (e.g., "Helped 500+ companies rank #1") if relevant.

**Deadline**: Most queries have 24-48 hour deadline. Respond ASAP (within 2 hours gets best pick rate).

## Part 4: Best Practices for Higher Pick Rate

### Rule 1: Respond Early
Journalists pick first responses. Respond within 1 hour = best chance.

### Rule 2: Be Specific
- BAD: "Yes, SEO is important in 2024"
- GOOD: "The top-ranking sites in SaaS are using internal linking + topical clusters to get 3x faster rankings"

### Rule 3: Offer Unique Angle
- Most responses are generic
- If you provide contrarian/specific insight → you get picked

### Rule 4: Mention Publication
Reference why you respect their publication in opening.

Example: "I love your coverage of marketing trends; here's our data on..."

### Rule 5: Make It Easy
- Clear name/company/contact info (no hunting)
- One 2-3 paragraph response (not 5 paragraphs)
- Phone number (journalists prefer calling for quick follow-up)

### Rule 6: Avoid Self-Promotion
- DO: "We surveyed 500 companies and found..."
- DON'T: "Our product helps companies do X"

Journalists hate overtly promotional responses. Share findings, not pitches.

## Part 5: After Being Quoted

### Track Your Links
1. When article publishes, note the link
2. Add to your "backlink tracker" spreadsheet
3. Track: Publication, DR, date, anchor text

### Monitoring
1. Set Google Alert for your name + HARO (notifies when article published)
2. Or check HARO profile for published articles (sometimes listed)
3. Use SEMrush backlink tracker to see when link appears

### Follow Up
If journalist quotes you:
1. Thank them (brief email)
2. Share the article on social
3. Reach out again for future queries (relationship building)

## Part 6: Advanced HARO Strategy

### Multi-Angle Responses
For same query, respond multiple times with different angles:

**Query**: "Best productivity tools for remote teams"

**Response 1**: "From communication perspective, X tool excels because..."
**Response 2**: "From documentation/knowledge base perspective, Y tool is best because..."
**Response 3**: "From project management perspective, Z tool stands out because..."

Different journalists may pick different responses. Increases chances.

### Build Relationships
After being quoted:
1. Note journalist's name
2. Follow their articles on LinkedIn
3. When you see future query from them → prioritize response
4. Over time: Journalist knows your name, picks your response first

### Create "Quote-Worthy" Content
Write articles/research specifically designed for HARO:
- Survey data ("We surveyed 1000 marketers and found...")
- Contrarian takes ("Everyone says X, but our data shows Y...")
- Case studies with metrics ("We improved X by 150%")

### Leverage Publications Strategically
Some publications are tier 1 (Forbes, WSJ, FastCompany):
- If query from tier 1 → prioritize, spend more time
- Can mention "As quoted in Forbes" for credibility

## Part 8: Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Mistake 1: Responding to Low-Quality Queries
Check journalist publication before responding. Verify:
- Publication has real domain authority
- Not a spam request
- Not a scam (HARO is generally safe but verify)

### Mistake 2: Generic Responses
Journalists see hundreds of generic answers. Stand out with specific insights.

### Mistake 3: Being Promotional
Journalists immediately reject overtly promotional responses. Focus on sharing knowledge.

### Mistake 4: Forgetting Contact Info
Include: Name, title, company, phone, email. Make it easy.

### Mistake 5: Missing Deadlines
Respond within first 2 hours. After 24 hours, chance drops significantly.

### Mistake 6: Not Following Up
One-time HARO responder ≠ long-term success. Build relationships with journalists.
