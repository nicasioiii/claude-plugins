## The Five Framings / Visual Grammar (E05c Bob Krist)

These five framings form the "grammar" of visual storytelling. Every shot you take falls into one of these categories. Varying between them creates visual rhythm.

| Framing | Purpose | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Establishing / Long Shot** | Scene-setting; shows where action takes place | Opening of any sequence; transitions between locations; context |
| **Medium Shot** | Closer context; people are recognizable | Standard talking head; conversational content; "default" shot |
| **Portrait / Two Shot** | Person or two people at conversational distance | Interviews; dialogue between two subjects; relationship dynamics |
| **Close-Up / Extreme Close-Up** | Detail, emotion, editing transitions | Emotional moments; product details; emphasis; reaction shots |
| **Point of View (POV)** | Character's perspective; immersion | Tutorial steps; "walk with me" content; experiential moments; often over-the-shoulder |

### Framing Progression for Short-Form
1. **Hook (0-3 seconds):** Close-up or extreme close-up -- creates intimacy, urgency, stops the scroll
2. **Context (3-10 seconds):** Medium shot -- establishes who is speaking and general setting
3. **Body (10+ seconds):** Alternate between medium, close-up, and B-roll inserts for visual variety
4. **CTA (final seconds):** Return to medium or close-up for personal connection

## Camera Angles and Their Emotional Impact (E04a, E01)

| Angle | Effect | Use For |
|---|---|---|
| **Eye level** | Neutral, conversational, equal | Standard talking head, educational content |
| **Slight low angle** | Authority, power, confidence | Expert positioning, motivational content |
| **Slight high angle** | Vulnerability, approachability | Personal stories, confessional content |
| **Head-on (straight to camera)** | Direct connection, intimacy | Hooks, CTAs, important revelations |
| **Three-quarter angle** | Dynamic, cinematic feel | Main body content, storytelling |
| **Over-the-shoulder** | POV, subjective experience | Tutorials, "walk with me," demonstrations |

### Common Beginner Mistakes with Angles (E04a)
- Shooting from below chin level (unflattering nostril shot)
- Never changing angle throughout entire video (visual monotony)
- Tilting camera instead of moving camera position (looks amateur)
- Placing camera too far away for hook section (lacks intimacy)

## Camera Movements (E04a, E05c)

| Movement | Effect | Application |
|---|---|---|
| **Static (locked off)** | Stability, authority, focus | Talking head, educational points |
| **Slow push-in** | Building tension, increasing focus | Revealing important information, emotional moments |
| **Slow pull-out** | Reveal, context expansion | Establishing after a close-up, broadening perspective |
| **Pan (horizontal)** | Following action, surveying environment | Showing a workspace, following movement |
| **Tilt (vertical)** | Reveal height/scale, dramatic effect | Product reveals, before/after |
| **Handheld/walk** | Energy, authenticity, "in the moment" | BTS content, vlogs, casual delivery |

### The Walking Shot (E05c)
- Character moving from point A to point B is universally usable B-roll
- Creates visual energy and a sense of progression
- Can be used as transition between sequences
- Shoot from multiple angles: behind, beside, in front, low angle

## Building Sequences (E05c)

A sequence transforms static documentation into dynamic visual storytelling. Break any action into a series of steps:

### Sequence Construction Method
1. **Wide shot** -- establish where the action takes place
2. **Medium shot** -- show the person beginning the action
3. **Close-up of action** -- hands, tools, details of what is happening
4. **Close-up of face** -- capture emotion/concentration
5. **POV shot** -- show what the person sees
6. **Detail/insert** -- specific objects or results
7. **Wide pull-out** -- complete the sequence, show the finished state

### Example: Making Coffee (for B-roll)
- Wide: kitchen establishing shot
- Medium: person reaching for coffee beans
- Close-up: beans being scooped
- Close-up: steam rising from pour
- POV: looking down into cup
- Insert: timer on espresso machine
- Wide: person taking first sip at counter

## Passage of Time Technique (E05c)

### The Enter-and-Leave-Frame Method
- Let subjects enter and leave your frame between shots
- Audiences conditioned by movies and TV: subject leaving frame = time has passed
- When subject appears in a new frame, audience understands time elapsed (instant or years)
- This technique avoids the need for explicit "time passing" transitions

### Application for Social Content
- Film yourself walking INTO frame at the start of each scene/location
- Film yourself walking OUT of frame at the end
- In editing, cutting between these creates natural time progression
- Works for day-in-the-life content, tutorials, and transformation content

## B-Roll System: The Complete Framework

### Daily Filming Habit (E01 Mino Lee)
- Stack B-roll filming with existing routines: morning coffee, skincare, journaling, commute, work
- Film 4-5 clips each morning -- done by noon with fresh daily material
- Remove friction: dedicated tripod spots with preset angles at home and office

### Folder Organization System (E03a Gusten Sun)
Organize B-roll library by these categories for instant retrieval during editing:

| Category | Example Clips |
|---|---|
| **Working** | Typing, writing, desk setup, screen close-ups |
| **Thinking** | Looking out window, coffee contemplation, reading |
| **Moving** | Walking, driving, commuting, exercising |
| **Success/celebration** | Fist pump, smile, achievement moments |
| **Struggle/effort** | Deep focus, frustration, long hours |
| **Environment** | Office, home, city, nature, travel |
| **Objects/products** | Books, tools, screens, equipment, props |
| **Process** | Step-by-step actions related to your niche |

### B-Roll Editing Rules (E01)
- Each clip: 1-2 seconds maximum on screen
- Target: 30-50 clips per minute of finished video
- Front-load in first 5 seconds (5 different clips in 5 seconds for hooks)
- Match B-roll to what you are saying -- visual must support or illustrate the narration
- Build reusable "S-tier" clips that work across multiple videos

## Minimum Viable Shot List for Social Video (E05c, E01, adapted)

### For a Talking-Head Short-Form Video
1. Hook close-up (face, direct to camera)
2. Medium shot for body content
3. 3-5 B-roll clips matching key points
4. CTA medium/close-up shot

### For a Story-Driven Short-Form Video
1. Establishing shot (where are we?)
2. Medium shot of subject in action
3. Close-up of the action (hands/detail)
4. Close-up of subject's face (emotion)
5. POV shot (subject's perspective)
6. Detail shots/cutaways for editing flexibility
7. Walking/movement shot for transitions
8. B-roll that directly supports spoken hook message (E05a)
9. Visuals with enough motion + color to stop the scroll
10. Visuals switched every 1-2 seconds during the hook section

## The Complete Short-Form Video Production Sequence (E05 Cross-Source)

1. **Content strategy** -- define niche, IVP, content pillars
2. **Idea generation** -- research trending topics and angles
3. **Idea research** -- validate with niche creator analysis
4. **Storytelling** -- identify mission, conflict, transformation, vulnerability
5. **Scriptwriting** -- rapid context + contrast hook; match scripting level to section type
6. **Recording** -- multiple takes for A-roll hooks; capture B-roll for visual hooks
7. **Editing** -- hybrid style (retention techniques for hooks, story editing for body); 3-part alignment check
8. **Posting** -- platform-specific visual formatting
9. **Growth/Analytics** -- monitor average watch time as primary hook metric
10. **Monetization** -- trust (built through story depth) enables monetization
