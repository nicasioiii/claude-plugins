# Hook Writing Process

## The 10-Minute Hook Writing Framework (E05a Kallaway, 5 Steps)

### Step 0: Understand Your Visuals
Before writing, check if you already have sourced B-roll that could drive the hook. If yes, build the hook around that visual asset. If no, you will create the visual after writing.

### Step 1: Write the Spoken Hook

**Easy Mode**: Pick a validated spoken hook archetype from the templates vault (ref-hook-templates.md) or from research. Copy the structure, adapt to your topic.

**Normal Mode**: Write from scratch using two questions:
1. What context must I communicate so the viewer can decide to opt in?
2. What is the contrast between what they believe and what I believe?

Can I communicate both in a single sentence? If not, use 2 sentences maximum.

**Delivery tips**: Good pace/energy, clean high-quality audio, fewer words is better (add words only to decrease misunderstanding), redo if you trip over words.

### Step 2: Write the Text Hook
- Must elicit curiosity and ideally an emotional reaction (shock/surprise)
- Use fewest words possible
- Font as big as possible without overwhelming
- Easy to read when overlaid on visuals
- Super easy-to-read font
- Keep text near "eye level" of subject
- Do not block overlays; ensure visible in feed view
- Cheat code: white or colored background behind text

### Step 3: Build the Visual Hook

**If using Creator A-roll:**
- The more attractive and happy you look, the better
- Include initial motion (camera close then lean away)
- "Pope in the Pool" passive action: doing something passively (like makeup or cooking) while talking -- holds base attention

**If using B-roll:**
- Direct attention with arrows, circles, different colors
- Switch visuals every 1-2 seconds (each switch stuns viewer into resetting for 10-20ms)
- Too aggressive = visual confusion = bounce
- High contrast, high color, high motion

### Step 4: Record the Video
Record multiple takes if visual hook = only Creator A-roll. E01 (Mino Lee) recommends recording the hook 5-20 times to find the best energy and delivery.

### Step 5: Edit the Hook
Check for two things:
1. **Comprehension via alignment**: Do all hook components feel aligned, easy to comprehend, create curiosity loop?
2. **Pacing**: Is spoken speed + visual switching fast enough to hold attention?

## Hook Research Process for Niche Validation (E05a)

### Step-by-Step Process

1. **Build a Creator List**: 20-30 accounts in your niche targeting your ideal viewer avatar. Criteria: 50K+ followers, 50+ posts
2. **Where to find them**: Start with one you know -> their "Following" list -> find 20 more. Or: fresh account, type niche search terms, scroll and like on-topic posts -> 15 min and Explore feed is full
3. **Keep list in a note or spreadsheet**
4. **Use sortfeed.com** (Chrome extension) to sort any creator's Reels by views
5. **Filter**: Last 200 videos (proxy for 12 months), sort by views
6. **Analyze**: Open top videos in new tabs. For each, document what about the hook caught your eye across all 4 component buckets (text, visual, spoken, audio)
7. **Optional**: Store in Notion database for future reference

**Frequency**: Run this process every 2-4 weeks to assess what is working now that was not before. Hook trends shift rapidly on social platforms.

## CGOVE Revision Workflow (E01 Mino Lee)

### When to Use
After writing AND filming the hook. Never during creative flow. Right-brain creates, left-brain analyzes.

### The 5-Check Process

**Check 1: Clarity (C)**
- Is the hook simple and easy to understand?
- Could someone outside your niche follow it?
- Does it leave ONE clear question in the viewer's mind, not multiple?
- Are you using simple language, not jargon?
- Test: Read it to a friend who knows nothing about your niche. Do they get it?

**Check 2: Gap (G)**
- Is something left out to create curiosity?
- Do you know exactly what question you are creating in the viewer's mind?
- Does the hook make clear what question you are solving WITHOUT giving the answer?
- The gap should be specific, not vague

**Check 3: Originality (O)**
- Will the viewer assume they already know the answer?
- Is there novelty? Novelty bias is critical for informational content
- Ways to add novelty: explain your unique experience learning it, cite a recent finding, add a surprising number or stat
- If the hook sounds like 100 other hooks on the same topic, add your twist

**Check 4: Value/Trust (V)**
- Does the viewer believe you will provide something valuable for their time?
- Is social proof embedded subtly? ("In my time growing three accounts to 100K...")
- Is the pace fast enough to signal high value-per-second?
- Speed is a subtle trust signal: if you take 10 seconds for a simple point, they assume low value

**Check 5: Emotion (E)**
- Did you make them feel something?
- Visuals, scripting, and tonality all contribute to emotion
- Without emotion, even a good question will not hold attention
- Fear, excitement, curiosity, surprise, empathy -- any strong emotion works

## Hook Three-Step Method (E01 Mino Lee, simplified)

### Step 1: Copy What Works
Use proven hook templates (ref-hook-templates.md). Pick a template, adapt to your niche. Templates force creative thinking because they do not fit perfectly -- that is the point.

### Step 2: Trial and Error
- Try different tonalities for the same hook (record 5+ takes)
- Try different visual approaches (fish-eye, wide angle, close-up, 0.5x iPhone lens)
- Try different camera angles
- Watch your own video as if you are a stranger

### Step 3: Revise Using CGOVE
Apply the 5-check process above.

## Trial Reels A/B Testing (E05a, E04a)

### Instagram Trial Reels Method
Create 4 hook variants for the same video concept. Publish as Trial Reels (Instagram feature) to test which hook performs best without cluttering your main feed.

### What to Test
- Same spoken hook, different text overlays
- Same topic, different archetype (Fortuneteller vs. Contrarian)
- Same script, different visual approach (A-roll vs. B-roll lead)
- Same content, different first sentence (you-framing vs. stated contrast)

### Metrics to Evaluate
- Average watch time: If higher than your account average = hook was more effective
- Higher watch time coincides with more views and better overall metrics
- Compare hook variants head-to-head over same audience sample

## Hook-to-Platform Adaptation (E05a, E02)

### Universal Psychology, Platform-Specific Execution
Good hook psychology is the same on every platform. Viewer psychology does not change. Customize the visual approach and format per platform, not the hook psychology.

### Platform Adaptations

**Instagram / TikTok:**
- Text hook on screen is critical (90% watch without sound)
- Visual changes every 0.5-2 seconds in hook
- First 1-3 seconds decide everything
- Captions mandatory

**LinkedIn:**
- Text-only hooks (first 200 characters before "See more")
- No visual/audio component for text posts
- Preview in tools before publishing (off by 2 words can demolish performance)
- For video: same hook psychology, but thumbnail + first frame matter more

**YouTube:**
- Hook works WITH thumbnail (complementary, not duplicative)
- First 10 seconds are crucial
- Viewer chose to click -- hook must validate decision to stay
- Can be slightly longer (5-10 seconds) since viewer is already invested

## Common Reasons Hooks Flop (E05a)

1. "In my opinion..." wastes most valuable real estate (first words)
2. "Me/I" framing forces viewer to guess if value is for them
3. Pace is extremely slow
4. Context is unclear after first sentence
5. Components are misaligned (spoken, visual, text telling different stories)
6. Text hook too long or not on screen long enough to read
7. Multiple disconnected concepts crammed into hook (confusion)
8. AI voice sounds fake, poor pacing/intonation
9. No contrast -- no gap between expectation and reality
10. Speed to value too slow (contrast does not appear until 3rd+ sentence)

## Common Reasons Hooks Succeed (E05a)

1. First line gives immediate context on what video is about
2. All 4 components perfectly aligned
3. Strong contrast between common belief and contrarian reality
4. "You/your" framing makes viewer feel spoken to directly
5. Visual matches spoken hook (saying "squat form" while showing squat examples)
6. Text hook acts as backup: if viewer gets bored with spoken, text re-engages
7. Universal framework that always works: "If you want to improve [X problem], do [Y thing]"

## Salesy Hooks (E05a Q&A)

The "too salesy" line is about predicted incoming value. If the expected value exceeds the push, viewers stay. Hooks that feel salesy are ones where the push (selling energy) exceeds the predicted value (what viewer expects to get). Solution: lead with massive value, make the sale feel like a natural next step.

## Physical Product Hooks (E05a)

Show the outcome (or emotion from the outcome) that the viewer gets when using the product. Do not lead with product features -- lead with the transformation.
