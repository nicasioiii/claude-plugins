# Storytelling Frameworks

## 4 Essential Story Elements (E05b Nicklas Christl)

Every great story contains:
1. **Mission**: What the character is trying to achieve (clear goal)
2. **Conflict**: Obstacles preventing the mission (creates tension)
3. **Character Transformation**: How the character changes through the journey
4. **Vulnerability**: Raw, authentic moments that make the character relatable

Without all four, stories feel incomplete. Mission without conflict = boring. Conflict without transformation = pointless. Transformation without vulnerability = unbelievable.

## Story Structure: The Emotional Arc

### 6 Stages of an Effective Arc (E05b)
Build anticipation, uncertainty, and emotional investment. Raise the right questions, then deliver satisfying resolutions.

**Tension** = anticipation of what might happen (built through unanswered questions)
**Resolution** = satisfying payoff to the tension

### The Three-Act Challenge Structure (E01 Mino Lee)
- **Act 1 (Setup)**: Get people bought into the goal. State the challenge clearly
- **Act 2 (Conflict)**: Introduce the obstacle. Stack difficulties. A video without challenge is super boring. Frame ALL events around the goal
- **Act 3 (Resolution)**: Binary result. Make it CLEAR. If you won: celebrate. If you lost: use music/storytelling to show genuine disappointment

### The Hero's Journey Applied (E05c Bob Krist)
Joseph Campbell's archetypal structure:
1. Protagonist called to make a journey (physical, emotional, or spiritual)
2. Typically reluctant but embarks, aided by mentors/friends
3. Faces and overcomes obstacles, forcing change (usually for the better)
4. Returns to share knowledge gained

Audiences are hardwired to look for this structure. Apply it even to simple subjects to keep audiences engaged.

## Story vs. Retention Editing Spectrum (E05b)

### Core Framework: Two Editing Intentions
- **Story editing**: Intention = tell the most impactful story with strongest emotions
- **Retention editing**: Intention = maintain viewer engagement to increase views

### 5 Characteristics of Story Editing
1. In-depth dialogue revealing character
2. Thoughtful, varied pacing (sometimes slow, sometimes fast)
3. Subtle foreshadowing (revealing/hiding context at the right time)
4. Visual storytelling (shot sizes, angles, compositions convey emotion)
5. Realistic, layered sound design for immersion

### 5 Characteristics of Retention Editing
1. Hyper dialogue (bare minimum, filler removed)
2. Rapid pacing (short scenes, quick cuts, never slows)
3. Directly stated goals and stakes (told early, repeated throughout)
4. Flashy visuals (dynamic footage, FPV, text graphics, animations)
5. Strong/amplified sound design (upbeat music, loud SFX)

### The Adaptive Hybrid Model (E05b -- Contrarian)

**Factor 1: Natural engagement of the story section**
- Naturally interesting section with strong emotions -> lean story editing (let it breathe)
- Less interesting but contextually needed section -> use retention techniques

**Factor 2: Timing within the video**
- Beginning -> lean retention editing (short attention spans, viewer not invested)
- Once viewer is invested -> shift toward story editing
- Story attention builds over time; retention attention fades. Use both to get benefits of each

### Two Types of Retention (E05b)
1. **Expensive retention** (story): Harder to achieve, viewer feels excited and rewarded
2. **Cheap retention** (flashy techniques): Easier to achieve, viewer feels manipulated

"The better you are at storytelling, the less you have to use cheap retention hacks."

### Mr. Beast Trend Shift (E05b)
Even Mr. Beast is moving toward more storytelling elements after audience feedback. Increasing TV consumption of YouTube = more patient viewers wanting story-driven content.

## "Show, Don't Tell" Levels (E05b)

Three levels of experience:
1. **Telling** = weakest (narrating what happened). "He was sad."
2. **Showing** = stronger (demonstrating visually). Show the character's face, body language, environment
3. **Experiencing** = strongest (making viewer feel they are there). POV shot, immersive audio, first-person perspective

When to use which: Telling for transitions and context. Showing for key emotional beats. Experiencing for climactic moments. Match level to importance of the story moment.

## How to Find Stories (E05b, 3 Strategies)

1. **Draw from personal experience**: Your own struggles, failures, and wins are the most authentic material
2. **Observe your environment**: Untold stories surround you daily. Train yourself to notice them
3. **Find the unique angle**: You cannot cover everything about a broad topic

### 4-Step Process to Find Your Angle (E05b)
1. Identify the broader topic
2. Find what is unique or different about your perspective
3. Narrow the focus to create depth, clarity, and intrigue
4. Test whether the angle creates enough curiosity

## Physical and Emotional Journey (E05b)

Every character has both:
- **Physical journey**: External actions, events, travel, tasks
- **Emotional journey**: Internal changes, realizations, feelings

The combination brings structure and transformation. A story with only physical journey = documentary. A story with only emotional journey = therapy session. Both together = compelling narrative.

## Storytelling for Short-Form Video (E01)

### The Neurochemical Journey
- Dopamine: anticipation, excitement, reward
- Cortisol: tension, stress, urgency
- Oxytocin: warmth, trust, connection
- Serotonin: satisfaction, pride, well-being
- Endorphins: relief, joy, humor

Making viewers feel sad/pain FIRST enables a GREATER height of dopamine later. Without the valley, there is no peak.

### Emotional Spikes
Insert "I realized something" or "something bad happened" or "then everything changed" to create emotional spikes in otherwise flat storytelling. Without these spikes, you are just listing events.

### The Emotion-to-Editing Connection (E01)
Match editing choices to intended emotion at each story beat:
- High saturation = dopamine/excitement
- Warm tones = oxytocin/connection
- Simple/calm visuals = sadness/empathy
- Musical shifts = mood transitions (recapture attention)

## Story Revision Checklist

After writing any story-based script, verify:

1. **Mission clear?** Can viewer state the goal in one sentence?
2. **Conflict present?** Is there a real obstacle, not just a setup?
3. **Transformation visible?** Does the character (you) change by the end?
4. **Vulnerability included?** Is there a raw, honest moment?
5. **Outside-niche clarity?** Would someone unfamiliar with your niche understand?
6. **Specific details?** Numbers, names, dates grounding the story?
7. **Past continuous tense?** Open loops via "was doing" language?
8. **Emotional variety?** Journey through multiple emotions, not just one?
9. **Clear ending?** Action step, moral, or punchline?
10. **"You/your" framing?** Viewer feels spoken to, not talked at?

## Documentary/Cinematic Storytelling Principles (E05c Bob Krist)

### Story Drives Every Technical Decision
Story informs gear choice, shot selection, music, and editing style. "You wouldn't pick your tools before you know what the job is." A clear storyline determines if people engage or stop watching.

### The Storyteller's Role
You are the guide, not the hero. Your role = curate the experience for the viewer. Personal storytelling approaches create deeper viewer connection. Always give your story a personal touch.

### Passage of Time Technique (E05c)
Let subjects enter and leave your frame. Audiences conditioned by movies: subject leaving frame = time has passed. When they appear in new frame, audience understands time has elapsed. Use for vlogs and day-in-the-life content.
