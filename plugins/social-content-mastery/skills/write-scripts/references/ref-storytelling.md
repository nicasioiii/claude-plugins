# Storytelling Reference

## 4 ELEMENTS OF A GREAT STORY (Nicklas, E03)

### 1. Mission
Character pursues a clear goal. Without a mission, there is no story -- just a sequence of events. The mission creates forward momentum and gives the audience something to root for.

### 2. Conflict
Obstacles block the mission. Without conflict, the story is boring. The bigger the obstacle, the more compelling the story. Conflict creates tension, and tension creates attention.

### 3. Character Transformation
The character changes through the journey. The person at the end is not the same person at the beginning. This is what gives a story meaning beyond entertainment.

### 4. Vulnerability
Authentic emotional exposure creates connection. Vulnerability is what transforms information into a story. Without it, you're just reporting events.

**Key insight (Nicklas, E03):** Making it personal doesn't mean making it about you -- it means filtering universal truths through your specific experience.

## 3 STRATEGIES TO FIND STORIES (Nicklas, E03)

### Strategy 1: Your Life as a Story

Draw from personal experiences. The most powerful stories come from your own transformation -- the gap between who you were and who you became.

**Discovery questions:**
- What was the hardest moment of the past year?
- What did I believe 2 years ago that I no longer believe?
- What's a mistake I made that taught me something valuable?
- What moment changed the direction of my life?
- "If you care, they care."

### Strategy 2: Look for the Heroes

Find inspiring characters worth documenting. Not all stories need to be yours. Sometimes the most compelling content comes from telling someone else's story.

**Where to find heroes:**
- Your clients/customers
- People in your community
- Historical figures in your niche
- Friends and family with interesting journeys

### Strategy 3: Explore a Global Topic

Pursue curiosity about world issues and topics. Connect broader themes to your niche through a unique angle.

## FOCUS ON ONE ANGLE (Nicklas, E03)

You can't cover everything. Narrow a broad topic through one unique angle.

### 4-Step Process

1. **Identify broad topic** -- What general area are you exploring?
2. **Find your unique perspective** -- What's YOUR take on this?
3. **Check for depth/intrigue** -- Does this angle create curiosity?
4. **Ensure clarity** -- Can the audience follow this angle without confusion?

One angle = depth. Multiple angles = shallow confusion. Especially in short-form content where you have 15-60 seconds.

## THE 6-STAGE STORY ARC (Nicklas, E03)

### Stage 1: Setup / Status Quo
Establish the world as it is. Give the audience enough context to understand what's normal. This is the baseline against which all change is measured.

### Stage 2: Inciting Incident
Something happens that disrupts the status quo. This is the moment that sets the story in motion. Without it, there's no reason to keep watching.

### Stage 3: Rising Action / Complications
Obstacles increase. The mission gets harder. Tension builds. Each complication should feel like it raises the stakes.

### Stage 4: Climax / Crisis
The peak of tension. The moment of greatest challenge or revelation. Everything has been building to this point.

### Stage 5: Resolution
The crisis is resolved (or not). The answer to the question the story has been asking is revealed.

### Stage 6: New Normal
The world after the story. How has the character/situation changed? This is where transformation is crystallized.

### Tension and Resolution Balance

- Tension = raising questions in viewer's mind (anticipation, uncertainty, emotional investment)
- Resolution = delivering satisfying answers to those questions
- Structure serves tension. Every story needs rising tension and payoff.
- Too much tension without resolution = frustration
- Too much resolution without tension = boring

## SHOW, DON'T TELL (Nicklas, E03)

### Levels of Experience (Lowest to Highest Immersion)

| Level | Technique | Engagement |
|---|---|---|
| Level 1 | Pure narration -- "I was sad" | Lowest |
| Level 2 | Narration with descriptors -- "I sat on the cold floor, staring at the wall" | Low-Medium |
| Level 3 | Dialogue -- "She said, 'I don't think this is going to work'" | Medium |
| Level 4 | Action -- showing the character doing something that reveals emotion | Medium-High |
| Level 5 | Full visual experience -- the viewer sees, hears, and feels without narration | Highest |

### When to Use Each Level

- Level 1-2: Setup, context, transitions (low-stakes moments)
- Level 3-4: Key emotional moments, conflict, turning points
- Level 5: Climax, most impactful moments

### Macro Perspective

Consider the overall balance of showing vs. telling across your entire piece. A video that is 100% narration is disengaging. A video that is 100% visuals may lack clarity. The best content moves fluidly between levels based on story needs.

## PHYSICAL AND EMOTIONAL JOURNEY (Nicklas, E03)

Every story has two layers:

### Physical Journey
What happens externally -- events, actions, locations, circumstances. This is the plot.

### Emotional Journey
What happens internally -- feelings, realizations, growth, doubt. This is the meaning.

### How They Interact

- Both layers are essential but should intersect at key moments
- Common mistake: All physical, no emotional journey -- viewer sees events but doesn't feel anything
- Use pauses, reflections, voice tone changes to progress the emotional side
- The emotional journey is what makes the viewer care about the physical events

### Application to Short-Form Content

Even in a 30-second video:
- Physical: "I got my first 10K view video" (event)
- Emotional: "After months of doubting myself, this was the moment I believed it was possible" (meaning)

## VISUAL GRAMMAR (Story Hero, E03)

### 5 Shot Framings and Their Emotional Purpose

| Framing | Shows | Creates |
|---|---|---|
| Extreme Wide | Full environment, tiny character | Loneliness, scale, context |
| Wide | Full character in environment | Setting, atmosphere, journey |
| Medium | Character from waist up | Conversation, casual engagement |
| Close-Up | Character's face | Emotion, intimacy, tension |
| Extreme Close-Up | Eyes, hands, details | Intensity, vulnerability, emphasis |

### Movement and Emotion

- Static shots: stability, reflection, calm
- Slow movement: building tension, contemplation
- Fast movement: urgency, chaos, excitement
- Tracking shots: following the character's journey literally
- Handheld: raw, authentic, documentary feel

## AUDIO PILLARS (Story Hero, E03)

### Three Audio Layers

1. **Dialogue/Narration** -- The spoken content (your voice, interviews, voiceover)
2. **Music** -- Background music that sets emotional tone
3. **Sound Design** -- Ambient sounds, SFX, room tone

### Emotional Matching

- Music should match the emotional beat of the story
- Quiet moments: minimal or no music
- Building tension: rising music, increasing complexity
- Resolution: full, satisfying musical completion
- Contrast: occasionally using unexpected music for impact

### Audio Quality Priority

Audio quality is the single most impactful production element (consensus across Sunny E08, Frabrand E01, Mino Lee E01). Bad video with good audio = watchable. Good video with bad audio = unwatchable.

## SCRIPT WRITING HABITS (Nicklas, E03)

### 7 Writing Habits

1. Optimize workspace (clean, organized, distraction-free)
2. Remove distractions (phone away, browser tabs closed)
3. Establish routine (same time, same place)
4. Just get started (perfectionism is procrastination)
5. Be easy on yourself (first draft is supposed to be rough)
6. Stay focused (finish one section before starting another)
7. Set deadlines (open-ended projects never finish)

### 4-Step Writing Process

1. **Research** your topic thoroughly
2. **Outline** the structure (story arc, key points, flow)
3. **Write** the script (get it down without editing)
4. **Edit** for clarity (cut, compress, strengthen)

### Levels of Scripting

Choose based on comfort and project type:
- **Bullet points:** Key points only, speak naturally between them
- **Outline:** Section headers with 1-2 sentence notes each
- **Full script:** Every word written out
- **Hybrid:** Full script for hook and CTA, bullet points for body

## MINO LEE SCRIPTING BENCHMARK

Mino spent 30 minutes minimum scripting each video for his first 2 years. This discipline helped him go from 0 to 100K followers in 30 days on his personal development TikTok account. The time investment in scripting is what separates consistent performers from inconsistent ones.

## HOOK EDITING PRINCIPLES FOR SCRIPTS (Mino Lee, E01)

- Cut out redundant wording from first 3 sentences -- they're the most sensitive part
- Be more specific in niche ways (e.g., "my Korean mom" instead of "my mom")
- The first 3 sentences are where retention hangs by a thread -- strangers aren't sold on you yet
- After revising, read aloud to find hiccups in flow
